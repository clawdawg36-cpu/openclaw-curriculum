AI Explorers

Build Your AI Assistant, Piece by Piece

A Complete Guide for Homeschool Families  |  Version 1.0 — March 2026

Welcome, Homeschool Parents!

You've found something genuinely different. AI Explorers isn't a slideshow about robots or a quiz on AI vocabulary — it's a project-based curriculum where your child builds a real, working AI assistant from scratch, one capability at a time, across seven modules.

By the end, your child won't just know about AI. They'll own one. They built it. They named it. And that changes everything.

What Is AI Explorers?

AI Explorers is a 7-module curriculum designed for students in grades 6–8 (bright 5th graders and motivated 9th graders thrive in it too). Each module adds one new "superpower" to the AI assistant your child is building:

ModuleWhat They BuildSuperpower Gained
1Deploy an agent, name it, write its personalityIdentity
2Connect image and audio modelsPerception (Vision + Hearing)
3Write a custom skill with domain knowledgeKnowledge
4Train a real machine learning modelLearning
5Connect the agent to a live channelVoice / Communication
6Add guardrails, bias checks, usage policiesEthics & Responsibility
7Present the agent at an AI ExpoSynthesis & Presentation

The through-line is simple: "What can your agent do now that it couldn't before?" Every module answers that question with something your child actually built.

Who Is This For?

AI Explorers works best for students who are curious about technology but don't need to be coders, who learn by doing, and who are ready for a semester-long project with meaningful output. No prior coding experience is required for Modules 1–3. Python familiarity is helpful (not required) for Module 4.

Session Structure

Each session works well in three parts: Warm-up (5–10 min, review what was built last time), Core work (30–45 min, lesson/activity/lab), and Journal (5–10 min, update the Agent Design Journal).

Module-by-Module Overview

Module 1: What Is AI? — Identity

Duration: 2–4 weeks  |  Prerequisite: None

Your child starts from zero and ends up with a running AI agent they named themselves. They explore what AI actually is (not the sci-fi version), set up OpenClaw, and write the personality file that defines who their agent is.

What they do: Install and deploy OpenClaw · Name their agent and write its personality · Run their first real AI conversation

What they learn: What an AI agent actually is · How AI systems are structured (models, tools, memory) · Introduction to computational thinking

Key lab: "Deploy Your OpenClaw" — the moment their agent goes from zero to alive.

Module 2: Give Your Agent Senses — Perception

Duration: 2–4 weeks  |  Prerequisite: Module 1

The agent can talk. Now it can see and hear. Your child connects their agent to image recognition and audio classification models from Hugging Face — a real ML model library used by professional engineers.

What they do: Connect an image description model · Connect an audio classification model · Test perception with real photos and sounds

What they learn: What multimodal AI means · How pre-trained models work · What an API is

Key lab: Upload a photo, watch their agent describe it.

Module 3: Give Your Agent a Knowledge Base — Knowledge

Duration: 2–4 weeks  |  Prerequisite: Module 2

Your child picks a topic they love — marine biology, Minecraft, basketball stats, local history — and teaches it to their agent. They write a custom OpenClaw "skill" with specialized knowledge and reasoning logic.

What they do: Choose a domain (their choice!) · Write a custom skill file · Test their agent's expertise in that domain

What they learn: How knowledge representation works in AI · Introduction to structured reasoning · What makes AI genuinely useful vs. just conversational

Key lab: "Build Your Agent's Brain" — their agent becomes an expert in something they care about.

Module 4: Give Your Agent the Ability to Learn — Machine Learning

Duration: 2–4 weeks  |  Prerequisite: Module 3

The technical heart of the curriculum. Your child trains a real machine learning model using ML for Kids (a visual interface) or Python, then integrates it into their agent. Abstract concepts become concrete when you see them in action.

What they do: Label training data · Train a model and observe accuracy · Evaluate and improve the model · Integrate it into their agent

What they learn: What machine learning actually is (math + data, not magic) · How training data quality affects outcomes · Basic model evaluation · Introduction to Python (optional)

Key lab: "Train a Model and Integrate It" — their agent now includes something they trained themselves.

Module 5: Give Your Agent a Voice — Communication

Duration: 2–4 weeks  |  Prerequisite: Module 4

The agent leaves the terminal and goes live. Your child connects their agent to a real communication channel (Discord is most common) so real people can actually talk to it. They also master prompt engineering.

What they do: Learn how LLMs generate text · Practice prompt engineering · Connect their agent to Discord (or another channel) · Test with real users

What they learn: How large language models work under the hood · Prompt engineering as a skill · What it means to deploy software to real users

Key lab: Someone outside your household can now talk to the agent they built.

Module 6: Make Your Agent Ethical — Responsibility

Duration: 2–4 weeks  |  Prerequisite: Module 5

Power requires responsibility. Your child becomes the policy-maker for their agent — auditing it for bias, writing guardrails, and creating a usage policy in their own words. This isn't a lecture; it's a design challenge.

What they do: Conduct a bias audit · Write and deploy guardrail logic · Draft a usage policy · Participate in ethics debates

What they learn: What algorithmic bias is · How ethical AI design works in practice · How to think about accountability · Their own values, reflected in a system they built

Key lab: "Ethics Audit" — their agent gets a full review for bias, fairness, and responsible behavior.

Module 7: Capstone — AI Expo

Duration: 1–2 weeks  |  Prerequisite: Modules 1–6

The finish line — and a celebration. Your child presents their completed AI assistant to a live audience. At home, that audience might be family, neighbors, homeschool co-op friends, or a virtual group. The presentation covers what they built, how it works, and what they'd do differently.

The moment: They stand in front of people and say, "I built this. Let me show you what it does." That's not a test. That's a milestone.

What You Need

Hardware

  • Computer: Any modern laptop or desktop (Mac, Windows, or Linux)
  • RAM: 8GB minimum; 16GB recommended
  • Internet: Reliable broadband (most labs require internet access)
  • No special hardware required — no robotics kits, no cameras, no microphones

Software & Accounts (All Free)

  • OpenClaw — free, open-source, self-hosted (installation guide in Module 1)
  • Hugging Face account — free at huggingface.co
  • ML for Kids — free at machinelearningforkids.co.uk (Module 4)
  • Discord account — free (Module 5)
  • Python — free, optional for Module 4 extended track

Time Commitment

  • Year-long: 3–5 hours per week (student) + 1–2 hours (parent)
  • Semester: 5–8 hours per week (student) + 1–2 hours (parent)
  • Summer intensive: 7–10 hours per week (student) + 2–3 hours (parent)
  • Initial setup: 2–4 hours one-time (Module 1)

Assessment Options

You don't need letter grades to know your child is learning. Here are several approaches:

Portfolio Approach (Recommended)

Each module produces artifacts — collect them into a portfolio:

  • Agent Design Journal entries (written reflections per module)
  • Lab reports and worksheets
  • The agent itself (screenshots, demos, exported configs)
  • Ethics essay and usage policy (Module 6)
  • AI Expo presentation materials and photo/video (Module 7)

Competency Checklist

  • Module 1: Agent deployed, named, and conversational
  • Module 2: Agent describes images and classifies audio
  • Module 3: Agent has custom domain knowledge
  • Module 4: Agent includes student-trained model
  • Module 5: Agent connected to live channel
  • Module 6: Bias audit complete, usage policy written
  • Module 7: AI Expo presentation delivered

Other Options

  • Narrative assessment: Write a paragraph per module — what they did, what challenged them, what they figured out. Excellent for homeschool records.
  • Self-assessment: The Agent Design Journal has built-in reflection prompts. Take these seriously — they're often more accurate than parent observation.
  • Demonstration-based: Can they show what the agent does and explain why it works that way? The AI Expo is the ultimate version of this.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Does my child need to know how to code?

No coding is required for Modules 1–3. Module 4 introduces Python gently through ML for Kids (a visual interface). We recommend trying the visual tools first, then introducing Python if your child is curious.

2. How much does it cost?

The curriculum is free and open-source. OpenClaw is free and self-hosted. All required tools have free tiers. No paid subscription is needed to complete any module.

3. My child is 5th grade (or 9th grade). Will this work?

The curriculum is designed for grades 6–8 but has been used successfully from 5th through 10th grade. Younger students may need more parent support during Module 4. Older students can go deeper on the Python track and ethics essay.

4. What if we get stuck on the tech setup?

Start with Module 1's lab guide — it has step-by-step setup instructions. If you hit a wall, open a GitHub issue with your question. The community is active and helpful.

5. Can we skip modules?

Each module builds on the previous one, so skipping isn't recommended. However, a 1-week sprint version of each module is available if you want to cover the concepts without full depth.

6. How do I organize an AI Expo at home?

Think small and real: kitchen table works. Invite grandparents via video call. Ask your co-op group. Contact your local library — many love hosting student tech demos. Document it with photos for your portfolio.

7. What is OpenClaw, and is it safe for kids?

OpenClaw is an open-source AI agent runtime. It runs locally on your home computer — no student data is sent to the cloud unless your child explicitly connects to a public channel in Module 5. It's the same platform used by adult developers, which is intentional: students learn real tools, not toys.

8. My child finished a module early. What next?

Each module has extension activities — look for "Extension Track" sections in the lesson materials. You can also let your child go deeper on their chosen domain from Module 3, or explore the Python track in Module 4.

9. How does this fit with other subjects?

AI Explorers integrates naturally with Math (probability, data analysis — Module 4), ELA (ethics essay — Module 6, presentation — Module 7), Science (how models work, data collection), and Social Studies (AI's societal impact — Module 6). It can run as a standalone course or alongside your other subjects.

10. What do I do when my child knows more than I do?

Celebrate it. Your job is not to be the AI expert — it's to ask good questions, review their journal, help them articulate their thinking, and organize the AI Expo. "Can you explain how that works to me?" is one of the most valuable things a homeschool parent can say.
