# AI Explorers: Homeschool Parent Guide
## *Build Your AI Assistant, Piece by Piece*

**A Complete Guide for Homeschool Families**
Version 1.0 — March 2026

---

## Welcome, Homeschool Parents!

You've found something genuinely different. AI Explorers isn't a slideshow about robots or a quiz on AI vocabulary — it's a **project-based curriculum** where your child builds a real, working AI assistant from scratch, one capability at a time, across seven modules.

By the end, your child won't just know *about* AI. They'll own one. They built it. They named it. And that changes everything.

### What Is AI Explorers?

AI Explorers is a 7-module curriculum designed for students in **grades 6–8** (though bright 5th graders and motivated 9th graders thrive in it too). Each module adds one new "superpower" to the AI assistant your child is building:

| Module | What They Build | Superpower Gained |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Deploy an agent, name it, write its personality | Identity |
| 2 | Connect image and audio models | Perception (Vision + Hearing) |
| 3 | Write a custom skill with domain knowledge | Knowledge |
| 4 | Train a real machine learning model | Learning |
| 5 | Connect the agent to a live channel | Voice / Communication |
| 6 | Add guardrails, bias checks, usage policies | Ethics & Responsibility |
| 7 | Present the agent at an AI Expo | Synthesis & Presentation |

The through-line is simple: *"What can your agent do now that it couldn't before?"* Every module answers that question with something your child actually built.

### Who Is This For?

AI Explorers works best for students who:
- Are curious about technology but don't need to be coders
- Learn by doing, not by reading textbooks
- Want to build something real, not a simulation
- Are ready for a semester-long project with meaningful output

No prior coding experience is required for Modules 1–3. Python familiarity is helpful (not required) for Module 4.

## How to Use This Curriculum at Home

### Pacing Options

You have flexibility. Here are three proven approaches:

#### 🗓️ Year-Long (Recommended)
**~36 weeks | 2–3 sessions per week | 45–60 min per session**

Spend 4–6 weeks per module. This is the richest experience — time for deep exploration, optional extension activities, and letting ideas breathe.

- Modules 1–3: Fall semester (Sept–Dec)
- Modules 4–6: Spring semester (Jan–Apr)
- Module 7: AI Expo in May (invite family, friends, neighbors!)

#### 📚 Semester (Standard)
**~18 weeks | 3–4 sessions per week | 45–60 min per session**

Spend 2–3 weeks per module. Matches a traditional school semester. You'll complete all 7 modules and have time for the Module 7 capstone.

#### ☀️ Summer Intensive
**6–8 weeks | Daily sessions | 60–90 min per session**

One module per week with a two-week capstone. Ideal for motivated students who want an immersive summer project. Move faster through discussions, focus on the key labs.

### Session Structure

Each session works well in three parts:
1. **Warm-up** (5–10 min) — Review what you built last time, preview what's next
2. **Core work** (30–45 min) — Lesson, activity, or lab
3. **Journal** (5–10 min) — Update the Agent Design Journal with what was built and why

### Parent Involvement Level

| Phase | Your Role |
|-------|-----------|
| Setup (once) | Moderate — help install software, create accounts |
| Modules 1–3 | Light — available for questions, review journal entries |
| Module 4 | Moderate — Python concepts may need discussion |
| Modules 5–6 | Light — review ethics discussions, be a thought partner |
| Module 7 | Active — help organize the AI Expo! |

You don't need to understand AI to support this curriculum. Curiosity and willingness to ask "why?" alongside your child is enough.

## Module-by-Module Overview

### Module 1: What Is AI? — Identity
**Duration:** 2–4 weeks | **Prerequisite:** None

Your child starts from zero and ends up with a running AI agent they named themselves. They explore what AI actually is (not the sci-fi version), set up OpenClaw, and write the personality file that defines who their agent is.

**What they do:**
- Install and deploy OpenClaw (their AI agent platform)
- Name their agent and write its personality
- Run their first conversation with their own AI

**What they learn:**
- What an AI agent actually is vs. the movie version
- How AI systems are structured (models, tools, memory)
- Introduction to computational thinking

**Key lab:** "Deploy Your OpenClaw" — the moment their agent goes from zero to alive.

---

### Module 2: Give Your Agent Senses — Perception
**Duration:** 2–4 weeks | **Prerequisite:** Module 1

The agent can talk. Now it can *see and hear*. Your child connects their agent to image recognition and audio classification models from Hugging Face — a real machine learning model library used by professional engineers.

**What they do:**
- Create a free Hugging Face account
- Connect an image description model to their agent
- Connect an audio classification model
- Test their agent's perception with real photos and sounds

**What they learn:**
- What multimodal AI means
- How pre-trained models work (and how to use them without training them)
- What an API is and how models connect to applications

**Key lab:** "Connect Your Agent to a Hugging Face Vision Model" — upload a photo, watch their agent describe it.

---

### Module 3: Give Your Agent a Knowledge Base — Knowledge
**Duration:** 2–4 weeks | **Prerequisite:** Module 2

Your child picks a topic they love — marine biology, Minecraft, basketball stats, local history — and teaches it to their agent. They write a custom OpenClaw "skill" that gives the agent specialized knowledge and reasoning logic.

**What they do:**
- Choose a domain (their choice!)
- Write a custom skill file with knowledge and if/then reasoning
- Test their agent's expertise in that domain

**What they learn:**
- How knowledge representation works in AI
- Introduction to structured reasoning and logic
- What makes AI "useful" vs. just conversational

**Key lab:** "Build Your Agent's Brain" — their agent becomes an expert in something they care about.

---

### Module 4: Give Your Agent the Ability to Learn — Machine Learning
**Duration:** 2–4 weeks | **Prerequisite:** Module 3

This is the technical heart of the curriculum. Your child trains a real machine learning model using ML for Kids (a visual interface) or Python, then integrates it into their agent. Abstract concepts like "training data" and "model accuracy" become concrete when you see them in action.

**What they do:**
- Label training data for a classification task
- Train a model and observe accuracy
- Evaluate the model (what makes it better or worse?)
- Integrate the trained model into their agent

**What they learn:**
- What machine learning actually is (not magic — math + data)
- How training data quality affects outcomes
- Basic model evaluation (accuracy, confusion matrices)
- Introduction to Python (optional deeper track)

**Key lab:** "Train a Model and Integrate It" — their agent now includes something they trained themselves.

---

### Module 5: Give Your Agent a Voice — Communication
**Duration:** 2–4 weeks | **Prerequisite:** Module 4

The agent leaves the terminal and goes live. Your child connects their agent to a real communication channel (Discord is most common for home use) so real people can actually talk to it. They also develop a deep understanding of how large language models work and master the art of prompting.

**What they do:**
- Learn how LLMs generate text (tokens, probabilities, temperature)
- Practice prompt engineering techniques
- Connect their agent to Discord (or another channel)
- Test their agent with real users

**What they learn:**
- How large language models work under the hood
- Prompt engineering as a skill (not just guessing)
- What it means to deploy software to real users

**Key lab:** "Connect Your Agent to a Live Channel" — someone outside your household can now talk to the agent they built.

---

### Module 6: Make Your Agent Ethical — Responsibility
**Duration:** 2–4 weeks | **Prerequisite:** Module 5

Power requires responsibility. Your child becomes the policy-maker for their agent — auditing it for bias, writing guardrails, and creating a usage policy in their own words. This isn't a lecture on ethics; it's a design challenge.

**What they do:**
- Conduct a bias audit of their agent's outputs
- Write and deploy guardrail logic
- Draft a usage policy (what their agent will and won't do)
- Participate in structured ethics debates

**What they learn:**
- What algorithmic bias is and how it emerges
- How ethical AI design works in practice
- How to think about accountability in automated systems
- Their own values, reflected in a system they built

**Key lab:** "Ethics Audit" — their agent gets a full review for bias, fairness, and responsible behavior.

---

### Module 7: Capstone — AI Expo
**Duration:** 1–2 weeks | **Prerequisite:** Modules 1–6

The AI Expo is the finish line — and it's a celebration. Your child presents their completed AI assistant to an audience. At home, that audience might be family, neighbors, homeschool co-op friends, or a virtual group online. The presentation covers what they built, how it works, and what they'd do differently.

**What they do:**
- Finalize all agent capabilities and documentation
- Prepare a demo and presentation
- Present to a live audience
- Field questions about their agent's design and ethics

**What they learn:**
- How to explain complex technical work to non-technical audiences
- Project retrospective and reflection
- Professional presentation skills

**The moment:** They stand in front of people and say, "I built this. Let me show you what it does." That's not a test. That's a milestone.

## What You Need

### Hardware
- **Computer:** Any modern laptop or desktop (Mac, Windows, or Linux)
- **RAM:** 8GB minimum; 16GB recommended for smooth performance
- **Internet:** Reliable broadband (most labs require internet access)
- **No special hardware required** — no robotics kits, no cameras, no microphones (though a camera/mic is fun for Module 2 audio activities)

### Software & Accounts (All Free)
- **OpenClaw** — free, open-source, self-hosted (installation guide in Module 1)
- **Hugging Face account** — free at huggingface.co
- **ML for Kids** — free at machinelearningforkids.co.uk (Module 4)
- **Discord account** — free (Module 5, for live channel)
- **Python** — free, optional (helpful for Module 4 extended track)

### Time
- **Year-long:** 3–5 hours per week
- **Semester:** 5–8 hours per week
- **Summer intensive:** 7–10 hours per week

### Parent Time
- **Initial setup:** 2–4 hours (one-time, Module 1)
- **Ongoing:** 1–2 hours per week (reviewing journal, answering questions, helping problem-solve)
- **AI Expo:** 2–4 hours to organize the event

## Handling Labs Without a Classroom

Most AI Explorers labs were designed for classroom settings but adapt beautifully to home. Here's how:

### Module 1 — "Sorting Cards" Activity
**Classroom version:** Groups sort AI/not-AI cards and debate.
**Home version:** Do it together — parent and child. Or invite a sibling. The debate is the point, not the team size.

### Module 2 — "Pixel Portrait" and "Sound Sorter"
**Classroom version:** Students compare their agent's image descriptions with classmates.
**Home version:** Use household objects, family photos, or nature images. Compare your child's descriptions to what the AI produces. Discuss where they agree and disagree.

### Module 3 — "Stump the AI" Activity
**Classroom version:** Students try to stump each other's agents.
**Home version:** Invite family members to try to stump the agent. Video call a grandparent. Make it a family game night.

### Module 4 — "Labeling Sprint"
**Classroom version:** Class labels hundreds of items together in a sprint.
**Home version:** Set a timer. Parent and child label together. Discuss disagreements — they're actually the most educational part.

### Module 5 — "Prompt Olympics"
**Classroom version:** Teams compete on prompt challenges.
**Home version:** Turn it into a parent-child competition. Who can write the prompt that gets the best output? Winner chooses dinner.

### Module 6 — "AI Debate" Activity
**Classroom version:** Formal classroom debate with assigned sides.
**Home version:** Assign positions. Give everyone time to prepare. Even a 2-person debate (parent vs. child) produces great thinking. Consider recording it.

### Module 7 — AI Expo
**Classroom version:** School event with parents, community members, tech guests.
**Home version:** Organize within your homeschool network or co-op. Virtual options work great — invite family via video call. Reach out to local library or makerspace for a public venue. Even a "family demo night" at your kitchen table is a real presentation.

## Assessment Options

You don't need letter grades to know your child is learning. Here are several approaches, ranked by formality:

### 1. Portfolio Approach (Recommended)
Each module produces artifacts. Collect them:
- Agent Design Journal entries (written reflections after each module)
- Lab reports and worksheets
- The agent itself (screenshots, demos, exported configurations)
- Ethics essay and usage policy (Module 6)
- AI Expo presentation materials (Module 7)

A completed portfolio is compelling evidence of learning — far more so than a test score.

### 2. Checklist / Competency Tracking
Each module has a clear capability unlock. Simple checklist:
- [ ] Module 1: Agent deployed, named, and conversational
- [ ] Module 2: Agent describes images and classifies audio
- [ ] Module 3: Agent has custom domain knowledge
- [ ] Module 4: Agent includes student-trained model
- [ ] Module 5: Agent connected to live channel
- [ ] Module 6: Bias audit complete, usage policy written
- [ ] Module 7: AI Expo presentation delivered

### 3. Narrative Assessment
Write a paragraph per module describing what your child did, what challenged them, and what they figured out. These are excellent for homeschool records and transcripts.

### 4. Self-Assessment
The Agent Design Journal prompts include built-in self-reflection questions. Students rate their own understanding and identify what they want to explore further. Take these seriously — they're often more accurate than parent observation.

### 5. Demonstration-Based
Can they show you what the agent does and explain *why* it does it that way? That's the assessment. The AI Expo is the ultimate version of this.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Does my child need to know how to code?**
No coding is required for Modules 1–3. Module 4 introduces Python concepts gently through ML for Kids (a visual interface). A Python extension track is available for students who want to go deeper. We recommend trying Module 4 with the visual tools first, then introducing Python if your child is curious.

**2. How much does it cost?**
The curriculum is free and open-source. OpenClaw is free and self-hosted. All required tools (Hugging Face, ML for Kids, Discord) have free tiers. No paid subscription is needed to complete any module.

**3. My child is 5th grade (or 9th grade). Will this work?**
The curriculum is designed for grades 6–8 but has been used successfully from 5th through 10th grade. Younger students may need more parent support during Module 4. Older students can go deeper on the Python track and the ethics essay.

**4. What if we get stuck on the tech setup?**
Start with Module 1's lab guide — it has step-by-step setup instructions. If you hit a wall, open a GitHub issue with your question. The community is active and helpful.

**5. Can we skip modules?**
Each module builds on the previous one, so skipping isn't recommended. However, if your child has prior experience (e.g., already knows Python), you can move faster. The 1-week sprint version of each module is available if you want to cover the concepts without the full depth.

**6. How do I organize an AI Expo at home?**
Think small and real: kitchen table works. Invite grandparents via video call. Ask your co-op group. Contact your local library — many libraries love hosting student tech demos. Document it with photos and video for your portfolio.

**7. What is OpenClaw, and is it safe for kids?**
OpenClaw is an open-source AI agent runtime. It runs locally on your home computer — no student data is sent to the cloud unless your child explicitly connects to a public channel in Module 5. It's the same platform used by adult developers, which is intentional: students learn real tools, not toys.

**8. My child finished a module early. What next?**
Each module has extension activities — look for the "Extension Track" sections in the lesson materials. You can also let your child go deeper on the domain they chose in Module 3 (there's no ceiling on custom knowledge) or explore the Python track in Module 4.

**9. How does this fit with other subjects?**
AI Explorers integrates naturally with:
- **Math:** probability (Module 4), data analysis (Module 4)
- **ELA:** the ethics essay (Module 6), the AI Expo presentation (Module 7)
- **Science:** how models work, data collection methods
- **Social Studies:** AI's societal impact (Module 6)
It can run as a standalone course or alongside your other subjects.

**10. What do I do when my child knows more than I do?**
Celebrate it. Your job is not to be the AI expert — it's to ask good questions, review their journal, help them articulate their thinking, and organize the AI Expo. "Can you explain how that works to me?" is one of the most valuable things a homeschool parent can say.
