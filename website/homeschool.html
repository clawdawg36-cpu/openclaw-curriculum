How It Works

Three phases. One real project.
Zero fluff.

Every module builds on the last. Your child starts by exploring, moves into building, and ends by presenting something they made themselves. The goal isn't a grade — it's a finished thing.

🔭

Explore the concepts

Watch, read, experiment. Your child learns what AI actually is — not the science-fiction version, but how it really works. We start with curiosity, not code. By the end, they can explain AI to you in plain English.

⚙️

Build the project

Hands-on labs. Real skills. No fluff. Each week adds a new capability to their AI — vision, memory, language, ethics. They're not just learning what AI does. They're doing it.

🎓

Finish. Show it off.

Common Questions

Questions homeschool families
always ask — answered honestly.

What age range is this designed for?

Ages 10–18. The core project scales: a 10-year-old and a 16-year-old can work through the same curriculum at different depths. We don't use grade levels — we use interest levels.

A curious, persistent 10-year-old will thrive in Modules 1–3 and stretch into 4–5. A motivated 16-year-old will sprint through the basics and go deep on the machine learning and ethics modules. Both will finish with something real. The curriculum includes a "slow lane" pacing guide and extension challenges built into every lesson, so no one is left behind and no one is bored.

Do my kids need any prior experience with coding or math?

None required for Modules 1–3. The curriculum is explicitly designed for students with zero coding background. Module 1 starts with no assumptions — students explore AI concepts, deploy their first agent step-by-step, and don't write a single line of code until they're ready.

Modules 4 and 5 introduce some light Python (for machine learning) — but both have browser-based alternatives using Google Colab and Teachable Machine that work without any local setup. Many students complete the full curriculum without ever writing traditional code. They learn to configure, train, and govern AI — which is where the real skills are anyway.

Math: Middle school arithmetic is sufficient. No algebra, no calculus. Concepts like probability and "more data = better model" are explained intuitively, with hands-on experiments doing the heavy lifting.

How much time per week does this take?

Roughly 3–5 hours per week at a standard pace — typically 3 sessions of 60–90 minutes each. But homeschool families have run it three very different ways:

  • Sprint mode: 1 module/week × 7 weeks — great for summer or intensive periods
  • Standard mode: 1 module every 2 weeks — the most common approach
  • Deep dive: 1 module/month with lots of extensions — ideal for unschoolers who want to go wide

You can also stop at any point and still have a meaningful outcome. A child who completes Modules 1–3 has deployed a named AI agent with vision and custom knowledge. That's real and worth celebrating on its own.

Is this appropriate for unschoolers? It sounds like a curriculum.

Yes — and we've thought hard about this. Unschoolers make up a significant portion of our community, and the feedback has been consistent: this doesn't feel like school.

The difference: every module is driven by a question ("What can my agent do now that it couldn't do before?") rather than a topic to cover. There are no tests. No grades. No right answers to fill in. The deliverable is always a capability added to their agent — something they designed and care about.

Many unschool families skip the structured facilitator guides entirely and just use the activity files as jumping-off points for wherever the child wants to go. The curriculum is modular by design — take what's useful, leave what isn't, and let curiosity drive the depth. That's not an accident.

Can multiple kids in our family do this at the same time?

Yes — this is one of our most-requested use cases. Siblings can work through the same curriculum at different paces, building different agents, and often end up collaborating naturally as the older one explains concepts to the younger.

The Module 7 AI Expo works especially well for families: siblings present to each other, to parents, to grandparents. It becomes a family event, not just a school assignment. Several co-op groups have used this format with 4–8 kids of mixed ages presenting together — it works beautifully.

What does my child actually build by the end?

A fully functioning AI agent — named by them, trained by them, governed by them. By Module 7, their agent can:

  • Recognize and respond to images (vision)
  • Access a knowledge base they built (reasoning)
  • Learn from examples they provided (machine learning)
  • Communicate in natural language (LLM integration)
  • Operate within ethical guardrails they wrote (governance)

They also produce an Agent Design Journal — a portfolio documenting every decision, reflection, and capability unlock across all 7 modules. This is excellent evidence for homeschool transcripts, high school portfolios, or college applications.

Does this count for high school transcript credit?

Many families use AI Explorers to fulfill a Computer Science, STEM elective, or Digital Literacy credit on their homeschool transcript — typically as a half-credit or full-credit elective depending on depth and pacing.

The curriculum is aligned to CSTA K–12 CS Standards and AI4K12 Big Ideas, which provides a solid standards-based foundation for transcript documentation. The curriculum includes a sample transcript description and learning objectives you can adapt.

We're not a credentialing body, so we don't issue official certificates — but the Agent Design Journal and AI Expo presentation are concrete, assessable portfolio artifacts that demonstrate mastery more compellingly than any certificate would. We're exploring a community badge program; watch the GitHub repo for updates.

What if we start and my kid loses interest partway through?

Stopping at any module still leaves them with something real. That's intentional. Unlike curricula that frontload theory and save the interesting stuff for the end, AI Explorers delivers a meaningful outcome at every module boundary.

Module 1 alone: a deployed AI agent with a name and personality. Module 3: an agent with vision and a custom knowledge base. Module 5: an agent that communicates in natural language. Any of these is a genuine accomplishment worth stopping at.

That said — the most common feedback from families is that kids who start Module 2 tend to finish. The capability unlock pattern ("what can it do now?") creates its own momentum. Our 84% completion rate isn't luck — it's the result of designing every module to be a satisfying destination, not just a waypoint to something better later.
