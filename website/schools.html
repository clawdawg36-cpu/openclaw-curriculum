Concern #1 — Cost
✓ Resolved

What does it actually cost?

The curriculum is free. Always. Published under CC BY-SA 4.0 — no licensing fees, no per-student costs, no subscription tiers, no "premium" content behind a paywall. Student data never leaves your infrastructure. OpenClaw is self-hosted — deployed on your server, inside your network. No student data reaches us, any cloud provider, or any analytics service. Teacher Professional Development

Teachers don't need a CS degree.
The PD proves it.

The biggest adoption barrier for AI curriculum is teacher readiness. We eliminated it. The included PD program prepares any motivated teacher to facilitate AI Explorers — from a science teacher to a humanities instructor.

  • 🎯

    Designed for non-CS teachers

    The PD program is explicitly written for teachers who are not programmers. Each unit explains concepts in plain language first, then connects them to classroom facilitation strategies. Math and science teachers, humanities teachers, elective instructors — all have successfully completed the program.

  • ⏱️

    ~4 hours, fully self-paced

    No scheduled sessions, no required cohorts. Teachers complete the PD on their own schedule — over a weekend, across lunch breaks, or during summer prep time. The program is broken into short, completable units so progress isn't lost if time runs out mid-session.

  • 🧪

    Hands-on: teachers do the modules first

    The most effective part of the PD is that teachers experience the curriculum as students do. They deploy their own OpenClaw instance, give it a name, and build through Module 1–3 themselves before teaching it. That first-person experience makes facilitation intuitive — teachers know what students will struggle with because they struggled with it too.

  • 🤝

    Educator community & GitHub Discussions

    Teachers using AI Explorers aren't alone. The GitHub Discussions board is active — teachers post questions, share adaptations, and help each other navigate tricky modules. A growing educators' Slack community provides real-time support. Several experienced pilot teachers have volunteered as informal mentors.

  • 🏫

    In-person district PD available

    For districts that want facilitated in-person professional development, we offer workshops on request. Full-day and half-day formats are available. Contact us to discuss scheduling and district pricing — we work within educator professional development budgets.

  • 📄

    Facilitator guides for every lesson

    Every lesson in every module ships with a detailed facilitator guide written for the teacher, not the student. It explains what's happening under the hood, anticipates student questions, flags common stumbling points, and suggests responses. Teachers who skip the PD program can still teach effectively using the facilitator guides alone — though we recommend the full PD.

Frequently Asked Questions

The questions boards and admins
actually ask

Real questions from real administrators during pilot adoptions — answered honestly, without marketing language.

What's the total budget impact — including hidden costs?

The curriculum itself is free under CC BY-SA 4.0. Here's a full accounting of realistic district costs:

  • Curriculum license: $0. Always.
  • Server infrastructure: $0 if you use existing school hardware. $150–$500 one-time if you want a dedicated machine (a refurbished desktop is sufficient for a single school). No recurring cloud costs.
  • Teacher PD (self-paced): $0. Approximately 4 hours of teacher time per educator.
  • Teacher PD (in-person facilitated): Available on request. Contact us for district pricing — we work within educator PD budgets.
  • New devices: $0. Works on existing school hardware and Chromebooks.
  • External services: Hugging Face free tier, Google Teachable Machine — both free. No accounts required for core modules.
  • Ongoing maintenance: Updates are a git pull command. No vendor maintenance contracts.

The total cost of deploying AI Explorers in a classroom using existing hardware and the self-paced PD is, in practice, zero dollars — plus the time your teacher invests in learning the curriculum before teaching it.

Is this COPPA compliant for students under 13?

Yes — and the compliance is architectural, not just policy-based.

Because OpenClaw is self-hosted on your infrastructure, students under 13 never create accounts with external services that collect personal information. OpenClaw accounts are managed by your school, under your FERPA/COPPA obligations — the same as any school-managed login system.

Teachable Machine (used in Module 2) processes images locally in the student's browser. Images never leave the device during training. No Google account is required for basic use.

Hugging Face is used for model access in later modules. For maximum privacy, the curriculum supports local model inference, fully documented for your IT team. Students use Hugging Face anonymously for API calls with no personal data required.

The Data Privacy Guide (included in the repo) maps every service touched in every module with recommended age-appropriate configurations. We recommend your district's privacy officer review it before deployment — it's written to answer exactly the questions they'll ask.

What happens if OpenClaw (the platform) shuts down or changes its license?

Nothing bad happens — and this is a structural guarantee, not a promise.

OpenClaw is MIT-licensed open source software. The entire codebase is publicly available on GitHub. Even in the absolute worst-case scenario — project abandoned, organization dissolved — your district retains a complete, working copy of the platform that runs indefinitely on your own hardware. The MIT license is irrevocable.

Your deployment is not dependent on our servers, our cloud, or our organization's continued existence. Students' work is stored on your infrastructure. Updates are available but never required. The platform works in air-gapped environments.

The curriculum itself (CC BY-SA 4.0) is similarly irrevocable. No future license change can affect materials you've already downloaded.

Does our IT department need to do anything ongoing?

Minimal ongoing IT involvement. Initial setup takes under two hours. After that:

  • Updates: Run git pull in the curriculum repo when new modules or patches are released. Optional — the curriculum works indefinitely without updates.
  • Server maintenance: Standard Linux server maintenance (OS updates, monitoring). No different from any other school server.
  • Student accounts: Your IT team manages OpenClaw accounts the same way they manage any school application — no special access controls or complex permission systems.
  • Backups: Student work (agent configurations, journal files) lives in the OpenClaw data directory. Back it up using whatever backup process your school uses for other server data.

There are no vendor portals to check, no renewal processes, no license management, no endpoint clients to maintain on student devices. A single server and a git repo covers your entire infrastructure footprint.

How does this fit into our existing elective or CS class structure?

Flexibly — it's designed for multiple placement contexts. Here's how pilot schools have fit it in:

  • Dedicated semester elective (ideal): 18–20 weeks, 3–5 sessions per week. Full 7-module arc with AI Expo capstone. This gives students the complete experience.
  • Existing CS or coding class: Replace or augment a semester unit. Many CS teachers use M1–M4 as an AI unit within a broader CS course, then invite students to continue on their own.
  • STEM elective or maker space: Several pilot teachers ran AI Explorers as a STEM elective. Works well when there's flexibility on exact standards alignment.
  • After-school or enrichment: Not our primary recommendation (we believe AI literacy belongs in the regular school day, not just for students whose families can support enrichment), but the curriculum works in this format.
  • Partial adoption: M1–M3 alone is a valid stopping point. Students end with a deployed agent with vision and custom knowledge — that's real, even without the full arc.

The curriculum includes a scope-and-sequence that maps to standard semester structures. If you need to map it to your district's specific course structure, the GitHub repository includes a blank mapping template.

What if a teacher leaves mid-year — is the curriculum teachable by a substitute?

Yes — though "substitute teaching AI Explorers" is easier than it sounds. Here's why:

The curriculum is written so that the facilitator guides provide full lesson context for anyone who picks them up cold. A substitute or incoming teacher doesn't need prior familiarity with the curriculum to facilitate a lesson — the guide tells them what to do, what to say, what questions to expect, and how to respond.

For continuity within a module, the student Agent Design Journals document what each student has built so far. A new teacher can review journals to understand where each student is in the arc without needing notes from the prior teacher.

For mid-year teacher transitions, we recommend: (1) the incoming teacher spend 2–3 hours with the PD program before their first class, (2) they read the facilitator guides for all completed modules so they understand the arc students have followed, and (3) they post in GitHub Discussions if they have questions. The community is responsive and helpful for exactly this scenario.
