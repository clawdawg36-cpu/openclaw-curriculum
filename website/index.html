The 7-Module Arc

One agent. Seven capability unlocks.
A semester-long story.

Students follow a single through-line: they are building their own personal AI assistant. Each module adds one new power. By Module 7, it's real — and they built it.

🐣
Module 1 — Identity

What Is AI? — Deploy Your Agent

Students get their first look at AI, explore its history and vocabulary, then deploy their own OpenClaw instance. They name their agent, write its personality, and take their first steps as AI creators.

Capability Unlock: Identity
👁️
Module 2 — Perception

Give Your Agent Vision

How do computers see? Students explore image recognition, train a Teachable Machine classifier, connect Hugging Face models to OpenClaw, and give their agent the ability to perceive the world.

Capability Unlock: Vision & Hearing
🧠
Module 3 — Knowledge

Give Your Agent a Knowledge Base

Students dive into decision trees, pattern recognition, and reasoning. They build a custom OpenClaw skill — giving their agent domain knowledge and logic they wrote themselves.

Capability Unlock: Reasoning & Knowledge
📚
Module 4 — Learning

Give Your Agent the Ability to Learn

What is machine learning, really? Students explore training data, supervised learning, and overfitting — then train a real sentiment classifier and integrate their trained model directly into their agent.

Capability Unlock: Machine Learning
🎙️
Module 5 — Voice

Give Your Agent a Voice

LLMs, prompt engineering, and how language models actually work. Students connect their agent to a live channel — Discord, iMessage, or a webhook — so it can speak to the real world.

Capability Unlock: Communication
⚖️
Module 6 — Ethics

Make Your Agent Ethical

Students audit the agent they built. What biases crept in? What should it refuse? They write guardrails, conduct a bias testing lab, and create a real usage policy — governing the thing they made.

Capability Unlock: Responsibility
🎓
Module 7 — Synthesis

Capstone: The AI Expo

Students present their fully built, named, governed AI agent to a real audience — peers, teachers, parents, community members. Not a test. A demonstration of something real they created.

Capability Unlock: Synthesis & Presentation

Homeschool & Unschool Families
For families directing their own learning journey
Is this too advanced for my kid?

Probably not — that's one of the things we obsessed over. AI Explorers is built for grades 6–8, which means students who can read a paragraph, follow multi-step directions, and use a web browser are ready to start.

Module 1 starts with zero assumptions: no coding background, no math beyond middle school, no prior tech experience. Students begin by exploring what AI is and deploying a simple agent — guided step-by-step. Each module scaffolds on the last, so concepts build naturally rather than appearing out of nowhere.

If your child is curious and persistent, they can do this. If you find a section moves too fast, each module includes a "slow lane" pacing option. And if they fly through it, extension challenges are built in for every lesson.

Do I need a CS background to teach this?

No. Full stop. The curriculum is written for the parent/facilitator who is learning alongside their student — not delivering polished lectures on neural networks.

Every module includes detailed teacher/facilitator guides that explain the concepts in plain language before you introduce them to your child. You don't need to know how a transformer works before Module 5. You just need to be willing to read the guide and explore together.

Many homeschool families find the curriculum doubles as a parent education experience. You'll both come away knowing more than you started with. That's a feature, not a bug.

How long does it take?

The full 7-module curriculum is designed as a semester arc — roughly 18–20 weeks at a standard school pace of 3–4 sessions per week. But homeschool families have used it in wildly different ways:

• Sprint mode: 1 module per week × 7 weeks (intensive, great for summer)

• Standard mode: 1 module per 2–3 weeks (most families)

• Deep dive: 1 module per month (lots of project extension, great for unschoolers)

You can also stop at any point and still have a meaningful outcome. A student who completes Modules 1–3 has deployed an agent, given it vision, and built custom knowledge. That's real.

Is there a certificate or credential at the end?

The curriculum culminates in the AI Expo (Module 7) — a presentation of the student's fully built AI agent to a real audience. That's the credential: a live demonstration of something they built, not a paper test.

There's no official third-party certification at this time, but the curriculum includes a completion portfolio (the Agent Design Journal) that documents the student's learning journey across all 7 modules. This is excellent evidence for transcripts, college applications, or simply a proud record of what was accomplished.

We're exploring a community badge program — stay tuned to the GitHub repo for updates.

Classroom Educators
For teachers bringing this into their classroom
Does this require special equipment or a computer lab?

Any modern web browser on any device will work for the majority of the curriculum. Modules 1–3 run entirely in a browser. Teachable Machine (used in Module 2) works on tablets and Chromebooks. Students don't need high-powered machines.

For Module 4 (machine learning / training), a Python-capable device helps — but the module includes a browser-based fallback using Google Colab, so a Chromebook or school-issued laptop is sufficient.

OpenClaw itself can be deployed on a single school server (a modest Raspberry Pi 4 or old desktop can run it for an entire classroom), so students never need their own cloud accounts or paid services.

How does this fit existing standards (CSTA, NGSS, Common Core)?

The curriculum includes a full standards alignment document mapping each module to CSTA K–12 CS Standards, ISTE Student Standards, and relevant NGSS and ELA/Math connections where they apply.

Highlights: M1 and M6 align strongly to CSTA 2-IC (Impacts and Culture) and 3A-IC. M3–M4 align to CSTA 2-AP (Algorithms and Programming). M5 and M7 hit ISTE Communicator and Creator standards. M6 (ethics) maps to several state-level digital citizenship frameworks.

If your district uses a specific framework not listed, the GitHub repo includes a blank mapping template you can complete. Several pilot schools have done this in under two hours.

Is there teacher support? What if I get stuck?

Yes — and you won't be alone. Every module ships with a detailed facilitator guide that walks through each lesson from the teacher's perspective, including anticipated student questions, common stumbling points, and suggested responses.

There's also a growing community of educators using this curriculum. The GitHub Discussions board is active, and we're building a teachers' Slack community where you can ask questions, share wins, and get support from others who have taught the same module.

For deeper onboarding, a teacher PD program (self-paced) is included in the curriculum repo — it covers the technical setup and pedagogical philosophy in about 4 hours total.

What if OpenClaw goes down or changes? Are we locked in?

You're not locked in, and going down is structurally very hard. OpenClaw is fully open source (MIT license) — the entire codebase is on GitHub. Even if the project were abandoned tomorrow, you'd have a fully working copy that runs indefinitely on your own infrastructure.

Because students deploy their own instance, a service outage at a third party never disrupts your classroom. Your instance, your uptime, your control.

The curriculum is also written to be platform-resilient. Core concepts are taught using multiple tools (Teachable Machine, Hugging Face, OpenClaw), so if one tool changes, the lesson still runs with minimal adjustment. We maintain a change log in the repo and push updates when tools evolve.

School Boards & Administrators
For decision-makers evaluating district adoption
What's the total cost?

The curriculum itself is free. No licensing fees, no per-student costs, no subscription. It is published under CC BY-SA 4.0 and always will be.

Realistic implementation costs for a district:

• Infrastructure: A single modest server (or a repurposed machine) to run OpenClaw for an entire school. Many districts deploy on existing hardware. Estimated cost: $0–$500 one-time for a dedicated box.

• Teacher PD: The self-paced PD program is included. If you want in-person professional development, we offer that separately — contact us for district pricing.

• Devices: No new devices required — any browser-capable device works.

The total cost of a classroom deployment with existing hardware and a teacher doing the self-paced PD is essentially zero dollars.

Is there vendor lock-in?

None. This is one of the curriculum's core design principles. OpenClaw is MIT-licensed open source software. The curriculum is CC BY-SA. The tools used (Teachable Machine, Hugging Face) are free and widely available.

No single company controls this curriculum. There is no vendor that can change pricing, discontinue a product, or require contract renewal. The materials live in a public Git repository that any school can fork and own forever.

If you want long-term support, we offer optional district support agreements — but they're not required, and the curriculum works perfectly without them.

How does it handle student data privacy?

Student data never leaves your infrastructure. OpenClaw is self-hosted — you deploy it on your own server, inside your own network. No student data is transmitted to us, to any third-party cloud, or to any analytics service.

Teachable Machine (Google) processes images locally in the browser — images never leave the student's device during training. Hugging Face model inference can be run locally or through the free Hugging Face API; for maximum privacy, local inference is documented and supported.

The curriculum includes a Data Privacy Guide for administrators that maps every data touch point across all 7 modules and provides recommended configurations for maximum privacy protection.

Is it COPPA compliant? What about FERPA?

Yes, with standard district controls in place. Because OpenClaw is self-hosted and student data stays on your infrastructure, the compliance posture is straightforward: your district controls the data, your district determines what's collected, and no third party has access to student information.

For COPPA: students under 13 using the curriculum never create accounts with external services that require personal information. OpenClaw accounts are managed by the school. Teachable Machine does not collect or store student data. The curriculum's Data Privacy Guide details age-appropriate configurations.

For FERPA: since student work (agent configs, journal entries) lives on school-controlled infrastructure, FERPA obligations are straightforward. No third-party data sharing agreements are required for basic use.

We recommend your district IT team review the Data Privacy Guide before deployment. It's written to answer exactly these questions.

How does this address equity gaps in AI education?

Equity is a structural design constraint, not an afterthought. AI Explorers was built with a specific observation: most AI literacy programs are informal enrichment (after-school, camp, private tutoring) that only reach students with resourced families. This curriculum is designed for the regular school day, in regular classrooms, with no paid add-ons.

Specific equity features:

• Zero cost: No per-student fees ever. A rural Title I school has access to the same curriculum as a well-funded private school.

• Device-agnostic: Works on Chromebooks, tablets, and older hardware — not just MacBooks.

• No home internet required: The curriculum can be configured to run entirely on a school's local network, including offline mode for OpenClaw.

• ELL and IEP support: Every module includes scaffolded differentiation for English language learners and students with learning differences.

• Low teacher prerequisite: Designed for teachers without CS backgrounds — expanding the pool of schools that can teach it.

How does this compare to other AI literacy programs?

There are several good AI literacy programs. Here's an honest comparison on the dimensions that matter most for policy:

vs. Day of AI / MIT: Strong conceptual foundation. AI Explorers extends further into hands-on building and includes a full agentic deployment arc that MIT's materials don't cover. Both are free.

vs. AI4K12 (CSTA/AISE): The gold standard for standards alignment and breadth. AI Explorers is more opinionated and project-driven — students build one thing over a semester rather than surveying many topics. Complementary, not competing.

vs. commercial programs (STEMscopes, etc.): Commercial programs require per-seat licenses and create ongoing budget dependencies. AI Explorers is permanently free, open source, and district-owned.

Unique to AI Explorers: The only free curriculum we're aware of where students deploy a real, live AI agent on real infrastructure as part of the core arc — not as a bonus or enrichment activity.

Is this safe for kids? What content will they encounter?

Yes. The curriculum is age-appropriate and the platform is school-controlled.

Content: All curriculum materials are written for grades 6–8 and reviewed for age appropriateness. There's no internet browsing required for core lessons. Any AI model interactions are structured activities with defined inputs — students aren't just chatting freely with an unmoderated AI.

Platform: OpenClaw is self-hosted by the school. There's no public-facing chat, no social component, no exposure to external users. It's a closed, school-controlled environment — closer to a word processor than a social network.

Data: Your child's work stays on the school's server. It's not uploaded to any external service, not used for advertising, not shared with any third party.

If you have specific concerns about content in a particular module, all curriculum materials are publicly available at the GitHub link — you can read exactly what your child will encounter before they do.

Will this replace my child's teacher?

No — and it's not designed to. The curriculum is built around the assumption that a skilled human teacher is irreplaceable, and the best AI education happens in relationship, not in isolation.

The teacher's role in AI Explorers is as a facilitator and guide — someone who helps students make sense of what they're building, asks the right questions, and creates the conditions for genuine inquiry. The curriculum doesn't automate that role; it supports it.

In fact, one of the things AI Explorers does explicitly is help students develop a nuanced understanding of what AI can and can't do — including the many things that require human judgment, creativity, and care that AI genuinely cannot replicate. Your child's teacher is one of those things.
