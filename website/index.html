The 7-Module Arc

One agent. Seven capability unlocks.
A semester-long story.

Students follow a single through-line: they are building their own personal AI assistant. Each module adds one new power. By Module 7, it's real — and they built it.

🐣
Module 1 — Identity

What Is AI? — Deploy Your Agent

Students get their first look at AI, explore its history and vocabulary, then deploy their own OpenClaw instance. They name their agent, write its personality, and take their first steps as AI creators.

Capability Unlock: Identity
👁️
Module 2 — Perception

Give Your Agent Vision

How do computers see? Students explore image recognition, train a Teachable Machine classifier, connect Hugging Face models to OpenClaw, and give their agent the ability to perceive the world.

Capability Unlock: Vision & Hearing
🧠
Module 3 — Knowledge

Give Your Agent a Knowledge Base

Students dive into decision trees, pattern recognition, and reasoning. They build a custom OpenClaw skill — giving their agent domain knowledge and logic they wrote themselves.

Capability Unlock: Reasoning & Knowledge
📚
Module 4 — Learning

Give Your Agent the Ability to Learn

What is machine learning, really? Students explore training data, supervised learning, and overfitting — then train a real sentiment classifier and integrate their trained model directly into their agent.

Capability Unlock: Machine Learning
🎙️
Module 5 — Voice

Give Your Agent a Voice

LLMs, prompt engineering, and how language models actually work. Students connect their agent to a live channel — Discord, iMessage, or a webhook — so it can speak to the real world.

Capability Unlock: Communication
⚖️
Module 6 — Ethics

Make Your Agent Ethical

Students audit the agent they built. What biases crept in? What should it refuse? They write guardrails, conduct a bias testing lab, and create a real usage policy — governing the thing they made.

Capability Unlock: Responsibility
🎓
Module 7 — Synthesis

Capstone: The AI Expo

Students present their fully built, named, governed AI agent to a real audience — peers, teachers, parents, community members. Not a test. A demonstration of something real they created.

Capability Unlock: Synthesis & Presentation

📄 Lesson 1.1 What Is AI? ⏱ 50–60 min 🎓 Grades 6–8 📐 CSTA Aligned

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  1. Define Artificial Intelligence in their own words.
  2. Describe at least three historical milestones in AI development (Turing, Deep Blue, AlphaGo, ChatGPT).
  3. Identify the 5 Big Ideas in AI (AI4K12 framework) and give one example of each.
  4. Articulate how AI differs from human intelligence.

🔥 Warm-Up: What Makes Something Intelligent? 10 min

💬 Think-Pair-Share

Post this question on the board: "What makes something intelligent?"

  • Students write down 3 examples of "intelligent" things — human, animal, or machine. (2 min)
  • Turn and talk with a partner. (2 min)
  • Share out — chart responses into two columns: Humans/Animals | Machines/Systems

💡 Teacher tip: Students often confuse AI with sci-fi robots. Gently redirect early — but let the misconceptions surface first, they're great discussion fuel.

📖 Direct Instruction: A Brief History of AI 15 min

YearMilestoneWhy It Matters

1997 Deep Blue defeats Garry Kasparov at chess AI beats humans at complex strategy

🧠 The 5 Big Ideas in AI 10 min

1 Perception AI senses the world through cameras, microphones, and sensors
2 Representation & Reasoning AI stores and processes knowledge like a structured library
3 Learning AI improves from data and experience — no one codes every answer
4 Natural Interaction AI communicates with humans in natural language and images
5 Societal Impact AI affects jobs, fairness, and the future — your students will inherit this

📝 Exit Ticket 10 min

"In your own words: What is AI? Give one example from history and one from your own life. What's one question you still have?"

Frequently Asked Questions

The real questions, answered honestly.

We hear the same concerns from different audiences. Here's what homeschool families, classroom educators, school administrators, and parents actually ask — and the honest answers.

⭐ Most Asked Questions
Is this too advanced for my kid? My child has never coded before.

Probably not — that's exactly what we obsessed over when designing this. AI Explorers is built for grades 6–8, and Module 1 starts with zero assumptions: no coding background, no math beyond middle school, no prior tech experience of any kind.

Students begin by exploring what AI actually is (versus what movies say it is), then take their first steps deploying a simple agent — guided step-by-step, with screenshots and video walkthroughs. Each module scaffolds on the previous one, so concepts build naturally rather than appearing out of nowhere.

If your child is curious and persistent, they can do this. Every module includes a "slow lane" pacing option if you need to linger, and extension challenges if they fly through the core content. We designed for the full range of the 6–8 grade band.

What is the total cost — infrastructure, licensing, professional development?

The curriculum is free. That's not a trial or a limited tier — it's the whole thing. CC BY-SA 4.0, no licensing fees, no per-student costs, no renewal. Here's what realistic implementation actually costs:

• Curriculum materials: $0. Download from GitHub.

• Infrastructure: A single modest server runs OpenClaw for an entire school. Many districts use a repurposed desktop or a ~$200 mini-PC. Cost range: $0 (existing hardware) to $300–$500 one-time.

• Teacher professional development: The self-paced PD program (4 hours) is included in the repo. If your district wants facilitated in-person PD, we offer that separately — contact us for district rates.

• Devices: No new devices required. Any browser-capable device — including Chromebooks — works for the full curriculum.

Total cost of a classroom deployment using existing hardware with self-paced PD: effectively zero. This is not a budget conversation — it's a decision conversation.

How does student data privacy work? Is this COPPA and FERPA compliant?

Student data never leaves your infrastructure. OpenClaw is self-hosted — you deploy it on your own server, inside your own network. No student data is transmitted to us, to any third-party cloud, or to any analytics service. There is no external data processor to add to your vendor list.

Teachable Machine (used in Module 2) processes image data locally in the student's browser — images are never uploaded to Google's servers during training. Hugging Face model inference can be run locally or through the free API; local inference is fully documented and supported for maximum privacy.

For COPPA: students never create accounts with external services requiring personal information. OpenClaw accounts are managed by the school. The curriculum's Data Privacy Guide details every data touch point across all 7 modules with recommended privacy-maximizing configurations.

For FERPA: student work lives on school-controlled infrastructure. No third-party data sharing agreements are required for basic use. We recommend your district IT team review the Data Privacy Guide before deployment — it's written specifically to answer board and legal team questions.

For Homeschool Families
Questions from parents directing their child's education at home

Do I need a CS or tech background to facilitate this?

No — not even close. The curriculum is written for the parent who is learning alongside their student, not delivering polished expert lectures on neural networks.

Every module includes detailed facilitator guides that explain each concept in plain language before you introduce it to your child. You don't need to understand how a transformer model works before Module 5. You need to be willing to read the guide, explore the material yourself first, and sit alongside your child as you both figure it out.

Many homeschool families report that the curriculum becomes a shared learning experience — parent and student both come away knowing substantially more about AI than they started with. We consider that a feature, not a side effect.

How long does the full curriculum take? Can we go at our own pace?

Yes — pacing is entirely up to you. The full 7-module arc is designed as a semester-length experience at a traditional school pace (3–4 sessions per week, 18–20 weeks), but homeschool families use it in many different ways:

• Sprint mode: 1 module per week × 7 weeks — intensive, works great for summer or a dedicated elective block

• Standard mode: 1 module every 2–3 weeks — the most common homeschool pace

• Deep dive: 1 module per month with heavy project extension — great for unschoolers or interest-led learners

You can also stop at any module and still have a meaningful outcome. A student who completes Modules 1–3 has deployed a working AI agent, given it vision capabilities, and built a custom knowledge skill. That's a real achievement, regardless of whether you continue to Module 7.

Is there a certificate or credential at the end — something we can include in a transcript?

The curriculum culminates in the AI Expo (Module 7): a live presentation of the student's fully built AI agent to a real audience — family members, neighbors, community members, or a local co-op group. That demonstration is the credential: evidence of something real they built and can explain.

Every student who completes the curriculum also has an Agent Design Journal — a documented portfolio spanning all 7 modules that records every decision, build, reflection, and capability unlock. This is strong portfolio evidence for transcripts, high school applications, or scholarships.

We're actively developing a community verification program — a digital badge that certifies curriculum completion and can be attached to portfolios and college applications. Watch the GitHub repo for updates on that program.

For Educators
Questions from teachers and instructors bringing this into a classroom
Does this require a computer lab or special equipment?

Any modern web browser on any device works for the majority of the curriculum. Modules 1–3 run entirely in a browser and are fully functional on school-issued Chromebooks, tablets, or older laptops. Teachable Machine (used in Module 2 for image classification) runs entirely in-browser and works on any device with a camera.

Module 4 (machine learning and model training) is the most compute-intensive. The module includes a browser-based fallback using Google Colab — so a school Chromebook is sufficient, no local Python installation required.

OpenClaw (the platform students deploy their agents on) runs on a single modest school server — a repurposed desktop, a Raspberry Pi 4, or a small cloud VM. One server handles an entire classroom. Students connect to it through the school's local network — no personal cloud accounts or outside services needed.

How does this align to CSTA, NGSS, Common Core, and other standards?

The curriculum ships with a full standards alignment document that maps every module to CSTA K–12 CS Standards, ISTE Student Standards, AI4K12 Big Ideas, and notes relevant NGSS and ELA connections where they apply. No additional mapping work required.

Key alignments by module: M1 and M6 hit CSTA 2-IC (Impacts and Culture), 3A-IC, and multiple state digital citizenship frameworks. M3–M4 align to CSTA 2-AP (Algorithms and Programming). M5 and M7 align to ISTE Communicator and Creator. M6's ethics content maps to equity and justice standards in several state frameworks.

If your district uses a framework we haven't covered, the GitHub repo includes a blank mapping template that several pilot schools completed in under two hours. We're happy to review and publish district-submitted mappings.

Is there teacher support? What if I run into something I don't understand?

Yes — and you won't be alone. Every module ships with a detailed facilitator guide covering each lesson from the teacher's perspective: anticipated student questions, common stumbling points, suggested responses, and technical troubleshooting steps for the most common setup issues.

A self-paced teacher PD program (roughly 4 hours total) is included in the curriculum repo. It covers the full technical setup, the pedagogical philosophy behind the agent arc, and gives you a "student's eye view" of Modules 1 and 2 before you teach them. It's designed to get you classroom-ready in a weekend.

Beyond the included materials, a growing community of educators using this curriculum is active on GitHub Discussions. When you have a specific question mid-lesson, someone who has taught the same module will respond — often within a few hours. We're also building a dedicated educators' Slack community.

What if OpenClaw changes or this curriculum becomes outdated? Are we locked in?

You're not locked in — and "going down" is structurally very hard to do. OpenClaw is fully open source under the MIT license. The entire codebase lives on GitHub. Even if the project were abandoned tomorrow, you'd have a fully working copy running on your own infrastructure indefinitely. It doesn't phone home, it doesn't require our servers, it doesn't expire.

The curriculum itself is CC BY-SA — you own your copy. If the primary repo disappeared, your downloaded copy is yours forever and you can teach from it as-is.

For tool evolution: the curriculum is deliberately written to be platform-resilient. Core concepts are taught using multiple tools (Teachable Machine, Hugging Face, OpenClaw) specifically so that if one tool changes its interface, the lesson concept still holds with minimal adjustment. We maintain an active change log and push curriculum updates when tools change in ways that affect lessons.

For Parents
Questions from parents whose child is enrolled or being considered for enrollment
What will my child actually be able to do after this curriculum?

Your child will have built a real, working AI agent from scratch. Not a toy. Not a simulation. A functioning AI assistant with a name they chose, a personality they designed, capabilities they added one at a time, and ethical guardrails they wrote themselves — then presented to a live audience at the AI Expo.

Concretely, by the end of the curriculum a student will be able to:

• Explain what AI is and isn't — in plain language, to any adult

• Describe how machine learning works, including training data and why bias happens

• Build and deploy a functioning AI system using real tools used by adult developers

• Identify ethical risks in AI systems — including ones they built themselves

• Present a complex technical project to a non-technical audience with confidence

These aren't test-prep skills. They're durable capabilities that will matter throughout their education and career — regardless of which path they choose.

Is this safe for my child? What AI content will they encounter?

Yes — the curriculum is age-appropriate and the platform is school-controlled.

Content: All materials are written for grades 6–8 and reviewed for age-appropriateness. There's no unstructured internet browsing in the core curriculum. Every AI interaction is a structured activity with defined inputs — students aren't chatting freely with an unmoderated system.

Platform: OpenClaw is self-hosted by your school or district. There is no public-facing component, no social network, no exposure to external users. It functions more like a word processor than a social media platform — a closed, school-controlled environment where your child's work stays inside the school network.

Data: Your child's work stays on the school's server. Nothing is uploaded to external services, used for advertising, or shared with any third party.

If you want to review what your child will encounter before they do, every curriculum module is publicly available on GitHub. You can read each lesson yourself before your child sees it.

My child isn't interested in tech or coding. Will they still get something out of this?

Probably yes — and this curriculum was specifically designed with that student in mind. AI Explorers isn't a coding class. The core skill it builds is judgment: understanding what AI is, what it can and can't do, where it goes wrong, and how humans should govern it.

Students who go through this curriculum and don't end up in tech careers will still have something invaluable: the ability to evaluate AI systems critically, ask informed questions about how automated decisions are made, and understand what they're actually agreeing to when they use AI tools in their daily life.

Several of our pilot schools found that students who weren't "tech kids" were often the most engaged in the ethics modules — because those modules are fundamentally about power, fairness, and who gets to decide. That's not a tech conversation. It's a civics conversation, a humanities conversation, a life conversation.

Will this replace or undermine my child's teacher?

No — and it's not designed to. AI Explorers is built on the premise that the best AI education happens in relationship with a skilled human facilitator, not in isolation in front of a screen. The curriculum doesn't automate the teacher role — it supports it.

Teachers using AI Explorers report that the curriculum gives them a new and genuinely interesting context for discussions that connect to writing, ethics, math, science, and critical thinking. Students bring questions to class that their teachers haven't been asked before. That tends to energize teachers, not replace them.

One of the explicit goals of Module 6 is helping students develop a nuanced understanding of what AI can and cannot do — including the things that require human judgment, creativity, empathy, and relational trust that AI genuinely cannot replicate. Your child's teacher is a vivid example of that. The curriculum makes that case directly.
