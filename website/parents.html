The 7-Module Journey

What your child actually does —
and builds — each step of the way

Every module adds one new capability to your child's AI assistant. By the end, they have something real they designed, built, and can explain. Here's what that looks like, in plain English.

🐣
Module 1 — What Is AI?

They deploy their first AI agent and name it

Your child starts by answering a question most adults can't: what is AI, really? They learn the history, the vocabulary, and the difference between AI that sounds smart and AI that actually is. Then they set up their own AI assistant, give it a name and a personality, and make their first design decisions as a creator — not just a user.

✓ After Module 1, your child can explain how AI works. Most adults can't.
👁️
Module 2 — Perception

They give their agent the ability to see

How do computers see images? Your child will explore image recognition, train a visual classifier using Teachable Machine (a free Google tool — no coding required), and connect it to their agent. By the end, their AI can look at a picture and tell them what it sees — because they taught it how.

✓ After Module 2, your child has trained a real AI model — something millions of adults never do.
🧠
Module 3 — Knowledge

They write a custom skill for their agent

This is where your child writes their first piece of real logic — a custom "skill" that gives their agent specific knowledge and decision-making ability. Think of it like teaching your assistant a new trick. They decide what the skill does, what it knows, and how it responds. This is the module where students start to feel like actual engineers.

✓ After Module 3, your child has written working code that does something real.
📚
Module 4 — Learning

They train their agent to learn from examples

What does it actually mean for a machine to "learn"? Your child will train a sentiment classifier — an AI that can read a sentence and tell whether it's positive or negative — using real data they curate themselves. They'll encounter what happens when training data is bad or biased, and understand why that matters. This is machine learning, hands-on.

✓ After Module 4, your child understands why AI can be wrong — and why that's a design problem, not a mystery.
🎙️
Module 5 — Voice

They give their agent a real communication channel

Language models, prompt engineering, and how ChatGPT-style AI really works under the hood. Then your child connects their agent to a real channel — messaging, a webhook, or a live interface — so it can actually communicate with the world. By this module, their AI is no longer just a project; it's a working system.

✓ After Module 5, your child's AI assistant is live and responding in the real world.
⚖️
Module 6 — Ethics

They audit what they built — and govern it

This is the module we think matters most. Your child goes back through everything they built and asks hard questions: Is there bias in my training data? What should my agent refuse to do? Who is responsible when AI gets it wrong? They write a real usage policy — guardrails for the system they created. Ethics as practice, not lecture.

✓ After Module 6, your child has thought more carefully about AI ethics than most tech professionals.
🎓
Module 7 — The AI Expo

They demo their AI to a real audience

Not a test. Not a quiz. A public demonstration. Students present the agent they built — its capabilities, its limitations, its ethical guardrails, and what they learned — to an audience of peers, teachers, parents, and community members. This is the capstone event: your child walks in as a student and walks out as someone who built something real.

✓ After Module 7, your child has demoed their own AI assistant to a real audience. That's not nothing. That's a lot.

Your Questions, Answered Honestly

The things parents actually want to know

We've heard these questions from real parents. Here are straight answers — no spin, no marketing fluff.

Safety

Is this safe for kids? Will they be exposed to inappropriate AI?

The curriculum contains no open-ended AI chat interfaces and no connection to consumer AI products like ChatGPT. Your child works with curated, age-reviewed datasets and purpose-built tools. There are no unpredictable outputs, no social features, and no external content feeds.

The tools students use — Teachable Machine, Hugging Face classifiers, and the OpenClaw agent platform — are deterministic and controlled. What goes in is what we put in. What comes out is what we trained it for.

Screen Time

Is this just more screen time?

Yes, this uses computers. So does almost every professional skill your child will ever develop. The more useful question is: what are they doing with the screen?

Building, debugging, making decisions, and writing policy documents is categorically different from passive consumption. Most families report that their kids come away from AI Explorers more skeptical of AI, not more dependent on it — because they understand what's actually happening behind the screen.

Learning Basics

Will this replace learning the fundamentals?

No. AI Explorers isn't a replacement for math, writing, or science — it's a project that draws on all three. Students read, write, analyze data, reason through problems, and make ethical arguments. The curriculum is deliberately cross-disciplinary.

What it replaces: the false idea that AI is magic, and the helplessness that comes from not understanding the tools that are already shaping your child's world.

Dependency

Will this make my child dependent on AI for thinking?

The opposite. Students who build AI understand its limits viscerally — because they hit those limits while training it. They know what bad training data looks like. They know why AI gets things wrong. They know what an AI system can't do.

Kids who have built AI are far less likely to blindly trust it. They have inside knowledge. That's exactly what we're building for.

Tech Background

My child isn't a "tech kid." Will they be lost?

The students who do best in this curriculum aren't necessarily the ones who already love computers. They're curious kids — art kids, writing kids, science kids, sports kids. Each of them builds something different, and they're all equally capable of completing the full arc.

Module 1 requires zero prior experience. The only prerequisite is being willing to try.

Privacy

Is my child's data collected anywhere?

No. The curriculum runs on school-hosted infrastructure — student data never touches external servers. There are no accounts required, no cloud services processing student work, and no third-party data agreements needed.

If your child's school runs AI Explorers, their data stays at the school. Full stop.

The Ethics Component

This curriculum teaches kids to think critically about AI — not just use it

Ethics isn't a single lesson at the end. It's woven through every module, and it reaches its peak in Module 6 — where students audit the AI they built.

  • 🔍

    Ethics by doing, not by lecture

    Your child doesn't read about AI bias in a textbook. They encounter it in their own training data — and have to decide what to do about it. That experience doesn't fade.

  • ⚖️

    Students write real usage policies

    In Module 6, every student writes a governance document for the AI they built: what it should do, what it should refuse, and who is responsible when it goes wrong. Real professional engineers do this. So do our students.

  • 🧩

    Bias testing lab

    Students run a structured audit of their agent, probing for failures and unfair patterns. They document what they find. They fix what they can. They acknowledge what they can't. That's the job.

  • 🗣️

    Ethics as a design conversation

    Who gets harmed if this AI is wrong? Who benefits? These aren't hypotheticals for our students — they're questions about something they actually built, with answers they have to defend in front of an audience.

Why this matters for your child specifically

Your child will live in a world shaped by AI systems they didn't design and don't control. Algorithmic feeds will influence what they see. Automated decisions will affect their opportunities. Deepfakes will test their ability to tell what's real.

The students who can navigate that world aren't the ones who know AI exists — they're the ones who know how it works, how it fails, and how to question it.

That's not a technical skill. That's a civic one. And it starts here, with a 12-year-old who just audited the AI they built and found a flaw they didn't expect.

We think that moment — the one where they realize they put the bias in — is one of the most important things we teach.

Age Appropriateness

Designed for grades 6–8.
Tested with real middle schoolers.

This isn't a college course for kids. It's built from the ground up for the cognitive stage of a 11–14 year old — curious, capable, and ready to build something real.

6–8

Grade-level sweet spot

Middle school is when durable mental models form. A student who understands AI at 12 carries that understanding into high school, college, and their career — as background knowledge, not something they have to relearn.

No code

Modules 1–3 require zero coding

The first half of the curriculum is designed for students with no programming experience. Concepts are introduced through visual tools, guided activities, and interactive labs before any code appears.

Real

Real tools, age-appropriate scope

Students use the same tools adult developers use — but in controlled, curated environments. The scope is right for a middle schooler; the tools are real enough to matter when they see them again in five years.

Tested

Iterated with actual students

This curriculum was refined through real classroom pilots with real middle schoolers. Activities that didn't land were cut. Pacing that was too fast was slowed. What remains is what actually works at this age.

Flex

Scales for different levels

Each module includes scaffolded support for students who need more structure and extension challenges for those who want to go deeper. No student is left behind; no student is unchallenged.

Free

No cost, no accounts, no barriers

The full curriculum is free. No student account required. No software to install. It runs in a browser. The only requirement is curiosity — and a middle schooler willing to try something new.

For Parents

How to support your child — without needing to understand AI yourself

You don't need to know how machine learning works to be the best support your kid has during this curriculum. Here's what actually helps.

  • 🙋

    Ask "what did you build today?" not "how was class?"

    The curriculum is project-based. Every module has a deliverable. Your kid will have something to show you. Asking about the thing they made opens much better conversations than asking about school in general.

  • 👂

    Let them teach you

    When your child explains how their AI works, let them. Even if it's over your head. The act of explaining to someone who doesn't know is one of the most powerful learning tools there is — and your interest is real encouragement.

  • 🤔

    Ask the hard questions alongside them

    When Module 6 comes around (ethics), ask your child: What should your AI refuse to do? What if it gets something wrong about a real person? These aren't trick questions. They're the exact questions the curriculum is asking. Your curiosity helps.

  • 🎤

    Show up to the AI Expo

    Module 7 is a live demo event where students present their finished agents to a real audience. Parents are part of that audience. Being there matters. Your child will remember it.

  • 🚫

    Don't do it for them

    The curriculum is designed to be finished by the student. If your child is stuck, the right move is to encourage them to re-read the activity guide — not to Google the answer together. The struggle is the point. The learning is in the doing.

You don't need to be a tech parent

The most common thing parents tell us after the AI Expo is: "I didn't understand everything they built, but I understood that they understood it — and that was amazing to see."

Your job isn't to be the AI expert. Your job is to be the audience that matters most when your kid stands up and says "here's what I made."

Show up. Ask questions. Express genuine curiosity. That's the whole playbook. The rest, your child's teacher has covered.

And if you want to understand more yourself: the curriculum is entirely public. You can read Module 1 tonight. Everything is open, free, and written to be understood.

Parent FAQ

Questions parents always ask

Is this safe for my child? What content does it expose them to?

The curriculum contains no external AI chat interfaces and no connection to consumer AI products like ChatGPT or similar. All activities are self-contained. Students work with curated, age-reviewed datasets and purpose-built tools.

There are no unpredictable AI outputs, no social features, and no exposure to outside content. What your child's agent knows is exactly what they taught it. That's the whole point — they're the designer, not the user.

What age is this appropriate for?

The curriculum is designed for grades 6–8 (typically ages 11–14). It has been tested and iterated with real students in this range. Module 1 requires no prior experience whatsoever. Later modules introduce more complexity, but with scaffolding that matches the grade level.

Students younger than 11 may find some conceptual framing difficult, though motivated younger learners with parental support often do well. Students older than 14 will likely find the full curriculum engaging, and may move through it faster.

Will this make my child dependent on AI for thinking?

No — and we'd argue the opposite. Students who have built AI understand its limits in a way that passive users never do. They've seen bad training data produce wrong answers. They've encountered bias in their own models. They've written policies for what their agent should not do.

That inside knowledge makes them more skeptical, not less. When a classmate says "I'll just ask ChatGPT," your child will be the one who asks: "What was it trained on? How do we know if it's right?"

How much time will this take each week?

In a school setting, each module is designed to fit into a semester class schedule. The sprint version of each module takes about 1 week of regular class time. The extended version takes up to 4 weeks.

If your child is doing this at home, expect 3–5 hours per module for most students. Some students move faster; some take longer with the hands-on labs. There's no race — the goal is completion and understanding, not speed.

My child's school already does "something with AI." Is this different?

Probably yes. Most "AI" content in schools right now is about AI: its history, its uses, how to identify deepfakes, how to use tools responsibly. That's valuable — but it's passive.

AI Explorers is about building AI. Students train models, write logic, make design decisions, and govern the thing they created. The difference in depth of understanding — and in how students think about AI for the rest of their lives — is significant.

What if my child isn't a "tech person"?

This is one of the most common parent questions, and the answer is consistent: the students who excel in this curriculum aren't the ones who already love computers. They're curious students — regardless of their other interests.

We've seen art students design agents with remarkable creative vision. We've seen writers build agents that reflect their storytelling instincts. We've seen students who had never touched code build something they're proud of by Module 3. The prerequisite is curiosity, not a tech background.

Do I need to understand AI to help my child?

No. Your role isn't to be the AI expert — it's to be interested. Ask your child what they built. Let them explain it to you. Show up to the Module 7 AI Expo. That's the whole playbook for parents.

If you want to understand more yourself, the full curriculum is publicly available on GitHub. Module 1 is written to be understood by anyone, regardless of background. You can read it tonight if you're curious — and many parents do.

Is my child's data collected or stored anywhere?

No. The curriculum is designed so that all student work stays on school-managed infrastructure. There are no student accounts on external platforms, no cloud services processing student data, and no data processing agreements required with third-party vendors.

If your child's school adopts AI Explorers, their data — their agent, their models, their journal — stays with the school. We don't touch it. This is by design, not by accident.

How do I get my child's school to adopt this?

The easiest path: forward this page to a teacher you trust. AI Explorers costs nothing to adopt. A motivated teacher can start using Module 1 this week without any budget, approval process, or vendor relationship. Everything is free and public.

If you want to take it to administration or a school board, visit our educators page — it includes an adoption brief designed for exactly that conversation. Schools that have run pilots have consistently been able to expand to the full middle school the following year.
