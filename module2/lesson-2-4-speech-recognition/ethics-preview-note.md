# Ethics Preview Note — Teacher Guide

**For:** Lesson 2.4 — How AI Hears: Speech Recognition  
**Section:** Closing of "Who Does AI Hear?" discussion  
**Audience:** Teacher / Facilitator only

---

## What Just Happened

The bias discussion in Lesson 2.4 is not an interruption of the technical content — it **is** technical content. By examining who speech recognition systems fail, students are learning something real about how AI systems are built, what data is used to train them, and what happens when design decisions aren't examined critically.

This moment matters beyond today's lesson. It's the **first planted seed** for Module 6's deep work on AI fairness.

---

## The Bridge to Module 6

Module 6 (AI Ethics & Society) will return to these exact questions — but with more frameworks, more evidence, and more depth. The connection points are:

| Today (Lesson 2.4) | Module 6 |
|---------------------|----------|
| Speech recognition works unequally for different accents/languages | Lesson 6.1: What is algorithmic bias? |
| Training data shapes model behavior | Lesson 6.2: Data & representation in AI |
| "Who was this built for?" | Lesson 6.3: Design for whom? |
| Shared responsibility for AI harms | Lesson 6.4: Accountability in AI systems |

**Key framing to use today:**
> *"We're going to come back to this in Module 6 when we study AI fairness in depth. Today is the first look. Hold your questions."*

This framing:
- Validates that the questions are hard and don't have quick answers
- Creates anticipation for Module 6 rather than treating ethics as an afterthought
- Gives students "permission" to stay with uncertainty

---

## The Sticky Note Moment

At the close of today's discussion, ask students to write on a sticky note:

> *"Who was this system built for?"*

This question is intentionally open. It applies to speech recognition today. It will apply to every AI system they encounter in Module 6 — and beyond.

**Options for what to do with the sticky notes:**
1. Students keep them — physically — and bring them to the first class of Module 6
2. Post them on a classroom wall under the heading "Questions We're Holding"
3. Collect them and redistribute at the start of Module 6 as a callback
4. Have students photograph them and add to their project portfolios

There is no wrong answer. The goal is **continuity** — making Module 6 feel like a return to a conversation that started here, not a new topic dropped in.

---

## What If the Discussion Gets Heavy

Speech recognition bias isn't an abstract topic for all students. Some students may have:
- Personal experience with voice assistants that don't understand them
- Family members who have been failed by automated transcription systems
- Experiences navigating institutions (medical, legal, educational) where AI-assisted tools performed poorly for them

**If a student shares a personal story:**
- Slow down and honor it
- Don't rush to the next prompt
- You can say: *"Thank you for sharing that. That's exactly the kind of experience that data can't fully capture."*

**If the conversation surfaces frustration or anger:**
- That's appropriate. Don't suppress it.
- Channel it: *"That frustration is useful. In Module 6, we're going to look at what people are actually doing to push back on this — and you'll have a chance to figure out where you want to put your energy."*

**If students minimize the problem ("it's not that big a deal"):**
- Come back to the high-stakes examples: medical transcription, legal proceedings, 911 calls, accessibility
- Ask: *"Is it not a big deal if it's not your voice it fails on?"*

---

## Calibrating the Depth

You know your students. Some classes will go deep on this discussion in 7 minutes; others will need more time or scaffolding.

**If you have more time today:**
- Use the optional Prompt 9 from the discussion guide: *"When you add audio to your agent in Lab 2.4, how will you think about this?"*
- This is a powerful question that bridges ethics directly to student project work

**If you're running short on time:**
- The sticky note moment is the non-negotiable part — even 2 minutes for that
- The full discussion can be compressed to "name the problem, feel the stakes, mark it for Module 6"
- Don't skip it entirely — even a 3-minute version plants the seed

---

## A Note on Your Own Posture

This discussion asks students to critique systems built by major tech companies — systems that are widely used, widely celebrated, and possibly used by the students themselves (Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant, voice typing).

It's okay to:
- Acknowledge the genuine achievements of these technologies
- Hold complexity ("this is remarkable *and* it has real problems")
- Not have all the answers
- Express your own curiosity or uncertainty

It's important to:
- Not rescue students from discomfort prematurely
- Name that this is an ongoing problem, not a solved one
- Make clear that the people building these systems are human — they made choices, and different choices are possible
