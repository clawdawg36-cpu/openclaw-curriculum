# 🎨 Pixel Portrait Activity Guide
## Module 2 — How Computers See

**Time:** 30–45 minutes  
**Materials:** Printed pixel grid templates, colored pencils or markers, access to a device with an AI image tool (optional: Google Vision, Teachable Machine, or similar)  
**Group size:** Pairs or small groups

---

## The Big Idea

Computers don't see faces — they see **numbers**.

Every image on a screen is made of tiny squares called **pixels**. Each pixel has a number that represents its color. When an AI "looks" at a photo of your face, it's really just reading a giant grid of numbers and finding patterns.

In this activity, you'll:
1. Draw a pixel portrait of your partner
2. Challenge an AI to recognize your drawing
3. Discover what visual features actually matter to machines

> 💡 **Through-line:** "Your AI agent will soon see actual photos — but it still processes them as numbers, just like your pixel art."

---

## Part 1: Draw Your Pixel Portrait (15–20 min)

### Step 1 — Choose Your Grid

Pick a grid size based on how detailed you want to go:
- **8×8** — Super simple, abstract, great for beginners
- **16×16** — Enough for a recognizable face shape
- **32×32** — The most detail; you can show hair, eyes, and expression

> 🖨️ Print the grid from `pixel-grid-template.md` or draw your own on graph paper.

### Step 2 — Observe Your Partner

Before you draw, spend **2 minutes** really looking at your partner's face. Notice:
- Face shape (round, oval, square?)
- Hair (color, length, curly or straight?)
- Eye shape and color
- Any distinctive features (glasses, freckles, dimples, etc.)

### Step 3 — Plan Your Palette

Pick **3–5 colors** maximum. Pixel art works best with a limited palette. Common choices:
- Skin tone
- Hair color
- Eye color
- Background color
- One accent color (shirt, glasses, etc.)

### Step 4 — Draw!

Start from the outside in:
1. **Head shape** — fill the outer boundary first
2. **Hair** — block in the main color
3. **Skin** — fill the face area
4. **Features** — eyes, nose, mouth (each might only be 1–2 pixels!)
5. **Details** — glasses, freckles, smile lines

**Remember:** Every square must be one solid color. No blending!

---

## Part 2: The AI Recognition Challenge (10–15 min)

Now test your pixel portrait against an AI.

### Option A: Google Vision AI (if internet available)
1. Take a photo of your completed pixel art
2. Upload it to [Google Cloud Vision API Demo](https://cloud.google.com/vision) or [Vision AI Explorer](https://cloud.google.com/vision/docs/drag-and-drop)
3. See what labels the AI assigns

### Option B: Teachable Machine (classroom-friendly)
1. Go to [Teachable Machine](https://teachablemachine.withgoogle.com/)
2. Train a quick image model with examples from Google Images
3. Test your drawing against it

### Option C: Ask an AI Assistant
1. Take a photo of your drawing
2. Upload it to Claude, ChatGPT, or another vision-capable AI
3. Ask: *"What is this a picture of? Describe what you see."*

---

## Part 3: Compare and Discuss (5–10 min)

Share your results with the class:
- Which drawings did the AI recognize correctly?
- Which did it get wrong or give weird results for?
- What patterns do you notice?

**Facilitator prompts:**
- "What made one drawing easier for the AI to understand than another?"
- "Did having more pixels always help? Why or why not?"
- "What information is in a real photo that your pixel art is missing?"

---

## Key Takeaways

✅ Computers process images as grids of numbers — just like your pixel art  
✅ More pixels = more information, but not always more recognizable  
✅ AI looks for **features** (edges, shapes, patterns), not meaning  
✅ What a human sees as "a face" is very different from what a computer sees  

---

## Looking Ahead

In the next session, your AI agent will start working with real photos. But here's the thing — it still sees them as numbers. The only difference? A 12-megapixel phone photo has **12,000,000 pixels** instead of 64 (8×8) or 256 (16×16).

The math is bigger. The principle is the same.

> *"Computers see pixels, not people."*
