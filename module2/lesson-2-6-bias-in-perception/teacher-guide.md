# Teacher Guide — Lesson 2.6
## Bias in Perception AI (Ethics Preview)

**For:** Teachers facilitating this lesson  
**Sensitivity level:** Moderate — involves race, language, disability, and lived experience of exclusion

---

## Why This Lesson Needs Care

This lesson isn't theoretical for everyone in the room.

When we discuss facial recognition failing for darker-skinned people, some students are in that group. When we discuss speech recognition failing for certain accents, some students carry that accent. When we discuss whose world is represented in training data, some students already know they're often excluded.

This lesson can land very differently depending on who's in your classroom. The goal is not to make anyone feel like a problem to be solved or a case study in their own pain. The goal is to build *shared understanding* that these are structural failures — not individual ones.

This guide gives you tools to facilitate that well.

---

## Before the Lesson

### Know Your Room

Think about your class:
- Do you have students from communities directly affected by facial recognition policing?
- Do you have students for whom English is a second language, or who speak AAVE or another dialect?
- Do you have students from non-Western countries or immigrant families?
- Do you have students who have been stopped by police, had their identity questioned, or felt like technology wasn't built for them?

You don't need to know everything about every student. But awareness helps you anticipate moments that might land differently than expected.

### Set Your Own Posture

If you're not from a marginalized community, be honest with yourself about your limits. You can facilitate this discussion without having all the answers. "I don't fully know what this feels like" is more honest and more useful than pretending to.

If you are from a community discussed in the lesson, you get to decide how much of your own experience to share. Sharing can build connection. You're not obligated to perform your own marginalization.

## Warm-Up Facilitation Notes

The warm-up question ("Has technology ever failed you?") is designed to be open enough for everyone to answer — not just students from marginalized groups.

**If students share experiences that clearly connect to bias** (e.g., "Face ID doesn't work on me," "Siri never understands my mom's voice"):
- Acknowledge their experience directly: "Thank you for sharing that. That's exactly what we're going to look at today — why those things happen, and what they mean."
- Don't minimize: "It's improving" or "all tech has bugs" shuts the conversation down prematurely.
- Don't over-dramatize: You don't need to turn every warm-up share into a crisis. Name it, acknowledge it, move forward.

**If no one shares experiences connected to bias:**
- That's fine. The case studies will generate discussion regardless.
- Don't push for vulnerable sharing. Move on warmly.

**If a student makes a dismissive comment** (e.g., "tech doesn't discriminate, it's just code"):
- This is actually a great teaching moment: "That's a really common assumption — let's test it against what the research shows."
- Don't argue. Let the case studies do the work.

## During Case Studies: What to Watch For

### If a Student Feels Personally Targeted

Signs: Student becomes quiet or withdrawn, looks uncomfortable, makes a self-deprecating comment, or conversely, becomes defensive or agitated.

What to do:
- Don't single them out
- After class, check in privately: "I noticed today got pretty real. Are you doing okay? I'm here if you want to talk."
- If the discussion itself is causing harm, it's okay to redirect: "I want to make sure we're treating these as structural failures, not personal ones. The problem isn't with any person in this room — it's with who was in the room when these systems were built."

### If a Student Shares a Personal Story of AI Failure

This is valuable and should be received carefully:
- "Thank you for sharing that. It's real and it matters."
- Don't rush past it to the next topic
- If appropriate, note: "This is exactly why studying this matters — because the people in this room might be the ones who build these systems next."

### If a Student From an Affected Group Becomes the "Expert"

Watch for moments where Black students are implicitly expected to explain racism, or international students are expected to explain non-Western perspectives.

- Redirect to the research: "What does the data say about this?"
- Make it a collective question: "Let's all think about this together."
- After class, check in: "I want to make sure today wasn't a burden — you're here to learn, not to educate everyone else."

### If a White or Non-Affected Student Gets Defensive

Signs: "But I've had voice assistant problems too," "I don't think it's really about race," "These companies are trying their best."

What to do:
- Validate the feeling without letting it shut down the conversation: "It's true that tech fails everyone sometimes. Today we're specifically looking at *systematic* differences — patterns that show up consistently for specific groups. That's different from random bugs."
- Invite curiosity: "I wonder what the data would say about that. Let's look."
- Don't let one voice dominate. Return to the broader group.

## Discussing Race in a Tech Context

You don't need to teach a full unit on racism to teach this lesson. But race will come up.

**Useful framing:**
> "We're talking about structural bias — patterns baked into systems, often unintentionally, because of who was and wasn't in the room when they were built. That's different from saying any one person is racist."

**Phrases that help:**
- "The data shows that..."
- "Researchers found that..."
- "This doesn't mean anyone intended harm — it means the process had gaps."
- "This is why representation in tech matters — not as a feel-good slogan, but as an engineering necessity."

**Phrases to avoid:**
- "All lives matter" / "it happens to everyone" (minimizes documented disparities)
- "I don't see color / race / etc." (unhelpfully erases the issue)
- "This is getting too political" (algorithmic bias isn't partisan; it's documented)

## Discussing Accents and Language

This can be personal for students whose first language isn't English or who speak a dialect.

**Useful framing:**
> "AAVE, Nigerian English, Indian English — these are complete, rule-governed language varieties. They're not 'wrong' English. The speech recognition systems have gaps. That's on the systems."

**If a student feels self-conscious about their accent:**
- Reinforce: "Your accent is not the problem. The training data is."
- Offer: "Some of the most important work in fixing this has been done by researchers from those same communities — people who understood the problem because they'd lived it."

---

## The "Your Agent" Section: Handling Discomfort

When students audit their own agents and find potential bias, some will feel guilty or defensive. Others will feel energized.

**For students who feel bad:**
> "You didn't build the model. You used someone else's tool. The goal today isn't to make you feel guilty — it's to make you a more conscious builder going forward. Now you know to ask these questions before you deploy, not after."

**For students who feel defensive of their agent:**
> "It's not about your agent being 'bad.' It's about understanding its limits. Every tool has limits. Knowing them is what separates a thoughtful engineer from one who just ships and hopes."

**For students who feel energized:**
> Let them run with it. These are the students who might actually go fix something.

---

## If the Discussion Gets Heated

Bias in AI is ultimately about bias in society. Conversations can escalate.

**If students are in direct conflict:**
- Slow it down: "Let's step back to what the data says."
- Name the emotion: "This is clearly something people feel strongly about — that makes sense. Let's make sure we're discussing the systems, not each other."
- Take a break if needed: "Let's take two minutes, then come back."

**If a student says something harmful:**
- Address it directly but calmly: "I want to pause on that — that framing can be hurtful. Let's think about how to say what you mean more carefully."
- Don't shame, but don't ignore.

**If you're losing the room:**
- Return to concrete: "Let's go back to the research. What did the study actually find?"
- Redirect to the agent: "Here's something concrete — let's try running one of these tests right now."

## After the Lesson

### Check In With Yourself

Did anything come up that you weren't prepared for? Document it. Update these notes for next year.

### Follow Up With Students

Check exit tickets, especially Question 4 (the emotion check). If a student marked "Angry" or "Confused" or "Surprised," consider a brief one-on-one: "I noticed today hit differently for you. How are you doing?"

## Connection to Module 6

This lesson deliberately doesn't resolve the tension. Students should leave with:
- Understanding of what the problem is
- Some personal stakes (their own agent may have biases)
- Questions, not answers

Module 6 will revisit this directly when students add ethics guardrails to their agents. By then, they'll have context — because they built something, audited it, and saw the problem for themselves.

When you introduce Module 6, you can call back: "Remember in 2.6 when we looked at your agent's model card? We're going to fix some of those gaps now."
