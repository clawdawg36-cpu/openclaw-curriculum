# Curriculum Vision
## *Build Your AI Assistant, Piece by Piece*
### A Guide for Teachers and Administrators

---

## The Central Idea

Most AI curricula teach students *about* artificial intelligence — its history, how it works, its societal impacts. Those are important. But they leave students as spectators of a technology that is reshaping their world.

**This curriculum takes a different stance: students become builders.**

From the first day of Module 1 to the final presentation in Module 7, every student follows a single through-line: they are building their own personal AI assistant, one capability at a time. By the end of the course, they don't just understand AI — they own one, and they made it.

---

## The Arc

The curriculum is structured around **seven capability unlocks**, one per module:

| Module | What Students Build | Capability Gained |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| M1 | Deploy OpenClaw, name their agent, write its personality | Identity |
| M2 | Connect image and audio models from Hugging Face | Perception (Vision) |
| M3 | Write a custom skill with domain knowledge and reasoning | Knowledge |
| M4 | Train a real ML model and integrate it | Learning |
| M5 | Connect the agent to a live channel (Discord, iMessage) | Voice / Communication |
| M6 | Add guardrails, bias checks, and usage policies | Ethics / Responsibility |
| M7 | Present the finished agent at the AI Expo | Synthesis + Presentation |

Each module opens with a simple, memorable question: *"What can your agent do now that it couldn't do before?"* And each module closes with students updating their Agent Design Journal — a living document of what they built and why.

---

## Why This Approach Works

**Motivation through ownership.** Students who build something care about it. When a student names their agent "Aria" in Module 1, they're more invested in giving Aria vision in Module 2, teaching Aria about marine biology in Module 3, and making sure Aria doesn't say harmful things in Module 6. Ownership drives engagement.

**Concrete before abstract.** AI concepts are notoriously abstract — neural networks, training data, bias. This curriculum makes each concept *tactile* before making it theoretical. Students train a model before studying backpropagation. They encounter bias in their own agent's outputs before analyzing it in case studies. The build arc ensures that theory always follows experience.

**Coherence across 7 modules.** Traditional survey-style AI curricula can feel disjointed — a unit on ML here, a unit on ethics there. The build arc provides a narrative thread that ties everything together. Ethics in Module 6 isn't a separate topic; it's the natural conclusion to "I have a powerful agent — what are my responsibilities as its creator?"

**Authentic assessment.** The Module 7 AI Expo is not a test. It's a demonstration to a real audience of something real that students built. That is a fundamentally different — and more powerful — assessment modality than a written exam.

---

## Alignment with Learning Science

This curriculum is grounded in three well-established learning frameworks:

**Project-Based Learning (PBL).** The entire 7-module arc is a single, sustained project. Students have a driving question (*"How do I build an AI assistant?"*), create a public product (the AI Expo demo), and reflect on their process throughout.

**Constructivism.** Students construct understanding by building. Each new concept is introduced in the context of adding a capability to their agent — not as standalone knowledge to be memorized.

**Spiral Curriculum.** Core ideas — training, data, ethics, human-AI interaction — recur across all 7 modules at increasing depth. A student who first encounters "bias" in Module 1 as a concept will encounter it again in Module 4 when evaluating their training data, again in Module 6 when writing their usage policy, and again in Module 7 when fielding audience questions about their agent's behavior.

---

## What Students Will Walk Away With

By the end of the curriculum, every student will have:

1. **A working AI agent** — deployed, named, personalized, connected to the world, and governed by policies they wrote
2. **Technical literacy** — a real understanding of how ML models work, what training data is, and what an agentic AI system does
3. **Ethical awareness** — not as a lecture topic but as a lived design decision: "What should my agent do, and what should it refuse?"
4. **Presentation skills** — the ability to explain a complex technical system to a non-technical audience
5. **Agency** — the experience of having made something that didn't exist before

## Notes for Administrators

**Time commitment:** The full arc runs 7 modules at 2–4 weeks each, totaling approximately one semester. Each module can also be taught as a standalone unit with minor scaffolding adjustments.

**Technology requirements:** Students need access to an OpenClaw instance (self-hosted or teacher-shared), a Hugging Face account (free), and a modern web browser. No coding experience is required for Modules 1–3; Python familiarity is helpful for Module 4.

**Differentiation:** Each module includes a 1-week sprint version and a 4-week extended version. The arc scales up or down gracefully — even a student who only completes Modules 1–3 leaves with a named, personalized agent with vision and custom knowledge.

**The AI Expo:** Module 7 is designed to be a school event, not just a classroom activity. We encourage teachers to invite parents, community members, and local tech professionals. Students rise to real audiences.

## A Note on the Technology

This curriculum uses **OpenClaw** as the primary platform — an open-source AI agent runtime designed specifically for education. OpenClaw is:

- Free and self-hostable
- Privacy-respecting (no student data leaves the school's infrastructure)
- Designed for extension — students build real skills, not simulated ones
- Already used in production by adult developers, which means students are learning real tools

The agents students build in this curriculum are not toys. They are real software, running real AI models, connected to real communication channels. That's intentional. When students know they're building something real, they build with more care.
