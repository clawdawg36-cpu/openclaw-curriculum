# Slides Outline — Lesson 5.4: AI as a Study Partner

**Module:** 5 — Give Your Agent a Voice  
**Lesson:** 5.4 — AI as a Study Partner  
**Format:** ~10 slides, 50-minute period

---

## Slide 1 — Title Slide

**Headline:** AI as a Study Partner  
**Subhead:** Practical Skills · Academic Integrity · Where's the Line?  
**Visual:** Two figures studying together — one human, one robot — books and laptop  
**Note:** Don't linger. Go straight to the opening scenario.

---

## Slide 2 — The 10 PM Study Crisis

**Headline:** It's 10 PM. Test tomorrow. You're exhausted.  
**Content:**
> "You have a history test tomorrow on the causes of World War I. You've been at practice all evening. Your notes are a mess. You have AI access. What do you do?"

**Visual:** Dramatic clock at 10:00 PM, messy notebook, glowing laptop  
**Note:** Ask the class. Get range of honest answers. Don't judge yet.

---

## Slide 3 — Five Ways AI Can Actually Help

**Headline:** AI as a tutor — when you're in the driver's seat  
**Content (reveal one at a time):**

1. 📖 **Explanation on demand** — "Explain this to me like I've never heard of it"
2. ❓ **Practice questions** — "Give me 10 quiz questions on this topic"
3. 📇 **Flashcard generation** — "Turn my notes into flashcard pairs"
4. ✏️ **Writing feedback** — "Tell me what to improve — but do NOT rewrite it"
5. 🔄 **The explain-it-back check** — "Tell me if I got this right"

**Visual:** Five icons, each with a short example prompt  
**Note:** Demo each one live as you reveal it. Keep demos short (2–3 min each).

---

## Slide 4 — The Key Insight

**Headline:** You're the student. AI is the tutor.  
**Content:**
- Every good use keeps YOU thinking, deciding, writing
- AI should accelerate your learning — not replace it
- The goal: finish tomorrow smarter than you are today

**Visual:** Driver's seat / steering wheel metaphor  
**Note:** Pause here. Ask: "What's the difference between a good tutor and one who just does your homework?"

---

## Slide 5 — Three Ways AI Becomes a Bad Study Partner

**Headline:** When AI hurts you (even when it doesn't feel like it)  
**Content (animated reveal):**

1. ⚠️ **The Shortcut Trap** — Submit AI's essay. Learn nothing. Fail the test.
2. ⚠️ **The False Confidence Problem** — AI sounds right. Isn't always. Trust without checking = danger.
3. ⚠️ **The Dependency Spiral** — Never practice. Never build skill. Can't do it on the test.

**Visual:** Red warning icons for each  
**Note:** Connect #2 to upcoming Lesson 5.5 (When AI Gets It Wrong).

---

## Slide 6 — The Line-Drawing Activity

**Headline:** Where's the line?  
**Content:**
- You'll get 8 scenarios
- Sort each one: 🟢 Appropriate / 🟡 Gray area / 🔴 Not appropriate
- Then we compare

**Visual:** Green, yellow, red circles; sticky note graphic  
**Note:** Distribute cards (print or display). Give 5 minutes for individual/pair sorting, then debrief.

---

## Slide 7 — Scenarios Reference (stay on screen during activity)

**Headline:** The 8 Scenarios  
**Content:**
1. AI explains a math concept you didn't understand
2. AI writes your college application essay
3. AI checks your grammar on your own essay
4. AI writes a draft; you rewrite it completely
5. AI generates practice questions; you quiz yourself
6. AI summarizes a Wikipedia article for your research paper
7. AI gives feedback on your thesis statement
8. AI writes your lab report conclusion from data points

**Note:** Keep this slide up during the card sort. Students will debate — that's the point.

---

## Slide 8 — Who Decides?

**Headline:** Who gets to decide what's OK?  
**Content:**
- The school? The teacher? The student? The AI company?
- What's YOUR responsibility?
- Does it matter if no one finds out?

**Visual:** Three overlapping circles: School Rules / Personal Ethics / What Actually Helps You Learn  
**Note:** Run this as a Socratic discussion. Don't resolve it for them — the discomfort is the learning.

---

## Slide 9 — Your Personal AI Study Policy

**Headline:** Make it official — your own AI rules  
**Content:**
- What will you use AI for in your studies?
- What won't you use it for?
- How will you make sure you're actually learning?

**Visual:** Document / contract graphic with signature line  
**Note:** Distribute the template. Give 5–7 minutes. Ask 2–3 to share.

---

## Slide 10 — Exit Ticket

**Headline:** One good use. One thing to avoid.  
**Content:**
> In your journal: Describe ONE way you plan to use AI as a study tool this week that would actually help you learn. Then describe one thing you'll avoid.

**Visual:** Timer (2 minutes)  
**Note:** Collect or scan journals. These are useful formative data — look for students who only describe output-producing uses (flag for follow-up).

---

## Teacher Notes — Slide Deck Setup

- Slide 3: Build each use case as a click-through reveal; demo live before advancing
- Slide 7: Keep this visible during the sorting activity (don't advance)
- Slide 8: Consider a live poll (Mentimeter, Kahoot, or hand raise) for each scenario before the open discussion
- The scenario sort works well in pairs — assign roles: "advocate" (argue for appropriate) and "skeptic" (argue against)
