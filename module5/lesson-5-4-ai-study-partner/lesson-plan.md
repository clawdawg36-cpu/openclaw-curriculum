# Lesson 5.4 — AI as a Study Partner

**Module:** 5 — Give Your Agent a Voice  
**Duration:** 1 period (~50 minutes)  
**Grade Level:** 7–8 (adaptable for 9–12)  
**Prerequisites:** Lessons 5.1–5.3 (students can prompt effectively; understand how chatbots work)

---

## 🔗 Through-Line Connection

> *"You've learned how chatbots work, how to prompt them well, and you've competed. Now: how do you actually use this for your own life — specifically, your schoolwork? And what are the lines you shouldn't cross?"*

Lesson 5.4 makes prompt engineering practical and personal. Students explore AI as a legitimate study tool — and then have an honest, structured conversation about academic integrity. This lesson is deliberately positioned after the Olympics (5.3) because students now have real prompting skill; they're not experimenting blindly, they're applying competence. The academic integrity discussion is not a scare tactic — it's a real conversation about authorship, learning, and what school is for.

**Bridge to Lesson 5.5:** After building productive habits with AI, students will examine the risk side in 5.5 — when AI gets it wrong, and how to catch it.

---

## 🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Demonstrate at least three practical ways to use AI as a study tool (explanation, practice questions, feedback).
2. Evaluate whether a specific AI use in an academic context is appropriate, appropriate with disclosure, or inappropriate.
3. Articulate the difference between using AI to *support* learning and using AI to *replace* learning.
4. Draft a personal "AI Study Policy" for their own academic work.
5. Explain at least two ways AI assistance could harm their learning in the long run.

## ⏱️ Lesson Outline

### Opening: The Study Crisis (5 min)

**Scenario (read aloud or project):**
> *"It's 10 PM. You have a history test tomorrow on the causes of World War I. You've been at soccer practice all evening. You're exhausted. Your notes are a mess. You have access to your AI agent. What do you do?"*

Ask students:
- *"What would you actually do?"*
- *"What SHOULD you do?"*
- *"Are those the same thing?"*

Take 3–4 answers. Don't judge yet. Just surface the range — from "nothing wrong with asking AI" to "that's cheating."

**Bridge:** *"Today we're going to figure out exactly what's OK, what's a gray area, and what's over the line. And we're going to build skills that actually help you use AI to LEARN — not just to be done."*

---

### Segment 1: What AI Can Actually Do for Students (12 min)

**Five practical use cases — demo each one live:**

**1. Explanation on demand** (3 min)
> *"Explain the concept of supply and demand to me as if I've never taken economics. Then give me a real-world example from my daily life."*

Demo: paste the prompt, show the output. Discussion: Is this different from reading a textbook? What's better about it? What's worse?

**2. Practice questions** (2 min)
> *"I'm studying for a quiz on the American Revolution. Give me 5 practice multiple-choice questions at a difficulty level of 8th grade."*

Demo: show the output. Ask: How would you use this? What would you do if you got one wrong?

**3. Flashcard generation** (2 min)
> *"Turn these bullet points from my notes into flashcard pairs: [paste messy notes]. Format: FRONT: [term] / BACK: [definition]."*

Demo: paste sample messy notes, show clean flashcard output.

**4. Feedback on your writing** (3 min)
> *"Here's a paragraph I wrote for English class. Tell me what's working well and what I should improve — but do NOT rewrite it for me."*

Demo: use a (deliberately weak) student paragraph. Show the feedback. Key note: the prompt says "do NOT rewrite it" — that's intentional.

**5. The "explain it back" check** (2 min)
> *"I'm going to explain photosynthesis to you in my own words. Tell me if I got anything wrong."*

Demo: teacher "explains" photosynthesis with a deliberate error. Show AI catching it. Discussion: This makes AI a study partner, not a replacement.

**Key insight to name:**
> *"Every one of these use cases keeps YOU in the driver's seat. You're the one learning, deciding, writing. The AI is the tutor — not the student."*

---

### Segment 2: What Makes AI a BAD Study Partner (8 min)

**Flip the script:**

*"But what if you use AI in a way that STOPS you from learning?"*

**Three harmful patterns:**

**1. The Shortcut Trap**
Student: "Write me an essay on the causes of WWI."
AI: [writes essay]
Student: [submits it]

*"What did you learn? What will you remember tomorrow? What happens on the test when you have to explain it in your own words?"*

**2. The False Confidence Problem**
Student asks AI for help, AI gives confident-sounding answer, student doesn't check it, student uses wrong information.

*"This is especially risky in math and science — where the AI can sound right but be wrong. We'll get deeper into this in Lesson 5.5."*

**3. The Dependency Spiral**
Student uses AI for everything → never builds the skill → becomes unable to do the work without AI → AI can't do the test → student fails.

*"The goal of school isn't to produce assignments. It's to build your capability. AI can help you build capability — or it can let you skip it. The difference is in how you use it."*

---

### Segment 3: The Line-Drawing Activity (15 min)

**Scenario cards — where's the line?**

Set up: each student (or pair) gets a set of 8 scenario cards. They sort them:
- 🟢 Green sticky: clearly appropriate
- 🟡 Yellow sticky: gray area / depends
- 🔴 Red sticky: clearly not appropriate

**Scenario cards:**

1. You use AI to explain a math concept you don't understand from class.
2. You ask AI to write your personal essay for a college application.
3. You ask AI to check your grammar on an essay you wrote yourself.
4. You ask AI to write a first draft of your essay, then you rewrite it completely in your own words.
5. You ask AI to give you practice questions and quiz yourself until you get them all right.
6. Your teacher assigns a research paper. You ask AI to summarize the Wikipedia article on your topic.
7. You ask AI for feedback on your thesis statement.
8. You ask AI to write your lab report conclusion "based on these data points."

**Gallery debrief:**
- Reveal where each team placed each card
- Discuss disagreements — especially the gray areas (4 and 6 are great discussion drivers)
- Teacher shares their own position (and your school's policy, if one exists)

**Key question:** *"Who decides what's appropriate — the school? The teacher? You? The AI company?"*

---

### Segment 4: Your Personal AI Study Policy (8 min)

**Activity:**

Students draft their own "AI Study Policy" using the template (see `ai-study-policy-template.md`).

The policy should cover:
- What they'll use AI for in their studies
- What they won't use AI for
- How they'll use AI without hurting their learning
- How they'll make sure they're learning, not just producing

**Share:** 2–3 volunteers read their policies. Class gives feedback.

**Teacher option:** Collect these and use them at the end of the semester as a check-in — did you stick to it? What changed?

---

### Closing (2 min)

**Exit Ticket (in journal):**

> *"Describe ONE way you plan to use AI as a study tool this week that would actually help you learn — not just complete the assignment. Then describe one thing you'll avoid."*

## 🔧 Differentiation

**For students who finish early / advanced:**
- Research: Does your school have an AI policy? If not, draft one for the administration.
- Debate prep: "AI assistance in school should be treated the same way as calculator use in math class." Argue for or against.
- Design: Create a "spectrum" of AI use from fully appropriate to fully inappropriate — more nuanced than green/red.

**For students who struggle:**
- The scenario cards are accessible regardless of technical level — focus your support here
- Pair students for the policy drafting
- Provide a sentence starter: "I will use AI to help me ___, but I will not use AI to ___."

**For students with disabilities / accommodations:**
- AI as a study tool has natural accessibility benefits — this is worth naming
- Text-to-speech, language support, individualized explanation levels — these are legitimate accommodations, not cheating
- Help students distinguish between accessibility support and academic dishonesty

---

## 💬 Discussion Questions

1. *"Is using AI to help you study fundamentally different from using a tutor? From Googling? From asking a friend?"*
2. *"If AI can write a better essay than you, why should you learn to write essays?"*
3. *"Where does collaboration end and cheating begin — and who decides?"*
4. *"What's the difference between using AI to help you learn and using AI instead of learning?"*
5. *"In 10 years, will 'write this without AI' be a skill employers want — like 'write this without spell-check' is today?"*
