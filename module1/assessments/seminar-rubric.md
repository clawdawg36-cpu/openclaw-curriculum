# Module 1 Socratic Seminar Rubric

**Purpose:** To assess students' participation in the Socratic seminar discussion. This is an oral discussion assessment — look for evidence of engaged, substantive discourse rather than simply "talking a lot."

**How to use:** Score each criterion on a 1–4 scale. Total possible: 16 points.

---

## Scoring Rubric

### Criterion 1: Preparation

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Student arrives with clear evidence of preparation: references specific examples, data, or arguments from readings/lessons without prompting. Builds on prior knowledge throughout the seminar, not just at the start. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Student demonstrates solid preparation. Can recall and reference class materials when prompted or in context. Mostly prepared but may not bring evidence unprompted. |
| **2 — Developing** | Student shows some preparation but relies heavily on what others say in the discussion. Struggles to recall specifics from readings or activities. |
| **1 — Beginning** | Little evidence of preparation. Student cannot reference class materials or gives responses that are disconnected from the seminar topic. |

**Sample Responses:**

- **4:** *"In the Amazon hiring tool case study, the bias came from the training data — resumes from the past ten years that already reflected historical gender imbalance. That's what makes this hard: the AI wasn't programmed to be biased, it learned to be."*
- **3:** *"I remember we talked about how AI can be biased because of the data it's trained on. That applies here."*
- **2:** *"Yeah I think I read something about that. I agree with what Maya said."*
- **1:** *"I don't really know, I think AI can be good or bad."*

---

### Criterion 2: Listening

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Consistently demonstrates active listening. Builds directly on others' points, uses classmates' names, asks clarifying questions, and advances the conversation rather than redirecting to their own agenda. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Generally listens well and responds to what others say. May occasionally shift direction but usually connects back to prior contributions. |
| **2 — Developing** | Sometimes listens but often waits for a turn rather than engaging with what's being said. Responses may repeat earlier points without building on them. |
| **1 — Beginning** | Shows little evidence of listening. Responses are disconnected from the flow of conversation or only self-referential. Does not acknowledge other speakers. |

**Sample Responses:**

- **4:** *"Jalen made a great point about bias, and I want to push on that — he said bias comes from data, but what about when the engineers make choices about which data to include or exclude? Isn't that bias too, just one step earlier?"*
- **3:** *"Like Amara said, bias is a problem. I also think it matters who's deciding what 'fair' means in the first place."*
- **2:** *"I think AI is mostly beneficial. It helps a lot of people with health stuff."*
- **1:** *"My point is that AI is taking jobs. That's what I want to talk about."*

---

### Criterion 3: Contribution Quality

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Contributions move the discussion forward. Student introduces new angles, asks probing questions, makes distinctions, or synthesizes multiple ideas. Quality of thinking is evident — not just participation for participation's sake. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Contributions are relevant and add value to the discussion. Student shares an opinion or analysis with some backing. May not fully develop ideas but participates meaningfully. |
| **2 — Developing** | Contributions are on-topic but surface-level. Student restates what was already said or offers opinions without support. Limited original thinking. |
| **1 — Beginning** | Contributions are minimal, off-topic, or do not advance the discussion. Student may speak but not engage substantively with the seminar question. |

**Sample Responses:**

- **4:** *"We keep saying 'AI is beneficial' or 'AI is harmful' like it's one thing, but isn't the question really *whose* AI, *deployed by whom*, *for what purpose*? The same technology that runs facial recognition for unlocking phones is used in predictive policing. Those aren't the same situation."*
- **3:** *"I think AI is more beneficial overall, but we have to be careful about how it's used in high-stakes decisions like hiring or criminal justice."*
- **2:** *"AI can be good. Like self-driving cars could save lives. But it can also be bad."*
- **1:** *"I agree. Yeah."*

---

### Criterion 4: Evidence Use

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Consistently supports claims with specific evidence — from class activities, readings, real-world examples, or logical reasoning. Evidence is cited accurately and used to strengthen the argument, not just name-dropped. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Uses evidence to support most claims. References class content or real-world examples at least once. Evidence is relevant even if not always developed fully. |
| **2 — Developing** | Makes claims without evidence or uses very general evidence ("AI has caused problems before"). Rarely connects arguments to specific examples or sources. |
| **1 — Beginning** | No evidence used. Arguments are entirely opinion-based with no reference to facts, examples, data, or class content. |

**Sample Responses:**

- **4:** *"The MIT study we looked at found facial recognition had a 34% error rate for darker-skinned women versus less than 1% for lighter-skinned men. That's not a minor accuracy gap — that's a system that functionally doesn't work for a huge part of the population. Deploying that in policing isn't a neutral choice."*
- **3:** *"We saw in the bias lesson how AI can discriminate in hiring. That's a real example of harm, not just theoretical."*
- **2:** *"There are cases where AI has made bad decisions. I think that's pretty common."*
- **1:** *"I just think AI is dangerous. I feel like it could go wrong."*

- 5–8: Approaching expectations
- 1–4: Below expectations — schedule a conversation with the student

## Teacher Notes

- Socratic seminars reward **quality over quantity** — a student who speaks twice with precision should outscore one who talks constantly without listening.
- Watch for students who are clearly engaged but reluctant to speak. Consider a "warm call" system where students can opt into a question rather than being cold-called.
- If a student scores 1 on Listening consistently, try structured pair-share before the next seminar so they practice responding to a partner before responding to a group.
- The seminar question for this module: *"Is AI more beneficial or harmful to society today?"* — encourage students to complicate a simple yes/no answer.
- This rubric is also appropriate for smaller structured discussion days (fishbowl, Harkness), not just the full seminar.
