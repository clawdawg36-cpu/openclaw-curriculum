# Module 1 Reflection Journal Rubric

**Purpose:** To assess students' ongoing thinking and growth throughout Module 1. Journals are evaluated holistically — look for development over time, not perfection in a single entry.

**How to use:** Score each criterion on a 1–4 scale. Total possible: 16 points.

---

## Scoring Rubric

### Criterion 1: Depth of Observation

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Student makes detailed, specific observations about AI and their experiences with it. Connects observations to concrete examples and explains *why* something works the way it does. Goes beyond surface-level description. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Student makes clear observations with some specific detail. Mostly describes *what* happened with occasional attempts to explain *why*. |
| **2 — Developing** | Student makes general observations but lacks specificity. Descriptions are vague or repeat the same idea without going deeper. |
| **1 — Beginning** | Observations are minimal, off-topic, or simply restate the lesson prompt without genuine reflection. |

**Sample Responses:**

- **4:** *"When I asked OpenClaw what the weather would be like on Mars, it said it couldn't access real-time data. That made me realize AI isn't connected to the internet by default — it uses what it was trained on, which has a cutoff date. It's like asking someone who's been on a desert island for a year what's happening in the news."*
- **3:** *"OpenClaw was really good at explaining things but it got confused when I asked it about something that happened last week."*
- **2:** *"AI can do a lot of things. I used it and it worked most of the time."*
- **1:** *"AI is cool. We used it today in class."*

---

### Criterion 2: Vocabulary Use

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Accurately uses 3 or more unit vocabulary terms in context. Uses terms to explain ideas rather than just listing them. Demonstrates genuine understanding of what the terms mean. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Accurately uses 1–2 vocabulary terms in context. Terms are used correctly even if explanations aren't fully developed. |
| **2 — Developing** | Attempts to use vocabulary but uses terms loosely or slightly incorrectly. May confuse related terms (e.g., "algorithm" and "AI"). |
| **1 — Beginning** | Does not use unit vocabulary, or uses terms incorrectly in ways that show misunderstanding. |

**Sample Responses:**

- **4:** *"I think the reason OpenClaw sometimes gives wrong answers is because of bias in its training data — if the dataset it learned from had a lot of one point of view, the model would repeat that point of view without questioning it."*
- **3:** *"The algorithm OpenClaw uses must be really complicated to understand natural language the way it does."*
- **2:** *"The machine learning helped the AI do its neural network thing to understand what I was saying."*
- **1:** *"The robot AI computer thing figured it out somehow."*

---

### Criterion 3: Critical Thinking

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Student raises thoughtful questions, identifies tensions or trade-offs, challenges assumptions, or considers multiple perspectives. Shows genuine intellectual curiosity and evaluates ideas rather than just accepting them. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Student goes beyond describing what happened to ask *why* or *what if*. Shows some skepticism or curiosity. May consider one alternative perspective. |
| **2 — Developing** | Student mostly accepts what they observed at face value. Little questioning or analysis. Conclusions are stated without support. |
| **1 — Beginning** | No evidence of critical thinking. Entry reads as a summary or report without any evaluation or questioning. |

**Sample Responses:**

- **4:** *"If AI can pass the Turing Test, does that mean it's actually intelligent, or just really good at faking it? I'm not sure those are the same thing. A parrot can repeat words without understanding them. How do we know AI isn't doing the same thing at a much higher level?"*
- **3:** *"I wonder if AI could ever truly be creative, or if it's always going to just remix things that humans already made."*
- **2:** *"AI is smart because it knows a lot of things. It got most of my questions right."*
- **1:** *"AI is good and bad sometimes."*

---

### Criterion 4: Personal Connection

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Student clearly connects lesson content to their own life, experiences, values, or future. Reflection feels genuine and personal, not just academic. Makes specific references to their own context. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Student makes a personal connection but it is somewhat general. References their own experience or opinion with some specificity. |
| **2 — Developing** | Connection to self is weak or vague ("I think this is interesting"). Student describes the lesson content more than their relationship to it. |
| **1 — Beginning** | No personal connection evident. Entry reads as impersonal or detached. |

**Sample Responses:**

- **4:** *"My mom uses voice recognition at her job for patient notes and she's always frustrated when it mishears her. Now I understand why — natural language processing is hard because words can mean completely different things depending on context, and medical jargon makes it even harder. I want to show her this lesson."*
- **3:** *"This makes me think about how I use Siri every day and never really thought about how it works. It's kind of weird to think there's a model behind it predicting what I'm going to say."*
- **2:** *"AI affects everyone's lives. I use it sometimes."*
- **1:** *"This lesson was about AI."*

- 5–8: Approaching expectations
- 1–4: Below expectations — schedule a conversation with the student

---

## Teacher Notes

- Journals are **low stakes** — students should feel safe taking intellectual risks.
- Comment on *growth over time*, not just single entries.
- If a student scores 1 on Critical Thinking consistently, try asking them Socratic questions in one-on-one conversations to unlock that thinking in conversation before written form.
- Encourage students who struggle with vocabulary to keep their vocab list open while writing.
