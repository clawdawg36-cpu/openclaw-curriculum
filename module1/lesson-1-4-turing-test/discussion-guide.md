# Discussion Guide — The Turing Test & AI Limits

**Lesson 1.4 | Module 1 — What Is AI?**
**Format:** Socratic Seminar / Class Discussion
**Time:** 10–15 minutes (can expand to full class period)

---

## How to Use This Guide

These questions are designed to spark genuine thinking, not lead students to a "right" answer. The goal is to develop **critical reasoning** about intelligence, consciousness, and what makes something (or someone) *human*.

**Facilitator tips:**
- Resist the urge to answer your own questions
- Use follow-ups like: *"What makes you say that?"* or *"Does anyone see it differently?"*
- Embrace silence — give students 10–15 seconds to think before calling on anyone
- Write key phrases from student answers on the board

---

## Opening Questions
*(Use after the "Is It Human?" activity)*

1. **What surprised you most about the activity?**
   - *Follow-up:* Was there a pair where you felt really confident, but got it wrong? What does that tell you?

2. **When you were reading the responses, what were you actually looking for?**
   - *Listen for:* emotion, personality, opinions, humor, vulnerability, mistakes, hesitation

3. **Did the AI responses feel "wrong" in some way — even if you couldn't explain why?**
   - *Follow-up:* Can you describe what that feeling was?

---

## Core Questions — Intelligence & The Test

4. **Alan Turing said: if a machine can convince a human it's human, then it should be considered intelligent. Do you agree? Why or why not?**
   - *Push back:* Could a really good actor fool you? Does that make them more intelligent?
   - *Counter-angle:* What if the machine only memorized responses — would that count as thinking?

5. **Is being "convincing" the same as being "intelligent"?**
   - *Example:* A parrot can say "I love you." Is it expressing love?
   - *Example:* A calculator gives correct answers. Does it understand math?

6. **What would the Turing Test miss?**
   - *Prompts if stuck:* Does it test creativity? Feelings? Self-awareness? Knowing when you're wrong?

7. **Could you design a better test than Turing's? What would it look like?**

---

## Deep Questions — Consciousness & What It Means to Think

8. **What's the difference between *acting* like you understand something and *actually* understanding it?**
   - *Example:* An AI can describe heartbreak using words. Has it ever been heartbroken?
   - *Follow-up:* Does it matter, if the response helps someone feel better?

9. **If an AI says it feels sad, is it lying? Can it lie?**
   - *Note:* There's no clean answer here — push students to reason through it

10. **What would it take for you to believe that an AI was truly conscious — that it had real thoughts and feelings?**
    - *Follow-up:* Is that even knowable? How do you know *other humans* are conscious?

11. **Some people say AI is "just pattern matching" — predicting what word comes next. Does that mean it's not really thinking? Or could human thinking be a kind of pattern matching too?**

---

## Limits of AI — Practical Questions

12. **We saw that AI is great at language and information. Where did it seem to fail in our activity?**
    - *Listen for:* genuine emotion, personal experience, having actual opinions, common sense, admitting uncertainty

13. **Why do you think AI struggles with empathy — or does it?**
    - *Push:* Response B in Pair 3 sounded empathetic. Was it? What's the difference between saying the right words and *meaning* them?

14. **An AI will never get tired, angry, or distracted. Is that an advantage or a problem when it comes to conversations?**

15. **If AI keeps getting better, do you think it will eventually "pass" the Turing Test reliably? What happens then?**

---

## Closing / Bridge Questions
*(Use to transition toward Module 2)*

16. **We've been thinking about AI and language. But what about seeing, hearing, and feeling? Do you think AI experiences the world the same way you do?**
    - *Tease Module 2:* "We're going to explore exactly that next time."

17. **After today, has your opinion about AI changed at all? In what direction?**

18. **What's one question about AI that you still have — one this lesson didn't answer?**
    - *(Great to collect these on sticky notes or a shared doc for future lessons)*

## Vocabulary to Surface During Discussion

| Word | When It Comes Up |
|---|---|
| **Consciousness** | When students debate whether AI "really" thinks |
| **Empathy** | When comparing AI vs. human emotional responses |
| **Pattern matching** | When explaining how language models work |
| **Imitation** | In contrast to "real" intelligence |
| **Intentionality** | Did the AI *mean* what it said? |
| **Ambiguity** | When there's no clear right answer |

---

## Common Student Arguments (and How to Extend Them)

**"AI is just copying from the internet."**
→ *"Aren't humans also shaped by everything they've read and heard? What's the difference?"*

**"AI doesn't have feelings, so it's not really intelligent."**
→ *"What if we built an AI that could feel pain — would that make it intelligent? What counts as a feeling?"*

**"Humans will always be smarter than AI."**
→ *"In what ways? At chess, translation, diagnosis, art? Is 'smarter' even the right word?"*

**"It doesn't matter if AI thinks — it just matters if it's useful."**
→ *"Useful for who? If an AI gives bad advice confidently, does that matter?"*

---

## Optional Extension: Thought Experiment

> Imagine you've been texting with someone for a month. They've helped you through a tough time. You later find out they were an AI the whole time.

Ask students:
- **How would that make you feel?**
- **Does it change the help you received?**
- **Was it wrong to not tell you?**
- **Would you want to talk to them again?**

This hits honesty, trust, and the ethics of AI deception — rich ground for extended discussion.
