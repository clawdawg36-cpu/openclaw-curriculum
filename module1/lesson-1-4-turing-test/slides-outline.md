## Slide 4 — Who Was Alan Turing?

**Heading:** Meet Alan Turing

**Body:**
- British mathematician, 1912–1954
- Cracked the Nazi Enigma code during WWII — arguably changed the outcome
- Father of computer science and theoretical AI
- 1950: Published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence"
- Proposed a deceptively simple question: *Can machines think?*

**Visual:** Black-and-white photo of Turing; Enigma machine in background

*Presenter: "Turing's life was extraordinary — and tragic. He helped win the war and then was persecuted by the same government he saved. He died at 41. His question outlived him by decades."*

## Slide 5 — The Turing Test Explained

**Heading:** The Imitation Game

**Body:**
- Turing's original idea: the **Imitation Game**
- Setup:
  - A human interrogator chats with two players — one human, one machine — via text
  - The interrogator can't see either player
  - Goal: figure out which is which
- If the machine **fools the interrogator** reliably → it passes the Turing Test

**Visual:** Diagram showing interrogator at a terminal, two anonymous chat windows

*Presenter: "Simple, right? But this test has been debated for 75 years. Let's play our own version and see what we think."*

## Slide 8 — What Trips People Up

**Heading:** The Tricks AI Plays

**Body:**
- AI is good at:
  - Fluent, grammatically correct prose
  - Structured, comprehensive-sounding answers
  - Mimicking empathy ("I can understand why that would be frustrating…")
  - Producing multiple perspectives on a question
- AI is bad at:
  - Being specific and personal ("When I was in 5th grade…")
  - Inconsistency and humor that actually lands
  - Admitting genuine uncertainty ("I honestly don't know")

**Visual:** Two columns: AI strengths vs. human tells

*Presenter: "Empathy is interesting — AI can produce the *words* of empathy without the *experience* of empathy. Is that still empathy? Does it matter if the output is the same?"*

## Slide 9 — AI Limitations: What It Can't Do

**Heading:** Real Limits of Current AI

**Body:**
- **Knowledge cutoff:** AI only knows what was in its training data — it can't learn new information after that date
- **No real-time awareness:** AI doesn't know what's happening right now
- **No sensory experience:** It can't see, hear, taste, or touch the physical world (in text-only mode)
- **Confidently wrong:** Can produce false information with complete certainty
- **No true memory:** (In most systems) each conversation starts fresh — it doesn't remember you

**Visual:** Red X icons next to each limitation

*Presenter: "These aren't bugs to be fixed someday — they're fundamental to how current AI works. Understanding them is the difference between a powerful tool and a dangerous one."*

## Slide 10 — AI Limitations: What It Struggles With

**Heading:** The Harder Problems

**Body:**
- **Causation vs. correlation:** AI finds patterns but can't always reason about cause and effect
- **Common sense:** Things a child knows ("You can't put a river in your pocket") can baffle AI
- **Counting and spatial reasoning:** Counting letters in words, visualizing 3D objects
- **Moral reasoning:** AI can produce ethical-sounding responses without genuine ethical reasoning
- **Consciousness:** There is no evidence AI has subjective experience

**Visual:** Puzzle pieces, some fitting, some not

*Presenter: "Here's a test that trips up AI: 'How many r's are in the word strawberry?' Try it. Then ask a human. There's something interesting going on there about how different types of intelligence work."*

## Slide 11 — Does Passing the Turing Test Mean Intelligence?

**Heading:** The Deeper Debate

**Body:**
- **Argument for:** If a machine behaves intelligently in every observable way, who are we to say it isn't intelligent?
- **Argument against:** Mimicking intelligence isn't the same as *having* it (Chinese Room thought experiment)
- **The Chinese Room** (John Searle, 1980):
  - Imagine you're in a room, following rules to respond to Chinese symbols
  - You don't understand Chinese — but your responses are perfect
  - Is the room "understanding" Chinese?

**Visual:** Illustration of the Chinese Room thought experiment

*Presenter: "This is philosophy of mind as much as it is computer science. There's no consensus answer. Your job today is to form your own view — and defend it."*
