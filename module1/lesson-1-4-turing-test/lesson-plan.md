# Lesson 1.4 — The Turing Test & AI Limits

**Module:** 1 — What Is AI?
**Grade Level:** 6th–8th Grade
**Duration:** 50 minutes
**Prerequisites:** Lessons 1.1–1.3 (What is AI, How Machines Learn, Types of AI)

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Explain what the Turing Test is and why Alan Turing invented it
2. Evaluate whether a response seems "human" or "AI-generated" using reasoning
3. Identify at least two things AI does well and two things it struggles with
4. Connect the concept of AI limits to the idea that computers perceive the world differently than humans do

---

## Lesson Outline

### 🔁 Warm-Up — The Imitation Game (5 minutes)

**Prompt for class:**
> "Imagine you're texting with someone, but you can't see them. How would you figure out if they're a human or a robot?"

Give students 1 minute to write down 3 questions they'd ask to figure it out. Then share out a few answers.

Transition: *"A scientist named Alan Turing asked this exact question back in 1950 — and it changed how we think about intelligence forever."*

---

### 📖 Direct Instruction — What Is the Turing Test? (10 minutes)

**Key concepts to cover:**

**Alan Turing (1912–1954)**
- British mathematician and computer scientist
- Helped crack Nazi codes during WWII
- Asked the question: *"Can machines think?"*

**The Turing Test (1950)**
- Turing proposed a test called the "Imitation Game"
- Setup: A human judge has a text conversation with two players — one human, one machine
- If the judge can't reliably tell which is which, the machine is said to have "passed"
- It's not about being *right* — it's about being *convincing*

**Important discussion point:**
> "Does passing the Turing Test mean a computer is actually *thinking* — or just really good at *pretending* to think?"

**What AI can do well:**
- Generate grammatically correct, fluent text
- Answer factual questions quickly
- Follow patterns it learned from millions of examples
- Be consistent and never get tired

**Where AI struggles:**
- Genuine emotion and empathy
- True understanding (vs. pattern matching)
- Common sense reasoning in new situations
- Knowing when it's wrong
- Creativity that comes from lived experience

---

### 🎮 Activity — "Is It Human?" (20 minutes)

Hand out or display the `is-it-human-activity.md` worksheet.

**Instructions to students:**
1. Read each pair of responses (Response A and Response B)
2. Circle or mark which one you think was written by a **human** and which by **AI**
3. For each pair, write *one reason* why you made that choice

**Working format:** Pairs or small groups (more fun + more debate!)

After ~12 minutes of independent/group work:
- Go through answers as a class
- For each pair, ask: *"What clued you in?"*
- Reveal the answer key — some may surprise them!

**Key teaching moment:** Some AI responses are very convincing. Some human responses seem robotic. What does that tell us about the test?

---

### 💬 Class Discussion (10 minutes)

Use the `discussion-guide.md` for Socratic prompts, but here are the core questions:

1. **Did any of the answers surprise you? Why?**
2. **What made the human responses feel different — even if you couldn't explain it?**
3. **Is being human just about what you *say*, or is there something more?**
4. **If an AI could perfectly imitate a human, would it matter if it wasn't "really" thinking?**

Try to surface: *intuition, emotion, lived experience, uncertainty* — things that are hard to fake.

---

### 🌉 Bridge to Module 2 — How AI Perceives the World (5 minutes)

Wrap up with a forward-looking question:

> "We've seen that AI can be very good at language. But what about seeing, hearing, and sensing the world? In Module 2, we're going to look at how AI perceives its environment — and how that's totally different from how *you* do it."

Teaser examples:
- A self-driving car "sees" with radar and cameras, not eyes
- A voice assistant "hears" sound waves converted to numbers
- An AI image recognizer doesn't know what a dog *feels* like to pet

## Differentiation

**For students who finish early:**
- Ask: "Could you write a response that would fool a judge? Try writing one in each style."
- Challenge: "Research ELIZA or GPT — how do they work?"

**For struggling students:**
- Focus on just 5 of the 10 pairs in the activity
- Provide sentence starters for discussion ("I think this is human because...")

**For ELL students:**
- Preview key vocabulary: *imitation, intelligence, convince, consciousness*
- Allow partner work throughout

---

## Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Turing Test | A test to see if a machine can convince a human judge it's also human |
| Artificial Intelligence | Computer systems designed to do tasks that normally require human intelligence |
| Imitation Game | Alan Turing's original name for the Turing Test |
| Consciousness | Awareness of yourself and your surroundings; still debated whether AI has this |
| Pattern recognition | Finding repeated structures in data — something AI is very good at |

## Teacher Notes

- Students often assume AI is either "dumb" or "smarter than humans" — this lesson helps them see it's *different*, not better or worse
- The activity works best when students debate with a partner before class reveal
- Consider projecting response pairs on screen and using a class vote (hands or polls) for more energy
- The answer key has some intentional surprises — lean into the "whoa" moments
