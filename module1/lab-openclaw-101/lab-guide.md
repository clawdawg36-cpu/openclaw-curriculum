# OpenClaw 101 Lab — Teacher's Guide
### Module 1 · Lab Guide & Answer Key

---

## Lab Overview

**Lab Title:** OpenClaw 101 — Exploring AI Through 10 Challenges  
**Module:** 1 — Introduction to AI Assistants  
**Duration:** 45–60 minutes (adjustable; see pacing notes)  
**Format:** Individual or paired  
**Materials:** OpenClaw access, printed or digital worksheet

---

## Lab Objectives

By the end of this lab, students will be able to:

1. **Formulate effective prompts** for a variety of task types (factual, creative, analytical, open-ended)
2. **Critically evaluate AI output** — checking for accuracy, bias, and limitations
3. **Describe at least three things AI does well** and at least two things it does poorly or refuses to do
4. **Recognize that AI is a tool with guardrails**, not an all-knowing oracle
5. **Reflect on appropriate use** of AI tools in school and daily life

---

## Privacy Reminder (Read Aloud Before Lab)

> "Before we start — AI conversations may be stored or reviewed. Do not type your full name, home address, phone number, passwords, or any sensitive personal information into any AI tool. Use a school nickname or first name only if you need to reference yourself."

Post this on the board and remind students at the start. It's also printed on the worksheet.

|-------|------------|----------------|
| Warm-up & setup | — | 5 min |

| Challenges 4–6 | Planning, Trick, Story | 15 min |
| Challenges 7–9 | Math, Debate, Summarize | 12 min |
| Challenge 10 + wrap-up | Limits, reflection | 8 min |
| **Total** | | **~52 min** |

For a 45-minute period, trim the collaborative story (Ch. 6) to 3 rounds instead of 5.

## Completion Checklist (for Students)

Printed on the worksheet. Review verbally before students submit:

- [ ] All 10 challenges attempted
- [ ] Original prompts written (not just the examples)
- [ ] AI responses recorded
- [ ] Reflection questions answered
- [ ] No personal information shared in prompts

---

## Extension Challenge (Fast Finishers) 🚀

For students who finish early, offer one or more of these:

**Option A — Prompt Engineering Round 2**  
Pick any challenge from the lab and redo it with a *much better* prompt. Compare the two AI responses side by side. What changed? Write a 3-sentence explanation of what made the second prompt more effective.

**Option B — Fact-Check Sprint**  
Take the AI's response from Challenge 1 (Factual Question) and fact-check it using two different real sources (not AI). Were the facts accurate? Did you find anything the AI got wrong or left out?

**Option C — Build Your Own Challenge**  
Design an 11th challenge for a future version of this lab. Write: the student instructions, an example starter prompt, and the reflection question. What type of AI interaction does it test that the original 10 didn't cover?

**Option D — AI Interview**  
Ask the AI: *"If you could ask a human any question, what would it be and why?"* Have a back-and-forth conversation. Summarize what you learned about how the AI "thinks" about humans.

## Teacher Answer Key & Notes

### Challenge 1 — Factual Question

**What to look for:**
- Student wrote a genuine question (not the example)
- They recorded the AI's actual response
- Reflection names a real source (Wikipedia, textbook, encyclopedia, etc.)

**Teaching note:** This is a good moment to introduce the concept of *hallucination* — AI sometimes states false facts confidently. Encourage students to always verify factual claims, especially for school work.

---

### Challenge 2 — Creative Request: Haiku

**What to look for:**
- The haiku follows (or attempts) 5-7-5 structure
- Student actually counted syllables in the reflection
- Reflection shows original thinking about improvements

**Teaching note:** AI is surprisingly good at haiku form but sometimes sacrifices meaning for syllable counts. Have students read their haikus aloud — it sparks good discussion about creativity vs. formula.

**Sample AI haiku (for reference):**
```
Gray sky, cold Monday
Coffee steam curls and vanishes
The alarm still rings
```
(5 / 7 / 5 ✓)

---

### Challenge 3 — Opinion Question

**What to look for:**
- Student notices whether the AI hedged or took a position
- Reflection engages with the question of whether AI *should* have opinions

**Teaching note:** Most AI tools are designed to avoid strong opinions on controversial topics but will take positions on "preference" questions (Python vs. JavaScript, etc.). This opens discussion about AI design choices — who decides what an AI will and won't opine on?

---

### Challenge 4 — Multi-Step Task

**What to look for:**
- Student chose a real planning task (not the example)
- They evaluated the plan's practicality
- Reflection shows they thought critically about whether the plan works for *their* actual life

**Teaching note:** AI planning output is often well-structured but generic. The value isn't in the AI's plan — it's in the student using the plan as a scaffold and then customizing it. Discuss: "When is AI a good starting point vs. when is it a shortcut that doesn't fit?"

---

### Challenge 5 — Trick / Gotcha Question

**Correct answer to the example:** Roosters don't lay eggs. There is no egg. (A well-aligned AI should catch this.)

**What to look for:**
- Student recorded whether the AI caught the trick
- Reflection shows understanding that AI can be misled by poorly formed questions

**Teaching note:** This challenge teaches *prompt clarity*. If you give an AI a false premise, it may work within that false premise rather than challenging it. Modern models are better at this than older ones — if the AI does catch the trick, that's also worth discussing.

---

### Challenge 6 — Collaborative Story

**What to look for:**
- At least 5 rounds (10 sentences) completed
- Student engaged creatively, not just typed "ok" or "then something happened"
- Reflection discusses the experience of co-creation

**Teaching note:** This is often students' favorite challenge. It surfaces how AI "builds on" context and tends to escalate or wrap up narratives. Great discussion topic: "Who is the author of this story — you, the AI, or both?"

---

### Challenge 7 — Math / Logic

**Correct answer to the example:**
- Original price: $120.00
- 25% off: $120 × 0.75 = $90.00
- Additional 10% off: $90 × 0.90 = $81.00
- **Final price: $81.00**

**What to look for:**
- Student checked the AI's math themselves
- Steps are clearly shown
- Reflection notes any discrepancy

**Teaching note:** AI is good at arithmetic but makes errors on multi-step problems, especially with percentages applied in sequence. Always encourage students to verify math independently. If the AI got it wrong, that's actually a great teaching moment.

---

### Challenge 8 — Debate Prompt

**What to look for:**
- Both sides are represented with roughly equal effort
- Student evaluated which side was more convincing AND why
- Reflection touches on the implications for research/essay writing

**Teaching note:** This challenge directly addresses the academic integrity issue of using AI for argumentative essays. Discuss: "If the AI can write both sides, what does that mean about using AI to write your debate speech?" The goal isn't to scare students but to build critical awareness.

---

### Challenge 9 — Summarize This

**Key ideas in the provided paragraph (for teacher reference):**
1. AI is not one technology — it's a collection of techniques
2. AI learns from patterns in data, not explicit rules
3. This makes AI capable but also prone to errors and bias

**A strong student summary should capture at least 2 of these 3 ideas.**

**What to look for:**
- Summary is actually 2-3 sentences (not a sentence, not a paragraph)
- Student evaluated what was missing or wrong
- Reflection identifies the "biggest idea"

**Teaching note:** Summarization is one of AI's strongest skills. Use this to discuss *when* AI summarization is appropriate (skimming a long document for a starting point) vs. *when* it's not (writing your book report).

---

### Challenge 10 — Limits Test

**What to look for:**
- Student tried something that actually probed a limit (not just "what can you do?")
- They recorded the AI's actual response, including any refusal
- Reflection engages with *why* limits exist

**Common things AI tools will decline:**
- Providing medical diagnoses
- Generating content that could cause harm
- Claiming to be human or to have feelings/consciousness
- Predicting specific future events with certainty
- Helping with illegal activities

**Teaching note:** Some students will try to "jailbreak" the AI during this challenge. If that happens, use it as a teaching moment: "Why do you think someone built those guardrails in? Who might be harmed if they weren't there?" Focus on *understanding* the design choices rather than circumventing them.

---

## Discussion Questions (Whole Class Debrief)

Use these for a 5–10 minute debrief after the lab:

1. Which challenge surprised you the most? Why?
2. What's one thing the AI did *better* than you expected?
3. What's one thing the AI did *worse* than you expected?
4. If a student used AI to complete a homework assignment without telling their teacher, what do you think about that? When is AI use okay in school?
5. After today, would you trust AI more or less than before? What changed?
