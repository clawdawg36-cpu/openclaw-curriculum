## Slide 5 — How to Talk to an AI

**Heading:** Prompting 101

**Body:**
- A **prompt** is what you type to an AI
- Better prompts = better responses
- Tips:
  - Be specific: "Explain photosynthesis to a 7th grader" > "Explain photosynthesis"
  - Provide context: Tell it who you are, what you're working on
  - Iterate: If the first answer is wrong, ask a follow-up
  - Push back: If it says something odd, challenge it

**Visual:** Two side-by-side prompt examples — vague vs. specific — with response quality compared

*Presenter: "AI is not a search engine. You don't just type keywords. You have a conversation. Let's practice."*

## Slide 7 — Challenge Spotlight: Factual Questions

**Heading:** Challenge 1 — Asking for Facts

**Body:**
- AI can answer factual questions — but it can also be **confidently wrong**
- This is called a **hallucination**: AI generates plausible-sounding information that isn't true
- Always verify important facts with a primary source
- Example: Ask AI about a historical event, then check Wikipedia

**Visual:** Fact-check icon; warning triangle labeled "Hallucination"

*Presenter: "Hallucination is the AI equivalent of your friend who always has an answer — but you've learned not to trust them without checking. AI is like that friend. Smart, fast, and sometimes completely wrong."*

## Slide 9 — Challenge Spotlight: AI Limits

**Heading:** Challenge 10 — Where AI Breaks Down

**Body:**
- Things AI genuinely struggles with:
  - Real-time information (its knowledge has a cutoff date)
  - Consistently accurate math (especially multi-step)
  - True empathy and lived experience
  - Physical world awareness — it can't see or touch anything (yet)
  - Knowing what it doesn't know

**Visual:** Red-X checklist of AI limitations

*Presenter: "Finding where AI fails is just as important as finding where it succeeds. If you're going to work with AI, you need to know its blind spots."*

## Slide 10 — What Is Natural Language Processing?

**Heading:** How Does OpenClaw Actually Understand You?

**Body:**
- **Natural Language Processing (NLP):** AI's ability to read, understand, and generate human language
- It was trained on enormous amounts of text — books, websites, code, conversations
- It learns patterns: what words go together, what responses are appropriate
- It doesn't *understand* like you do — it predicts what text should come next

**Visual:** Text → Token → Prediction pipeline diagram (simplified)

*Presenter: "Here's a mind-bender: OpenClaw doesn't 'know' anything. It's a very sophisticated pattern-matcher. What does it mean to 'understand' something? We'll keep wrestling with that question."*

---

## Slide 11 — What Is Training Data?

**Heading:** Where Did AI Learn All This?

**Body:**
- AI language models are trained on **training data** — massive text collections
- Includes: books, Wikipedia, news articles, websites, code, academic papers
- The model learned patterns from billions of examples
- Problem: if the training data has bias, the model inherits it

**Visual:** Funnel graphic — diverse data sources → neural network → AI output

*Presenter: "The training data is AI's 'experience.' It shapes everything the AI knows and how it speaks. What it hasn't seen, it can't know. What it saw unfairly, it might repeat unfairly."*

## Slide 13 — Responsible AI Use

**Heading:** Using AI Responsibly

**Body:**
- **Privacy:** Don't share personal information with AI systems
- **Critical evaluation:** AI can be wrong — always verify important claims
- **Academic integrity:** Know your school's policy on AI-generated work
- **Feedback loop:** When AI gets something wrong, that matters — it shapes how people think about AI quality

**Visual:** Shield icon with each principle

*Presenter: "You're going to use AI tools in school, in work, in life. These habits — checking AI outputs, protecting your privacy, being honest about when you used AI — will matter. Start building them now."*
