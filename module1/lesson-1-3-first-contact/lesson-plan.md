# Lesson 1.3 — First Contact with OpenClaw

**Module:** 1 — What Is AI?
**Grade Level:** 6–10
**Duration:** 1–2 class periods (50–100 minutes)
**Prerequisites:** Lessons 1.1 and 1.2 (What Is AI? / AI in Your World)

---

## Overview

Students have learned what AI is and seen it in their daily lives. Now it's time to actually *talk* to one. In this lesson, students use OpenClaw for the first time — exploring how it responds to three types of questions (factual, creative, and opinion-based), completing the OpenClaw 101 Lab worksheet, and reflecting on the experience.

This lesson is about curiosity, not mastery. Students should feel free to experiment, make mistakes, and be surprised.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Launch and use OpenClaw to have a basic conversation
2. Distinguish between factual, creative, and opinion-based prompts
3. Observe and describe patterns in AI responses
4. Reflect on what AI can and cannot do well
5. Begin thinking critically about AI as a tool (not an oracle)

---

## Materials

- Devices with OpenClaw access (1 per student or pair)
- OpenClaw 101 Lab worksheet (`openclaw-101-lab.md`) — printed or digital
- Reflection Journal template (`reflection-journal-template.md`)
- Timer or visible clock
- Whiteboard or shared doc for class observations

## Part 1 — Warm-Up (5 min)

**Quick poll (hands or Mentimeter):**
> "Have you ever talked to a chatbot or AI assistant — Siri, Alexa, ChatGPT, a customer service bot?"

Briefly discuss: What was that like? What could it do? What couldn't it?

**Transition:** "Today, you're going to have your first real conversation with an AI called OpenClaw. We're not just going to talk to it — we're going to *test* it."

---

## Part 2 — Setup & Tutorial Walkthrough (10 min)

Walk students through the setup together:

1. Open a browser and navigate to your OpenClaw instance URL (teacher provides)
2. Log in with student credentials
3. Find the chat input box and type something simple: `Hello!`
4. Read the response together

**Point out:**
- Where the input box is
- How to scroll through conversation history
- That conversations aren't saved forever (explain your school's policy)
- The concept of a "prompt" — what you type to OpenClaw

**Tip for teachers:** Have a projected screen showing your own demo conversation. Model the behavior you want to see: thoughtful typing, reading the full response, not just mashing keys to see what happens.

**Ethics Touchpoint:**
> **Reflection prompt:** *"When you type something into OpenClaw, what information are you sharing — and who might have access to it?"* Before students dive into open-ended experimentation, take a moment to discuss data privacy: conversations may be logged, and sharing personal details with AI systems is a choice worth making consciously.

---

## Part 3 — Guided Exploration: 3 Question Types (20 min)

As a class, you'll ask OpenClaw three questions together — one of each type. Use the projector so everyone can see responses.

### Question Type 1: Factual (~5 min)

Factual questions have a correct answer. AI should be accurate and concise.

**Example prompt to try together:**
> "What is the capital of France?"

- Read the response aloud
- Ask: Was it correct? Was it more than you expected? Did it explain anything extra?
- Introduce vocabulary: **hallucination** — when AI states something incorrect with confidence

**Try a trickier one:**
> "Who invented the telephone?"

Discuss: Why might there be more than one name? Does the AI acknowledge uncertainty?

---

### Question Type 2: Creative (~7 min)

Creative prompts ask the AI to generate something original — there's no single "right" answer.

**Example prompt:**
> "Write a short poem about a robot who wants to learn how to dream."

- Read the response aloud
- Ask: Is this good poetry? What do you notice about the style? Did it surprise you?
- Discuss: Can a machine be *creative*? What does "creative" even mean?

**Try a variation:**
> "Now write the same poem, but make it funny."

Observe the difference. What changed? What stayed the same?

---

### Question Type 3: Opinion (~8 min)

Opinion prompts ask the AI for a viewpoint or preference — something it doesn't technically *have*.

**Example prompt:**
> "What do you think is the best movie ever made?"

- Read the response. Notice: Does it say "I think" or "In my opinion"? Does it hedge?
- Ask: Does an AI *actually* have opinions? What's it doing when it answers this way?
- Discuss: Is that honest? Is it useful? Is it dangerous?

**Follow-up discussion question:**
> "If an AI sounds confident about its opinion, should you trust it more or less than a human's opinion?"

---

## Part 4 — OpenClaw 101 Lab (25–45 min)

Distribute (or have students open) the `openclaw-101-lab.md` worksheet.

Students work individually or in pairs through 10 challenges. Each challenge has:
- A prompt to try
- Space to write down what OpenClaw said
- A reflection question

**Teacher moves during lab:**
- Circulate and read over shoulders
- Ask students to read their favorite responses aloud
- Prompt deeper thinking: "Why do you think it said that?"
- Note surprising or funny responses to share during debrief

**Challenges overview:**
1. Factual question
2. Creative haiku
3. Opinion question
4. Multi-step task
5. Trick question
6. Collaborative story
7. Math/logic puzzle
8. Debate prompt
9. "Summarize this" challenge
10. Limits test

See `openclaw-101-lab.md` for full instructions.

---

## Part 5 — Debrief & Share-Out (10 min)

Bring the class back together. Use the whiteboard to capture responses.

**Discussion questions:**
1. "What response surprised you most — in a good way or a bad way?"
2. "Was there anything OpenClaw got *wrong*? How did it handle being wrong?"
3. "Did it ever refuse to answer? Why do you think it did that?"
4. "What's one thing you thought AI could do that it struggled with?"
5. "What's one thing you *didn't* expect AI to do well?"

**Key takeaways to surface (don't just tell — draw out from students):**
- AI is good at: summarizing, generating text, following patterns, explaining concepts
- AI struggles with: real-time information, true reasoning, self-awareness, emotional nuance
- AI can be confidently wrong (hallucination)
- AI reflects the humans who trained it

---

## Part 6 — Reflection Journal (5–10 min)

Students complete the `reflection-journal-template.md` individually.

If time is short, assign as homework. The journal is worth completing thoughtfully — it connects to the broader module arc of critical AI literacy.

## Differentiation

**For students who finish early:**
- Try getting OpenClaw to change its answer through follow-up questions
- Try the same prompt 3 times — does it give the same answer?
- Ask OpenClaw to explain how it works

**For students who struggle:**
- Pair with a more confident student
- Provide sentence starters for reflection prompts
- Focus on just 5 of the 10 lab challenges

**For ELL students:**
- Allow prompts in their home language
- Compare how OpenClaw responds in different languages
- Discuss: Does the quality change?

---

## Teacher Notes

- **Technical hiccups are learning moments.** If OpenClaw goes down or gives a weird response, lean into it: "Why might that happen? What does this tell us about AI?"
- **Don't sanitize the weirdness.** If a student gets an unexpected or funny response, share it with the class. Unexpected outputs are pedagogically rich.
- **Beware the "right answer" trap.** Students may expect AI to be like a search engine with perfect answers. Help them see it as a language model — a *pattern matcher* — not a truth machine.
- **See `teacher-debrief-guide.md`** for detailed talking points and what "good" responses look like.
