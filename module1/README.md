# Module 1: What Is AI? — Exploring the Minds We're Building

> *"Intelligence is not just about knowing answers — it's about asking better questions."*

**Duration:** 2–3 weeks (10–15 class periods, ~50 min each)
**Grade Band:** 6–8 (primary target: 7th grade)
**Subject Integration:** Computer Science · ELA · Critical Thinking

---

## Essential Questions

1. **What makes something "intelligent"?** How do we decide whether a machine is truly thinking or just following rules?
2. **How do machines learn from data?** What does it mean to train an AI, and what happens when the training goes wrong?
3. **Who benefits — and who is harmed — when AI makes decisions?** How does bias get into AI systems, and whose responsibility is it to fix it?
4. **What role do humans play in an AI-powered world?** As AI takes on more tasks, what uniquely human skills become more valuable?
5. **What does it mean for AI to act autonomously?** How is an AI agent different from a chatbot, and what new possibilities — and risks — does that create?

---

## Student "I Can" Statements

By the end of this module, students will be able to say:

- **I can** define artificial intelligence and explain how it differs from traditional programming.
- **I can** describe how machine learning works using the concept of training data.
- **I can** identify real-world examples of AI in everyday life and explain what problem each one solves.
- **I can** explain what a neural network is and give an analogy for how it processes information.
- **I can** define bias in AI and describe at least one real-world consequence of a biased AI system.
- **I can** use domain vocabulary accurately when discussing AI (algorithm, model, dataset, NLP, computer vision, etc.).
- **I can** evaluate an AI system by asking questions about fairness, accuracy, and purpose.
- **I can** make and support a claim about AI using evidence from class activities and discussions.
- **I can** define "AI agent" and describe the Perceive → Reason → Act loop with real-world examples.
- **I can** identify the difference between a reactive chatbot and an autonomous AI agent on a spectrum.
- **I can** reflect on my own deployed OpenClaw as an example of an agentic AI system.

## Module Arc Narrative

**Module 1** is the foundation of the entire curriculum — the "why this matters" before we get to the "how to build it." It moves students through three distinct phases:

**Phase 1 (Lessons 1–2): Building the Mental Model**
We start by dismantling misconceptions. Students often think of AI as either magical (like a sci-fi robot) or trivial (like autocorrect). Lesson 1 grounds them in a precise definition and introduces the key idea that separates AI from traditional software: *AI learns from examples, it isn't just programmed with rules.* Lesson 2 makes this concrete — students don't just hear about machine learning, they train a model themselves with Teachable Machine and feel the "aha" when the webcam recognizes their gesture.

**Phase 2 (Lessons 2–3): Under the Hood**
With the core concept in place, we go deeper. Students explore how neural networks process information, how natural language processing works (and why it's hard), and how computer vision interprets images. The emphasis is on intuition, not math — students should walk away able to explain these systems to a friend, not implement them. The dataset activity on Day 10 is a pivot point: it asks students to think like AI designers, not just users.

**Phase 3 (Lesson 4): The Human Dimension**
The final stretch zooms out. Now that students understand *how* AI works, we ask *whether* it's working well — and for whom. Bias, automation, and ethics are introduced not as abstract concepts but through real cases that affect real people. The Socratic seminar on Day 14 asks students to synthesize everything they've learned and defend a position. The final reflection is the culminating assessment.

**The through-line:** Every lesson returns to the same questions — What is intelligence? Who decides? Who benefits? This keeps the module coherent and ensures students leave with both technical literacy and ethical awareness.

Use when: sub unit, preview module, or compressed schedule.

| Day | Focus |
|---|---|
| Day 1 | What Is AI? + Algorithms (combine Days 1–3) |
| Day 2 | Machine Learning + Teachable Machine demo (Days 6–7) |
| Day 3 | NLP + Computer Vision overview (Days 8–9) |
| Day 4 | AI Bias case study + discussion (Day 11 + 13) |
| Day 5 | Socratic Seminar + vocab assessment (Days 14–15) |

**Skip:** Deep dataset activity, automation research, kinesthetic neural network activity
**Compress:** All formative assessments become exit tickets

### 📅 4-Week Extended Version (20 days)

Use when: standalone elective unit, or when students need more scaffolding.

**Add in Week 2:**
- Day 7b: Students label their own mini-dataset by hand (appreciate the labor)
- Day 9b: Students build a simple NLP rule-based chatbot in Scratch

**Add in Week 3:**
- Day 11b: Students research and present their own AI bias case study (pairs)
- Day 13b: Guest speaker or virtual field trip (e.g., AI company, university lab)

**Add in Week 4 (new):**
- Day 16: "Design Your Own AI" brainstorm — what problem would you solve?
- Day 17: Prototype planning — sketch the data, model, and interface
- Day 18: Peer feedback on AI product designs
- Day 19: Refine and prepare presentations
- Day 20: Gallery walk — student AI product pitches

**Extended assessments:** Add a design document (product proposal) as a summative artifact alongside the written reflection.
