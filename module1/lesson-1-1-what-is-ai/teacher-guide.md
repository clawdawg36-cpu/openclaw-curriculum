## Warm-Up: "What Makes Something Intelligent?" — Facilitation Tips

### Anticipated Student Responses

**Common "intelligent" examples students name:**
- Humans, dogs, dolphins, octopuses
- Computers, Alexa/Siri, robots, self-driving cars
- Chess programs, Google, ChatGPT

**Students who struggle to name machines:** Prompt with: *"Have you ever talked to a voice assistant? What does it do that seems... smart?"*

**Students who go too far:** Some may say phones, calculators, or TVs are intelligent. This is a great teaching moment:
> "Great — what does a calculator *need* from you to work? What would a truly intelligent calculator do differently?"

### Facilitation Moves

| Student Says | Facilitation Response |
|---|---|
| "Alexa is smart because it answers questions" | "What makes answering questions a sign of intelligence? What if it just looked things up?" |
| "Calculators aren't intelligent, they just do math" | "Interesting. What if a calculator could learn from its mistakes — would it be intelligent then?" |
| "AI is smarter than humans" | "In what specific ways? And in what ways might a human still be smarter?" |
| "AI is just code, it can't really think" | "What does 'really think' mean? How would you know if something was thinking?" |

### Debrief the Chart

After collecting responses, step back and say:
> "Look at this list. Some of these things are definitely intelligent — you, your dog. Some are debatable — Alexa, Chess AI. What pattern do you notice about the debatable ones?"

Goal: Surface the idea that **intelligence has multiple dimensions** — memory, learning, creativity, emotion, problem-solving. AI may excel at some and lack others.

## AI History Section — Facilitation Tips

### Alan Turing

**Anticipated questions:**
- "Did the Turing Test ever get passed?" → Yes and no. ELIZA (1966) fooled some users. Eugene Goostman (2014) claimed to pass. Experts debate whether it counts. The test itself has critics — it tests *imitation*, not real understanding.
- "Why was Turing important?" → Beyond AI: he cracked the Nazi Enigma code in WWII. Also a tragic figure — persecuted by the British government for being gay.

**Optional depth:** If students are engaged, mention *imitation game* framing — the test was originally called "the imitation game." Ask: "Is imitating intelligence the same as having intelligence?"

### Deep Blue

**Anticipated response:** "That's not really intelligent — it just calculated moves."

**Facilitation:**
> "That's exactly what Kasparov said before the match. After losing, he changed his mind. What changed? Does *how* you arrive at a correct decision affect whether it counts as intelligence?"

**Optional video clip:** [IBM Deep Blue documentary excerpt, ~3 min]

### AlphaGo

**Key hook:** AlphaGo made a move (Move 37 in Game 2) that no human would have made — a move so unusual, the human commentators thought it was a mistake. It turned out to be a stroke of genius. This is often called the moment AI demonstrated *creativity*, not just calculation.

**Anticipated skepticism:** "It just played itself a million times — that's not creative."

**Facilitation:**
> "Interesting. How do YOU get better at something? Practice, right? What's the difference between AlphaGo's practice and yours?"

### ChatGPT

**Be prepared:** Some students may have used it to cheat. Don't shame — redirect:
> "A lot of people reacted to ChatGPT with fear or excitement. What reaction do *you* have, and why?"

**Also common:** "Is ChatGPT actually intelligent or just predicting words?"

**Facilitation:**
> "That's a deep question that researchers are still debating. What would you need to see to convince you it was 'actually' intelligent?"

## 5 Big Ideas — Facilitation Tips

### Sticky Note Sort Activity

Expect some disagreement about which Big Idea a tool belongs to. **This is good.** Many AI tools span multiple categories.

> "If a student places Netflix recommendations under 'Perception' instead of 'Learning' — great discussion starter! Ask: 'How would you explain your choice?'"

### Common Confusions

| Misconception | Correction |
|---|---|
| "Learning means the AI has feelings" | Learning in AI means adjusting based on feedback/data — no emotions required |
| "Natural Interaction means it understands us like a human" | NLP processes patterns in language — it doesn't need to 'understand' to respond helpfully |
| "Societal Impact is just about jobs" | It's much broader: privacy, bias, accessibility, democracy, creativity, war |

### Go Deeper on Societal Impact (if time allows)

Ask: *"Can you think of a way AI could help people? Now can you think of a way the same AI could hurt people?"*

Example: Facial recognition — helps unlock your phone, but has been used for mass surveillance and has higher error rates for darker skin tones.

## Curriculum Arc Preview — Facilitation Tips

Don't rush this. Students orient better when they see the full map.

Ask: *"Knowing what's coming — is there a module you're nervous about? Why?"*

Note: Students who express anxiety about AI often have heard doom-and-gloom narratives. Validate their concern:
> "Those are real questions worth asking. That's exactly why Module 5 (Ethics & Bias) is in the curriculum — we're going to dig into those questions together."

## Exit Ticket — What to Look For

When reviewing exit tickets, flag students who:

**Need support:**
- Can't produce a definition (may need vocabulary scaffolding in Lesson 1.2)
- Can't name an AI they've encountered (may have limited tech exposure — adjust examples)
- Ask basic comprehension questions (re-teach moments in Lesson 1.2 warm-up)

**Ready for extension:**
- Define AI with nuance (e.g., distinguish narrow vs. general AI)
- Question the Turing Test or the definition of intelligence
- Ask about specific technical topics (neural networks, bias, consciousness)

**Common misconceptions to address in Lesson 1.2:**
- "AI is the same as robots"
- "AI can think and feel like humans"
- "AI knows everything"
- "AI is always right"

## Discussion Protocol Options

If students are disengaged or discussion is lagging, try:

- **Cold call with wait time:** "I'll give you 30 seconds to think, then I'm calling on three people."
- **Gallery walk:** Print AI examples on cards, students rotate and add sticky-note labels
- **1-2-4:** Think alone → pair → combine into group of 4 → share out
