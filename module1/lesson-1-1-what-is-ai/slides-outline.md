## Slide 4 — AI History: Alan Turing (1950)

**Heading:** 1950 — Alan Turing

**Body:**
- British mathematician and code-breaker
- Published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence"
- Proposed the **Turing Test**: if a machine can fool a human in conversation, can we call it intelligent?
- Considered the father of computer science and AI

**Visual:** Timeline bar with 1950 highlighted

*Presenter: Briefly explain the Turing Test — a game where you chat with someone (or something) without seeing them. If you can't tell if it's human or machine, the machine passes.*

---

## Slide 5 — AI History: Dartmouth Conference (1956)

**Heading:** 1956 — AI Gets Its Name

**Body:**
- Summer workshop at Dartmouth College
- Scientists John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, and others gathered
- Officially coined the term: **"Artificial Intelligence"**
- Set the agenda for AI research for decades

**Visual:** Timeline bar with 1956 highlighted; old photo of early computer

*Presenter: "Before this meeting, there was no 'AI' — just math, logic, and computing. This is when the field was born."*

---

## Slide 6 — AI History: Deep Blue (1997)

**Heading:** 1997 — Deep Blue vs. Kasparov

**Body:**
- IBM's chess-playing computer **Deep Blue** defeats world champion Garry Kasparov
- Chess was considered the ultimate test of human intellect
- Kasparov: "I could feel — I could smell — a new kind of intelligence"
- Milestone: AI can beat humans at complex strategic thinking

**Visual:** Chessboard photo; timeline bar with 1997 highlighted

*Presenter: "Chess has 10 to the power of 120 possible games. More than atoms in the universe. And a computer figured it out." Pause for reaction.*

---

## Slide 7 — AI History: AlphaGo (2016)

**Heading:** 2016 — AlphaGo Makes History

**Body:**
- Google DeepMind's **AlphaGo** defeats Go world champion Lee Sedol
- Go has more possible positions than chess — exponentially more
- Experts said Go required *intuition*, not just calculation
- AlphaGo learned by playing itself millions of times (**reinforcement learning**)

**Visual:** Go board photo; timeline with 2016 highlighted

*Presenter: "This was a big deal. Experts thought AI couldn't master Go for another 50 years. It happened in 2016. And AlphaGo taught itself."*

---

- OpenAI launches **ChatGPT** in November 2022

## Slide 10 — What IS AI, Actually?

**Heading:** So… What Is AI?

**Body:**
> Artificial Intelligence is the ability of a computer system to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence — like recognizing speech, making decisions, translating languages, or learning from experience.

**Key distinction:**
- Not magic. Not a robot mind.
- A set of techniques that let computers *seem* intelligent

**Visual:** Simple icons for: speech bubble, decision tree, translate icon, learning curve

*Presenter: "AI is a toolbox, not a single thing. Let's look at the five main categories."*

## Slide 12 — Big Idea 1: Perception

**Heading:** Big Idea 1 — Perception

**Body:**
- AI senses and interprets the world through data
- Inputs: cameras, microphones, sensors, text
- Examples: Face ID, voice recognition (Siri/Alexa), self-driving car cameras
- Challenge: The world is messy — humans handle ambiguity naturally; AI has to learn it

**Visual:** Camera icon + waveform icon + sensor icon

*Presenter: "Before AI can do anything useful, it needs input. Perception is how AI sees, hears, and senses."*

---

## Slide 13 — Big Idea 2: Representation & Reasoning

**Heading:** Big Idea 2 — Representation & Reasoning

**Body:**
- AI stores knowledge and uses it to make decisions
- Knowledge graphs, rules, logic trees
- Example: Medical AI that diagnoses based on symptoms + patient history
- Like a doctor's mental map — but built from data

**Visual:** Decision tree or knowledge graph diagram

*Presenter: "AI doesn't just collect data — it has to organize it and use it to make decisions. That's reasoning."*

---

## Slide 14 — Big Idea 3: Learning

**Heading:** Big Idea 3 — Learning

**Body:**
- AI improves its performance through experience and data
- **Machine Learning:** learns patterns from examples
- **Deep Learning:** layered neural networks, inspired by the brain
- Example: Spotify learns your taste; Netflix recommends shows; spam filters learn spam

**Visual:** Simple neural network diagram

*Presenter: "This is the 'magic ingredient' everyone talks about. AI doesn't get programmed with every answer — it learns from data. Just like you learn from experience."*

---

## Slide 15 — Big Idea 4: Natural Interaction

**Heading:** Big Idea 4 — Natural Interaction

**Body:**
- AI communicates with humans in human-friendly ways
- **NLP** (Natural Language Processing): understanding and generating text/speech
- **Computer Vision:** understanding images
- Examples: Google Translate, ChatGPT, image captioning for the blind

**Visual:** Chat bubble + translation icon

*Presenter: "Early AI required humans to speak its language — code, commands. Now AI speaks ours."*

---

## Slide 16 — Big Idea 5: Societal Impact

**Heading:** Big Idea 5 — Societal Impact

**Body:**
- AI changes how we work, learn, create, and govern
- Benefits: medical breakthroughs, accessibility, automation of dangerous jobs
- Risks: bias, privacy, job displacement, misinformation, surveillance
- You have a role in shaping how AI is used

**Visual:** Scale/balance icon — opportunity vs. risk

*Presenter: "This is the one that keeps researchers and policymakers up at night. And it's why learning about AI — not just using it — matters."*
