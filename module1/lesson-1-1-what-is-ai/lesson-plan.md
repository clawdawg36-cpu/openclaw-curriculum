# Lesson 1.1 — What Is AI?

**Module:** 1 — Introduction to Artificial Intelligence  
**Grade Level:** 6–10 (adaptable)  
**Duration:** 50–60 minutes  
**Standards Alignment:** AI4K12 Five Big Ideas in AI; CSTA K-12 CS Standards

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Define Artificial Intelligence in their own words.
2. Describe at least three historical milestones in AI development (Turing, Deep Blue, AlphaGo, ChatGPT).
3. Identify the 5 Big Ideas in AI (AI4K12 framework) and give one example of each.
4. Articulate how AI differs from human intelligence.
5. Preview the full curriculum arc and how each module connects.

## Lesson Sequence

### Warm-Up: What Makes Something Intelligent? (10 min)

**Activity: Think-Pair-Share**

1. Post the question on the board: *"What makes something intelligent?"*
2. Students write down 3 examples of "intelligent" things (can be human, animal, or machine). (2 min)
3. Turn and talk with a partner. (2 min)
4. Share out — teacher charts responses into two columns: **Humans/Animals** | **Machines/Systems**
5. Bridge question: *"Is there anything on the machine side that surprises you? What makes it feel intelligent?"*

**Discussion Prompts:**
- "Can something be intelligent without being alive?"
- "Is a calculator intelligent? Why or why not?"
- "What would a machine need to be able to do to *seem* intelligent to you?"

---

### Direct Instruction: A Brief History of AI (15 min)

**Format:** Guided slide presentation with check-ins

**Key Events to Cover:**

| Year | Milestone | Why It Matters |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| 1950 | Alan Turing — "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" / Turing Test | First formal question: *Can machines think?* |
| 1956 | Dartmouth Conference — term "Artificial Intelligence" coined | Birth of the field |
| 1997 | Deep Blue defeats Garry Kasparov at chess | AI beats humans at complex strategy |
| 2011 | IBM Watson wins Jeopardy! | AI handles natural language at scale |
| 2016 | AlphaGo defeats Lee Sedol at Go | AI masters intuition-heavy games once thought "impossible" |
| 2022 | ChatGPT launches | Generative AI becomes mainstream |

**Check-in questions (after each milestone):**
- "What was surprising about this?"
- "Does this change how you'd define intelligence?"

---

### Introduce: The 5 Big Ideas in AI (10 min)

**Format:** Overview presentation + sticky note exit ticket

Introduce the AI4K12 Five Big Ideas framework:

1. **Perception** — AI senses the world through cameras, microphones, sensors
2. **Representation & Reasoning** — AI stores and processes knowledge
3. **Learning** — AI improves from data and experience
4. **Natural Interaction** — AI communicates with humans naturally
5. **Societal Impact** — AI affects society, raises ethical questions

For each idea, provide one concrete example students will recognize (Siri, Netflix recommendations, Google Translate, facial recognition, etc.).

**Quick Activity:** Students write one AI example they use or have heard of on a sticky note, then place it under the Big Idea it fits best.

---

### Curriculum Arc Preview (5 min)

**Format:** Visual roadmap slide

Walk students through the full curriculum:

- **Module 1:** What is AI? (this module)
- **Module 2:** How AI Sees the World (perception, computer vision)
- **Module 3:** How AI Learns (machine learning basics)
- **Module 4:** Language and AI (NLP, chatbots)
- **Module 5:** AI Ethics & Bias
- **Module 6:** Build Your Own AI (project-based)
- **Module 7:** AI and Your Future (careers, society)

Ask: *"Which module are you most excited about? Why?"*

---

### Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 min)

**Prompt (students write in journal or on card):**

> "In your own words: What is AI? Give one example from history and one from your own life. What's one question you still have?"

Collect exit tickets or leave in journals for teacher review.

---

## Differentiation

### Support (Struggling Learners)
- Pre-teach vocabulary: *algorithm, data, neural network, automation* with visual glossary
- Provide a graphic organizer for the AI history timeline
- Pair students strategically during Think-Pair-Share
- Reduce exit ticket to one sentence + one question

### Extension (Advanced Learners)
- Challenge: Read Alan Turing's 1950 paper abstract and respond — do you agree with his test?
- Research extension: "What was the AI Winter? Why did it happen?"
- Compare AlphaGo's learning method to how a human learns Go
- Explore AI4K12.org interactive modules

### ELL / Language Support
- Provide bilingual vocabulary cards if available
- Use image-heavy slides with minimal text
- Allow exit tickets in student's first language

## Teacher Notes

- This is the first lesson — tone matters. Keep it fun and conversational, not lecture-heavy.
- Students often confuse AI with *sci-fi robots*. Gently redirect early.
- The ChatGPT milestone is a great hook — most students have used it or heard of it.
- Leave time for the exit ticket; it informs Lesson 1.2 pacing.
- See `teacher-facilitation-guide.md` for anticipated responses and debrief tips.
