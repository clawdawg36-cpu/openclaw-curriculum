# Lesson 1.2 — AI in Your World

**Module:** 1 — What Is AI?  
**Lesson:** 1.2  
**Duration:** 60 minutes  
**Level:** Beginner / High School  
**Prerequisites:** Lesson 1.1 — What Is AI? + Homework: Document 5 AI systems

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify AI systems present in their everyday lives
- Categorize AI by domain (transportation, health, entertainment, etc.)
- Articulate both the benefits and risks of AI in daily life
- Reflect critically on their relationship with AI systems

---

## Materials

- Printed or digital AI Bingo cards (one per student) — see `ai-bingo-card.md`
- Markers or tokens for Bingo
- Whiteboard or shared display
- Student homework from Lesson 1.1 (documented 5 AI systems)
- Timer (optional)

---

## Lesson Outline

### 1. Homework Debrief (10 minutes)

**Warm-up & share-out**

Ask students to take out their homework worksheets (see `homework-assignment.md`).

**Discussion prompt:** *"What's one AI system you documented that you didn't expect to find?"*

- Cold-call 4–5 students to share one AI system each
- Write their examples on the whiteboard, grouped loosely by category
- Note: It's okay if students weren't sure whether something was AI — that uncertainty is part of the lesson!

**Quick debrief questions:**
- How many AI systems did you find? More or fewer than you expected?
- Which was the hardest to identify? Why?
- Did you notice anything in common about where AI shows up?

---

### 2. AI Bingo Game (20 minutes)

**Setup (2 min)**
- Distribute AI Bingo cards (printed or shared digitally)
- Explain the rules: find a real-world example of AI that fits each category on your card
- Students can use their homework, the whiteboard list, or new examples from discussion

**Play (15 min)**
- Students work individually or in pairs to fill in their Bingo cards
- For each square, they must name a specific AI system (e.g., "Transportation → Tesla Autopilot")
- They cannot repeat an example across squares
- First to complete a row (horizontal, vertical, or diagonal) calls "Bingo!"
- Winner shares their examples aloud — class validates and discusses

**Debrief (3 min)**
- Which categories were easiest to fill? Which were hardest?
- Did anyone find AI in a category they hadn't thought about before?

---

### 3. Discussion: AI in Society (20 minutes)

Break into small groups (3–4 students). Each group gets one prompt to discuss for 5 minutes, then shares with the class.

**Prompt A — Surprise:**  
*"Which AI system you found surprised you the most? Why didn't you think of it as AI before?"*

**Prompt B — Dependency:**  
*"Which AI system would you miss the most if it disappeared tomorrow? What would your life look like without it?"*

**Prompt C — Risk:**  
*"Which AI system seems the most risky or concerning to you? What could go wrong?"*

**Full-class share-out (10 min):**
- One spokesperson per group shares their 2–3 most interesting points
- Teacher facilitates: look for common themes, surprising examples, and genuine disagreements

**Teacher talking points:**
- AI is already deeply embedded in daily life — often invisibly
- "Risky" doesn't mean bad, but it means we should pay attention
- The goal isn't to fear AI or trust it blindly — it's to understand it

**Ethics Touchpoint:**
> **Discussion question:** *"Looking at the AI examples you found — who benefits most from each one? Are there groups of people who might be left out or even harmed by these systems?"* This introduces the idea that AI is designed with specific users in mind, and that thinking about who benefits (and who doesn't) is a core part of responsible AI literacy.

---

### 4. Wrap-Up & Preview (10 minutes)

**Exit ticket (individual, 3 min):**
Students write one sentence each for:
1. One AI system I never thought about before today
2. One question I now have about AI

**Preview — Lesson 1.3 (First Contact):**
- Next class: students will interact directly with an AI system (OpenClaw!)
- They'll learn how AI actually responds, makes mistakes, and surprises us
- Tease: "Does AI actually understand you, or is it doing something else?"

**Assign homework (if applicable):**
- Optional: Pick one AI system from today's discussion and find out more about who made it, what data it uses, and what it's designed to do. Bring a 2-minute summary next class.

---

## Differentiation

| Student Need | Adaptation |
|---|---|
| Advanced | Ask them to research the AI system's training data or potential bias |
| Struggling | Pre-fill 3–5 squares on their Bingo card; let them work in pairs |
| ELL | Allow use of home language during group discussion; visual Bingo card with icons |

---

## Assessment

- Participation in homework debrief (observation)
- Bingo card completion (formative — check for accuracy of examples)
- Exit ticket (quick written check for understanding)

---

## Teacher Notes

- The Bingo game tends to spark great organic discussion — don't rush it
- Students are often surprised to learn that recommendation engines, autocorrect, and spam filters are AI
- The "risky" discussion can go deep fast — guide toward curiosity rather than fear
- Avoid framing AI as purely good or bad; model balanced thinking
