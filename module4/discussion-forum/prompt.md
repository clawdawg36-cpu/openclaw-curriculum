# Module 4 — Async Discussion Prompt
**"ML Engineer" Forum | Estimated response time: 10–15 minutes**

---

## 📣 Discussion Prompt

### "What should AI learn — and what should stay human?"

Now that you've seen how machine learning works, it's time to think critically about *where* we use it.

**Machine learning is already used for:**
- Deciding which videos you see next on YouTube
- Detecting fraud on your parents' credit cards
- Diagnosing diseases from medical scans
- Recognizing faces to unlock phones

**Here's your prompt — pick ONE of the following questions to answer (or answer all three for extra credit!):**

1. **The Data Question:** Your AI can only be as good as the data it was trained on. What could go wrong if training data is biased or incomplete? Give a real-world example or make up a detailed one.

2. **The Stakes Question:** Think of a decision that's currently made by an AI. Do you think that decision should be made by a human, an AI, or both working together? Why? What are the risks if the AI gets it wrong?

3. **The Design Question:** If you were building a machine learning system to solve a problem at your school (cafeteria lines, lost items, study help — you pick!), what data would you collect? How would you label it? What could go wrong?

---

## 📝 How to Respond

**Your initial post should:**
- Be at least **3–4 sentences**
- Reference at least **one concept from Module 4** (training data, labels, overfitting, accuracy, etc.)
- Include your own original opinion or idea — not just a summary of the video

**Then reply to at least ONE classmate's post:**
- Add something new they didn't think of
- Ask them a follow-up question
- Respectfully challenge their reasoning if you disagree

---

## 💡 Tips for a Great Post

- It's okay to say "I'm not sure, but I think…" — this is a discussion, not a test
- Real-world connections get full credit — bring in news, your own experience, a movie you've seen
- Disagreeing is encouraged — just be respectful and explain your reasoning

---

## 🔗 Connections to Make

- How does what you learned in the Training Simulator connect to real AI systems?
- What's the difference between a model being *accurate* and being *fair*?
- Should people who are affected by AI decisions have a say in how those systems are trained?
