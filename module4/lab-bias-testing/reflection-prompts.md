# Bias Lab — Reflection Prompts
## Deep-Dive Questions for Post-Lab Discussion and Written Response

---

## Overview

These prompts are designed for post-lab discussion or written response. They move from concrete observations (what students found) to broader critical thinking (what it means for society). Use them flexibly — as Socratic discussion starters, journal prompts, essay topics, or exit tickets.

---

## Tier 1: Understanding the Evidence
*Good for: Checking comprehension, grounding in data, immediate post-lab discussion*

### Prompt 1.1 — Your Key Finding
Describe the most significant example of bias you found in your testing. What question did you ask? What did OpenClaw say? Why do you consider this a form of bias?

---

### Prompt 1.2 — Comparing the Gap
You tested OpenClaw on mainstream vs. niche topics. In your own words, explain the difference in quality between those responses. Was the gap bigger than you expected? Smaller?

---

### Prompt 1.3 — The Counterexample
Was there a niche or underrepresented topic where OpenClaw actually performed well? What might explain this? What does it tell you about what affects AI training data?

---

### Prompt 1.4 — Explaining the Score
You rated OpenClaw's responses on a 1–5 scale. Pick one response you rated a "2" or lower. What specifically made it weak — was it incomplete, inaccurate, overly hedged, or something else?

---

## Tier 2: Analyzing the Cause
*Good for: Deeper discussion, connecting to how AI is built*

### Prompt 2.1 — Training Data as a Mirror
AI models like OpenClaw are trained on text from the internet. In what ways is the internet itself biased in terms of what topics are covered, what languages dominate, and whose voices are heard? How does that affect OpenClaw?

---

### Prompt 2.2 — The Language Factor
Most large AI training datasets are heavily English-language. If you were a student whose first language is Hausa, Tagalog, or Quechua, how might your experience with an AI like OpenClaw be different from an English-speaking student in the US? What are the fairness implications?

---

### Prompt 2.3 — Representation in the Pipeline
AI systems are designed, trained, and evaluated by people. If the teams building AI are not demographically diverse, how might that affect what gets noticed and fixed? What would be different if the development team included people from the communities whose topics are underrepresented?

---

### Prompt 2.4 — Bias vs. Error
Is AI bias the same as an AI making errors? Explain the difference using an example from your lab. Which is more harmful, and why?

---

## Tier 3: Evaluating Impact
*Good for: Ethics discussions, cross-curricular connections, connecting to students' lives*

### Prompt 3.1 — High-Stakes Decisions
AI systems are now used to help with hiring, medical diagnosis, loan approvals, criminal sentencing, and school admissions. What would happen if an AI with the biases you found in OpenClaw was used in one of these contexts? Who gets hurt?

---

### Prompt 3.2 — The "Good Enough" Trap
Some people argue that AI bias is acceptable because "the AI is still mostly accurate." Do you agree or disagree? Is 80% accuracy across all topics good enough? What if that 80% average hides 95% accuracy for some groups and 50% for others?

---

### Prompt 3.3 — Trust and Verification
You now know that OpenClaw has knowledge gaps and biases. How does this change how you would use it in your own life — for homework, research, or decision-making? What habits would you adopt to verify its outputs?

---

### Prompt 3.4 — Your Own Blind Spots
Humans also have biases — we learn from our experiences, and our experiences are shaped by where we grew up, what media we consume, and who we know. In what ways are you similar to an AI model? In what ways are you different when it comes to bias?

---

## Tier 4: Proposing Solutions
*Good for: Action-oriented discussions, creative problem-solving, final projects*

### Prompt 4.1 — Redesigning Training
If you were on the team building the next version of OpenClaw, what changes would you make to the training data to reduce the biases you found? Be specific — what sources would you add? What languages? What topics? How would you verify coverage?

---

### Prompt 4.2 — The Transparency Argument
Should AI systems be required to disclose when they are uncertain or when their knowledge about a topic might be limited? Design a feature for OpenClaw that would help users know when to trust its answers less. How would it work?

---

### Prompt 4.3 — Who Should Decide?
Who should get to decide what counts as "fair" AI? The companies that build it? Governments? Users? Affected communities? Make a case for your view.

---

### Prompt 4.4 — The "What Would You Change?" Challenge
Write a formal recommendation memo addressed to the OpenClaw development team. Summarize your three most significant findings, explain why each matters, and propose a specific, actionable fix for each one. Use evidence from your lab to support your claims.

---

## Discussion Protocols

### For Small Groups (4–5 students):
1. Each person shares their most surprising finding (2 min each)
2. Group identifies the most significant shared finding
3. Group drafts one recommendation and shares with the class

### For Whole Class (Socratic Seminar style):
- Open with Prompt 3.2 ("The Good Enough Trap") — it's contentious and generates debate
- Move to Prompt 2.1 (training data) once students understand the stakes
- Close with Prompt 4.3 (who decides) — leaves students with something to think about

### For Individual Written Response:
- Have students respond to one Tier 3 and one Tier 4 prompt in 300–400 words total
- Require at least two specific examples from their lab data
