# A Note to Students

You are living through one of the most significant technological shifts in human history.

Artificial intelligence — something that seemed like pure science fiction just a generation ago — is now woven into nearly everything: the music you listen to, the apps you use, the news you read, the jobs your parents do. It filters your email. It powers the search engine. It drives (literally) some of the cars on your street.

Most people interact with AI every single day without understanding it. This book is going to change that for you.

But here's what makes this different from most books about technology: **you won't just read about AI. You'll build it.**

Throughout this course, you'll create your own AI assistant using a tool called **OpenClaw**. You'll give it a name and a personality. You'll teach it to see and hear. You'll fill it with knowledge. You'll watch it learn. You'll test its limits, discover its blind spots, and grapple with the real ethical questions that come with building something powerful.

By the time you finish, you'll understand AI not as a user — but as a builder.

That's a skill that will matter for the rest of your life.

Let's get started.

# A Note to Teachers

*AI Explorers* is the student companion to the OpenClaw AI Literacy Curriculum, a 7-module, project-based course designed for grades 6–8. The textbook is designed to be read *alongside* hands-on lab work, not instead of it. Each chapter introduces concepts that students will apply in the same module's labs and activities.

**How to Use This Book**

- **Before lab:** Students read the chapter (or sections of it) to build conceptual background.
- **During lab:** The textbook stays available as a reference — particularly vocabulary definitions and concept explanations.
- **After lab:** Chapter review questions and OpenClaw Challenges connect hands-on experience back to the reading.
- **Discussion anchor:** Opening stories and "AI in the Wild" sidebars are designed to spark class discussion.

**Differentiation Notes**

- Reading level targets grades 6–8, with vocabulary explicitly introduced and defined.
- "Going Further" sections at the end of each chapter provide extension for advanced students.
- Opening stories use diverse characters and varied contexts to support engagement across student backgrounds.
- Vocabulary tables can be pre-taught or used for reference during independent reading.

**QR Codes**

Each chapter includes QR codes linking to videos, interactive tools, and supplementary readings. A full URL list is available in the Teacher Guide (see `module*/teacher-guide/`).

**Assessment**

Review questions at the end of each chapter are designed for formative assessment. They range from recall (define this term) to application (give an example) to analysis (evaluate this argument). Rubrics for open-ended questions are included in each module's `assessments/` folder.

# A Note to Families

Your student is taking a course in AI literacy — and this is the textbook they'll use.

**What is AI literacy?**

AI literacy means understanding what artificial intelligence is, how it works, and how to use it critically and responsibly. It's as important as reading literacy or math literacy — arguably more urgent right now, given how quickly AI tools are entering every area of life.

**What will my student learn?**

By the end of this course, your student will be able to:

- Explain what AI is and how it works (in real terms, not just buzzwords)
- Build and customize a simple AI assistant
- Evaluate AI tools critically — knowing what to trust and what to verify
- Understand the ethical questions AI raises around bias, privacy, and fairness
- Think about what kind of AI future they want to help build

**How can I support my student at home?**

- Ask them what they're building. The agent they build during this course is something they can demonstrate and explain.
- Have conversations about AI you encounter together — in apps, news, and everyday life.
- If you're curious, read the chapters too. They're written to be accessible to adults as well as students.

## The Structure

*AI Explorers* follows the same arc as the OpenClaw curriculum's seven modules:

| Module | Chapters | Theme |
|--------|----------|-------|
| Module 1 | Ch 1–2 | What is AI? Where did it come from? |
| Module 2 | Ch 3–4 | How AI perceives the world (sight and sound) |
| Module 3 | Ch 5–6 | How AI thinks (logic, patterns, reasoning) |
| Module 4 | Ch 7–8 | How AI learns (training, data) |
| Module 5 | Ch 9–10 | How AI communicates (prompts, failures) |
| Module 6 | Ch 11–12 | Ethics and society (bias, privacy) |
| Module 7 | Ch 13–15 | AI futures (limits, careers, your path) |

## Chapter Features

Every chapter includes:

**🚀 Opening Story** — A narrative that introduces the chapter's central concept through a character's experience. These stories are designed to be read aloud, discussed, or used as discussion starters.

**📖 Main Sections** — The core content, written at a grades 6–8 reading level. Complex vocabulary is bolded on first use and defined in the chapter's vocabulary table.

**🌍 AI in the Wild Sidebars** — Real-world examples of AI in action, including both impressive achievements and cautionary failures. Each includes a QR code for deeper exploration.

**🤖 Your Agent's Journey Sidebars** — Connect chapter concepts directly to the agent-building work happening in labs. Show students *why* the concept matters for what they're building.

**💡 Concept Boxes and Tables** — Visual organization for key ideas, comparisons, and definitions.

**📝 Key Vocabulary** — A table of defined terms at the end of each chapter.

**🐾 OpenClaw Challenges** — Hands-on exercises using the OpenClaw AI tool. Designed to take 15–30 minutes each.

**❓ Review Questions** — End-of-chapter comprehension and analysis questions.

**📚 Going Further** — Extension resources for students who want to dig deeper.

# Glossary

*Terms are listed alphabetically. Chapter of first introduction is noted in brackets.*

---

**Algorithm** [Ch 1] — A step-by-step set of instructions for solving a problem. Every computer program is built from algorithms; AI algorithms are ones that learn or adapt from data.

**Algorithmic bias** [Ch 11] — Systematic, unfair differences in how an AI system treats different groups of people, usually traceable to biased training data or flawed design choices.

**Annotation** [Ch 8] — The process of adding labels or tags to raw data to make it usable for supervised learning (e.g., labeling images as "dog" or "cat").

**Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)** [Ch 13] — A hypothetical future AI that can perform any intellectual task that a human can. No current AI achieves this; all existing AI is "narrow."

**Artificial Intelligence (AI)** [Ch 1] — Computer systems designed to perform tasks that normally require human-like thinking, such as learning, reasoning, understanding language, or recognizing patterns.

**Backpropagation** [Ch 7] — The algorithm used to train neural networks by working backward from errors to adjust the model's internal weights.

**Bias (in AI)** [Ch 2, 11] — Systematic errors or unfair differences in AI output, usually resulting from unrepresentative training data or flawed assumptions in the model's design.

**CAPTCHA** [Ch 8] — "Completely Automated Public Turing Test to tell Computers and Humans Apart." The puzzles asking you to identify fire hydrants or crosswalks — which simultaneously verify you're human and generate labeled training data.

**Chain-of-thought prompting** [Ch 6, 9] — A prompting technique that asks an AI to explain its reasoning step by step before giving a final answer, often improving accuracy and transparency.

**Class imbalance** [Ch 8] — When a training dataset has many more examples of some categories than others, which can cause an AI to perform poorly on underrepresented categories.

**Classification** [Ch 3] — The task of assigning a label or category to an input (e.g., "Is this email spam or not spam?").

**COMPAS** [Ch 11] — Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions — a commercial risk assessment algorithm used in US criminal courts. Subject of major bias research.

**Consent** [Ch 8, 12] — Informed agreement to something. In AI and data contexts, meaningful consent means people understand what data is being collected about them and how it will be used.

**Context window** [Ch 9, 10] — The amount of text (measured in tokens) that a language model can process at one time. Text outside the context window is not "remembered" by the model.

**Data broker** [Ch 12] — A company that collects personal data from multiple sources and sells it to third parties, often without the knowledge of the people whose data is being traded.

**Data poisoning** [Ch 8] — A cyberattack in which malicious actors corrupt training data to cause an AI model to behave incorrectly or harmfully.

**Data provenance** [Ch 8] — The documented history of where a dataset came from, how it was collected, and what processing it has undergone.

**Decision tree** [Ch 5] — An AI model that makes decisions by asking a series of yes/no questions about the input, following branches until reaching a conclusion.

**Deep learning** [Ch 1, 3] — A type of machine learning using multi-layered neural networks. The "depth" (many layers) allows these models to learn increasingly complex features.

**Deep neural network** [Ch 3] — A neural network with many hidden layers between input and output, enabling recognition of complex patterns.

**Differential privacy** [Ch 12] — A mathematical framework for analyzing data while adding controlled noise, so individual people's data can't be identified even when aggregate patterns are revealed.

**Epoch** [Ch 7] — One complete pass through the entire training dataset during model training.

**Ensemble** [Ch 5] — A machine learning technique that combines multiple models (e.g., a random forest is an ensemble of decision trees) to improve accuracy and robustness.

**Facial recognition** [Ch 4] — AI technology that identifies or verifies a person's identity from an image or video of their face.

**Feature** [Ch 3, 5] — A measurable property or characteristic of data used as input to a machine learning model (e.g., pixel brightness, word frequency, temperature).

**Feature detection** [Ch 3] — The process of identifying meaningful patterns in data (edges, shapes, parts) during neural network processing.

**Federated learning** [Ch 12] — A privacy-preserving technique where AI models are trained locally on users' devices and only the model updates (not the raw data) are shared with a central server.

**Few-shot prompting** [Ch 9] — A prompting technique that includes a small number of examples in the prompt to show the AI what kind of output is expected.

**Generalization** [Ch 7, 13] — An AI model's ability to perform well on new data it wasn't trained on. A model that has generalized well has learned underlying patterns, not just memorized specific examples.

**Hallucination** [Ch 10] — When an AI language model generates text that sounds plausible but is factually incorrect or completely fabricated. Named by analogy to perceiving things that aren't there.

**Hugging Face** [Ch 3] — An AI platform where researchers and companies share pre-trained models, datasets, and interactive demos. Often described as "GitHub for AI."

**Image captioning** [Ch 3] — An AI task that generates a natural language sentence describing the contents of an image.

**Image classification** [Ch 3] — An AI task that assigns a category label (or probability scores for multiple labels) to an image.

**Inference API** [Ch 3] — A web service that accepts data as input, runs it through a pre-trained model on remote servers, and returns the model's output.

**Jailbreaking** [Ch 10] — Attempts to bypass an AI system's safety guidelines or restrictions through clever prompting.

**Knowledge cutoff** [Ch 10] — The date after which an AI language model has no information, because training data was collected before that point.

**Labeled data** [Ch 7, 8] — Data that has been tagged with correct answers (e.g., images labeled "cat" or "dog") to train supervised learning models.

**Large language model (LLM)** [Ch 9] — A type of AI trained on massive text datasets that can generate, summarize, translate, and converse in natural language.

**Loss function** [Ch 7] — A mathematical measure of how wrong a model's prediction is. Training minimizes the loss function over many iterations.

**Machine learning** [Ch 1, 7] — A type of AI that learns patterns from data rather than following hand-coded rules.

**Model** [Ch 7] — In machine learning, the mathematical structure (with learned weights/parameters) that takes input and produces output.

**Natural language processing (NLP)** [Ch 9] — The field of AI focused on enabling computers to understand, generate, and interact using human language.

**Neural network** [Ch 1] — An AI system with interconnected layers of processing units, loosely inspired by neurons in the brain. Can learn complex patterns from large datasets.

**Object detection** [Ch 3] — An AI task that identifies multiple objects in an image and localizes them with bounding boxes.

**Overfitting** [Ch 5, 7] — When a model performs well on training data but poorly on new data, because it has memorized specific examples rather than learning general patterns.

**Pixel** [Ch 3] — The smallest unit of a digital image, storing a color value as three RGB numbers.

**Pre-trained model** [Ch 3] — An AI model already trained on a large dataset by researchers, made available for others to use or fine-tune.

**Prompt** [Ch 9] — The input text given to a language model. The quality of the prompt significantly affects the quality of the output.

**Prompt engineering** [Ch 9] — The practice of carefully designing prompts to get better, more useful, or more accurate responses from AI language models.

**Prompt injection** [Ch 10] — An attack where malicious text hidden in an AI's input attempts to override its instructions or safety guidelines.

**Random forest** [Ch 5] — An ensemble of many decision trees, each trained on a random subset of data and features, whose results are combined by majority vote.

**Recidivism** [Ch 11] — The tendency of a person convicted of a crime to commit another crime. AI risk assessment tools attempt to predict recidivism rates, with contested accuracy and fairness.

**Reinforcement learning** [Ch 7] — A type of machine learning where an AI learns by taking actions, receiving rewards or penalties, and adjusting behavior to maximize future rewards.

**RGB** [Ch 3] — Red, Green, Blue — the three color channels used to describe pixel color values in digital images (each channel ranges from 0–255).

**Speech recognition** [Ch 4] — AI technology that converts spoken audio into text.

**Spectrogram** [Ch 4] — A visual map of the frequency content of audio over time, used as input for speech recognition models.

**Supervised learning** [Ch 7] — Training an AI model using labeled examples (input + correct output pairs).

**Surveillance capitalism** [Ch 12] — An economic model in which companies generate profit by collecting and selling personal behavioral data, often without users' full awareness.

**Synthetic data** [Ch 8] — Artificially generated training data, created computationally rather than collected from the real world.

**Training data** [Ch 1, 7, 8] — The collection of examples used to teach an AI model.

**Training loop** [Ch 7] — The iterative cycle of: present data → make prediction → measure error → adjust weights → repeat.

**Turing Test** [Ch 1] — A test proposed by Alan Turing in 1950: if a human cannot distinguish a machine's responses from a human's in conversation, the machine is considered to have passed.

**Underfitting** [Ch 7] — When a model is too simple to capture the patterns in the data, performing poorly on both training and new data.

**Unlabeled data** [Ch 8] — Raw data without tags or correct-answer labels. Useful for unsupervised learning or as a starting point for annotation.

**Unsupervised learning** [Ch 7] — Training an AI model to find patterns in data without labeled examples — the model discovers structure on its own.

**Waveform** [Ch 4] — A digital representation of sound as a sequence of amplitude values sampled over time.

**Whisper** [Ch 4] — OpenAI's open-source speech recognition model, trained on 680,000 hours of multilingual audio. Available free on Hugging Face.
