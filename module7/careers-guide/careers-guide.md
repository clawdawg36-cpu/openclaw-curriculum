## 1. Machine Learning Engineer

### What they do
Machine learning engineers build the systems that make AI "learn." They design algorithms (sets of rules) that allow computers to get better at tasks — like recognizing speech, predicting what you'll watch next, or detecting diseases in medical images — without being explicitly programmed for each case. They also build the data pipelines, model training systems, and infrastructure that keeps AI working at scale.

### What to study
- **Math:** Statistics and probability — these are the foundations of learning algorithms
- **Computer science:** Programming (Python is the dominant language), data structures, algorithms
- **Machine learning fundamentals:** How models learn, what makes them fail, how to evaluate them

**A great early step:** Learn Python. Then try an intro ML tutorial on a free platform like Google Colab or Kaggle. You don't need a fancy computer to start.

### Inspiring example
**Fei-Fei Li** is a Stanford professor and co-director of the Stanford Human-Centered AI Institute. She led the creation of ImageNet — a massive labeled image dataset that became the foundation for modern computer vision. She's also a passionate advocate for diversity in tech and co-founded AI4ALL, a nonprofit that brings underrepresented students into AI education. She's proof that rigorous science and inclusive advocacy belong together.

## 2. AI Ethicist

### What they do
AI ethicists ask the hard questions: *Is this system fair? Who does it harm? Should we build it at all?* They work inside tech companies, research institutes, government agencies, and nonprofits to evaluate AI systems for bias, privacy risks, and societal impact. They write guidelines, conduct audits, advise on policy, and sometimes sound the alarm when something goes wrong.

This is a relatively new field — which means it's still being shaped, and the people entering it now have real power to define what it looks like.

### What to study
- **Ethics and philosophy:** Moral reasoning, fairness, rights — these are your core tools
- **Social sciences:** Sociology, psychology, and political science help you understand how technology affects people
- **Technical literacy:** You don't need to build AI to evaluate it, but understanding how it works helps enormously

**A great early step:** Read about a real AI controversy — hiring algorithms, facial recognition in policing, social media recommendation systems. Pick a side. Write a paragraph arguing for it, and then write a paragraph arguing the opposite. That skill — understanding competing perspectives — is what ethicists do every day.

### Inspiring example
**Timnit Gebru** is a computer scientist and AI ethics researcher who co-authored a landmark paper on the risks of large language models and helped found the Distributed AI Research Institute (DAIR). She's known for her courageous work studying and speaking out about bias in AI systems — particularly as it affects marginalized communities. Her work has shaped how the entire industry thinks about responsible AI.

## 3. Data Scientist

### What they do
Data scientists find meaning in large amounts of data. They ask questions like: *Why are customers churning? What predicts a student's grade? Where is crime most likely to happen next?* They clean messy data, build models, run analyses, and visualize results so that decision-makers can act on them. Data scientists work in nearly every industry — healthcare, sports, finance, government, nonprofit — because data is everywhere.

### What to study
- **Statistics:** The core skill — understanding what data actually tells you (and what it doesn't)
- **Programming:** Python and R are the main tools; SQL for working with databases
- **Domain knowledge:** The best data scientists combine technical skill with deep understanding of their field (medicine, education, climate, etc.)

**A great early step:** Explore a free dataset that interests you. Sports statistics, climate data, and public health records are all freely available. Ask a question. Try to answer it with the data. See where it gets messy. That's the job.

### Inspiring example
**David Sánchez** is a researcher who used data science to study language bias in AI models — specifically how some languages are dramatically underrepresented in large language models. His work highlights that being a data scientist isn't just about numbers; it's about who gets counted, and whose experience the data represents.

## 4. AI Educator

### What they do
AI educators teach others how artificial intelligence works — in classrooms, online courses, corporate training programs, and public media. They translate complex concepts into clear explanations, design activities that help people learn by doing, and make AI accessible to people who didn't study computer science. As AI becomes part of everyday life, the need for good AI educators is growing fast.

**Sound familiar?** The curriculum you just completed was designed and written by AI educators.

### What to study
- **Education theory and design:** How people learn, how to design activities, how to assess understanding
- **Communication:** Writing clearly, speaking clearly, making complexity approachable
- **AI fluency:** You don't need to be an expert AI researcher to teach AI concepts — but you need a solid foundation

**A great early step:** Explain something you learned in this curriculum to someone who hasn't taken it. A younger sibling, a parent, a friend. Notice where they get confused. That confusion points you toward what needs to be explained better. That's curriculum design in action.

### Inspiring example
**Sal Khan** founded Khan Academy in 2005 with a mission to provide free, world-class education to anyone, anywhere. Khan Academy now includes AI tutoring tools designed to help students learn at their own pace. His work demonstrates how education technology — when designed thoughtfully — can dramatically expand access to learning.

## 5. Product Manager (AI Products)

### What they do
Product managers (PMs) are the people who decide *what gets built* and *why*. For AI products, a PM might lead the development of a new AI feature, coordinate between engineers, designers, and researchers, define what "success" looks like, and make sure the product actually solves a real problem for real users. PMs don't need to write code — but they need to deeply understand both technology and people.

### What to study
- **Business and economics:** How products create value, how companies work, how markets function
- **Psychology and user research:** What users actually want vs. what they say they want
- **Communication:** PMs spend most of their time writing, presenting, and facilitating decisions

**A great early step:** Next time you use an app or tool, ask: *Why did they design it this way? What problem are they trying to solve? What's missing?* Write down your answers. That critical perspective is a PM's most important skill.

### Inspiring example
**Mira Murati** served as CTO of OpenAI and was instrumental in bringing ChatGPT and DALL-E to the public. She's an example of someone whose career crossed engineering, research leadership, and product strategy — showing that technical skill and product thinking work together.

## 6. AI Policy Analyst

### What they do
AI policy analysts work at the intersection of government, technology, and society. They research how AI affects laws, elections, privacy, healthcare, and national security — and they advise lawmakers, agencies, and organizations on how to regulate, use, or govern AI responsibly. This career is especially important right now: AI is moving faster than existing laws, and the people helping figure out the rules are shaping the future.

### What to study
- **Political science and public policy:** How governments work, how laws are made, how policy gets implemented
- **Law:** Understanding legal frameworks, rights, and liability
- **Economics:** How AI affects markets, labor, and inequality

**A great early step:** Read about a recent AI law or regulation — the EU AI Act, state laws on facial recognition, or FTC guidance on AI in advertising. Try to understand: what problem is this law trying to solve? Who pushed for it? Who opposed it? Policy thinking starts with those questions.

### Inspiring example
**Alondra Nelson** is a sociologist who served as the first deputy director for science and society at the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy (OSTP). She led the development of the Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights — a landmark document that describes what fairness, transparency, and protection from harmful AI should look like for American citizens. She's an example of how social science training leads directly to shaping national AI policy.

## 7. AI Artist

### What they do
AI artists use artificial intelligence as a creative tool — generating images, music, video, poetry, interactive experiences, and more. Some use AI to create works that would be impossible any other way; others use it to collaborate with or challenge the machine. AI artists work in fine art, film, advertising, video games, and interactive media. They're also often the first people to raise important questions about authorship, creativity, and what it means to make something.

### What to study
- **Art, design, and media:** Understanding composition, aesthetics, visual storytelling, and creative process
- **Technology literacy:** How generative AI models work — enough to use them intentionally, not just randomly
- **Critical thinking:** Asking hard questions about authorship, ethics, and the role of AI in culture

**A great early step:** Try generating an image with an AI tool (Midjourney, DALL-E, or Adobe Firefly). Then try to recreate the same effect by hand, or by adjusting your prompt carefully. Notice what you control and what you don't. That process of intention and iteration is artistic practice.

### Inspiring example
**Refik Anadol** is a media artist and director known for creating immersive large-scale installations that use AI to visualize data as living art. His work has appeared on the facade of the Smithsonian Institution and in museums around the world. He's also known for thinking carefully about the ethics of the data his models learn from — demonstrating that artists can be both technically sophisticated and ethically engaged.

## 8. Robotics Engineer

### What they do
Robotics engineers design and build physical machines that can sense their environment, make decisions, and take action in the real world. AI is central to modern robotics: robots use computer vision to see, machine learning to adapt, and language models to communicate. Robotics engineers work in manufacturing, healthcare (surgical robots), agriculture, space exploration, and disaster response.

### What to study
- **Physics and mechanical engineering:** How physical systems work, forces, motion
- **Computer science:** Coding, sensors, actuators, control systems
- **AI and machine learning:** Vision, reinforcement learning, planning

**A great early step:** If you have access to a robotics kit (LEGO Mindstorms, VEX, Arduino), build something and try to make it respond to its environment. Even a simple light sensor that makes a motor change direction teaches you the core loop of robotics: sense → decide → act.

### Inspiring example
**Ayanna Howard** is a robotics professor and Dean of Engineering at The Ohio State University. She builds robots designed to interact with humans — including robots that help children with physical therapy. She's also a leading voice on bias in AI systems used in healthcare. Her career spans technical depth and human impact in equal measure.

## 9. AI Researcher

### What they do
AI researchers push the boundaries of what's possible. They develop new algorithms, publish papers, run experiments, and try to understand why AI systems behave the way they do. Some focus on fundamental research (understanding intelligence itself); others focus on applied research (making AI better at specific tasks). AI research happens in universities, tech companies, and independent institutes.

### What to study
- **Mathematics:** Calculus, linear algebra, probability — this is the language of AI research
- **Computer science:** Algorithms, programming, software systems
- **Curiosity and persistence:** Research is slow. Most experiments fail. The people who thrive are the ones who find failure interesting.

**A great early step:** Read a simplified AI research blog post or explainer (Distill.pub, The Batch, or Andrej Karpathy's writing). You won't understand all of it — that's fine. Notice what excites you. That's a signal worth following.

### Inspiring example
**Rediet Abebe** is a computer scientist and junior fellow at the Harvard Society of Fellows. She co-founded Black in AI, an organization that promotes inclusion of Black researchers in artificial intelligence. Her research focuses on the intersection of algorithms and social good — finding ways that computational tools can address inequality rather than deepen it.

## The Bigger Picture

These nine careers represent a tiny slice of what's possible. The AI field touches medicine, climate science, law, music, agriculture, education, and almost every other domain you can think of.

Here's what they all have in common:

> **They need people who can build AND think AND communicate.**

You've been practicing all three.

Whatever direction you go — technical, creative, policy-focused, people-centered — the skills from this curriculum transfer. You know how to build a system. You know how to think about its impact. You know how to explain it to someone who didn't build it.

That's more than most people know.
