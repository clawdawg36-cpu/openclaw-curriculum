# Lesson 7.1 — Demo Design Workshop

**Module:** 7 — Capstone: AI Expo  
**Grade Level:** 6–10 (adaptable)  
**Duration:** 50–60 minutes  
**Session Type:** Workshop / Direct Instruction  
**Standards Alignment:** AI4K12 Five Big Ideas; CSTA K-12 CS Standards; NGSS Science & Engineering Practices (Communicating Information)

---

## Overview

This is the first session of Module 7. Students are NOT building anything new — their agents are (mostly) built. This session is entirely about learning *how to present* what they've built. The core insight: a great demo is a story, not a feature list. Students learn the Hook-Build-Demo-Reflect arc, storyboard their own demo, and critique live examples of good and bad demos.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Explain the four-part structure of a compelling 5-minute demo (Hook → Build → Demo → Reflect).
2. Identify what makes demos land or fall flat (with reference to real examples).
3. Complete an initial storyboard for their own 5-minute agent demo.
4. Articulate the emotional job of each demo segment: *Why should the audience care?*

## Lesson Sequence

### Warm-Up: The Worst Presentation You've Ever Seen (5 min)

**Think-Pair-Share**

1. Prompt on board: *"Think about the most boring presentation you've ever watched. What made it painful?"*
2. Students write 3 specific things that made it bad. (1 min)
3. Quick pair share. (1 min)
4. Class callout — teacher charts: **What Makes Demos Die**
   - Reading slides word-for-word
   - Starting with "So, um, today I'm going to talk about..."
   - Showing everything before you show anything *interesting*
   - Apologizing ("I know this isn't working...")
   - Losing the audience in technical jargon on slide 1

**Bridge:** "You have 5 minutes at the Expo to show someone something real you built. What do they need to feel, not just hear?"

---

### Direct Instruction: The Hook-Build-Demo-Reflect Arc (12 min)

**Format:** Slides + live annotation

Walk through the four-part framework. Emphasize: this is a *story structure*, not an agenda.

#### Part 1: The Hook (30 seconds)
> *Make them care before you explain anything.*

- Start with your agent doing something surprising, funny, or useful — live.
- Do NOT start with "Hi, my name is..." or "So I built an agent that..."
- Example hooks:
  - Ask your agent a weird question and show the answer live
  - Show a before/after: "Without my agent, I had to do this. With it — watch."
  - Open with a problem your agent solves: "Has this ever happened to you?"
- **The test:** If someone walked in 30 seconds late, would they still be curious?

#### Part 2: The Build (2 minutes)
> *Show the journey, not just the destination.*

- Walk through what your agent gained in each module (M1–M6)
- You don't need to demo every capability — narrate the progression
- Frame it as growth: "I started with just a name. Then I taught it to see. Then..."
- Keep it moving — 20–30 seconds per module milestone
- **The test:** Does the audience understand *how* the agent got to where it is?

#### Part 3: The Demo (2 minutes)
> *Show don't tell — live, with real inputs.*

- Demo at least 3 capabilities live (not screenshots, not videos of the agent)
- Use real, interesting inputs — not "test" or "hello world"
- Have a backup plan (screenshots) in case something breaks
- **The test:** Would the demo still be interesting if nothing broke AND if something broke?

#### Part 4: The Reflect (30 seconds)
> *Honesty is the most impressive thing you can say.*

- One honest limitation: "Here's what my agent doesn't do well yet."
- One thing you'd add if you had more time
- End with confidence, not apology: "This is what I built. It does X, Y, Z."
- **The test:** Does the audience trust you more after the reflection than before?

---

### Video Analysis: Good Demo vs. Bad Demo (10 min)

**Format:** Class watch + annotation

Show 2 short clips (2–3 min each) — see resource suggestions below or use teacher-curated examples.

For each clip:
1. Watch without interruption
2. Students write on sticky notes or in journals: *What worked? What didn't?*
3. Class debrief — chart observations against the Hook-Build-Demo-Reflect framework

**Discussion questions:**
- "Where did the hook land or miss?"
- "When did you feel the most interested? The least?"
- "What would you cut if you only had 30 more seconds?"

**Resource Suggestions (teacher preview before class):**
- Short student-made demo from prior year (if available)
- A 3-min clip from a science fair or innovation challenge
- A famous product demo moment (e.g., Steve Jobs iPhone intro first 60 sec)
- An intentionally weak demo the teacher creates as a teaching tool

---

### Work Time: Storyboard Your Demo (15 min)

**Format:** Individual work + brief pair consult

Students use the Demo Storyboard Template to draft their own 4-part structure.

**Storyboard Template prompts:**

| Section | Time | Prompt |
|---------|------|--------|
| **Hook** | 30 sec | What's the single most interesting thing my agent can do? How can I show it in the first 30 seconds? |
| **Build** | 2 min | What's the story of how my agent was built? Which module milestones will I mention? |
| **Demo** | 2 min | Which 3 capabilities will I demo live? What inputs will I use? What's my backup plan? |
| **Reflect** | 30 sec | What's one honest limitation? What would I add? How will I close with confidence? |

After 10 min of individual work, students share their Hook with one partner. Partner responds: "Did I immediately want to see more? Why or why not?"

---

### Debrief + Next Steps Preview (5 min)

**Class discussion:**
- "Who found a Hook they're excited about? What is it?"
- "What's the hardest part of this structure for you right now?"
- "What do you still need to figure out about your agent before you can fill out the Build section?"

**Preview Lesson 7.2:** "Next session, you'll do a full capability inventory of your agent — every single thing it can do across all 6 modules. That's your raw material for the demo. Come ready to dig in."

**Journal prompt (exit ticket or homework):**
> *Write your Hook sentence — the one thing your agent does that you'd open with. Then write one sentence about your biggest uncertainty for the demo.*

## Differentiation

### For students who need support:
- Provide a completed example storyboard from a fictional agent to model the format
- Reduce the Build section to 3 modules (their strongest 3) rather than all 6
- Pair with a stronger presenter during the Hook share for encouragement

### For advanced students:
- Challenge: write TWO hooks and decide which is stronger — and why
- Add a "What would you do in Module 8?" teaser to their Reflect segment
- Ask: "How would you change your hook if your audience was parents vs. classmates vs. tech professionals?"

### For students with incomplete agents:
- Normalize: "The demo is about what you *did* build, not what you *wish* you'd built."
- Help them identify their strongest 2–3 capabilities and design around those
- Focus the Build section on the learning journey even if the tech is partial

---

## Assessment

**Formative:** Storyboard completion — teacher circulates and checks:
- Is the Hook specific and surprising (not generic)?
- Does the Build section reference actual module work?
- Are 3 demo capabilities identified with specific inputs listed?
- Is the Reflect honest and confident (not apologetic)?

**Journal prompt completion** (exit ticket)

---

## Teacher Notes

- This session sets the tone for the whole module. Emphasize: *this is a communication and design challenge, not a technical one.*
- Students who feel their agent isn't "good enough" need reassurance early: the Expo celebrates the journey, not perfection.
- The video analysis works best with at least one example the class finds genuinely bad — lean into the humor.
- If you have a prior year student willing to do a mock demo (or a video of one), that's gold.
