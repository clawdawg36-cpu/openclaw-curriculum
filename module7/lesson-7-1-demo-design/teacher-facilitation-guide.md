# Teacher Facilitation Guide — Lesson 7.1: Demo Design Workshop

---

## Before Class

### Preparation Checklist
- [ ] Preview and queue 2–3 demo video clips (2–3 min each recommended)
- [ ] Print or set up digital Demo Storyboard Template for each student
- [ ] Set up visible timer (projector timer or phone timer)
- [ ] Prepare one example storyboard for a fictional agent (for modeling/support)
- [ ] Read students' most recent journal entries to know where they are emotionally heading into Module 7

### Tone Setting
Module 7 opens with a tone shift: the building phase is over (or nearly over). Some students feel proud, some feel their agent isn't "done," some feel nervous about presenting. Your job in this lesson is to:

1. Normalize that all agents are presentation-ready (they have something real to show)
2. Shift the challenge from technical to communicative
3. Make the demo feel exciting, not scary

Open with genuine enthusiasm about seeing what they've built. This is the payoff.

## Facilitation Notes by Segment

### Warm-Up (5 min)
The "worst presentation ever" exercise works best when you model vulnerability first. Share your own worst presentation memory briefly before asking them. Gets a laugh and lowers the stakes.

When charting "What Makes Demos Die" — push for specifics. "It was boring" isn't useful. "They read every word from the slide" is useful. The goal is a class-generated list they can actively avoid.

**Common student answers to expect:**
- "They talked too fast"
- "Too many slides"
- "They were nervous and it made me nervous"
- "I couldn't tell what the point was"
- "Nothing worked and they panicked"

All of these are addressable with the framework. Bridge directly into the instruction.

---

### Hook-Build-Demo-Reflect Instruction (12 min)
Don't rush through the four parts. Each needs a concrete example.

**For the Hook:** Live-model a bad hook vs. a good hook for an imaginary agent. Start by saying "Hi, my name is [teacher name] and today I will be presenting my AI agent which I built over the last several weeks..." Then stop. "Was that interesting? No. Let me try again." Then open with the agent doing something surprising. The contrast lands hard.

**For the Build:** Remind students this is narration, not re-teaching. They're not re-explaining what machine learning is — they're saying "In Module 4, I gave my agent the ability to recognize patterns in data. That's how it does this." Quick, confident, sequential.

**For the Demo:** The most common mistake is demoing with "test" inputs. Ask: "If you were watching a cooking show and the chef used fake food, would you trust the recipe?" Real inputs make the demo feel real.

**For the Reflect:** This is counterintuitive for students. Many think admitting a weakness makes them look bad. Flip it: "Has anyone ever trusted someone MORE because they were honest about what they didn't know?" Let them answer. Honesty = credibility.

---

### Video Analysis (10 min)
Run both clips without interruption. Resist the urge to pause and comment — let students form their own reactions first.

During debrief, don't evaluate clips yourself before hearing from students. Ask: "What did you notice?" not "Did you see how they did X wrong?"

**If you don't have video clips ready:**
Alternatively, role-play two demos yourself (1 bad, 1 good, 2–3 min each). Hamming up the bad demo is usually funnier and more effective than any video.

---

### Storyboard Work Time (15 min)
Circulate actively. The Hook is where most students get stuck — they default to "I'll start by explaining what my agent is." Redirect gently: "What's the single most surprising thing your agent can do? Can you lead with that?"

For the Demo section — make sure they're identifying *specific* inputs, not just capability names. "I'll demo image recognition" is not specific. "I'll take a photo of a random object in the room and show what my agent identifies it as" is specific.

The partner Hook share (last 2 min of work time) is quick but important. "Did they immediately want to see more?" is a high-bar question — push them to be honest with each other.

---

### Debrief (5 min)
Invite 2–3 volunteers to share their Hook. React genuinely. If one is particularly strong, name why: "That works because you lead with action, not introduction."

The debrief question "What's the hardest part for you right now?" often surfaces anxiety about agent completeness. Acknowledge it directly: "Some of you are worried your agent isn't finished enough. We're going to address that in 7.2. For now — everyone has something real to show. Let's figure out how to show it well."

## Common Student Concerns + Responses

| Student Concern | Teacher Response |
|----------------|-----------------|
| "My agent doesn't work very well." | "The demo is about what you *did* build. 7.2 will help you figure out your best 3 things to show." |
| "I don't know how to start." | "Start with the most surprising thing your agent does. Show it first, explain later." |
| "What if something breaks during the demo?" | "That's why we have a backup plan. And if it breaks live, you narrate: 'This usually works like X — let me show you a screenshot of it.'" |
| "I'm nervous about presenting." | "Everyone is. Rehearsal is coming in 7.4. Today is just planning." |
| "I have too many things to show." | "Good problem to have. What's the ONE thing that would make someone say 'wow'? Start there." |

## Differentiation Notes

**Students with stronger agents:** Challenge them to design two different hooks (for two different audiences) and choose the better one. Also push them: "What's the *most* honest thing you could say in your Reflect? What does your agent really not do well?"

**Students with incomplete agents:** Work with them 1:1 during work time to identify 2–3 solid capabilities they *do* have. Help them see that an honest, complete presentation of a simpler agent is more powerful than an apologetic presentation of an incomplete complex one.

**English Language Learners / students with presentation anxiety:** The storyboard is a script scaffold — encourage them to write their Hook sentence word-for-word, not just bullet points. In 7.4 Rehearsal, they can read from it and build comfort gradually.

---

## Assessment Notes

Circulate during storyboard work. Quick mental checklist:
- Hook: specific + surprising (not "I'll introduce my agent")
- Build: references actual module milestones
- Demo: 3+ capabilities with specific inputs named
- Reflect: honest limitation named + confident closing statement

Journal prompt completion can be done in last 2 minutes of class or assigned as homework. Check at start of 7.2.
