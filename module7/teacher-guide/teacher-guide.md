# Module 7 Teacher Guide
## AI Expo Capstone — Comprehensive Educator Resource

**For educators without a CS or AI background**
*Module 7 of 7 in the OpenClaw Curriculum*

---

> **This is not a teaching module. It's a launching module.**
>
> You've spent six modules teaching concepts, facilitating labs, troubleshooting tech, and helping students build something real. Module 7 is different. Your job here is not to introduce new content — it's to help students *see what they've built*, craft a story around it, and share it with the world. You are part director, part coach, part logistics coordinator. And on Expo Day, you get to step back and watch your students shine.

## 1. Module Overview + Arc Context

### The Arc — What Students Have Built

This is the end of a journey. Over six modules, every student has:

| Module | Capability Unlocked |
|--------|---------------------|
| M1 | Understood what AI is — gave their agent a name, a purpose, a personality |
| M2 | Taught their agent to perceive — image recognition, audio processing |
| M3 | Built their agent's knowledge base — custom domain expertise |
| M4 | Trained their agent on data — a model that learns |
| M5 | Connected their agent to the world — live messaging, real users |
| M6 | Made their agent responsible — bias audit, usage policy, guardrails |
| **M7** | **Presenting their agent to the world — the AI Expo** |

Some students built exactly what you'd expect. Others surprised everyone, including themselves. A few didn't make it all the way to M6 — and that's okay. (See Section 5.)

The point of this module is not to add more capabilities. It is to help students articulate what they already built, why it matters, and what they learned along the way.

### What Your Role Is in M7

In Modules 1–6, you were primarily a content facilitator. You introduced concepts, led discussions, and helped students debug technical problems.

In Module 7, you wear three hats:

**Workshop Director (Sessions 1–3):** Facilitate brainstorming and proposal work. Ask the right questions. Help students find their narrative. Push back gently when a demo plan is unclear or too vague.

**Build Coach (Sessions 4–5):** Float during work time. Check in on every student. Troubleshoot technical issues, help students practice, and scope problems down to something manageable.

**Event Producer (Sessions 6–7):** Coordinate the room, guests, schedule, and tech. Be the calm center when things go sideways. Celebrate your students.

### What Makes This Module Different

Every other module in this curriculum has a clear conceptual destination — a thing students are supposed to understand. Module 7's destination is relational: students have to *explain* their work to someone who wasn't there for the journey.

This is genuinely hard. Some students will struggle to explain things they understand deeply. Others will need help organizing scattered technical knowledge into a coherent narrative. A few will panic about public speaking. Your facilitation work in this module is mostly about building confidence.

The AI Expo is, at its core, a moment of ownership. Let students own it.

### Pacing Overview

| Session | Focus | Approx. Time |
|---------|-------|--------------|
| 1 | Brainstorming: What did you build? | 50 min |
| 2 | Proposal workshop: What will you demo? | 50 min |
| 3 | Story structure: How will you tell it? | 50 min |
| 4 | Build session: Refine + rehearse | 50 min |
| 5 | Build session: Dress rehearsal | 50 min |
| 6 | Expo Day (setup + presentations) | 90–120 min |
| 7 | Reflection + closing the year | 50 min |

> **Flexible timing:** Sessions 1–5 can be compressed to 4 sessions if you're pressed for time. The non-negotiables are the Proposal Workshop (Session 2) and the Dress Rehearsal (Session 5). Do not skip either.

## 2. Session-by-Session Facilitation Guide

---

### Session 1: Brainstorming — What Did You Build?

**Goal:** Help students take inventory of their agent and surface what they're most proud of.

**Why it matters:** Students often don't know how good their work is. This session is about excavation — pulling out what exists before deciding what to show.

#### Warm-Up (10 min)

> Prompt (display on board): *"Open your agent in OpenClaw. Spend 5 minutes just using it — as if you're a visitor who's never seen it before. What does it do? What surprises you?"*

Give students 5 minutes of silent exploration, then 2 minutes of partner share.

#### Main Activity: Agent Inventory (25 min)

Distribute the **Agent Inventory worksheet** (or have students open a blank journal page) with these prompts:

1. **What can my agent do?** (List every capability — don't evaluate, just list)
2. **What's the coolest thing it does?** (Pick one — the thing you'd want to show first)
3. **What doesn't it do well?** (Be honest — this is useful, not shameful)
4. **Who would actually use this?** (Real person, real problem, real scenario)
5. **What did I learn that surprised me?**

Walk the room during this time. When a student says "it's not that impressive," ask: "What does it do?" Then respond with genuine curiosity, not cheerleading — "Huh. That's actually interesting. Tell me more about how that works."

#### Gallery Walk (10 min)

Students post their "coolest thing" answer on a sticky note on the wall. Class does a silent gallery walk, then small group shares: "Whose agent surprised you? Why?"

#### Closing (5 min)

> Exit ticket: "In one sentence: What is your agent? Who is it for?"

**Teacher checkpoint:** Collect exit tickets. Identify:
- Students who are energized and clear → they'll lead in Session 2
- Students who seem uncertain or discouraged → check in individually at the start of Session 2
- Students whose agents are in significantly different states → plan your Session 2 small-group configurations accordingly

---

### Session 2: Proposal Workshop — What Will You Demo?

**Goal:** Every student leaves with a written demo plan — a specific, structured description of what they'll show and say.

**Why it matters:** Without a concrete plan, students improvise under pressure and it doesn't go well. This session converts "I'll just show what I built" into "Here's exactly what I'll do for 5 minutes."

#### Opening (8 min)

Show a 2-minute video example of a good student tech demo. (See recommendations in Section 10, or use a short clip from a previous cohort if you have one.) Then ask: "What made that demo easy to follow? What made it interesting?"

Write student responses on the board. You're looking for: **clear problem → clear demo → clear reflection**. This is the narrative arc you'll reinforce all module.

#### Proposal Workshop (30 min)

Students work on their **Demo Proposal** — a one-page plan with:

- **Hook:** One sentence to open that makes the audience care (not "Hi I'm going to show you my agent")
- **Problem:** What does your agent solve? For whom?
- **Live demo sequence:** 3–5 specific things you'll show, in order
- **What goes right:** What you want the audience to see
- **What you'll say when something goes wrong:** One line, ready to go
- **Closing line:** How do you end? (Not "That's it!" — something that lands)

Your role during this time is **proposal conferences** — spend 3–4 minutes with each student. Use the Project Scoping Guide (Section 3) for students whose plans are too big or too small.

#### Pair Feedback (10 min)

Partners read each other's proposals and give one piece of warm feedback and one piece of "I'm curious about" feedback. This is not critique — it's curiosity.

#### Closing (2 min)

> "Write down: What's the one thing you want your audience to remember about your agent?"

**Teacher checkpoint:** By end of Session 2, every student should have a draft proposal. If someone doesn't — that's your flag. They may need more individual support, a scoping conversation, or help with incomplete work (see Section 5).

---

### Session 3: Story Structure — How Will You Tell It?

**Goal:** Students build the narrative arc of their demo — not just what they show, but how they take the audience on a journey.

**Why it matters:** Technical demos often fail because they're lists of features, not stories. A story has stakes, a moment of tension, and a resolution. Students learn this structure and apply it to their demo.

#### Opening: The Demo as Story (15 min)

Mini-lesson on the 3-act structure for a technical demo:

| Act | What it is | For your demo |
|-----|-----------|---------------|
| **Set-up** | Who, what, why | "This is who my agent is for. Here's the problem they have." |
| **Confrontation** | The demo itself | "Watch what happens when I do this. Notice this." |
| **Resolution** | What it means | "Here's what this shows. Here's what I'd do next." |

Stress: *The most memorable demos are the ones where something interesting happens — even if it's the agent making a mistake.* If your agent has a known failure mode, showing it is more impressive than hiding it.

#### Storyboard Activity (25 min)

Students create a **storyboard** — a simple 6–8 panel sketch of their demo:
1. How you open
2. Setting up the scenario
3. First action (live input to the agent)
4. Agent response — what happens
5. A second demonstration (different angle, edge case, or deeper feature)
6. One thing that could go wrong and how you respond
7. What you say it means
8. Closing line

You don't need to be an artist. Stick figures are fine. The goal is to *think through* the demo before performing it.

Float the room and look for:
- **Missing panels:** Students who skip "what happens if something goes wrong" — push them here
- **Too many panels:** Students trying to show everything — guide them toward one clear narrative (see Section 3)
- **Weak openings:** If the hook is "Hi my name is ___ and I made an AI," help them find a more interesting entry point

#### Peer Run-Through (10 min)

Students pair up and verbally walk each other through their storyboard — no devices, just narration. Partners ask: "What's the one thing you most want me to remember?"

---

### Session 4: Build Session — Refine and Rehearse

**Goal:** Students tighten their demos, practice out loud, and solve any remaining technical issues.

**Why it matters:** Many students haven't practiced saying words out loud. Silent rehearsal is not rehearsal. This session is about getting reps in.

#### Structure (50 min)

| Time | Activity |
|------|---------|
| 5 min | Review your demo proposal — what's changed since Session 2? |
| 20 min | Work time: Refine your demo sequence. Test your agent on the exact inputs you'll use. |
| 20 min | Partner practice: Full run-through (timed — 5 minutes hard stop) |
| 5 min | Debrief: One thing that went well. One thing to improve. |

**During work time:** Rotate through the room. For each student, ask: "Can you show me the first 30 seconds of your demo?"

This is your diagnostic. In 30 seconds, you'll know:
- Are they ready? (Rare — let them practice more)
- Do they know what they're doing but haven't practiced the words? (Most students — needs reps)
- Are they stuck on a technical issue? (Some students — fix it now or scope it down)
- Are they avoiding doing the demo because they don't believe in their work? (A few — needs confidence conversation, see Section 7)

**Timer for peer practice:** Use a visible timer for the partner run-through. 5 minutes is tighter than students expect. When the timer goes off, it's a natural teaching moment: "If you weren't done, that's good information — what needs to get cut?"

**Tech issues:** If a student encounters a bug or broken feature during this session, treat it as a scoping problem, not a crisis. See Section 5 for guidance on how to handle incomplete or broken functionality.

---

### Session 5: Dress Rehearsal

**Goal:** Full run-through under simulated Expo conditions. Identify last issues. Build confidence.

**Why it matters:** A dress rehearsal under mild pressure is dramatically more valuable than two more hours of solo practice. The goal is to surface anything that might go wrong in front of an audience.

#### Setup (5 min)

Rearrange the room to approximate Expo conditions — a "stage" area, an audience area. Tell students: "This is as close to the real thing as we're going to get today. The goal is not perfection — the goal is to find out what you still need to fix."

#### Rehearsal Format (40 min)

Break the class into groups of 4–5. Students present within their groups, not to the whole class. Each presenter gets:
- 5 minutes to present (hard stop)
- 2 minutes for feedback from group

**Feedback protocol for peer groups:**

> "Before we give feedback, presenter: What's one thing you want us to pay attention to?"

Then after the demo, peers respond:
- ⭐ **"The thing that landed for me was..."**
- 🤔 **"I was curious about..."**
- 💡 **"If I were an audience member who knew nothing, I'd want to know..."**

**What you're doing during rehearsal:** Float between groups. Watch for students who rush through their demo nervously, students who go over time without realizing it, and students who get flustered when something doesn't work as expected. These are the students to support individually before Expo Day.

#### Closing (5 min)

Full class: "Who had something go wrong during rehearsal? Raise your hand."

Normalize it. Then ask: "What did you do? What worked?" This is a crucial moment — sharing how to handle failure *before* it happens on Expo Day builds resilience.

Assign pre-Expo homework: Review your demo proposal one more time. Practice your opening line three times out loud before bed.

---

### Session 6: Expo Day

*See Section 4 (Expo Day Logistics) for full details on room setup, scheduling, guest management, and tech backup plans.*

**Teacher's core job on Expo Day:**
1. Keep the schedule running
2. Make every presenter feel seen and supported before they present
3. Deal calmly with anything that goes wrong
4. Be proud of your students (genuinely — they'll feel it)

**The 60-second check-in:** As students arrive, do a quick check-in with anyone who seemed shaky in the dress rehearsal. Not a coaching session — just: "How are you feeling? You've got this. I watched you nail that demo yesterday." That 60 seconds matters more than any last-minute instruction.

---

### Session 7: Reflection + Closing the Year

*See Section 8 (Post-Expo) for the full facilitation guide for this session.*

**Core goal:** Help students integrate the experience. Not just "what did you present" but "what did you learn about yourself as a builder?"

## 3. Project Scoping Guide

One of the most important skills you'll use in Sessions 1–3 is helping students scope their demo. You'll encounter two directions of misalignment:

---

### 3A: The "Too Big" Problem

**Signs of an over-scoped demo:**
- "I'm going to show every feature my agent has"
- "I want to demonstrate all 7 capability unlocks"
- "I'll walk them through the whole build process"
- The demo plan is 15+ steps long
- The student can't explain their demo in one sentence

**Why this happens:** Students are proud of their work and feel that showing less is unfair to what they built. They don't yet have the experience to know that a focused demo is more powerful than a comprehensive one.

**How to help:**

Ask: "If someone in the audience could only remember one thing about your agent, what would you want it to be?"

That's your anchor. Everything else in the demo should serve that one thing.

Then: "What's the most dramatic moment — when the audience will lean forward? Let's build the demo around that."

Useful framing: "The best demos don't show everything — they show the most important thing so clearly that the audience wants to see the rest."

**Specific techniques:**
- **The "one live demo" rule:** Aim for one clear, live input → response → discussion sequence. Additional demonstrations should be shorter and in service of the main one.
- **Cut to the interesting part:** If setup takes longer than 60 seconds, something needs to be pre-loaded or pre-explained.
- **Narrative coherence test:** After each planned segment, ask: "Why does this come next? How does it serve the story?"

---

### 3B: The "Too Small" Problem

**Signs of an under-scoped demo:**
- "I'm just going to show the chatbot part"
- "My agent doesn't do very much"
- "I don't think anyone will find this interesting"
- The student seems disengaged or apologetic about their work
- Demo plan is 2–3 steps, well under 5 minutes

**Why this happens:** Often this student has been comparing themselves to a classmate with a flashier or more technically complex agent. They may have incomplete capability unlocks (see Section 5) and feel embarrassed. Or they genuinely have a simpler agent that works well, but can't see its value.

**How to help:**

First: explore what they actually have. Ask: "Walk me through your agent. Just show me — I'll watch."

Often the agent is more interesting than the student thinks. Your genuine curiosity is the intervention: "Wait, it does *that*? How does that work?" The student starts explaining and realizes they know a lot.

Then: help them find the story. "Who would actually use this? Let's imagine that person. What problem do they have? Show me your agent solving it."

**Specific techniques:**
- **Find the edge case:** A small agent with one known interesting failure mode can be a great demo — show the edge case, explain why it happens, discuss what you'd do to fix it.
- **Zoom in on the design:** "Your agent does one thing really well. Let's make the demo about why that one thing is hard to do, and why your solution works."
- **Add the 'what I'd build next' layer:** If the actual demo is short, a strong "here's where I'd take this" segment adds substance and shows forward thinking.
- **Peer context:** "You're the only person in this class who built an agent for [domain]. That means you know things about this problem that no one else does. Tell the audience that."

---

### 3C: The "Wrong Thing" Problem

Sometimes a student has a demo plan that's neither too big nor too small, but they're planning to show the wrong thing — the part that looks impressive but doesn't reflect their actual learning, while hiding the part that's genuinely interesting.

**Signs:** The student is eager to demo a polished feature but reluctant to discuss the design decisions behind it. Or they want to skip over a module that was hard.

**How to help:** Ask them about the hard part. "What was the most difficult thing to figure out in this whole project?" Listen carefully. Then: "That's the most interesting part of your story. How do you tell the demo so that moment comes through?"

## 4. Expo Day Logistics

The AI Expo is a real event. It deserves real planning. Use this section as your production checklist.

---

### 4A: Room Setup Options

**Option 1: Science Fair / Gallery Walk Style (Recommended)**

- Students set up at individual stations (tables or desks)
- Guests circulate freely between stations
- Each student presents 3–4 times to small groups of 2–5 people
- Duration: 45–90 minutes of active presenting

*Pros:* Students get multiple reps; less performance pressure; guests can spend time at stations they find interesting; no single failure derails the whole event
*Cons:* Louder, harder to manage; students can feel like they're stuck at their station

**Setup:**
- [ ] Assign station locations in advance (post a map for students)
- [ ] Each station: one device, one chair for presenter, 2–3 chairs or standing space for guests
- [ ] Clear sightlines — presenter should be able to see the screen easily
- [ ] Power strips at every station or nearby (devices may run low over 90 min)
- [ ] Small table card with student name + agent name (print or index card is fine)

---

**Option 2: Presentation Stage Style**

- Students present one at a time to the full audience
- Each presenter gets 5 minutes + 2 minutes Q&A
- Audience includes peers, teacher, guests

*Pros:* More formal and celebratory; every student is seen by everyone; easier to manage
*Cons:* Higher individual pressure; an audience of middle schoolers can be restless during 7-minute slots; timing requires strong facilitation; one tech failure is very visible

**Setup:**
- [ ] Clear staging area at front of room (remove clutter)
- [ ] Projector or large screen + HDMI cable confirmed working
- [ ] Visible timer for presenters
- [ ] Seating for audience
- [ ] Presentation order posted visibly

---

**Option 3: Hybrid (Recommended for Mixed-Readiness Classes)**

- 10–15 min: Three selected students present to full audience (brief spotlight)
- 60 min: Gallery walk format for all students
- 10 min: Full class debrief + awards

This is often the best of both worlds — some students get the spotlight experience, everyone presents in the lower-stakes gallery context, and the event has a clear opening and closing.

---

### 4B: Guest Coordination

**Who to invite:**
- Other teachers (especially those who've heard about the project)
- School administrator (even for 15 minutes — the signal to students is significant)
- Parents/guardians (coordinate through school communication channels)
- Community members with relevant expertise (local tech professionals, college students, etc.)
- Students from other classes who might take this course next year

**Logistics for guests:**
- [ ] Send invitations 2 weeks out. Include: date, time, location, what they'll see, what their role is
- [ ] Explain that this is a student-led event — they're the audience, not the evaluators
- [ ] Distribute **Audience Evaluation Cards** (assessments/audience-evaluation-card.md) — simple 3-question feedback card guests fill out at each station
- [ ] Brief guests at arrival: "Ask questions, express genuine curiosity. Don't test students — be a learner."
- [ ] Prepare for last-minute cancellations. The event works fine with just the class if guests don't show.

**If you have very few guests:** That's okay. Tell students: "Real demos happen for audiences of one all the time. The goal is to communicate clearly — audience size doesn't change that." Peer audience is fully sufficient.

---

### 4C: Schedule Management

**Gallery Walk Format — Sample Schedule**

| Time | What's Happening |
|------|-----------------|
| T-15 min | Students set up stations. Teacher does final check-in with shaky presenters. |
| T-5 min | Guests arrive. Teacher briefs guests (2 min). Students take their stations. |
| T+0 (Opening) | Teacher welcome: 3 minutes, no longer. Acknowledge the year's work. Set expectations for guests. |
| T+3 to T+53 | Gallery walk — guests circulate freely. Teacher floats, watching for struggling students. |
| T+53 | 2-minute warning. Guests wrap up at current stations. |
| T+55 | Guests take seats. Brief student choice awards or shout-outs (see assessments/student-choice-awards.md). |
| T+70 | Thank guests. Students stay for debrief. |

**Stage Presentation Format — Sample Schedule**

| Time | What's Happening |
|------|-----------------|
| T-10 min | Room setup complete. Students in holding area (hallway or corner). |
| T-5 min | Guests and audience seated. |
| T+0 | Teacher introduction: 2 minutes |
| T+2 to T+[7×N] | Presentations, 7 min each (5 + 2 Q&A). Teacher manages time with visible timer. |
| Last 10 min | Awards, closing remarks, thank guests. |

**If you're running behind:** In gallery format, you can extend the circulating period. In stage format, cut Q&A from 2 to 1 minute and skip the last 1-minute extension in the teacher intro.

---

### 4D: Tech Backup Plans

Technology will fail. Plan for it.

**Before Expo Day — Checklist:**
- [ ] Every student has tested their demo on the exact device they'll use at Expo
- [ ] Backup screenshots or screen recordings of key moments in the demo (every student should have these)
- [ ] Charging cables at every station or full-charge policy morning of
- [ ] Know your school's Wi-Fi limitations — if 30 devices running AI agents hit the network at once, you may have slowdowns
- [ ] Have a hotspot available (your phone or a school device) as backup
- [ ] Know who to call for IT support and if they're available during the event

**In the moment — What to do:**

| Problem | Response |
|---------|---------|
| Student's device won't connect to Wi-Fi | Use hotspot; if that fails, switch to screenshots |
| Agent is returning errors / not responding | "Let me show you what it normally does" → screenshots/video backup |
| Projector dies (stage format) | Gather audience close to presenter's device. Keep going. |
| Student freezes / goes blank | Quietly stand near them. Say: "Tell them what your agent does." Start with that. |
| Demo crashes mid-presentation | Coach the student: "Explain what you were showing before the crash." Then: "Any questions about that?" |

**The key mindset:** A technical failure at the Expo is not a disaster — it's a real-world engineering experience. Students who handle it gracefully often make the strongest impression. Brief students on this *before* the Expo, not during.

**Script for students to have ready:** "This is actually a good example of something real AI systems deal with — let me tell you what should have happened, and why this kind of failure matters."

## 5. Handling Incomplete Agents

Not every student will arrive at Expo Day with all seven capability unlocks complete. Some will have missed sessions, struggled with technical modules, or run into bugs they couldn't resolve. This is normal, and it is not a failure — for them or for you.

---

### The Framing That Matters

The wrong framing: "Show everything you were supposed to build."
The right framing: **"Show what you built."**

These are different invitations. The first implies a deficit. The second invites ownership. Students who have built three out of seven capabilities have still built something real. Your job is to help them see that.

---

### Talking to Students With Incomplete Builds

**Before the Expo, in Session 2 or 3:**

> "Your agent has [X, Y, Z capabilities]. That's what you built. Let's figure out how to show those three things in a way that makes the audience understand what they're seeing and why it matters."

Avoid: "Even though you didn't finish..." or "I know you only got to Module 4..." — this centering of incompleteness undercuts the work they did finish.

**If a student is embarrassed:** "Do you know how your agent works? Can you explain it?" Almost always yes. "That's what we're showing. Someone who built and understands an agent with three capabilities is more impressive than someone who copy-pasted a seven-capability agent they don't understand."

---

### Demo Adjustments for Incomplete Builds

| Student's Situation | Demo Approach |
|---------------------|--------------|
| Completed M1–M3 (agent with name, perception, knowledge) | Demo can focus on what the agent *knows* and how you built that knowledge base. This is genuinely interesting and underappreciated. |
| Completed M1–M4 (above + trained model) | Demo the training loop — show what the agent learned from data. This is technically impressive even at this stage. |
| Completed M1–M5 but M6 is partial | Show the live channel connection — that's often the most visceral moment for an audience. Mention ethics work in progress as part of the story. |
| Completed M6 but some pieces are broken | Show what works. Acknowledge what doesn't. Explain why it broke. This is honest engineering. |
| Significant incomplete: Only M1–M2 | Focus on the *concept* — what did they set out to build, what did they learn, what would the full version do? Presentation of a design idea is a legitimate demo format. |

---

### Rubric Considerations for Incomplete Builds

See Section 6. The rubric does not require a complete seven-capability agent to earn full marks. It assesses communication, reflection, and understanding — all of which are fully available to students with partial builds.

## 6. Assessment Guidance

---

### 6A: The Capstone Rubric

The **AI Expo Presentation Rubric** (assessments/ai-expo-presentation-rubric.md) has five criteria:

| Criterion | What You're Assessing |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Agent Explanation** | Can the student explain what their agent does in terms a non-technical person understands? |
| **Live Demo Quality** | Is the demo clear, purposeful, and coherent — even if technical things go wrong? |
| **Reflective Thinking** | Can the student speak to what went well, what they'd change, and what they learned? |
| **Q&A Engagement** | Does the student engage genuinely with questions? (Not: does they have all the answers) |
| **Ownership + Pride** | Does the student communicate genuine investment in what they built? |

Notice what's **not** on this rubric: number of capabilities completed, technical sophistication, or whether the demo worked perfectly. This is intentional. You cannot fairly grade students on how complex their agent is — some students had far more prior experience, faster devices, or more homework time. You *can* assess how well they communicate and reflect.

---

### 6B: Using the Rubric During Live Presentations

**Practical setup:** Use a clipboard or tablet with a copy of the rubric for each student. In gallery walk format, visit each station for 5–7 minutes and score during that visit. In stage format, score during each presentation.

**Tips for reliable scoring:**
- Score each criterion immediately after the student finishes — don't wait until the end to score all students
- Focus on what you observe, not what you know about the student from prior modules
- If you can't score a criterion because the student rushed past it, leave it blank and follow up with a brief individual conversation
- Aim for consistency: read through all rubric descriptions at least once before Expo Day

**The "4" problem:** Be honest with your 4s (top marks). A 4 should represent something you couldn't have expected — a student who handled a technical failure with grace, gave a strikingly honest reflection, or fielded a hard question in a way that showed genuine depth. Most good presentations earn 3s. That's not a problem.

---

### 6C: Handling Live Demo Failures

A live demo failure is not an automatic grade reduction. What matters is *how the student responds*.

**During scoring:** If a student's demo fails live, note it on the rubric. Then observe: Does the student panic and shut down? Or do they transition gracefully, explain what should have happened, and keep the audience engaged? The latter is a professional skill. It deserves recognition.

**What to assess in a failed demo:**
- Did the student have a backup plan? (Using screenshots, pivoting to explanation)
- Did they communicate *why* the failure is interesting? ("This is actually a real bug I discovered last week...")
- Did they maintain composure and continue presenting?

**What to do if a student can't continue after a failure:** Step in briefly as moderator. "Let's take 30 seconds to reset — [Student], while we're waiting, can you tell us what you were showing?" This gives the student a verbal bridge while tech issues resolve.

**After the Expo:** If you suspect a demo failure significantly impacted a student's score, offer them a brief 10-minute follow-up conversation where they can walk you through what they were trying to show. This is not a re-do — it's completion of an interrupted assessment.

---

### 6D: Portfolio Assessment

In addition to the presentation rubric, students submit a **full-year portfolio** assessed by the **Full-Year Portfolio Rubric** (assessments/full-year-portfolio-rubric.md).

Key points for teachers:
- The portfolio is cumulative — it includes journal entries, reflections, and artifacts from all 7 modules
- Score it separately from the Expo presentation — they measure different things
- If a student has spotty journal completion from earlier modules, check in individually before final submission. Missing journal entries may reflect absences, not engagement failures.
- The **Final Reflection Prompts** (assessments/final-reflection-prompts.md) in Session 7 generate the best capstone journal material. Don't skip Session 7.

---

### 6E: Peer Evaluations and Audience Cards

**Peer Evaluation Forms** (assessments/peer-evaluation-form.md): These are formative and should not be averaged into the final grade. They are useful for:
- Student self-reflection ("What did my peers notice?")
- Your own sense of which presentations landed
- Identifying standout moments to highlight in Session 7

**Audience Evaluation Cards** (assessments/audience-evaluation-card.md): These are for guests and should be collected and shared with students after the Expo, not used for grading. Students almost universally appreciate guest feedback — it validates the work in a way teacher grades alone can't.

## 7. Differentiation Strategies

---

### 7A: ELL Students Presenting

English Language Learner students may face real challenges with a public presentation module — not because they don't understand their work, but because presenting in English to an audience is a high-stakes language performance.

**Before the Expo:**

- Allow ELL students to practice their opening line and key phrases with you or a partner multiple times
- If a student is significantly more comfortable in their home language, consider allowing them to present in their home language with a brief English summary — this is a legitimate scaffold, not a lesser demonstration
- Help them identify technical vocabulary they'll need and practice it ahead of time: "My agent can ___ because ___" sentence frames work well
- Connect with any language support staff at your school — they may be able to observe the dress rehearsal and offer coaching

**During the Expo:**

- Seat ELL students at a gallery walk station rather than giving a stage presentation if possible (lower pressure, more natural conversation)
- Brief guests: "This student is presenting in English as an additional language — be patient, ask simple follow-up questions, celebrate their work"
- If a student freezes due to language difficulty, you can model: "Let me start — [Student] built an agent that... can you tell them more?"

**Assessing ELL students:** You are assessing understanding and communication, not fluent English. If a student communicates their design thinking clearly with imperfect English grammar, that should earn full marks. If you have doubts about a student's understanding that the language barrier prevented them from demonstrating, use the follow-up conversation format (Section 6C) to clarify.

---

### 7B: Students With Stage Fright

Stage fright is real and should be taken seriously. It's not a character flaw or a failure to prepare — for some students, it's physiological, and dismissing it ("You'll be fine!") makes it worse.

**Identify early:** Look for students who:
- Avoid eye contact when you discuss the Expo
- Keep "forgetting" their demo plan
- Ask to go last or be excused
- Mention feeling sick during Sessions 4–5
- Had visible anxiety during the dress rehearsal

**What helps:**

*Before the Expo:*
- Give anxious students extra rehearsal reps in small, low-stakes settings (just you and them)
- Help them identify one specific thing to focus on instead of their anxiety: "Your job is to show three things. That's it."
- Let them stand, sit, or arrange their station however feels most comfortable
- Normalize the experience: "Every presenter feels nervous. Here's what professional presenters do..."

*On Expo Day:*
- Check in privately and warmly before they present
- Give them a "job" that anchors them: "Your job is to make the first person who comes to your station feel welcome."
- For gallery walk: allow them to start presenting to you privately before guests arrive
- For stage format: consider letting them go earlier in the order (not last — waiting is agony)

*During the presentation:*
- Make yourself visible in the audience — a friendly face helps
- If they freeze completely, see the facilitation tips in Section 4D

**What not to do:**
- Don't ask anxious students to "practice in front of the class" the day before — this is the worst version of the rehearsal pressure
- Don't offer to grade them differently if they're anxious — this tends to increase worry, not decrease it
- Don't broadcast their anxiety to the class

---

### 7C: Students Who Want to Go Deeper

Some students will have outpaced the module content entirely. Their agents are impressive, their demos are polished by Session 4, and they're looking for more.

**Options for extending:**

*Within the module:*
- Invite them to serve as a **peer consultant** during Sessions 4–5: they visit other students' stations during rehearsal and offer feedback (use the peer evaluation protocol from Session 5)
- Ask them to write a **"What I'd build next"** section in their Expo materials — a one-page design doc for version 2.0 of their agent
- Challenge them to make their demo accessible to a 4th-grade audience (explaining complex work simply is genuinely hard)

*Beyond the module:*
- Connect them with computer science electives, clubs, or camps
- Suggest contributing to open-source AI projects (many beginner-friendly options exist)
- Invite them to be a **peer mentor for next year's cohort** — explaining curriculum concepts to younger students is a meaningful experience
- If your school has a STEM showcase, science fair, or maker faire, support them in entering their project

**At the Expo:**
- Advanced students often attract longer conversations at their stations — be aware of their time
- Consider inviting a community expert (local tech professional, college student) who can have a genuinely substantive conversation with them about their work

---

### 7D: Students With IEPs / 504 Plans / Accessibility Needs

For students with documented accommodations, apply them consistently for the Expo presentation. Common accommodations and how they typically apply:

| Accommodation | Expo Application |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Extended time | Allow more time at station; score at the end of the extended period |
| Reduced audience | Gallery walk to 1–2 guests at a time rather than a group; or present to teacher + one trusted peer |
| Written alternative | Allow supplementary written explanation of demo; assess that alongside oral presentation |
| Assistive technology | Ensure their device setup supports any AT they use; test in advance |
| Noise sensitivity | Station at a quieter corner of room; noise-canceling option if available |

Consult with your special education coordinator for students whose accommodations may require more significant modification to the Expo format.

## 8. Post-Expo: Closing the Year

Session 7 is the final session of a 7-module curriculum. Do not rush it. This is where the learning lands.

---

### 8A: Collecting Journals and Portfolios

**Logistics:**
- Collect physical journals at the start of Session 7, or set a digital submission deadline the day after the Expo
- For digital portfolios: give students a checklist of what should be included (journal entries, agent screenshots, reflection pieces)
- Allow 1 extra day for students who were absent during the Expo

**For incomplete journals:** Don't grade the absence of entries harshly if you know a student struggled. Have a brief conversation: "What do you remember from that unit? Write that — your memory of learning it is part of the portfolio."

**Quick audit before grading:** Flip through all journals before Session 7 so you're not surprised. Students who are missing significant material should be flagged for a brief conversation during Session 7 work time.

---

### 8B: Sharing Standout Projects

In Session 7, take 15 minutes to share standout moments from the Expo — not just the most technically impressive work, but the most memorable *moments*:

- "The demo that surprised me most was..."
- "The question that stopped me in my tracks was..."
- "The agent I keep thinking about is..."
- "The student I saw grow the most this year..."

**Student choice awards:** Run the voting from assessments/student-choice-awards.md. These awards are student-chosen, not teacher-chosen — the results are often more thoughtful than you'd expect. Give out all awards with genuine celebration.

**Photo or video of student work:** If possible and with appropriate permissions, document standout demos. Students appreciate seeing their work documented. A simple slideshow of agent screenshots with captions can be a meaningful artifact for end-of-year communications.

---

### 8C: Debrief — The Curriculum Arc Reflection

Use the **Final Reflection Prompts** (assessments/final-reflection-prompts.md) for individual journal writing. Before students write, facilitate a brief class conversation:

**Whole-class discussion (10–15 min):**

1. "What was the hardest moment in this whole curriculum? What got you through it?"
2. "What's one thing you understand about AI now that you didn't understand in September?"
3. "If you could change one thing about your agent, what would it be? Why didn't you?"
4. "Do you think AI is going to change your life? How?"

These questions don't have right answers. Hold the space for genuinely different perspectives — some students will be excited about AI's future, others will be skeptical. Both are valid responses to a rigorous curriculum.

**Then: individual writing (15–20 min)**

Use the final reflection prompts for journal entry. This is the last journal entry of the year — remind students it's part of their portfolio.

---

### 8D: Closing Ritual

Find a way to mark the end. Some options that work well:

**The "One Word" closing:** Go around the room. Each student says one word that describes their year in this curriculum. No explanation required. Write them on the board. Read them aloud together.

**The "Message to Next Year" letter:** Students write one paragraph to next year's 7th graders: "Here's what I'd tell you about this class." Collect and actually share them with next year's students at the start of M1. Students love knowing their advice reached someone.

**The "I Built This" moment:** Pull up the AGENT_JOURNEY.md or equivalent and read the arc of what students built — M1 through M7 — together. Quietly. Let it land.

Whatever you choose: don't skip it. The year deserves a proper ending.

## 9. Cross-Curricular Connections

Module 7 is one of the most naturally cross-curricular modules in the curriculum. Use these connections to coordinate with colleagues and strengthen the Expo experience.

---

### 9A: ELA / Language Arts — Public Speaking

**Explicit connection:** The demo is a speech. It has structure, audience awareness, and a purpose.

**What ELA teachers can do before the Expo:**
- Teach the hook-body-close structure in ELA, then explicitly connect it to the demo: "The speech structure you're learning in ELA is exactly what you'll use for your AI demo."
- Practice open-ended Q&A skills — field hard questions, handle "I don't know," stay composed
- Work on active listening and peer feedback vocabulary (the warm/cool/wonder protocol used in Sessions 4–5 comes from ELA)

**Standards connections:**
- CCSS ELA SL.6-8.4: Present claims/findings, using appropriate eye contact, adequate volume, and clear pronunciation
- CCSS ELA SL.6-8.5: Include multimedia components in presentations
- CCSS ELA SL.6-8.6: Adapt speech to a variety of contexts and tasks

**Quick win:** Ask ELA teachers to give students one class period for demo practice in ELA — a different classroom audience before the Expo is extremely valuable.

---

### 9B: ELA — Expository Writing

The demo proposal (Session 2) and the final reflection (Session 7) are both expository writing tasks.

**Connection:** The demo proposal is a persuasive technical document. The final reflection is a personal essay with analytical elements. Both align with ELA writing standards.

**Standards connections:**
- CCSS ELA W.6-8.2: Write informative/explanatory texts
- CCSS ELA W.6-8.4: Produce clear and coherent writing appropriate to task, purpose, and audience

---

### 9C: Social Studies / Civics — Community Engagement

**Connection:** The AI Expo is a public event. Students are presenting to community members. They are participating in a form of civic discourse — sharing technical knowledge with non-expert stakeholders.

**What social studies teachers can connect:**
- Community engagement and civic participation
- How experts communicate with the public (science communication, public hearings, town halls)
- The role of transparency in technology governance — "who gets to know how the algorithm works?"

**Deeper extension:** Post-Expo, social studies teachers can facilitate a discussion: "What do you think community members have a right to know about AI systems that affect their lives? How does that connect to what you presented?"

---

### 9D: Math — Data Literacy

**Connection:** Students with trained models (M4) have worked with data, made decisions about training/test splits, and interpreted accuracy metrics. These are mathematical literacy skills.

**What math teachers can connect:**
- Percentage, ratio, proportion (accuracy rates, error rates)
- Reading data tables and interpreting results
- The concept of a model as a function: input → output
- Statistical thinking: what does 90% accuracy mean? What about the 10%?

---

### 9E: Science — Engineering Design Process

**Connection:** The entire curriculum — and especially M7's retrospective — mirrors the engineering design process: define, ideate, prototype, test, revise, present.

**What science teachers can connect:**
- Engineering design: How does iterative design apply to software?
- Empirical thinking: How did students test their agents? What would a controlled experiment look like?
- Science communication: How do scientists share their work at conferences?

**For schools with a NGSS-aligned science curriculum:** The AI Expo functions exactly like a scientific poster session or science fair — design a study, conduct it, present findings, field questions.

---

### 9F: Art / Media — Visual Communication

**Connection:** The design of the demo station, the quality of screenshots used as backup materials, and how students present themselves visually are all design decisions.

**What art/media teachers can connect:**
- Visual hierarchy: Does the station communicate clearly at a glance?
- Presentation design: How is information arranged in a screenshot or slide?
- Brand identity: How did students design their agent's personality and name? (M1)
