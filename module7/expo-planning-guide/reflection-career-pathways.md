# Reflection: Connecting the AI Expo to Next Steps and Career Pathways

**Module 7 | Teacher Resource**

*The reflection period is where the Expo becomes more than a performance — it becomes learning. This guide covers the post-Expo reflection lesson, journal prompts, and how to connect students' AI experience to real career pathways.*

---

## Why Reflection Matters

Students who demo without reflecting often leave the experience as a memory, not a lesson. The reflection is what turns "I showed my project" into "I understand what I built, what I learned, and where I want to go next."

The AI Expo reflection has three layers:
1. **Looking back** — What did I build? What did I learn?
2. **Looking in** — What am I good at? What do I want to get better at?
3. **Looking forward** — Where does this lead? What comes next?

---

## Lesson Plan: Post-Expo Reflection (50 minutes)

### Opening (5 minutes)

Begin with a quick temperature check. Ask students to raise their hand if:
- Their demo worked exactly as planned
- They got a question they didn't know how to answer
- They felt proud of at least one thing
- They learned something from watching another student's demo

*This gets them back in the Expo headspace without needing a long re-cap.*

---

### Part 1: Journal Reflection (20 minutes)

Students write independently in their Agent Design Journal. Use the prompts below.

**Final Reflection Prompts:**

```
AGENT DESIGN JOURNAL — FINAL REFLECTION
Module 7 | AI Expo

Your name: _______________________
Your agent's name: _______________________
Date: _______________________

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LOOKING BACK

1. What is your agent able to do right now? List the capabilities it actually has.




2. What was the hardest part of building it? How did you work through it?




3. What moment during the Expo felt best? What made it feel that way?




4. What question did a guest ask that made you think? How did you answer it?




━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LOOKING IN

5. What skill surprised you most — something you got better at that you didn't expect?




6. What would you do differently if you started Module 1 again today?




7. What does your agent get wrong? Be specific. Be honest.




━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LOOKING FORWARD

8. If you had one more month, what capability would you add? Why?




9. What would Module 8 be? What should this curriculum teach next?




10. Where do you want to go from here? (This can be about AI, or about anything else you discovered about yourself through this course.)




━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

---

### Part 2: Class Debrief (15 minutes)

After journal writing, facilitate a whole-class discussion. These questions work well:

**Debrief discussion prompts:**

> "What's one thing you saw in another student's agent that you thought: 'I wish I'd thought of that'?"

> "What's one thing your agent can do that you're genuinely proud of?"

> "Be honest: what does your agent still get wrong? What would it take to fix it?"

> "Did anything happen during the Expo that surprised you — a guest reaction, a question, a demo that didn't work?"

> "If someone your age asked you, 'What is AI, really?' — what would you say now that you couldn't say at the beginning of the year?"

---

### Part 3: Career Pathways (15 minutes)

This is the bridge between the course and the world beyond it. The goal is NOT to push students toward a tech career — it's to show them that the skills they built are real, valued, and connected to a wide range of futures.

#### What Students Now Know How To Do

*Read or project this list:*

> In this course, you learned to:
> - Give an AI system a defined personality and purpose
> - Teach a system to perceive images and interpret audio
> - Connect AI to a domain-specific knowledge base
> - Work with training data and fine-tuned models
> - Deploy an agent to a real communication channel
> - Write ethical guidelines and usage policies for AI systems
>
> These aren't toy skills. These are the actual skills of AI product teams.

---

#### Career Pathway Cards

*Print and distribute, or project one at a time. Discussion: "Which of these sounds interesting? Which ones surprise you?"*

---

**🧑‍💻 AI/ML Engineer**
*Build and train the models that power AI systems*
What they do: Write code that creates, trains, and deploys machine learning models. The people who built the models your agent uses.
Path from here: Math + CS in high school → CS or Data Science degree → entry-level ML engineering roles

---

**🎨 AI Product Designer (UX)**
*Design how people interact with AI*
What they do: Figure out how an AI system should look, feel, and behave. They care about whether it's easy to use, honest, and helpful.
Path from here: Art/design + technology → UX/product design programs → AI product teams at companies

---

**📊 Data Scientist**
*Find patterns and meaning in large datasets*
What they do: Clean, analyze, and interpret data to help organizations make decisions. Often work closely with ML engineers.
Path from here: Statistics + programming → Data science bootcamp or degree → industry, government, research

---

**⚖️ AI Ethics Researcher**
*Ensure AI systems are fair, honest, and don't cause harm*
What they do: Study how AI systems can go wrong. Write policies and guidelines. Advocate for responsible development.
Path from here: Philosophy, law, or social science + interest in technology → policy/ethics programs → AI labs, government, advocacy orgs

---

**🔬 AI Researcher**
*Push the frontier of what AI can do*
What they do: Run experiments, publish papers, and invent new techniques. Academic or industry research labs.
Path from here: Strong math + science → computer science graduate school → research positions

---

**🧑‍🏫 AI Educator**
*Teach others how to build and think about AI*
What they do: Exactly what your teacher is doing. Create curriculum, run workshops, help people understand AI.
Path from here: Interest in teaching + technical background → education degree or teaching credential → schools, libraries, nonprofits, companies

---

**📱 AI Product Manager**
*Decide what AI products get built and why*
What they do: Work between engineers, designers, and users. Figure out what to build and how to prioritize.
Path from here: Communication + analytical skills → business or CS → product management roles

---

**🔒 AI Safety Engineer**
*Make sure AI systems don't go wrong in dangerous ways*
What they do: Test AI systems for failure modes, build guardrails, research what happens when things go wrong.
Path from here: CS + security interest → specialized AI safety programs → AI safety orgs, research labs

---

**🏥 AI in [Your Field]**
*Apply AI to a domain you already care about*
What they do: Use AI tools in medicine, law, journalism, music, sports, agriculture, public health... anything.
Path from here: Combine your passion (medicine, art, law, etc.) with AI literacy → hybrid career roles

---

#### Discussion: What Calls to You?

Ask students: "Which of these 9 paths surprised you most? Which one sounds like something you could see yourself in — even 10 years from now?"

*Give 5 minutes for partner discussion, then 5 minutes for class share-out.*

---

### Closing: The "What's Next" Challenge (5 minutes)

Close the course with a challenge, not a grade:

> "You built something real. You've earned the right to call yourself an AI builder.
>
> Here's your next challenge — not for a grade, just for yourself:
>
> In the next month, teach one person what an AI agent is. Show them what you built. Explain how it works. You'll be surprised how much you remember — and how much you actually understand.
>
> That's it. Go be the person who knows about this stuff."

---

## Journaling as a Summative Assessment

Collect Agent Design Journals as your final summative artifact. When reviewing:

**Look for:**
- Honest reflection on limitations (not just "my agent was great")
- Specific examples (not vague descriptions)
- Evidence of growth over the 7-module arc
- Personal voice — the student's actual perspective, not just what they think you want to hear

**Avoid:**
- Penalizing technical incompleteness — a reflective, honest student who finished 4 modules is often showing more learning than a student who finished all 7 but reflected superficially
- Over-indexing on polish — reflection quality matters, not grammar and formatting
