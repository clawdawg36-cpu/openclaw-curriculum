# AI Expo: Complete Event Planning Guide

**Module 7 | Teacher Resource**
*The AI Expo is the capstone moment of your curriculum — a science-fair-style showcase where students present their AI agents to a real audience. This guide walks you through everything from initial planning to post-event reflection.*

---

## Part 1: Overview and Timeline

### What Is the AI Expo?

The AI Expo is a student-led showcase where each student (or team) presents their custom AI agent at a dedicated station — science-fair style. Guests rotate through stations, students demo their agents live, and a judging panel evaluates presentations. The event ends with awards, certificates, and a class reflection.

**Recommended Time Allocation:**

| Period | Focus |
|--------|-------|
| 1–2 class periods before Expo | Prep and rehearsal |
| 1 class period | Rehearsal day (peer feedback) |
| 1–2 class periods | **AI Expo Day** |
| 1 class period | Debrief, reflection, wrap-up |

**Total: 4–6 class periods for the full Module 7 arc**

---

### Planning Timeline

**4–6 Weeks Before the Expo**
- [ ] Set the date and secure the room
- [ ] Determine format: in-class only vs. school-community event
- [ ] Draft guest list (admin, parents, other classes)
- [ ] Begin student demo prep in class

**2–3 Weeks Before**
- [ ] Send invitations to guests (see `guest-coordination-checklist.md`)
- [ ] Confirm room setup requirements with facilities
- [ ] Recruit judges (ideally 2–3 for a class of 30)
- [ ] Assign student presentation time slots
- [ ] Order/prepare awards and certificate materials

**1 Week Before**
- [ ] Send reminder to guests with logistics details
- [ ] Print all materials (schedules, scoring sheets, ballots, certificates)
- [ ] Test all technology (projectors, laptops, WiFi, speakers)
- [ ] Brief judges on scoring criteria

**Day Before**
- [ ] Set up room if available (or plan for early morning setup)
- [ ] Prepare student name tags / station signs
- [ ] Prepare ballot boxes for People's Choice vote
- [ ] Double-check printed materials

**Day Of — Before Students Arrive**
- [ ] Open doors early (suggest 30 min before class)
- [ ] Students set up stations
- [ ] Judge orientation (15 min before Expo opens)
- [ ] Welcome guests as they arrive

**Day Of — During the Expo**
- [ ] Run through schedule (see `presentation-schedule-template.md`)
- [ ] Collect ballots during open gallery period
- [ ] Tabulate votes and judging scores
- [ ] Announce awards and celebrate!

**Week After**
- [ ] Reflection lesson (see `reflection-career-pathways.md`)
- [ ] Return certificates and awards
- [ ] Collect and archive Agent Design Journals as final summative artifacts

## Part 2: Room Setup

*Full details in `room-setup-diagram.md`. Summary below.*

**The Science-Fair Model:** Students stand at individual stations (tables or desks) arranged in a U-shape or grid. Guests rotate freely during open gallery time, visiting stations that interest them.

**What Each Station Needs:**
- Table space for a laptop/device
- Station number sign (printable)
- Student name card
- Printed one-page agent summary (optional but recommended)
- Power outlet access or charged battery
- Internet connection (WiFi or hotspot)

**Room Layout Options:**
- **U-shape:** Best for 10–15 stations. Guests move inside the U.
- **Grid (2 rows):** Best for 20–30 stations. Leave 5-foot aisles between rows.
- **Perimeter:** Stations around the walls. Good for small rooms with moveable furniture.

## Part 4: Judging and Awards

*Full judge guide and scoring sheets in `judging-panel-guide.md`.*

### Judging Panel Setup

- **Recommended:** 2–3 judges for a class of 30
- **Good judge candidates:** Another teacher, an administrator, a parent with tech background, a community professional, an older student (high school helper)
- **Judge orientation:** 15 minutes before Expo; brief them on rubric and calibration example

### Judging Criteria Summary

| Category | Points (out of 20) |
|----------|--------------------|
| Technical completeness | 6 pts |
| Clarity of explanation | 5 pts |
| Live demo quality | 4 pts |
| Reflection and honesty | 3 pts |
| Q&A responses | 2 pts |

### Awards Menu

Choose based on class size and culture:

- 🥇 **Best Overall AI Agent** — top judging score
- 🎯 **Best Demo** — most compelling live presentation
- 💡 **Most Creative Concept** — most inventive agent idea
- 🏆 **People's Choice Award** — anonymous class vote (see `student-choice-award.md`)
- 🌱 **Most Growth** — teacher's choice for biggest improvement across the course
- 🤝 **Best Q&A** — most thoughtful responses under pressure
- ⚖️ **Best Ethics Statement** — strongest AI usage policy (Module 6 callback)

> **Tip:** Err toward more awards, not fewer. The goal is to celebrate the full range of student work. Consider giving every student a personalized certificate acknowledging something specific they built.

## Part 5: Student Choice Award

*Full voting system in `student-choice-award.md`.*

Students vote anonymously for the People's Choice Award during the open gallery period. Paper ballots work best — they're fast, tangible, and avoid tech issues during a tech-heavy event.

## Troubleshooting

**A student's demo isn't working on Expo day:**
→ Every student should have a screenshot/video backup. Build this into prep week.

**A student is very nervous or refuses to present:**
→ Offer alternatives: a partner presents with them, they demo to you only, or they present to a small group of 2–3 classmates first.

**Tech failure during the open gallery:**
→ Pause the clock, help the student restart, don't stress. This is a learning environment.

**Judges disagree significantly on a score:**
→ Have judges briefly discuss and converge, or average the scores.

**A student doesn't finish their project:**
→ They still present. Incomplete work with honest reflection is valuable. Grade on what they have, not the delta to "perfect."
