# Optional: Opening the Expo to the Wider School Community

**Module 7 | Teacher Resource**

*The standard AI Expo is a classroom event. This guide shows you how to scale it — to the whole school, to families in an evening event, or even to the public. Each scale comes with new logistics and new rewards.*

---

## Why Go Bigger?

A community-facing Expo changes the stakes in the best way:
- Students know they're presenting to an unfamiliar, real-world audience
- The community sees what students are capable of — often a revelation
- It builds goodwill for your program and the school
- It models what public-facing STEM looks like for younger students

**The risk:** More logistics, more lead time, more things that can go wrong. Only expand if you have bandwidth and administrative support.

---

## Scale Options

### Scale 1: Whole-School Expo (In-School Hours)

*Invite all students and staff to visit during lunch or a dedicated period.*

**Additions to standard Expo:**
- [ ] Book a larger space (gym, cafeteria, library commons) instead of a classroom
- [ ] Coordinate with the principal for an all-school announcement / invitation
- [ ] Build a 30-minute "open gallery" window during lunch or a free period
- [ ] Brief all students on handling large, unfamiliar audiences

**What's different:**
- Younger students visiting will ask simpler questions — coach students to welcome this
- More traffic per station — students get more reps
- Greater visibility = more school-wide pride

**Coordination needed:**
- Principal approval: 3–4 weeks out
- Announcement: 1 week out (morning announcements, flyer, or digital sign)
- Space booking: 3–4 weeks out
- Supervision: at least 1 additional adult in the room per 50 visitors

---

### Scale 2: Family Evening Expo

*Host the Expo as an evening event so working parents can attend.*

**This is often the most impactful format — families are your biggest advocates.**

**Additions to standard Expo:**
- [ ] Book evening time slot (5:30–7:30 PM is typical)
- [ ] Reserve a larger or more formal space
- [ ] Coordinate with administration for building access after hours
- [ ] Send home formal invitations (printed flyer + digital)
- [ ] Plan for non-technical family members — have a "what is AI?" explainer at the welcome table
- [ ] Consider light refreshments (hugely increases attendance)

**Planning timeline (additional lead time needed):**

| Task | When |
|------|------|
| Principal/admin approval | 6–8 weeks out |
| Venue booking | 6–8 weeks out |
| Family invitations sent | 4 weeks out |
| RSVP deadline | 2 weeks out |
| Reminder to families | 1 week out |
| Day-of logistics finalized | 3 days out |

**Sample invitation:**

> **You're Invited to the [School Name] AI Expo**
> 
> Join us for an evening showcase of student-built AI agents!
>
> 📅 Date: [Date]
> ⏰ Time: [Time] (Doors open at [Time])
> 📍 Location: [Space, School Name, Address]
>
> Students in [Teacher Name]'s AI class have spent the semester building original AI agents — programs they designed, trained, and taught to communicate. On [Date], they'll present their work in a science-fair-style expo open to families, friends, and the community.
>
> No RSVP required, but [RSVP link or form] helps us plan!
>
> Light refreshments provided.

**Staffing for an evening event:**
- [ ] At least 2 additional adults to help manage the room
- [ ] Consider student helpers (former students or older siblings) as greeters
- [ ] One person dedicated to photography
- [ ] One person managing the ballot box and welcome table

---

### Scale 3: Community-Facing Public Event

*Open to the general public — local families, media, community organizations.*

**High-impact, high-effort. Recommend only with full admin support and time to execute.**

**Additions:**
- [ ] Formal press release or media pitch to local news
- [ ] Social media promotion (school channels + personal invitation to community members)
- [ ] District superintendent notification (if this is a flagship event)
- [ ] Community partner outreach (library, makerspace, tech company, university)
- [ ] Formal program / printed event booklet
- [ ] Signage and wayfinding for guests unfamiliar with the school
- [ ] Security/front office coordination for external visitors

**Community partners to consider:**
- Local public library (AI literacy audience)
- Nearby university CS or education department
- Local tech companies (potential sponsors or mentors)
- City/town government (STEM initiative connections)
- Local news outlet (education reporter)
- Coding bootcamp or maker organization

**Media pitch template:**

> Subject: Middle schoolers built AI agents — want a look?
>
> Hi [Reporter name],
>
> I teach [subject] at [School], and my students have spent the semester building their own AI agents from scratch — real systems with custom personalities, perception, and communication channels.
>
> On [Date], we're hosting a public AI Expo where they'll present their work to the community. It's a science-fair-style showcase — not a robotics competition, not a hackathon, but something more personal: a 12-year-old explaining to their grandparent how they built a chatbot that knows everything about [topic they chose].
>
> If you'd like to cover it, I'd love to have you. The Expo runs [time–time] at [location]. Let me know if you'd like a preview.

## Room/Space Planning for Larger Events

| Scale | Suggested Space | Notes |
|-------|----------------|-------|
| In-classroom | Classroom | See `room-setup-diagram.md` |
| Whole-school | Gym or cafeteria | Add row numbers and map at entry |
| Evening/family | Gym, library commons, or cafeteria | Formal signage, consider draped tables |
| Public event | Auditorium lobby, community center | Professional signage, media area |

**For large spaces:**
- Station spacing matters more — 6 feet between stations feels comfortable in a gym
- Add more wayfinding: numbered zones, printed maps at entry
- Acoustic challenges: large empty rooms are loud. Coach students to project their voice.
- Lighting: gym fluorescents are unflattering. If possible, borrow clip lights or event lighting.

---

## Crowd Management

**Estimated visitors by format:**

| Format | Estimated Visitors |
|--------|--------------------|
| Classroom (in-school only) | 20–50 |
| Whole-school (lunch period) | 100–300 |
| Evening family event | 50–150 |
| Public community event | 100–500+ |

**For crowds over 100:**
- Assign students "start times" — stagger arrival to avoid rush
- Designate entry and exit paths
- Have a volunteer at the door to manage flow
- Consider a "wait here" sign for busy stations

---

## Documentation at Scale

Larger events create more documentation opportunities:

- [ ] Invite yearbook/journalism student
- [ ] Set up a photo station (backdrop + ring light) for individual student portraits
- [ ] Record a highlight reel for school social channels (with consent)
- [ ] Capture testimonials from parents/guests: "What surprised you about what these students built?"

---

## Repeat and Iteration

If you run a whole-school or evening Expo once, you can iterate toward an annual tradition:

**Year 1:** In-classroom event. Low logistics, build confidence.
**Year 2:** Add families (evening or after-school).
**Year 3:** Community guests as judges. Local press invited.
**Year 4+:** Annual school event. Other teachers join. Student emcees.

The goal isn't to maximize audience from day one. It's to build a culture where student AI work is worth celebrating publicly — and where that's expected, not exceptional.
