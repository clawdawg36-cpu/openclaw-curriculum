## For the Teacher: Setting Up the Judging Panel

### How Many Judges?

| Class Size | Recommended Judges |
|------------|-------------------|
| Up to 15 students | 1–2 judges |
| 16–25 students | 2–3 judges |
| 26–35 students | 3–4 judges |
| 35+ students | 4 judges + 1 teacher |

*If you can't recruit enough external judges, you as the teacher can serve as a judge — just disclose this to students in advance.*

### Who Makes a Good Judge?

**Best options:**
- Another teacher (especially one who knows the students' work)
- A school administrator
- A parent with professional background (tech, education, or just good communication skills)
- A community professional (developer, designer, researcher)
- An older student from a high school CS or robotics program

**Things to avoid:**
- A sibling or close family member of a student (conflict of interest)
- Anyone unfamiliar with the event format who doesn't have time for orientation

### Judge Briefing Checklist

**Send to judges 1 week before:**
- [ ] Expo date, time, and location
- [ ] This judging guide (or a simplified version)
- [ ] Scoring rubric (below)
- [ ] Reminder: arrive 15 minutes before Expo opens for orientation

**At judge orientation (15 min before Expo opens):**
- [ ] Review rubric together
- [ ] Show a calibration example (if available — a student sample or video)
- [ ] Clarify: judges are evaluating presentation + reflection, not just technical complexity
- [ ] Assign judge numbers (if multiple judges)
- [ ] Distribute scoring sheets
- [ ] Answer questions

## Judge Orientation Script

*Read this aloud or send to judges in advance.*

---

**Welcome, and thank you for joining us as a judge!**

Today's AI Expo is a capstone event where students present AI agents they've built over the past [N weeks]. Your role is to visit each student's station during the open gallery period, watch a brief demo, ask 1–2 questions, and score the presentation using the rubric below.

**What to look for:**
- Can the student explain their agent clearly to someone who didn't build it?
- Does the demo actually work? Does it show what the student describes?
- Does the student reflect honestly on what their agent gets wrong or how they'd improve it?
- Do they answer your questions thoughtfully?

**What NOT to penalize:**
- Minor tech glitches (this is a live demo environment)
- An agent that isn't "impressive" by industry standards — these are middle schoolers
- Nervousness (note: student improves under Q&A pressure is positive)
- An unfinished project that the student can explain clearly

**Your goal as a judge is to celebrate and calibrate — not to gatekeep.**

Please visit every station. Spend 3–5 minutes at each one. You don't need to see the full demo — a 2-minute snapshot plus one question is enough to score fairly.

During deliberation, we'll compare scores and identify award winners together.

## Award Categories and Selection

### Award Categories

| Award | Basis |
|-------|-------|
| 🥇 Best Overall AI Agent | Highest total judge score |
| 🎯 Best Demo | Highest Category 3 score |
| 💡 Most Creative Concept | Judge consensus — most inventive idea |
| 🌱 Most Growth | Teacher's choice — biggest improvement arc |
| 🤝 Best Q&A | Highest Category 5 score (with Category 4 tiebreak) |
| ⚖️ Best Ethics Statement | Teacher's choice — strongest Agent Usage Policy from Module 6 |
| 🏆 People's Choice | Anonymous class vote (see `student-choice-award.md`) |

### Deliberation Process (10 minutes)

1. Judges compare total scores — note any large discrepancies (>3 points difference on same student)
2. Briefly discuss discrepancies — did one judge see a different demo? Adjust if appropriate.
3. Average scores for each student
4. Identify top 3 by total score → Best Overall winner + 2 runner-ups
5. Review per-category leaders for specific awards
6. Discuss "Most Creative" and any ties
7. Return to teacher with final list

### Tiebreaker
In case of a tie, judges discuss qualitatively — "who left the strongest impression?" The teacher has final say.
