# Workshop 7.3 — Build Your Demo Script + Slide Deck

**Module:** 7 — Capstone: AI Expo  
**Grade Level:** 6–10 (adaptable)  
**Duration:** 50–60 minutes  
**Session Type:** Production Workshop  
**Standards Alignment:** AI4K12 Five Big Ideas; CSTA K-12 CS Standards; ELA Speaking & Listening Standards

---

## Overview

Students have a storyboard (7.1) and a capability inventory (7.2). Now they write. This session is a production workshop — students write their demo script and build their slide deck outline, then exchange with a peer for structured feedback using the Warm/Cool/Wonder framework. The session ends with a teacher approval checkpoint. No one moves to Rehearsal Day (7.4) without a complete draft.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this workshop, students will be able to:

1. Write a complete, timed 5-minute demo script with narration for all four sections (Hook / Build / Demo / Reflect).
2. Create a slide deck outline (5–8 slides) that supports — not replaces — their spoken demo.
3. Give and receive structured feedback using the Warm/Cool/Wonder framework.
4. Receive and incorporate teacher approval or revision notes on their draft.

## Lesson Sequence

### Brief Launch: From Blueprint to Script (5 min)

**Teacher framing (not a long lecture — just orientation):**

"You have two documents: a storyboard and a capability inventory. Today you're turning those into your actual script — the words you'll say and the slides you'll show. This is a workshop — most of the session is work time. But there are two deadlines today: peer review and teacher approval. Neither happens without a draft."

**Quick Q&A check:**
- "Does everyone have their storyboard?"
- "Does everyone have their capability inventory?"
- "Does anyone have a Hook they've changed since 7.1?"

Spend 2 min answering any quick clarifying questions. Then: "Okay. Let's write."

---

### Demo Script Template Overview (5 min)

**Teacher walks through the template quickly — students have it in front of them:**

#### Demo Script Template

**Section 1: Hook (Target: 30 seconds | ~75 words spoken)**
> *What is your opening action? What does the audience see/hear in the first 30 seconds?*

```
[Action: describe what you do / what the agent does]
[Spoken words — what you say while it happens]
```

**Section 2: Build (Target: 2 minutes | ~250 words spoken)**
> *Walk through M1–M6 milestones. ~25–30 seconds per module. Keep it narrative, not technical.*

```
M1 — [What you designed / what it can do]
M2 — [Perception capability]
M3 — [Knowledge / domain]
M4 — [What training added]
M5 — [How it connects to the world]
M6 — [How you made it ethical / safe]
```

**Section 3: Demo (Target: 2 minutes | ~100 words spoken + live demo)**
> *Narrate as you go. Name each capability before you show it. Use specific, interesting inputs.*

```
Demo Moment 1: [Capability] — Input: [specific input] — Expected output: [what they'll see]
[Narration words]

Demo Moment 2: [Capability] — Input: [specific input] — Expected output: [what they'll see]
[Narration words]

Demo Moment 3: [Capability] — Input: [specific input] — Expected output: [what they'll see]
[Narration words]
```

**Section 4: Reflect (Target: 30 seconds | ~75 words spoken)**
> *One honest limitation. One thing you'd add. A confident closing statement.*

```
[Limitation — stated plainly, not apologetically]
[What you'd add or fix next]
[Closing statement: "I built this. It does X, Y, Z."]
```

### Work Time: Write Your Script (20 min)

**Format:** Independent work — teacher circulates

Students write their full draft script. Encourage them to write actual words, not just bullet points — this will become their rehearsal script in 7.4.

**Teacher circulation focus:**
- **First 7 min:** Check Hook drafts. Push for specific, action-oriented openings. "What does the audience see in the first 5 seconds?" If still too generic, prompt: "Start your script with your agent doing something — not with you introducing yourself."
- **Middle 7 min:** Check Build sections. Are they skipping modules? Are they too technical? Balance: technical enough to be real, accessible enough for a non-expert audience.
- **Last 6 min:** Check Demo sections. Are the inputs specific? Are they planning for things that are "Solid" in their inventory, not "Shaky"?

**Pacing prompt at 10 min:** "You should have your Hook and at least 2 Build modules written by now. Keep going."

---

### Slide Deck Outline (8 min)

**After script draft, students create a 5–8 slide outline:**

Remind students: slides support the demo, they don't replace it. The agent IS the demo. Slides give context, not content.

**Suggested slide structure:**

| Slide | Content |
|-------|---------|
| 1 | Agent name + tagline (shown AFTER the live hook, not before) |
| 2 | Your journey: M1–M6 visual timeline |
| 3 | [Optional] Highlight 1 capability with a screenshot or diagram |
| 4–6 | [Optional] One slide per demo moment (name of capability + what you're about to show) |
| 7 | Honest reflection: "What it does / What it doesn't yet" |
| 8 | Thank you + Q&A invite |

Students write their slide outline (slide titles + one-sentence description of content per slide). No actual slide building yet — just the plan.

---

### Peer Review: Warm/Cool/Wonder (8 min)

**Format:** Pairs exchange scripts and give structured feedback

**Pair assignment:** Teacher assigns pairs (mix strong/developing writers when possible).

**Warm/Cool/Wonder Framework:**
- **Warm (1–2 items):** Something specific that works well — name it exactly
- **Cool (1–2 items):** A specific suggestion for improvement — concrete, not vague
- **Wonder (1 item):** A question the script raised for you — something you wanted to know more about

**Process:**
1. Exchange scripts (paper) or share digital doc. (1 min)
2. Read partner's script silently. (3 min)
3. Write Warm/Cool/Wonder feedback on the feedback form. (2 min)
4. Verbally share feedback — partner listens first, then asks one follow-up question. (2 min)

**Teacher note:** Circulate during feedback exchange. Redirect feedback that's too vague ("it's good" or "I don't really have suggestions"). Push for specificity: "Tell them which sentence made you curious. What would you cut to make it tighter?"

---

### Teacher Approval Checkpoint (5 min)

**Format:** Quick 60-second teacher review per student (or table rounds)

Teacher reviews each draft against the approval checklist:

**Approval Checklist:**
- [ ] Hook opens with action, not introduction
- [ ] Build mentions all 6 modules (or explains any skips)
- [ ] Demo section lists 3 capabilities with specific inputs
- [ ] All 3 demo capabilities are rated "Solid" in the capability inventory
- [ ] Reflect includes at least one honest limitation (not apologetic)
- [ ] Total estimated time is 4:30–5:30 (not wildly over or under)

**Outcomes:**
- **Approved:** Student is cleared for Rehearsal Day (7.4). No further changes required before 7.4.
- **Revise — minor:** Teacher notes 1–2 specific changes. Student can complete between sessions.
- **Revise — major:** Student needs another work session. Teacher schedules support.

If the full checkpoint can't happen in 5 min in class, continue at the start of 7.4.

---

### Wrap-Up (2 min)

"You now have a draft script. Before Rehearsal Day, incorporate your peer feedback and any teacher notes. If you were approved, you're ready to rehearse. If you have revisions, complete them before next session."

**Journal prompt:**
> *Read your script out loud once tonight. How long does it take? What felt awkward to say? Note one thing you want to smooth out before Rehearsal Day.*

## Differentiation

### For students who need support:
- Provide sentence starters for each script section (e.g., "The first thing you'll see is...", "I started by building...", "One thing my agent doesn't do yet is...")
- Allow students to dictate their script to a partner who transcribes
- Offer a simplified template with shorter required sections

### For advanced students:
- Challenge: write the script for two different audiences (student peers vs. adult community members)
- Add a "What would Module 8 look like?" teaser at the end of the Reflect section
- Ask: "What's the most honest, most vulnerable thing you could say in your Reflect? Can you say that?"

### For students with incomplete agents:
- Help them write a script for a "portfolio presentation" format: "Here's what I built and what I learned, even though not everything is working."
- Focus on the strongest 2 capabilities (not 3)
- Emphasize: honest + proud is more powerful than apologetic + comprehensive

---

## Assessment

**Formative:** Approved draft script with all four sections complete.

**Peer feedback form** (Warm/Cool/Wonder) submitted at end of session.

**Journal prompt** read-aloud self-assessment for homework.

---

## Teacher Notes

- Budget extra time for students who come in without their 7.1 or 7.2 work. Have blank templates ready.
- Peer review quality varies widely. Model a Warm/Cool/Wonder example on the board before pairs begin.
- The approval checkpoint is important — students who skip straight to rehearsal with a weak script waste both their time and their partner's in 7.4.
- "Read it out loud" is the single most useful homework prompt you can give. Students almost always discover awkward phrasing they didn't notice when reading silently.
