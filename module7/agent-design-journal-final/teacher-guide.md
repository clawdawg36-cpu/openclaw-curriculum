# Teacher Guide — Final Journals & Portfolio Assessment
## Module 7 · Agent Design Curriculum

---

## Overview

The Module 7 journal entry is the capstone of the Agent Design Journal arc — a reflective writing practice students have maintained across all seven modules. This final entry is structured, honest, and deeply personal. It asks students to synthesize a year of building, struggling, and growing.

The portfolio that accompanies it is a **summative artifact**: a tangible, sharable record of the full arc from first-day assumptions to a functional, presented AI agent.

This guide covers:
1. How to collect final journals and portfolios
2. How to read and respond to them
3. How to use them as summative assessment artifacts
4. Suggestions for sharing with parents and families

---

## Part 1 — Collecting Journals and Portfolios

### Logistics

**Format options:**
- **Digital:** Google Docs/Drive folder, Notion portfolio, PDF export, or a class portfolio platform
- **Physical:** Printed and bound (even a folder with dividers works)
- **Mixed:** Digital journal + printed cover page + physical artifacts like expo materials

**Recommended collection method:**
- Require students to submit a PDF or shared Google Doc link containing:
  1. Portfolio cover page
  2. All 7 module artifacts (organized by module)
  3. Full-year portfolio checklist (self-rated)
  4. Final journal entry (Module 7)

**Deadline advice:**
- Give students the journal prompt at least 3–4 days before the due date — this isn't a one-sitting assignment
- Allow in-class time (at least one full period) for students to write the journal while you're available to answer questions

### What to Accept

Accept incomplete portfolios rather than no submission. A student with 5 out of 8 artifacts who writes an honest, thoughtful final journal tells you more than a student with a perfect portfolio and a three-sentence reflection.

Don't penalize for missing early artifacts that were lost — let the final journal address gaps honestly.

## Part 2 — How to Read Final Journals

### What to Look For

The six prompts are designed to surface different kinds of evidence:

| Prompt | What it reveals |
|--------|----------------|
| M1 comparison | Self-awareness of growth; ability to reflect on past thinking |
| Capability map | Technical understanding of their own agent; depth of build |
| Hardest moment | Resilience, problem-solving process, emotional honesty |
| What it gets wrong | Critical thinking; intellectual honesty about limitations |
| Two sentences | Synthesis and ownership — can they articulate what they built? |
| What's next | Motivation and forward-looking curiosity |

### Red Flags to Watch For

- **Vague answers:** "I learned a lot" with no specifics — probe in a conference
- **Skipped prompts:** Note which ones and why (some students freeze on the "what does it mean that YOU built it?" prompt — it's a big question)
- **Disconnection from M1:** Students who can't find their M1 entry or claim they don't remember it may need help locating earlier work
- **Overly polished / inauthentic:** If a journal reads like marketing copy, it may not be the student's genuine voice — a gentle conversation often opens things up

### Red Flags That Are Actually Good Signs

- Raw emotion about the hard moments → this is healthy and exactly what the prompt is designed to surface
- Frustration with what their agent can't do → this is critical thinking, not failure
- Uncertainty about what's next → intellectual honesty; far better than false confidence

## Part 3 — How to Respond

### Written Feedback

Keep written feedback on final journals **personal and specific.** Generic praise ("Great job!") lands flat after a year of real work.

**What to respond to:**
- Name the specific thing they said: *"When you wrote that the hardest moment was when the API broke the night before the expo — that's the kind of thing builders never forget."*
- Connect their M1 thinking to their final entry directly: *"You wrote in M1 that you thought AI was just autocomplete. Your two-sentence description of your agent proves that's not how you see it anymore."*
- Ask one follow-up question: *"You said you'd fix the context window issue first — how would you approach that?"*

**Avoid:**
- Commenting on grammar or polish (this is a reflective journal, not a writing test)
- Correcting technical details unless they're significantly wrong in a way that matters
- Comparing students to each other

### One-on-One Conferences (Optional but High-Value)

If time allows, a 5-minute check-in with each student after you've read their journal is worth it. Use it to:
- Acknowledge something specific they wrote
- Ask about something that stood out or seemed incomplete
- Ask: *"Is there anything you wanted to say in this journal that you didn't?"*

These conversations often surface things the journal didn't — and they matter to students more than any grade.

## Part 4 — Using the Portfolio as a Summative Artifact

### What the Portfolio Demonstrates

The full-year portfolio is evidence of:
- **Technical growth** (from concept to working agent)
- **Reflective practice** (seven journal entries tracking thinking over time)
- **Communication** (design brief, evaluation report, demo materials)
- **Persistence** (the hardest prompt — what they did when things broke)

It is not a test of perfection. It is a record of process.

### Summative Assessment Options

**Option A: Portfolio + Journal as the Summative Assessment**
Use the portfolio checklist as a base rubric (are the artifacts present? are they substantive?), and use the final journal as the primary quality indicator. A student with a complete portfolio and a shallow journal has done less work than a student with a gappy portfolio and a genuinely reflective journal.

**Option B: Portfolio Defense**
Have students present their portfolio in a 5–10 minute "defense" — walking through one artifact from each module and ending with their two sentences from Prompt 5. This works especially well if you held an AI Expo (students are already practiced at presenting to an audience).

**Option C: Self-Assessment as the Grade**
Use the portfolio checklist ratings as a starting point for a grade conversation. Have students defend their self-ratings. This teaches metacognition and accountability simultaneously.

### Suggested Rubric Dimensions (if grading)

| Dimension | Weight |
|-----------|--------|
| Completeness (artifacts present per checklist) | 25% |
| Depth of final journal reflection | 35% |
| Evidence of growth from M1 to M7 | 25% |
| Self-assessment honesty and accuracy | 15% |

## Part 5 — Sharing with Parents and Families

### Why Share

Most parents have no window into what happens in a CS or AI elective. The portfolio is a chance to show — in concrete, accessible terms — what their student spent a year building and thinking about.

### Options for Sharing

**Portfolio Night / Expo Extension:**
If you hosted an AI Expo, invite families to an open portfolio session where students walk parents through their portfolio in person. This is especially powerful when the student reads their two sentences aloud and explains the cover page tagline.

**Portfolio PDF to Families:**
With student permission, email portfolios home with a short note from you explaining what the portfolio represents. Include one line from the final journal that you think captures the student's year.

**Portfolio Day in Class:**
Have students share their portfolios with each other before final submission. A gallery walk format (portfolios open on desks, students circulate) lets students see the range of what was built and reflect on their own work in context.

### Note on Student Privacy

Some students will have written genuinely personal things in their journals — about struggles, frustration, family context that affected their build, etc. Always ask students before sharing specific journal passages with parents. The portfolio artifacts are generally fine to share; the journal is theirs.

---

## Closing Note for Teachers

This curriculum asked students to build something real, explain it to strangers, and reflect on what it means. The final journal is the last time they do that in this structure.

Read these journals like they matter — because they do. Students know when feedback is phoned in. A specific, human response to something they wrote is one of the things they'll remember years from now.

You built this curriculum year with them. The portfolio is their record. The final journal is their voice.
