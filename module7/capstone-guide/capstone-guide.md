# Module 7 Capstone Guide
## The AI Expo: Student Handbook

> **You built something real. Now you show it.**

Welcome to Module 7. This is the finish line — and also a celebration. You've spent six modules building a personal AI agent from scratch. The AI Expo is your chance to show the world what you made.

Read this guide carefully before anything else. It covers everything you need to know: what the Expo is, what you're presenting, how you'll be graded, and what to do when things go sideways (they might).

---

## 1. What Is the AI Expo?

Think science fair, but for AI agents.

The AI Expo is a live, public showcase where you present the personal agent you built during Modules 1–6. It's not a new project. It's not a test. It's a demonstration of work you already did.

You'll stand at your station with your laptop (and your Agent Design Journal). Visitors — classmates, teachers, and invited guests — will come by, watch your demo, and ask you questions. You get about 5 minutes per audience.

**The key idea:** You're not proving you can build AI. You already did that. You're showing what you built and reflecting honestly on it.

---

## 2. What You're Presenting: The 5-Minute Demo Format

Every presentation follows this structure. Nail it and you'll feel confident every time someone walks up.

### ⏱ The 5-Minute Breakdown

| Segment | Time | What You Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Hook** | 30 sec | Grab their attention. What does your agent do? Why does it matter? |
| **Build** | 2 min | Walk them through what you made — capabilities, design choices, what changed over time |
| **Live Demo** | 2 min | Show it working. Actually run it. Let them see it do something. |
| **Honest Reflection** | 30 sec | What went well? What broke? What would you do differently? |
| **Q&A** | 3 min | Answer questions. Engage. You know this better than anyone. |

### Tips for Each Segment

**Hook (30 sec):** Don't start with "Um, so I built an agent that..." Start with the thing it does. *"My agent helps me track what I owe people — it remembers, it reminds me, and it never forgets who paid."* One sentence. Make them curious.

**Build (2 min):** Tell the story of the build. You don't need to show code. Talk about what capabilities you unlocked and why you chose the path you did. Reference your Agent Design Journal — it's living proof of your decisions.

**Live Demo (2 min):** This is the money shot. Run it. Show the actual output. If your agent does five things, pick the most impressive two and demo those. Don't narrate through a slideshow — show the real thing.

**Honest Reflection (30 sec):** This is not a weakness. Saying "I tried to get it to do X and it kept failing because Y" shows you understand what you built at a deep level. Audiences respect honesty. Graders reward it.

**Q&A (3 min):** Listen to the question before you answer. It's okay to say "I'm not sure about that" or "I'd want to test that before answering." These are good answers.

## 3. What "Done" Looks Like

Your agent should have all **6 capability unlocks** from Modules 1–6:

1. **M1** — Basic agent loop (prompt → response → repeat)
2. **M2** — Memory: your agent remembers things across sessions
3. **M3** — Tools: your agent can do things (search, calculate, look things up)
4. **M4** — Personality: your agent has a consistent voice and style
5. **M5** — Judgment: your agent decides when to act vs. when to ask
6. **M6** — Integration: your agent connects to at least one real-world system

**What if some aren't complete?**

That's okay. Be honest about where you landed. The rubric rewards transparency. "I got through M5 but M6 integration was incomplete — here's why and here's what I'd do next" is a better answer than pretending you finished something you didn't.

The question isn't "did you finish everything?" The question is "do you understand what you built?"

## 4. Your Audience: Who Comes, What They Expect

The AI Expo is open to:
- Your classmates (also presenting — they'll rotate through)
- Your teacher(s) and graders
- Invited guests: other teachers, parents, school staff, maybe local professionals

**The audience is not all technical.** Most visitors won't know what a "context window" is. That's fine. Your job is to explain your agent to someone who doesn't code.

### Talking to Non-Technical People

- Use analogies. *"Memory in my agent works like a sticky note — it writes down key facts so it can refer to them later."*
- Show, don't tell. Don't describe what it does — run it and let them watch.
- Avoid jargon. If you say "API" or "LLM," pause and explain it in one sentence.
- Welcome dumb questions. There are none. Someone asking "but how does it actually know?" is the best conversation you'll have all day.

## 5. How You'll Be Graded

Your Expo presentation is assessed on **5 criteria**, each worth 20 points (100 total):

### The Rubric

| Criterion | What We're Looking For |
|---|---|
| **Technical Completeness** | How many capability unlocks does your agent have? Are they working? Do you understand how they work? |
| **Clarity of Explanation** | Can you explain your agent to someone who doesn't code? Is your demo easy to follow? |
| **Demo Execution** | Does the live demo work? Does it show off what your agent can actually do? |
| **Honest Reflection** | Do you know what went wrong? Do you know what you'd fix? Is your self-assessment accurate? |
| **Q&A Engagement** | Do you listen to questions? Do you answer them directly? Do you engage with curiosity? |

### Scoring Notes

- You don't need a perfect agent to score well. You need honest, clear communication about the agent you have.
- Demo failures don't automatically hurt your score — see Section 7.
- Your Agent Design Journal is referenced during grading. Bring it.

## 6. Your Agent Design Journal: Bring It

Your journal is your evidence.

Throughout Modules 1–6, you've been logging your decisions, experiments, and reflections in your Agent Design Journal. That journal shows the thinking behind your agent — the dead ends, the pivots, the "oh wait, that actually worked" moments.

At the Expo, your journal:
- Backs up your claims (*"I tried three different memory approaches — here's where I landed"*)
- Shows graders the process, not just the result
- Helps you remember details when you're nervous

**Bring your journal in whatever form you kept it** — paper, digital, Notion, Google Doc, anything. Have it open and ready.

## 7. What to Do If Your Demo Fails

It might. That's not a catastrophe.

Live demos fail. APIs timeout. The model returns something weird. Your laptop decides to update right then. These things happen to professional developers at product launches. They'll happen at a student expo.

### Before the Expo: Prep a Backup

Record a short video (even a screen recording on your phone works) of your agent doing its best stuff. Or take 2–3 screenshots of it working well. These are your insurance policy.

### If It Fails Live

1. **Stay calm.** Take a breath. Don't apologize excessively.
2. **Narrate what *should* be happening.** *"Normally it would respond with the reminder list here — let me show you what that looks like in my backup."*
3. **Switch to your backup.** Show the screenshot or video.
4. **Explain what you think went wrong.** This actually demonstrates understanding.

The rubric includes "demo execution" — but graders are watching *how you handle* a failure, not just whether it ran perfectly. Composure and honesty under pressure are skills. Show them.

## 8. Bonus Challenges

Finished early? Already confident? Here are optional ways to go further — these won't affect your base grade but are worth doing if you want the extra stretch.

### 🟡 Level Up Challenges

- **Multi-modal demo:** Have your agent respond to something other than text (an image, a voice note, a file)
- **Stress test:** Show it failing gracefully — deliberately give it a bad input and show how it handles edge cases
- **Chain of thought transparency:** Make the agent "explain its reasoning" aloud during the demo
- **Deploy it:** Have your agent running somewhere accessible — a URL, a phone, a shared channel

### 🔴 Advanced Challenges

- **Compare versions:** Show an early version and the final version side-by-side. Talk about what changed and why.
- **Teach someone:** During Q&A, offer to walk a non-technical visitor through *how they could build something like this* in 3 steps
- **Write a README:** Ship a proper README explaining your agent's design, capabilities, and known limitations — a real artifact
