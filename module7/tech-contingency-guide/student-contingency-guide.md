# Student Contingency Guide: When Your Live Demo Fails

> **The golden rule of tech demos:** Assume something will go wrong. Prepare anyway. Stay calm regardless.

Every professional developer has lived this moment — the demo that worked perfectly all week decides to break in front of an audience. It happens to engineers at Google, at Apple, at startups. It will probably happen to you too. That's not a failure of your project. It's just tech.

This guide is your backup plan. Read it before Expo day. Do the prep work. Then relax.

---

## Part 1: Record Your Agent Working (Before Expo Day)

The single best thing you can do is record a short video of your agent doing its thing. If everything goes sideways at the Expo, you can play the video.

### How to Record a 2-Minute Screen Capture

**On Mac:**
1. Press `Shift + Cmd + 5` to open Screenshot toolbar
2. Click **Record Entire Screen** or **Record Selected Portion**
3. Click **Record** to start
4. Run your agent through its best use case — show a real conversation, a real task, a real result
5. Press `Cmd + Ctrl + Esc` (or click the stop button in the menu bar) to stop
6. The recording saves automatically to your Desktop

**On Windows:**
1. Press `Windows + G` to open Xbox Game Bar
2. Click the **Record** button (circle icon) or press `Windows + Alt + R`
3. Run your agent demo
4. Press `Windows + Alt + R` again to stop
5. Recording saves to `C:\Users\[YourName]\Videos\Captures\`

**On Chromebook:**
1. Press `Ctrl + Shift + Show Windows key` (the key with a rectangle and two lines)
2. Select **Screen record**
3. Choose full screen or partial
4. Record your demo, then click the stop button

### What to Record in 2 Minutes

Your recording should tell a story:
- **0:00–0:15** — Briefly say what your agent does (speak out loud, you're narrating)
- **0:15–1:30** — Show a real task your agent handles well. Type the prompt, wait, show the response.
- **1:30–2:00** — Show a second example or an interesting edge case

Keep it under 2 minutes. Judges and visitors have short attention spans.

### Where to Save Your Recording

Save it in **at least two places**:
1. **Google Drive or iCloud** — so you can access it from any device
2. **A USB drive** — offline backup in case internet is down at the Expo
3. Optionally: send yourself the file via email or iMessage as a third backup

**Name it clearly:** `[YourName]-[AgentName]-demo.mp4`  
Example: `maya-weather-agent-demo.mp4`

## Part 2: Screenshot Your Agent's Best Moments

A great screenshot gallery tells the story even when the video won't play. Screenshots are fast to open, easy to show on a phone, and never buffer.

### How to Take Screenshots

**Mac:** `Shift + Cmd + 4` → drag to select area → releases and saves to Desktop  
**Windows:** `Windows + Shift + S` → drag to select → pastes to clipboard (open Paint or Photos to save)  
**Chromebook:** `Ctrl + Shift + Show Windows` → drag to select

### Which 3–5 Moments to Capture

Pick screenshots that show:

1. **The "wow" response** — your agent doing something impressive or surprising
2. **A real conversation** — the agent asking a follow-up question or showing understanding
3. **A task completed** — the agent finishing something useful (a reminder set, a message sent, an answer given)
4. **An interesting failure handled well** — your agent saying "I'm not sure, but..." is actually impressive
5. **The setup/config** — the OpenClaw interface showing your agent is real and running

### Save and Label Your Screenshots

Put all screenshots in a folder named `expo-backup/` on your Desktop.  
Upload the folder to Google Drive before Expo day.

## Part 3: The Narrated Walkthrough (Your Ultimate Fallback)

If your video won't play and your screenshots won't load... you still have this.

A narrated walkthrough means you *describe* what your agent would show, as if you're a tour guide walking someone through an invisible museum. It sounds scary. It's actually pretty powerful.

### How to Prepare Your Narrated Walkthrough

Write out a 2-minute script in advance. Answer these questions:

1. **What does your agent do?**  
   "My agent helps you track homework due dates by texting reminders."

2. **How does a user talk to it?**  
   "You'd open iMessage and send it something like: 'Remind me about my history essay on Friday at 7pm.'"

3. **What does the agent do with that?**  
   "It parses the time, stores it, and sends a reminder message at exactly that time."

4. **What's the most impressive thing it does?**  
   "It can handle natural language — you don't have to use any special format."

5. **What would you show if it were working right now?**  
   "I'd show you a real conversation where I asked it three different reminders and it handled all of them correctly."

6. **What did you learn building it?**  
   "I learned how OpenClaw routes messages, and I had to debug why my agent was parsing 'Friday' as the wrong date."

Practice saying this out loud. Time yourself. You want 90–120 seconds of confident narration.

## Part 4: Handling a Failure Gracefully in Front of an Audience

This is the most important section. Read it twice.

### What to Say When Something Breaks

**Don't say:**
- "It was working five minutes ago!" (sounds like an excuse)
- "I don't know why this is happening." (sounds unprepared)
- "This always happens." (sounds like you don't care)

**Do say:**
- "Technology being unpredictable is part of working with AI — let me show you my backup."
- "This is actually a great example of why engineers test in different environments."
- "I have a recording of this running perfectly yesterday — let me pull that up."
- "Let me walk you through what it does, and you can imagine watching it live."

### How to Stay Composed

1. **Breathe.** One slow breath before you speak. It actually works.
2. **Smile.** Not a fake smile — just a relaxed "I got this" expression. It signals confidence.
3. **Don't apologize excessively.** One "sorry about the glitch" is fine. Repeated apologies make judges nervous.
4. **Pivot fast.** Move to your backup plan within 30 seconds. Don't spend 3 minutes trying to fix it live.
5. **Keep talking.** Silence is the real killer. Fill it with your narration.

### Why This Is Actually a Good Reflection Opportunity

Here's the truth: judges at tech showcases see a lot of demos that work. They remember the students who handle failure well.

When you stay calm, pivot to a backup, and explain your project clearly without the tech working — you're demonstrating:
- **Professional maturity** (real engineers do this)
- **Deep understanding of your project** (you can explain it without hiding behind the tool)
- **Communication skills** (you didn't freeze or panic)
- **Resilience** (you prepared for this moment)

A broken demo handled with grace is more impressive than a perfect demo where the student just clicks buttons.

After the Expo, write a short reflection: *What went wrong? Why? What would you do differently?* That reflection is worth more than a flawless demo.

## Pre-Expo Checklist (Do This the Day Before)

- [ ] Record a 2-minute demo video of your agent working
- [ ] Save video to Google Drive AND a USB drive
- [ ] Take 3–5 screenshots of your best moments
- [ ] Save screenshots to `expo-backup/` folder on Desktop + Google Drive
- [ ] Practice your 2-minute narrated walkthrough out loud
- [ ] Test your agent one more time on the actual device you'll use at the Expo
- [ ] Know the WiFi password for the Expo venue (ask your teacher)
- [ ] Charge your device the night before
- [ ] Have your Google Drive link written down somewhere offline

---

> **Remember:** The Expo isn't just about whether your agent works. It's about whether *you* can explain what you built, why it matters, and what you learned. No tech failure can take that away from you.
