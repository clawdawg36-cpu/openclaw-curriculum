# Script: How to Pitch Your AI Project
**Module 7 — Online Tutorial**
**Target duration:** ~5 minutes (300 seconds)
**Format:** Narrated screen-recorded presentation with on-screen graphics and agent demo inserts

---

## [00:00–00:30] INTRO — The Hook

*[Narrator speaks over a title card: a student at a laptop, an audience of blurry faces in the background]*

> "Imagine this: you've spent weeks building something. An AI agent with a name, a personality, real capabilities. It can see images. It knows things. It responds. You made that."

> "Now you're standing in front of an audience — classmates, teachers, maybe parents — and you have five minutes to make them care about what you built."

> "This tutorial is about how to do exactly that. Not just how to *show* your agent — how to make your audience actually feel it."

**[On-screen text]:** "How to Pitch Your AI Project"
**[Subtitle]:** "The Hook → Build → Demo → Reflect structure"

---

## [00:30–01:00] THE STRUCTURE — Hook, Build, Demo, Reflect

*[Animated timeline bar appears with four labeled segments]*

> "A great AI demo has four parts. We call them: Hook, Build, Demo, and Reflect."

> "**Hook** — 30 seconds. Start with something surprising your agent can do. Not a slide. Not an introduction. A *moment*."

> "**Build** — 2 minutes. Walk your audience through the journey: what you added in each module. Give them context — but keep it fast."

> "**Demo** — 2 minutes. Live. Real inputs. Real outputs. Show, don't tell."

> "**Reflect** — 30 seconds. Be honest. What does it get wrong? What would you change? This is the part most people skip — and it's actually the most powerful."

**[On-screen timeline bar]:**
- Hook: 0:00–0:30
- Build: 0:30–2:30
- Demo: 2:30–4:30
- Reflect: 4:30–5:00

---

## [01:00–01:30] THE HOOK — Show Don't Tell

*[Visual: two contrasting openers — a "bad" slide-heavy intro vs. a live demo moment]*

> "Most pitches start with something like: 'Hi, I'm [name], and I built an AI assistant called Zara. Today I'll be telling you about it.'"

> "Don't do that."

> "Start with your agent *doing something*. Open your laptop, type a message, and let the audience watch the response appear. Or show a photo to your agent and let it describe what it sees — out loud, in real time."

> "Your hook should make someone in the audience think: *Wait, that's real? The student built that?*"

> "Show, don't tell. Every time you say 'my agent can do X,' you've missed an opportunity to just *show X*."

**[On-screen callout]:** "Show, don't tell — every time."

---

## [01:30–02:30] THE BUILD — Telling Your Agent's Story

*[Visual: module progress tracker — each module lighting up as narrator speaks]*

> "After your hook, give your audience the journey."

> "You don't need to explain every technical detail. You need to give them the arc: *where your agent started and what it became.*"

> "Something like this:"

> "*'In Module 1, my agent got a name and a personality — she's called Zara, and she's designed to help students with homework questions. In Module 2, I gave her the ability to read images — so if you take a photo of a math problem, she can see it. In Module 3, I trained her on a knowledge base — she now knows things specific to my school's curriculum. By Module 5, she was connected to a real messaging channel, and my classmates could actually text her.'"*

> "That's two minutes of narration that tells a story. Not a list of features. A journey."

> "Focus on what changed at each step, and why you made the choices you made. The story of *why* is more interesting than the story of *what*."

**[On-screen text]:** "The build is a story of decisions, not features."

---

## [02:30–03:15] THE DEMO — Live and Real

*[Visual: screen recording of a live agent demo — student types a real question, agent responds]*

> "Now: the live demo."

> "Two minutes. Show at least three capabilities. Use real inputs — not pre-written examples you memorized. The audience can feel the difference."

> "Talk while your agent responds. Explain what's happening:"

> "*'I'm going to send her an image of this math problem. Watch — she'll identify it as an algebra equation and walk me through the steps.'*"

> "If something interesting happens — an unexpected response, a funny output — lean into it. That spontaneity is what makes live demos memorable."

> "**What NOT to do:**"

> "Don't narrate a slide deck of screenshots. Don't say 'here's a video of it working.' Do the real thing, live, in front of the audience. That's what makes it impressive."

**[On-screen callout]:** "Real inputs. Real outputs. No pre-recorded substitutes — unless your backup plan activates."

---

## [03:15–03:45] WHEN LIVE DEMOS FAIL — Your Backup Plan

*[Visual: a laptop showing a spinning loading indicator, then a cut to a calm presenter holding a tablet]*

> "Here's a truth every engineer knows: live demos fail. Wi-Fi drops. APIs time out. The thing that worked perfectly in rehearsal breaks in front of an audience."

> "So you prepare."

> "**Before your expo, record a backup video** of your agent working. A 60-second screen recording of it doing its three key capabilities. Save it offline — not in the cloud."

> "Also take **three or four screenshots** of your best moments. Your agent identifying an image. Your agent responding to a real question. Paste them into a simple slide deck."

> "If your live demo fails, stay calm. Say this:"

> "*'My internet seems to be having trouble — let me show you the backup video I prepared. This is from yesterday's rehearsal and shows exactly the same thing.'*"

> "Then show the video. Your audience will respect the preparation more than they would have respected a perfect live demo."

> "**The rule:** Never let a tech failure become a presentation failure. Prepare your fallback. Use it without apology."

**[On-screen callout]:** "Backup plan = backup video + screenshots + calm delivery"

---

## [03:45–04:20] REFLECT — The Honest Part

*[Visual: student presenter, audience asking questions]*

> "The last 30 seconds of your demo is the most important 30 seconds."

> "This is where you reflect — honestly — on what your agent gets wrong."

> "Here's the thing: the audience already knows AI isn't perfect. What they want to see is whether *you* know it. Whether you built this thing with open eyes."

> "Something like:"

> "*'The part I'd change is this: right now, my agent sometimes gives overconfident answers to questions it doesn't really know. I'm proud of what it can do, but I haven't fully solved that problem yet.'*"

> "That honesty? It makes your whole presentation more credible. It shows you're not just presenting a product — you're a builder who understands their own work."

> "And then there's Q&A. You'll get hard questions. Someone will ask: 'What if someone misuses it?' Or 'What are its limits?' Or 'Did it ever say something wrong?'"

> "Answer honestly. You're not selling this. You built it. You're explaining it."

**[On-screen callout]:** "Honest reflection = the most credible part of your pitch."

---

## [04:20–04:45] HANDLING TOUGH QUESTIONS

*[Visual: Q&A card with example questions and bullet responses]*

> "Here's how to handle the questions that feel hard."

> "**'What does your agent get wrong?'** → Tell them something real. Not the worst thing — but something true. Don't say 'it's pretty good.' Say what you'd actually fix."

> "**'Could someone use this for cheating? Or harm?'** → Acknowledge the concern. Explain the guardrails you added in Module 6. If you didn't fully solve the problem, say so."

> "**'How does AI actually work?'** → Give your honest answer, including what you still don't fully understand. 'I understand the general principles, but some of the math is still pretty complex — what I do understand is how to direct it and what it can do.'"

> "The through-line: you are allowed to not know everything. You built something. That's real. Own what you know, acknowledge what you don't."

**[On-screen text]:** "You don't have to know everything. You built something real — that's enough."

**Production notes:**
- Narrator pace: ~120–130 words/minute (energetic but not rushed — this is a how-to, not a lecture)
- Total word count: ~1,000 words → ~7–8 minutes raw; cut for pacing to hit 5 minutes
- Use a student-facing, second-person voice throughout — address the viewer as "you"
- Demo insert (Scene 04): record a sample agent demo in advance; use blurred/anonymized student agent if possible
- Agent in all examples: named "Zara" for consistency — swap out if a different name is established in curriculum
- Allow 1–2 second pauses after each structure label (Hook / Build / Demo / Reflect) for cognitive anchoring
- Background music: light, slightly energetic — this is a "you've got this" tutorial, not a somber one
