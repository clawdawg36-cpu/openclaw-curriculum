> 🎯 **Purpose:** After your demo, you'll take questions. Most of them will be easy. But some will be hard — and those are the ones that matter most. This card gives you 10 tough questions you're likely to face, with coaching on how to answer them *honestly and confidently.*

> **The golden rule:** Don't fake it. Audiences can tell when you're dodging. Saying "I don't know" or "good question, I hadn't thought about that" is *stronger* than making something up.

---

## How to Use This Card

Before the Expo:
1. Read all 10 questions.
2. Write your personal answer in the space below each one.
3. Practice saying your answers out loud — they should feel natural, not rehearsed.

During Q&A:
- Take a breath before answering. It's okay to pause.
- Answer honestly, even if your answer is "I'm not sure."
- If you don't know, say so — then say what you'd *look into* to find out.

## The 10 Tough Questions

---

### Q1: "What does your agent get wrong?"

**Why they ask this:** They want to know if you're honest about your work. A builder who claims their tool is perfect isn't trustworthy.

**How to answer well:**
- Name one specific, real failure — not a vague "sometimes it makes mistakes."
- Explain *why* it happens (technical reason, data gap, design choice).
- Tell them what you'd do to fix it.

**My answer:**
> My agent struggles with _______________ because _______________.  
> I'd fix this by _______________.

---

### Q2: "Who could be harmed by your agent?"

**Why they ask this:** AI systems — even small ones — can hurt real people if they're not designed carefully. This question tests your ethical thinking.

**How to answer well:**
- Take it seriously. Think about: who uses this? What could go wrong?
- Name at least one realistic scenario where someone could be harmed.
- Explain what you built (or would build) to prevent that.

**My answer:**
> One way someone could be harmed is _______________. This could happen if _______________.  
> To prevent this, I _______________.

---

### Q3: "Would you trust your agent with your own personal data?"

**Why they ask this:** It's easy to build something and not think about the privacy implications. This question asks you to put yourself in the user's shoes.

**How to answer well:**
- Be honest. Would you? Why or why not?
- What data does your agent have access to?
- What safeguards (if any) did you put in place?
- What would you add before you'd feel comfortable?

**My answer:**
> Honestly, I would / wouldn't fully trust it with my own data because _______________.  
> The safeguards I built are _______________.  
> Before I'd fully trust it, I'd want to add _______________.

---

### Q4: "What happens when your agent is wrong? How does a user know?"

**Why they ask this:** Confident-sounding wrong answers are one of AI's biggest problems. If your agent sounds certain about false information, that's dangerous.

**How to answer well:**
- Does your agent ever say "I'm not sure"? When?
- Does it tell users where it got its information?
- What would you add to make it clearer when the agent is uncertain?

**My answer:**
> When my agent is wrong, it _______________. Here's an example: _______________.  
> What I'd add to make uncertainty clearer: _______________.

---

### Q5: "Could someone use your agent for something you didn't intend?"

**Why they ask this:** Misuse is real. Every tool can be used in ways its creator didn't expect — including AI tools.

**How to answer well:**
- Think creatively: what are the unintended uses? (Even if they seem unlikely.)
- Explain what guardrails you built in Module 6 to prevent misuse.
- What would you add if you found out someone was misusing it?

**My answer:**
> Someone could potentially misuse my agent by _______________. 
> My guardrails prevent this by _______________. 
> If I discovered misuse, I would _______________.

---

### Q6: "How is your agent different from just using ChatGPT?"

**Why they ask this:** This tests whether you understand what you actually built. If your agent is just "ChatGPT with a name," that's worth knowing.

**How to answer well:**
- Name specific things your agent does that generic AI doesn't (custom knowledge, training data, specific channel, guardrails).
- Be honest if your agent is heavily based on a general model — explain what you added on top.

**My answer:**
> What makes my agent different from a generic AI is _______________.  
> Specifically, I added _______________ which you can't get from a basic AI.

---

### Q7: "What was the hardest part to build? Why?"

**Why they ask this:** This is a technical curiosity question — they want to understand your process and what you actually struggled with.

**How to answer well:**
- Pick the thing that was genuinely hardest, not the thing that sounds most impressive.
- Explain what made it hard (technically, conceptually, or practically).
- What did you learn from struggling through it?

**My answer:**
> The hardest part was _______________ because _______________.  
> What I learned from working through it: _______________.

---

### Q8: "How do you know your agent is actually doing what you think it's doing?"

**Why they ask this:** Testing and verification are core engineering skills. This question probes whether you just *hoped* it worked or actually *checked*.

**How to answer well:**
- Describe how you tested your agent (what inputs, what you checked for).
- Were there surprises when you tested? What did you find?
- What testing would you want to add before a real deployment?

**My answer:**
> I tested my agent by _______________. Some things I found when testing: _______________.  
> Testing I'd add before a real deployment: _______________.

---

### Q9: "If you were going to deploy this for real, what would you have to change?"

**Why they ask this:** Production deployment is very different from a school project. This question tests your understanding of the gap between "works in a demo" and "ready for the world."

**How to answer well:**
- Think about: security, scale, maintenance, privacy, cost, edge cases.
- What's the gap between your current version and something you'd actually publish?
- Be specific — don't just say "a lot more work."

**My answer:**
> Before a real deployment, I'd need to _______________.  
> The biggest gaps between my demo and production: _______________.

---

### Q10: "What did this project change about how you think about AI?"

**Why they ask this:** This is a reflection question. They want to know what you genuinely learned — not just technically, but intellectually or ethically.

**How to answer well:**
- Answer honestly. What surprised you? What made you think differently?
- "I used to think X, but now I think Y" is a great format.
- Don't give a generic "AI is amazing!" answer — go somewhere real.

**My answer:**
> Before building this, I thought _______________.  
> After building it, I now think _______________.  
> The thing that surprised me most was _______________.

---

## Bonus: If You Draw a Complete Blank

It's okay to say:
- *"I hadn't thought about it that way — give me a second."*
- *"That's a really good question. Honestly, I don't know yet — but here's what I'd look into..."*
- *"That's something I'd want to think more about. My first instinct is..."*

These are **strong answers.** They show self-awareness and intellectual honesty. The weakest move is to confidently make something up.
