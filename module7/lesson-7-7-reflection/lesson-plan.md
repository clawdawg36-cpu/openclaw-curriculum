# Lesson 7.7 — Reflection + What Comes Next

**Module:** 7 — Capstone: AI Expo  
**Grade Level:** 6–10 (adaptable)  
**Duration:** 50–60 minutes  
**Session Type:** Closing Lesson / Celebration  
**Standards Alignment:** AI4K12 Five Big Ideas; CSTA K-12 CS Standards; ELA Writing Standards

---

## Overview

The Expo is over. This is the last lesson. It is a session for honest reflection, genuine celebration, and forward-looking conversation. Students complete their final portfolio journal entry, debrief as a class ("What would Module 8 be?"), explore career pathways in AI, and close the curriculum with a sense of their own identity as builders. The tone is reflective and warm — not rushed.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Write a complete, reflective final portfolio journal entry that captures their arc from M1 to M7.
2. Articulate what they learned across the full curriculum — in their own words, not the vocabulary of the modules.
3. Name at least one thing that surprised them about building an AI agent.
4. Describe at least one career pathway or future project that connects to what they've learned.
5. Celebrate themselves and their peers for completing something genuinely hard.

## Lesson Sequence

### Opening: The Day After (5 min)

**Teacher opens by creating space:**

"You presented yesterday. How are you feeling today?"

Quick popcorn around the room — one word each:
- Energized? Relieved? Proud? Tired? Surprised?
- Name the feeling, no explanation needed.

**Teacher shares their own observation:** One specific thing from the Expo that they won't forget. Be real about it — a moment of genuine pride or surprise.

If peer appreciation cards from the Expo haven't been distributed, do it now. Give students 1 minute to read theirs.

---

### Final Portfolio Journal Entry (20 min)

**Format:** Individual writing — this is the most important writing students have done in the module

**Teacher framing:** "This journal entry is your letter to yourself about what you built. Not a summary of the modules — your actual experience. What changed? What surprised you? What would you tell yourself from Module 1?"

**Final Portfolio Reflection Template:**

"When I started Module 1, I thought AI was... Now, after building [agent name], I understand that AI is..."

**Part 2: What I Built**

*List every capability your agent has. (All of them, not just the demo-worthy ones.)*

From M1: _______________  
From M2: _______________  
From M3: _______________  
From M4: _______________  
From M5: _______________  
From M6: _______________  
Other: _______________

---

**Part 3: What I'm Most Proud Of**

*Describe in 3–5 sentences: What's the part of your agent you're genuinely most proud of — and why? It can be technical or not.*

---

**Part 4: The Hardest Moment**

*What was the single hardest moment in the entire build? What did you do? What did you learn?*

---

**Part 5: What Surprised Me**

*What's one thing that surprised you — about AI, about building, about yourself?*

---

**Part 6: If I Had More Time**

*What would Module 8 be for you? What's the one thing you'd add to your agent if you had three more weeks?*

---

**Part 7: Letter to a Future Student**

*Write 3–5 sentences to a student who is about to start Module 1. What do you wish you'd known? What advice would you give them?*

---

*This journal entry — and your agent — are the evidence that you can build with AI. Keep both.*

---

**Teacher circulates during writing.** Don't rush this. 20 minutes is appropriate. Students who finish early can go deeper in any part — or start "What Would Module 8 Be?" brainstorm while they wait.

---

### Class Debrief: What Would Module 8 Be? (10 min)

**Format:** Open discussion — one of the best conversations of the curriculum

**Teacher prompt:** "If there were a Module 8, what would it be? You've built something real. What's the next level?"

Capture student ideas on the whiteboard. Push for specifics:
- "A module about deploying your agent publicly" — what would that mean?
- "Making the agent smarter" — smarter how?
- "Adding memory" — what kind of memory?
- "Making it help other people" — who? With what?

This conversation has two purposes:
1. It reveals what students genuinely want to learn next — invaluable feedback for teachers
2. It invites students to see themselves as designers of curricula, not just consumers of it

**After the brainstorm:** "The reason you can have this conversation is because you built something. You know enough to know what's missing. That's the mark of a builder."

---

### Career Pathways in AI (10 min)

**Format:** Brief overview + personal reflection

**Teacher presents a few accessible pathways (not exhaustive — open a door, don't map the whole building):**

#### Pathways Overview

| Pathway | What You Do | Connects to This Curriculum |
|---------|------------|----------------------------|
| **AI/ML Engineer** | Build the models that make AI work | M4 — training and model architecture |
| **AI Product Manager** | Decide what AI products should do and for whom | M1, M3, M6 — design decisions, knowledge, ethics |
| **AI Ethics Researcher** | Study the societal impact of AI systems | M6 — guardrails, bias, usage policy |
| **Prompt Engineer** | Craft the instructions that shape how AI behaves | Every module — you've been doing this |
| **AI UX Designer** | Design the interfaces humans use to interact with AI | M5 — channel design, M1 — personality |
| **Data Scientist** | Find patterns in data; train and evaluate models | M4 — training, evaluation |
| **AI Entrepreneur** | Build products and companies on top of AI | Every module |
| **AI Teacher / Educator** | Help others learn what you now know | This lesson — you wrote a letter to future students |

**Reflection prompt (written or pair discussion):**
> *Which pathway sounds most interesting to you right now — and why? What would you need to learn next to take a step in that direction?*

Note: "I don't know" is a completely valid answer. The point is curiosity, not certainty.

---

### Celebration (5 min)

**Keep it genuine, not performative:**

- Acknowledge that finishing is real: "You made it through 7 modules. You built an AI agent from scratch. That is not a small thing."
- Optional: play a song the class chose, or a class-selected moment
- Optional: "What We Built" wall — post agent names or screenshots
- If peers haven't shared appreciation yet: round of applause for each student by name

**Closing ritual — teacher's choice (pick one):**

*Option A: One Word*  
Go around the room. Each student says one word about this experience.

*Option B: Pass the Mic*  
Each student names one peer who helped them (or whose demo impressed them) — one sentence.

*Option C: Future Self*  
Each student completes: "In five years, I will tell people that in 8th grade I built..."

*Option D: Quiet*  
Give students 60 seconds of silence to sit with the fact that they did this. Then end.

---

### Collect Journals (end of session or designated deadline)

If Agent Design Journals are the summative artifact:
- Collect at end of class, or
- Set a 2-day deadline to allow students to polish final entries

Confirm your deadline clearly before students leave.

## Differentiation

### For students who need support:
- The Portfolio Reflection Template parts can be done in any order — start with "What I'm Most Proud Of" if Part 1 feels too abstract
- Sentence starters provided for each section (see teacher guide)
- Pair discussion option for any section before writing

### For advanced students:
- Challenge: write Part 7 as if they're creating a real onboarding document for a future student
- Ask: "What's the most nuanced thing you understand about AI now that you couldn't have explained in Module 1?"
- Invite them to present their Module 8 idea to the class in 60 seconds

### For students who had a difficult Expo:
- Give extra space in the "Hardest Moment" section — validate struggle as part of the story
- Redirect from "what went wrong" to "what did I learn from what went wrong"
- Private check-in if needed: "Your journey mattered regardless of how the demo went. What are you most proud of about your build?"

---

## Assessment

**Summative:** Completed Final Portfolio Journal Entry (all 7 parts).

**Rubric guidance:**
- Part 1 (Full Arc): Evidence that student's understanding of AI genuinely changed
- Part 3 (Most Proud Of): Specificity — names a real capability or design decision, not "everything"
- Part 4 (Hardest Moment): Honesty and reflection on process, not just outcome
- Part 6 (Module 8): Demonstrates forward-thinking and connection to real AI concepts
- Part 7 (Letter): Shows perspective-taking and genuine advice (not generic)

## Teacher Notes

### Setting the Tone
This session needs to feel different from a regular class. Small things matter:
- Rearrange seating slightly — circle if possible, less "classroom" feel
- Distribute peer appreciation cards before students arrive
- Consider starting music softly as students enter

### The Module 8 Conversation
This is genuinely one of the best conversations in the curriculum. Students who have built something real have strong, specific opinions about what comes next. Their answers reveal both what they learned and what excited them most. Capture this feedback — it is invaluable for curriculum iteration.

Common student Module 8 ideas:
- "A module where we deploy our agent publicly and see real people use it"
- "A module where we add memory — my agent forgets everything between conversations"
- "A module where two agents talk to each other"
- "A module about making your agent make money"
- "A module about AI and art"
- "A module about AI and healthcare"
- "A module about how to tell when AI is wrong"

All of these are real fields. Take them seriously.

### The Career Pathways Conversation
Keep it exploratory and low-stakes. The goal is not for every student to decide on an AI career — it's to expand their sense of what's possible. Frame: "You built something. People get paid to do what you just did. Here's the landscape."

Many students at this age (6–8th grade) haven't met anyone who works in AI. Meeting the *concept* of "AI educator" or "prompt engineer" is itself meaningful.

### Closing
Don't rush the closing ritual. Even if it feels slow, the students deserve the acknowledgment. You've guided them through building something real, something technical, something ethical, and something theirs. That took seven modules and a lot of trust. End with intention.

*You made it too. 🎓*
