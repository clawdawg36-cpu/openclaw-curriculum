## Overview

Module 7 is a capstone presentation module. Students prepare a 5-minute live demo of their agent, deliver it to an audience that includes peers, teachers, and ideally community members or parents, and field Q&A about their design choices. The module includes scaffolded preparation: building a capability inventory, storyboarding the demo, writing the narrative arc, rehearsing with partners, and addressing the hardest questions ("What does your agent get wrong?").

The AI Expo is not just a showcase — it's the culminating summative assessment of the entire curriculum. The skills exercised here — clear explanation of technical work, honest reflection on limitations, engaging live demonstration — are professional-grade communication skills that transfer far beyond AI class.

---

## Essential Questions

1. How do you explain a complex technical system to someone who didn't build it?
2. What makes a demo compelling? What makes it land flat?
3. What are you most proud of about your agent? What would you do differently?
4. Where does AI go from here — and what role do you want to play?

---

## Student "I Can" Statements

By the end of this module, students will be able to say:

- **I can** explain every capability of my agent to a non-technical audience.
- **I can** deliver a 5-minute live demo that tells a clear story about what my agent does.
- **I can** answer honest questions about where my agent fails or could be harmful.
- **I can** reflect on the full 7-module arc: what I learned, what surprised me, what I'd do differently.
- **I can** articulate my own vision for the future of AI and my place in it.
- **I can** give and receive constructive peer feedback on a technical presentation.

## The AI Expo Format

**Duration:** 5-minute demo + 3-minute Q&A per student/team
**Audience:** Classmates + teacher (minimum); ideally + parents + community guests
**Setup:** Stations or sequential presentations (teacher's choice)
**Materials:** Live agent demo, optional poster or slide deck, Agent Design Journal

### Suggested Demo Script Structure

1. **Hook (30 sec):** Open with something your agent does that surprises people
2. **Build (2 min):** Walk through the journey — what you added in each module
3. **Demo (2 min):** Live demo of at least 3 capabilities, with real inputs
4. **Honest reflection (30 sec):** One thing it gets wrong, and what you'd fix

## Flex Options

### ⚡ Minimal Version — 3-Session In-Class Only

Use when: limited time, no external guests available, or as a streamlined in-class capstone.

| Session | Focus | What's Compressed |
|---|---|---|
| **Session 1** | Capability Inventory + Script Draft (combine Sessions 2–3) | Storyboard becomes a quick 4-box sketch, not a standalone lesson |
| **Session 2** | Rehearsal + Dry Run (combine Sessions 4–5) | One rehearsal round only; peer feedback is verbal, not written |
| **Session 3** | In-Class Expo + Reflection (combine Sessions 6–7) | Audience is classmates only; reflection done as exit ticket |

**Skip:** Guest invitations, poster boards, audience evaluation cards, school-wide logistics
**Compress:** All peer feedback to verbal only; rubric simplified to 3 criteria
**Keep:** Capability inventory (non-negotiable — students must articulate what they built), live demo (even if short), written reflection

**Minimum viable Expo:** Each student does a 3-minute demo for their partner + teacher. That's enough. The reflection is what matters most.

---

### 🏫 Full Version — School-Wide Event with Parents + Community (7 sessions + setup)

Use when: you want a real "Science Fair" energy, community buy-in, and maximum stakes for students.

**Add before Session 6:**
- Send guest invitations 2 weeks in advance (use template above)
- Coordinate with admin for room booking and setup support
- Consider evening event for working parents (6–8 PM; students set up 5–5:30)
- Recruit 2–3 community judges (local tech professionals, CS teachers, librarians)
- Prepare "People's Choice" voting system (sticky dot voting or digital form)

**Add during Session 6 (Expo Day):**
- Brief judges in advance with rubric and scoring expectations
- Designate a student emcee (optional — fun job for an enthusiastic student)
- Provide light refreshments if possible — it signals that this matters
- Take photos and video (with permission slips)

**Add after Session 6:**
- Share standout projects with next year's students (with permission) as inspiration examples
- Consider publishing student write-ups on class or school blog
- Invite community guests to stay for the Session 7 debrief if timing allows

**Logistics tip:** "Stations" format (all presenting simultaneously, audience rotates) works better than sequential for events with guests — keeps energy high and avoids long waits.

## For Teachers: Running the AI Expo

**Logistics tips:**
- Use Session 5 (Dry Run) to identify any students who need last-minute support — this is your safety net
- Invite real guests — community members and parents make the stakes real and validate students' work
- Consider a "People's Choice" award voted on by the audience (sticky dots on a board work great)
- Have a backup plan for live demos (screenshot/video fallback if internet is unreliable)
- The `expo-planning-guide/` has a full planning timeline and logistics checklist for larger events

**Creating a welcoming environment:**
- Celebrate effort and learning, not just polished outcomes
- Some agents will be more complete than others — that's fine; the Expo is about communicating what you built, whatever that is
- The reflection (Session 7) is often the most powerful part; give it real time and space
- Return audience evaluation cards to students — they are genuine, often surprising feedback from real people

**After the Expo:**
- Collect Agent Design Journals as final summative artifacts
- Consider a debrief class: "What would Module 8 be?"
- Share standout projects (with student permission) as examples for next year
- The `full-year-portfolio-rubric.md` in `assessments/` can be used if you collect the whole Agent Design Journal as a portfolio

**Assessment note:** The teacher-facing rubric (`ai-expo-presentation-rubric.md`) is designed for live scoring during the Expo. Print one copy per presenter and score in real time, or use a digital form equivalent.
