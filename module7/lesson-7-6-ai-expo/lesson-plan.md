# Lesson 7.6 — AI Expo

**Module:** 7 — Capstone: AI Expo  
**Grade Level:** 6–10 (adaptable)  
**Duration:** 60–90 minutes (or full period; adjust for class size)  
**Session Type:** Capstone Presentation Event  
**Standards Alignment:** AI4K12 Five Big Ideas; CSTA K-12 CS Standards; ELA Speaking & Listening Standards; NGSS Communicating Information

---

## Overview

This is the main event. Students deliver their 5-minute live agent demos to a real audience — classmates, teacher, and ideally parents, community members, or other guests. Each presenter gets 5 minutes for their demo and 3 minutes for Q&A. The format is either sequential (one presenter at a time) or science-fair (all stations running simultaneously), teacher's choice based on class size and audience logistics.

This is not a test. It is a celebration of what students built — and an authentic communication challenge with real stakes.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, students will have:

1. Delivered a complete 5-minute live agent demo to a real audience.
2. Answered at least 3 audience questions honestly and confidently.
3. Observed 3+ peers' demos and provided one piece of specific appreciation for each.
4. Experienced the satisfaction of sharing something they built with people outside the classroom.

## Before the Expo: Setup (15 min before class)

**Teacher pre-class checklist:**
- [ ] Expo display tested and working
- [ ] Presenter order posted (sequential) or station numbers labeled (science-fair)
- [ ] Guest chairs/seating arranged (if applicable)
- [ ] Timer visible to presenter and teacher
- [ ] Expo programs printed (if applicable)
- [ ] Student devices have their agents open and ready (students arrive a few min early to set up)

**Student pre-class setup (first 5 min of session):**
- Students arrive and immediately go to their stations
- Open agent, open slides, do one quick test of Demo Moment 1
- Signal teacher with thumbs up when ready
- Presenter order confirmed / science-fair stations numbered

---

## Session Structure

### Welcome + Audience Framing (5 min)

**Teacher opens the Expo:**

Welcome guests (if present) and students. Brief framing:

*"What you're about to see is the result of [X] weeks of work. Each student built an AI agent from scratch — and not just technically. They designed a personality, taught it to perceive the world, gave it knowledge, trained it, connected it to a live channel, and wrote the ethical rules that govern it. They're now going to show you what that looks like — live."*

*"For each presenter: 5 minutes for the demo, then 3 minutes for questions. We'd love genuine curiosity — the harder the question, the more interesting the answer."*

For student peers in the audience: distribute peer appreciation cards (see below).

For the presenter order (sequential format): announce the first presenter.

---

### The Demos — Sequential Format (variable, based on class size)

**Per presenter:**

| Phase | Time | What Happens |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Setup | 1 min | Presenter connects, slides ready (already done if pre-set up) |
| Demo | 5 min | Full Hook-Build-Demo-Reflect arc |
| Q&A | 3 min | Teacher opens floor; audience asks questions |
| Transition | 1 min | Audience appreciation; next presenter sets up |

**Total per presenter:** ~10 min

**Class size guide:**
- 10 students: ~100 min (plan for 2 periods or one longer block)
- 15 students: ~150 min (plan accordingly — or use science-fair format)
- 20+ students: Science-fair format strongly recommended

**Teacher's role during sequential demos:**
- Keep time (give presenter a 1-min signal)
- Open Q&A: "Who has a question for [name] about their agent?"
- Prompt if silence: use one question from the Adversarial Q&A bank (softer version)
- Close each presentation with one specific appreciation: "What I noticed about [name]'s demo was..."
- Use rubric to score during or immediately after each demo

---

### The Demos — Science-Fair Format

**How it works:**
- All students at their stations simultaneously
- Audience (classmates + guests) circulates on their own schedule
- Each presenter delivers their demo repeatedly as new audience members arrive
- Teacher sets a rotation signal every 8–10 minutes (bell, music, announcement)
- Each audience member visits at least 3 stations

**Audience guidance card (for guests):**

> *Welcome to the AI Expo! Students have built personal AI agents from scratch. At each station:*
> *1. Ask the student to demo their agent (they'll show you something it can do)*
> *2. Ask at least one question — even a simple "How does that work?" is great*
> *3. Before moving on, tell them one thing you found interesting*

**Teacher's role in science-fair format:**
- Float and observe all stations
- Listen for strong Q&A moments and note them for debrief
- Score each student using the rubric (may require a second observation pass)
- Occasionally redirect audience traffic to less-visited stations

---

### Peer Appreciation Cards

**How to use:**
- Each student gets 3–5 cards (one per demo they observe in science-fair format, or one per 3 demos in sequential)
- Card prompts:
  - "The most impressive thing about [agent name] was..."
  - "A question I had after seeing this demo was..."
  - "One word to describe this agent: ___"
- Cards are collected and given to the presenter at the end of the Expo — they go in the journal.

---

### Closing Ceremony (10 min)

**After all demos are complete:**

- Brief class circle (standing or seated)
- Teacher shares 2–3 specific highlights from the Expo (without ranking)
- If using People's Choice: count votes and announce (keep it celebratory, not competitive)
- Acknowledge the full arc: "At the start of Module 1, you asked 'What is AI?' Today you answered it — not with a definition, but with something you built."
- Invite 2–3 students to share one word for how they feel right now
- Optional: communal celebration (applause, snacks, photos with permission)

**Journal prompt (for end of class or homework):**
> *Write your Expo experience in 5 sentences: What you expected before you started, what happened during your demo, one audience question that surprised you, how you felt when it was over, and one thing you want to remember about this day.*

## Differentiation

### For students who need support:
- Allow presenter to read directly from script if needed — this is still a valid demo
- Pre-arrange 2 "friendly audience" questions from a known peer or adult
- Offer option to present to a smaller group (just teacher + 2 peers) if anxiety is severe
- "Portfolio presentation" format available: student explains what they built and what they learned, even if not all tech is working

### For advanced students:
- Challenge: no script during the demo
- Invite them to answer the hardest audience question with a full, nuanced response
- Ask: "What would you pitch to a real investor or employer about this agent?"

### For students with persistent technical issues:
- Backup screenshot / video demo is fully acceptable and pre-communicated to audience
- Teacher introduces these students with: "This student's demo will include some screenshots of capabilities they built — which gives us a chance to see the design work behind the scenes."

## Assessment: Capstone Rubric

*(Also available as a standalone rubric in `assessments/` directory)*

| Criterion | 4 — Exemplary | 3 — Proficient | 2 — Developing | 1 — Beginning |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Technical Completeness (30%)** | Agent has 5+ working capabilities across 4+ modules; all work reliably | Agent has 4+ capabilities across 3+ modules; most work | Agent has 3 capabilities; some work inconsistently | Agent has 1–2 capabilities or most are non-functional |
| **Clarity of Explanation (25%)** | Demo is fully clear to a non-technical audience; no jargon; confident narration | Demo is mostly clear; occasional jargon; generally follows script | Demo is sometimes clear; audience may lose track of what's happening | Audience cannot follow the demo; heavy reading from script without narration |
| **Live Demo Execution (20%)** | 3+ capabilities demoed live with interesting inputs; smooth narration during any hiccups | 3 capabilities demoed; mostly smooth; minor recovery from any issues | 2 capabilities demoed; some tech issues interrupt the flow | 0–1 capabilities demoed live; tech issues significantly disrupt demo |
| **Reflection Quality (15%)** | At least 1 honest limitation named specifically; confident closing; genuine insight | 1 limitation named; closing is present; mostly honest | Limitation is vague ("it's not perfect"); closing is generic | No honest reflection; closes with apology or trails off |
| **Q&A Responses (10%)** | Answers questions fully, honestly, and with specificity; says "I don't know" appropriately | Answers most questions adequately; occasionally vague | Answers are short or evasive; avoids hard questions | Does not engage with Q&A; deflects all questions |

## Teacher Notes

### Creating the Right Environment
- The energy you create matters more than the logistics. Students have worked hard. Let that be visible.
- Guests (parents, community members) make a profound difference in how seriously students take this. Even one or two outside guests elevates the stakes meaningfully.
- If you can only do one class period, science-fair format is almost always better for student energy and peer observation.

### Managing the Unexpected
- Something will break. A demo will fail. An agent will time out. This is fine and expected. Your job is to model calm: "Technical demos always have surprises — watch how [student] handles it."
- Have the Adversarial Q&A Question Bank on your phone to prompt Q&A if the audience is quiet.

### Grading
- Score each student during or immediately after their demo. Memory degrades quickly with 15+ presenters.
- If you can record the demos (with student/parent permission), reviewing video improves scoring accuracy.
- Holistic impressions matter. A student who built less but communicated with real clarity and honest reflection may score higher than a student with a complete agent who couldn't explain what it does.

### After the Expo
- Collect Agent Design Journals (if they're the summative artifact). Or set a collection deadline of 1–2 days post-Expo.
- Send peer appreciation cards to students — these are surprisingly meaningful.
- Consider sharing standout moments (with permission) for next year's class examples.
