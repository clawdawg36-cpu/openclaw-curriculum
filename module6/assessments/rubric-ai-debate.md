# AI Debate — Participation Rubric

**Module:** Module 6: AI Ethics, Bias & Fairness  
**Assessment Type:** Summative — Oral/Participatory  
**Total Points:** 16 (4 dimensions × 4-point scale)

---

## Overview

Students participate in a structured debate on an AI ethics topic (e.g., "Should facial recognition be banned in public spaces?" or "Who is responsible when AI causes harm?"). This rubric assesses the quality of their participation — not which side they argued.

---

## Scoring Rubric

### Dimension 1: Argument Quality

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Arguments are clear, logically structured, and consistently grounded in relevant concepts from the module and/or credible external sources. Claims are specific and well-reasoned; reasoning is easy to follow. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Arguments are generally clear and logical. Most claims are supported with reasoning or examples. Occasional lack of specificity or logical gaps do not significantly undermine the position. |
| **2 — Developing** | Arguments are present but often vague, repetitive, or loosely reasoned. Claims may be stated without adequate support. The position is discernible but not compellingly argued. |
| **1 — Beginning** | Participation is minimal, arguments are absent or incoherent, or student reads from notes without engaging with the debate format. |

---

### Dimension 2: Evidence Use

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Integrates specific, accurate evidence (data, real-world examples, course readings) to support arguments at multiple points. Evidence is clearly connected to the claim being made. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Uses relevant evidence to support most major arguments. Evidence is generally accurate; minor imprecision or weak connection to claim in one or two instances. |
| **2 — Developing** | Some evidence is cited but may be vague ("studies show…" without specifics), inaccurate, or disconnected from the argument. |
| **1 — Beginning** | Little to no evidence used. Arguments rely entirely on opinion or assertion. |

---

### Dimension 3: Counterargument Acknowledgment

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Actively engages with opposing arguments — restates them fairly before rebutting, identifies their strongest points, and provides specific, substantive rebuttals. Demonstrates genuine understanding of the other perspective. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Acknowledges counterarguments and attempts rebuttal. Rebuttal is generally relevant; may not fully engage with the strongest version of the opposing view. |
| **2 — Developing** | Counterarguments are mentioned but dismissed without substantive engagement ("That's wrong because…") or student avoids addressing the opposing side. |
| **1 — Beginning** | No acknowledgment of counterarguments. Student ignores or talks past the opposing position. |

---

### Dimension 4: Respectful Engagement

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Consistently models respectful discourse: listens actively when others speak, uses names, critiques ideas not people, maintains professional tone even under pressure, invites quieter participants to contribute. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Generally respectful. Minor lapses in listening or tone do not significantly disrupt the debate. Largely critiques ideas, not individuals. |
| **2 — Developing** | Mostly respectful but occasionally interrupts, dismisses peers, or uses language that could be perceived as personal rather than idea-focused. |
| **1 — Beginning** | Disrespectful language, dismissive tone, or behavior that shuts down or harms the debate environment. |

## Facilitation Notes

- Assign debate positions **before** the lesson so students can prepare evidence.
- Consider a "steelman" requirement: each side must articulate the strongest version of the opposing argument before rebutting it.
- This rubric applies to individual students; assess each participant separately.
- Remind students that the goal is rigorous, respectful intellectual engagement — not "winning."
- Dimension 4 scores below 2 should trigger a brief private conversation before returning to the debate.
