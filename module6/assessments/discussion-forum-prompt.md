# Async Discussion Forum Prompt — Module 6

**Module:** Module 6: AI Ethics, Bias & Fairness  
**Type:** Asynchronous Discussion (Formative / Participation)  
**Suggested Timing:** After Lesson 6.5 (Stakeholders & Fairness Frameworks) or as a module-long thread  

---

## Prompt

> **Find one AI system you use regularly. Who built it? Who benefits most from it? Who might be harmed by it?**

Post a response of **150–250 words** that:
1. Names and briefly describes the AI system
2. Explains who you think benefits most from it (and why)
3. Identifies one group who might be harmed — and what kind of harm

Then, **reply to at least one classmate's post** with a thoughtful response of 50–100 words. You might:
- Point out a stakeholder they didn't mention
- Challenge or expand on their analysis of harm or benefit
- Share a connection to something from the module (bias, feedback loops, transparency, etc.)

---

## Facilitation Notes for Teachers

**Where to post:** LMS discussion board, Padlet, Google Classroom, Discord — wherever async discussion lives in your class

**Timing options:**
- Open the thread at the start of Module 6 and let it run all week (students post when ready)
- Assign after Lesson 6.5 as a structured reflection
- Use as a flipped pre-work before the AI Debate

**Grading suggestion:**  
This is participation-based, not rubric-scored. Credit for:
- [ ] Original post submitted (150–250 words)
- [ ] Accurately names and describes an AI system
- [ ] Addresses all three questions (builder, beneficiary, harmed party)
- [ ] At least one substantive peer reply

**Amplification:** Screenshots or quotes from interesting posts (with permission) make excellent discussion starters at the top of the next synchronous class.

**Connections to module content:**
- Lesson 6.1: Algorithmic bias (what's the AI optimized for?)
- Lesson 6.5: Stakeholder analysis frameworks
- Lesson 6.7: Responsible disclosure, accountability

---

## Example Student Response *(for teacher reference — not shown to students)*

> *"I use Spotify every day. Spotify was built by a Swedish tech company and is now publicly traded. The people who benefit most are probably premium subscribers who get personalized playlists and recommendations — plus Spotify's shareholders and popular artists who get more streams. But independent and emerging artists might be harmed: the algorithm favors music similar to what you already listen to, which makes it hard for new artists to break through unless they're already popular. There's also a feedback loop issue — the more a song gets played, the more it gets recommended, which means the rich get richer. Smaller artists, especially those from underrepresented genres or languages, might get far fewer recommendations even if their music is just as good."*
