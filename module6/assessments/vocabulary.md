# Module 6 Vocabulary List

**Module:** Module 6: AI Ethics, Bias & Fairness  
**Purpose:** Reference and study guide for all key terms introduced in Module 6

---

## Core Terms

### Algorithmic Bias
A systematic error in an AI system's outputs that creates unfair outcomes for certain groups of people. Bias can be introduced at any stage — from problem framing and data collection to model design and deployment. *Example: A hiring algorithm that scores résumés from women lower than those from men, not because of qualifications, but because it was trained on historical data where men were more often hired.*

---

### Proxy Variable
A variable used to represent something that's difficult to measure directly. Proxy variables can introduce bias when they correlate with protected characteristics (like race or gender) even if those characteristics are not explicitly included in the data. *Example: Using ZIP code as a proxy for creditworthiness can indirectly encode racial segregation.*

---

### Feedback Loop
A cycle in which an algorithm's outputs influence future inputs, which then reinforce the original outputs. In AI systems, feedback loops can amplify existing biases over time. *Example: A predictive policing algorithm sends more police to neighborhoods already over-policed, leading to more arrests there, which makes the algorithm predict more crime there — reinforcing the cycle.*

---

### Surveillance
The monitoring of individuals or groups, often using technology, to collect data on their behavior, location, or activities. AI-powered surveillance includes facial recognition, license plate readers, and behavioral tracking systems. Raises significant concerns about privacy, consent, and disproportionate targeting of marginalized communities.

---

### Deepfake
Synthetic media — typically video, audio, or images — generated by AI to make it appear that a real person said or did something they did not. Created using deep learning techniques. Raises concerns about misinformation, non-consensual intimate imagery, and erosion of trust in authentic media.

---

### Usage Policy
A formal document that defines how an AI system may and may not be used. A strong usage policy includes: permitted use cases, prohibited uses, data privacy rules, stakeholder responsibilities, consequences for misuse, and a process for updates. *Also called: Acceptable Use Policy (AUP), Terms of Service (ToS) for AI tools.*

---

### Guardrail
A technical or procedural constraint built into an AI system to prevent specific harmful, unsafe, or unintended outputs or uses. Guardrails can be implemented as filters, rules, classifiers, rate limits, or human review requirements. *Example: A content moderation filter that blocks hate speech before it is published.*

---

### Alignment
The challenge of ensuring that an AI system's goals, behaviors, and outputs match the intentions and values of its designers and society. A misaligned AI system might technically do what it was optimized to do while still causing harm. *Example: An AI optimized to maximize user engagement might promote outrage and misinformation because these drive clicks.*

---

### Responsible Disclosure
The practice of reporting a security vulnerability, bias, or harmful flaw in an AI (or software) system to the developers or appropriate authorities — rather than exploiting it or publicizing it without warning — so they have the opportunity to fix it before widespread harm occurs.

---

### Stakeholder
Any person, group, or organization affected by an AI system — whether directly (users, subjects) or indirectly (communities, competitors, regulators). Good AI design requires identifying all stakeholders, especially those with less power to advocate for themselves.

---

### Fairness
In the context of AI, fairness refers to the equitable treatment of individuals and groups by an AI system. Fairness is complex because different definitions conflict: *Equal treatment* (the system applies the same rules to everyone) vs. *Equal outcomes* (the system produces comparable results across groups). No single definition of fairness fits all contexts.

---

### Transparency
The degree to which an AI system's design, training data, decision-making process, and limitations are visible and understandable to users, affected parties, and overseers. Transparency is a prerequisite for accountability. *Contrast with: black-box AI, where even designers may not fully understand why the system makes certain decisions.*

---

### Accountability
The principle that individuals, organizations, and systems should be answerable for the outcomes they produce. In AI, accountability asks: who is responsible when an AI system causes harm? Requires clear lines of responsibility — from data collectors to model designers to deployers to regulators.
