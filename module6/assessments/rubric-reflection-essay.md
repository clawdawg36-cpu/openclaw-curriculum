# Reflection Essay — Summative Rubric

**Module:** Module 6: AI Ethics, Bias & Fairness  
**Assessment Type:** Summative — Written Reflection  
**Prompt:** *"How should society decide what AI can and cannot do?"*  
**Total Points:** 16 (4 dimensions × 4-point scale)  
**Suggested Length:** 400–600 words

---

## Overview

Students write a structured reflection essay responding to the module's central ethical question. The essay should present a clear claim, support it with evidence, engage with at least one counterargument, and end with a purposeful conclusion. This is a *reflection* essay — personal voice and genuine engagement with the question are valued alongside analytical rigor.

---

## Scoring Rubric

### Dimension 1: Claim

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Opens with a specific, arguable claim that directly addresses the prompt. The claim goes beyond restating the question — it takes a clear, defensible position on how society *should* make decisions about AI. The claim guides the entire essay. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Claim is present and addresses the prompt. The position is identifiable and reasonably specific, though it may be slightly broad or not fully guide the rest of the essay. |
| **2 — Developing** | A claim exists but is vague ("It's complicated"), merely describes rather than argues ("Society uses AI in many ways"), or simply restates the prompt without taking a position. |
| **1 — Beginning** | No clear claim. Essay begins without a discernible argument or thesis. |

---

### Dimension 2: Evidence

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Supports the claim with multiple specific, accurate pieces of evidence — including at least one course concept (e.g., algorithmic bias, feedback loops, stakeholder analysis) and one real-world example. Evidence is clearly connected to the argument being made. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Uses relevant evidence to support the main argument. At least one piece of well-integrated evidence is present. Minor gaps in specificity or connection to claim do not significantly weaken the essay. |
| **2 — Developing** | Some evidence is present but may be vague, loosely connected, or used only in one section of the essay. General statements ("AI has caused harm") without specific examples. |
| **1 — Beginning** | Little or no evidence. Essay relies entirely on opinion or personal anecdote without connection to course concepts or real-world examples. |

---

### Dimension 3: Counterargument

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Clearly acknowledges a significant opposing viewpoint, restates it fairly, and provides a substantive rebuttal that strengthens (rather than abandons) the original claim. Demonstrates intellectual honesty and nuance. |
| **3 — Proficient** | A counterargument is present and addressed. Rebuttal is generally effective; may not engage with the strongest version of the opposing view or may be brief. |
| **2 — Developing** | Counterargument is mentioned but quickly dismissed without real engagement ("Some people think X, but they're wrong"), or is so weak as to not challenge the claim. |
| **1 — Beginning** | No counterargument present. Essay presents only one side without acknowledging complexity or opposition. |

---

### Dimension 4: Conclusion

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Conclusion restates the claim in fresh language, synthesizes the key evidence and counterargument discussion, and extends thinking beyond the essay — connecting to broader implications, personal commitment, or a call to action. Feels like a genuine ending, not just a summary. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Conclusion is present and restates the main argument. May summarize rather than synthesize; limited extension beyond what was already said. |
| **2 — Developing** | Conclusion is perfunctory ("In conclusion, AI is important") or simply repeats the introduction. Does not synthesize or add value. |
| **1 — Beginning** | No conclusion, or essay ends abruptly mid-thought. |

## Teacher Notes

- Personal voice is encouraged — students should feel free to draw on their own experiences with AI systems.
- The essay is *not* a research paper; students are not expected to use formal citations, though specific examples should be accurate.
- Consider using this essay as a portfolio anchor (see `portfolio-prompt.md`).
- Strong essays often begin with a specific scenario or story before zooming out to the claim.
