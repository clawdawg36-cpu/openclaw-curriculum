## Slide 4 — What to Look For: Images
**The checklist**

- 👋 **Hands/fingers** — extra digits, fused joints
- 💇 **Hair** — "painted" around fine strands
- 👁️ **Eyes** — asymmetry, reflection inconsistency
- 🌆 **Backgrounds** — blended objects, garbled text
- 💡 **Lighting** — inconsistent shadows
- 👂 **Ears/jewelry** — often misshapen

*These are current weaknesses. They change as AI improves.*

---

## Slide 5 — What to Look For: Text
**AI writing tells**

- Specific claims → no sources cited (AI fabricates citations)
- Oddly perfect sentences → no hedging, no ambiguity
- Missing concrete details → names and numbers that don't check out
- Confident tone on uncertain topics → real experts hedge

---

## Slide 6 — What to Look For: Audio
**Voice synthesis tells**

- No breathing or natural pauses
- "Too clean" — no room tone or ambient sound
- Emotion vs. pitch mismatch (words say "sad" but pitch doesn't)
- Perfect pronunciation (real people stumble slightly)

## Slide 10 — The Information Ecosystem
**How deepfakes spread**

[Flow diagram]

1. Deepfake created
2. Posted to platform
3. Algorithm amplifies (engagement → reach)
4. Enters group chats from "trusted" contact
5. Believed without checking

*The AI is the least important part. The amplification system is the threat multiplier.*

---

## Slide 11 — Prebunking vs. Debunking
**Inoculation works better than correction**

- **Debunking:** See false claim → correct it → often too late
- **Prebunking:** Learn the manipulation technique in advance → recognize it when you see it → resist it

Cambridge Social Decision-Making Lab research: inoculation to manipulation techniques (not specific claims) is more durable than fact-checking.

> *That's what we did today.*

## Slide 12 — The Liar's Dividend
**The meta-threat**

> Not just: "AI can make fake things look real."

> Also: "AI makes real things look possibly fake."

*This is the liar's dividend — and it's already being used.*
