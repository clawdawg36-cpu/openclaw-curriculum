# Lesson 6.5 — Deepfakes and Misinformation

**Module:** 6 — AI Ethics, Bias, and Accountability  
**Duration:** 1 period (~50 minutes)  
**Grade Level:** 7–8 (adaptable for 9–12)  
**Prerequisites:** Lessons 6.1–6.4

---

## 🔗 Through-Line Connection

> *"We've looked at bias in data and algorithms. We've seen surveillance. We've examined your data trail. Now we look at a different threat: what happens when AI is used to generate content designed to deceive?"*

Lesson 6.5 addresses deepfakes and AI-generated misinformation — one of the most visible and emotionally charged AI ethics topics for students. The lesson structure follows a deliberate arc: students encounter content first (the "Real or AI?" evaluation round), then develop analytical frameworks, then practice using verification tools. By working from instinct → framework → tools, students build genuine skill rather than absorbing abstract rules.

The broader information ecosystem framing is essential: deepfakes don't live in isolation. They're effective because they travel through amplification systems — social media feeds, group chats, recommendation algorithms — that were not designed for truth verification.

**Bridge to Lesson 6.6:** After understanding how AI can manufacture false narratives, students enter AI Debate Day — where they use their OpenClaw agents as research assistants. The irony is intentional, and students should be invited to notice it.

---

## 🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Distinguish between AI-generated and authentic images, audio, and text using at least two detection methods.
2. Explain how deepfakes and synthetic media can be used to cause harm.
3. Use at least one verification tool (reverse image search, metadata analysis, fact-checking resource) effectively.
4. Describe the information ecosystem conditions that make misinformation spread.
5. Articulate a personal media verification practice — a consistent habit, not a one-time action.

**Teacher Prep:**

- Prepare one "viral claim" example to work through as a whole-class demo (see Segment 2)

## ⏱️ Lesson Outline

### Opening: Real or AI? — Round 1 (10 min)

**No context, just instinct:**

Display 6 images one at a time (12–15 seconds each). For each image, students vote:
- A: Real photograph
- B: AI-generated
- ?: I can't tell

Using voting cards or hands or a quick poll tool.

After all 6: Reveal the answers. Tally the class score.

**Ask (before analysis):**
- *"What made you sure on some and uncertain on others?"*
- *"What features did you use to decide?"*

Note student answers on the board: they've discovered the heuristics intuitively. You'll validate and extend them in Segment 1.

---

### Segment 1: What to Look For (10 min)

**Build from student intuition:**

Review and validate the features students already found. Add the systematic checklist:

**Images:**
- **Hands and fingers** — AI models often generate extra fingers, fused joints, or asymmetric hands
- **Hair detail** — AI can look "painted" around fine strands
- **Eyes** — subtle asymmetry, reflection inconsistencies
- **Backgrounds** — objects blend into each other; text is garbled
- **Lighting** — inconsistent shadow directions across the same scene
- **Ears and jewelry** — often misshapen or asymmetric

**Text:**
- **Specific claims without sources** — real journalism cites; AI fabricates citations
- **Oddly perfect sentence structure** — AI avoids hedging and ambiguity
- **Missing concrete details** — AI generates plausible-sounding names and numbers that don't check out

**Audio:**
- **Breathing and pauses** — real speakers breathe; AI voice often doesn't
- **Room tone** — genuine recordings have ambient audio; TTS sounds clean
- **Emotion vs. pitch** — AI voice often mismatches emotional cues and pitch variation

---

### Segment 2: Real or AI? — Round 2 + Verification Tools (15 min)

**Display 6 more images (the harder set):**

This time, students vote AND get to use verification tools for the ones they're unsure about.

**Verification tool demo (3 min before Round 2 starts):**

Walk through quickly:
1. **Google Reverse Image Search** — drag an image to images.google.com; see where it appears
2. **TinEye** — tineye.com, specialized for finding original sources
3. **InVID / WeVerify** — browser extension for video verification
4. **Snopes / PolitiFact / FactCheck.org** — for text claims
5. **FotoForensics** — fotoforensics.com, EXIF metadata and JPEG artifact analysis

**Round 2 (8 min):**
Students work in pairs. For each image: initial vote + 60 seconds to use one tool if they want.

**Debrief (4 min):**
- *"Did the tools change your answer on any of them?"*
- *"Which tool was most useful?"*
- *"Were there any that none of us could tell — with or without tools? What does that mean?"*

---

### Segment 3: The Information Ecosystem (8 min)

**Why deepfakes spread — it's not about the content, it's about the system:**

Draw a simple diagram:
1. Deepfake created
2. Posted to platform
3. Algorithm amplifies (engagement = shares = reach)
4. Enters group chats and DMs
5. Person sees it from "trusted" contact
6. Person believes it without checking

The AI that creates the deepfake is the least important part. The amplification system is the threat multiplier.

**Key concepts:**
- **Prebunking vs. Debunking** — it's better to inoculate against manipulation techniques in advance than to correct misinformation after it spreads (research from Cambridge Social Decision-Making Lab)
- **Liar's dividend** — the existence of deepfakes makes people doubt real footage ("it could be AI")
- **Emotional amplification** — content that makes you angry or afraid spreads faster (because the algorithm rewards engagement)

---

### Closing: Your Verification Practice (7 min)

**From individual to ecosystem:**

> *"You can't check every piece of content. That's not realistic. But you can build a consistent habit that covers the things that matter most."*

Students design their own "Personal Verification Minimum":
- What content type do I need to be most careful about? (political claims? celebrity news? science headlines?)
- What one tool will I use when something feels off?
- What one habit will I build before I share something?

Students write this in their journals. They share with a partner.

**Closing question (no need to answer aloud — just sit with it):**
> *"If you can't tell whether something is real, does that mean you shouldn't trust anything? Or does it mean you need to think about what 'trust' means differently?"*

---

## 💬 Discussion Questions

**Warm-up level:**
- Have you ever shared something online that turned out to be fake? What happened? How did you feel?
- Is it wrong to share something if you believed it was true at the time?

**Core discussion:**
- Should social media platforms be responsible for removing deepfakes? What would that require technically? What could go wrong?
- Is a deepfake of a political figure making a fake speech different from a political ad that edits out context from a real speech? Where's the line?
- Who is most harmed by deepfakes — public figures, private individuals, society as a whole?

**Extension / advanced:**
- What is "media forensics" and how is it different from fact-checking?
- How do generative AI watermarking proposals work, and why haven't they been implemented widely?
- What is the "liar's dividend" and how does it change the deepfake threat landscape?

## 🎯 Differentiation

### Support Strategies

| Need | Strategy |
|------|----------|
| Students who feel overwhelmed | Normalize: "You don't need to verify everything. You need to verify the things that matter before you share or act." |
| ELL students | Pair with partner; image-based activity doesn't require strong English; text-based activities can be done in L1 first |
| Students without devices | Whole-class verification demo only; skip partner tool practice; use class vote for Round 2 |
| Students with history of encountering harmful deepfake content | Be aware this topic may resurface experiences with non-consensual intimate images; have counselor contact info ready |

### Extension Strategies

| Need | Strategy |
|------|----------|
| Advanced learners | Research C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) — how does AI content watermarking work technically? |
| Media-literacy curious | Read Cambridge Social Decision-Making Lab research on prebunking; design a "misinformation inoculation" for a specific claim type |
| Tech-curious students | Use FotoForensics to analyze the error level analysis of an image — what does it reveal about editing? |
| Fast finishers | Design a "deepfake detection checklist" for a specific audience (younger siblings, grandparents) — what would be practical for them? |

## ⚠️ Facilitation Notes

**Do not use deepfakes of celebrities in sexual or violent contexts.** Even for demonstration purposes, this is inappropriate and potentially traumatic for students who've encountered non-consensual intimate image abuse.

**The "Real or AI?" activity may surface perfectionism anxiety.** Some students feel embarrassed when they're fooled. Normalize it explicitly: *"If you were fooled, congratulations — you experienced exactly what the systems are designed to do. The fact that you can be fooled isn't a failure of intelligence. It's a feature of how these systems exploit human perception."*

**Be careful about overclaiming detection ability.** State-of-the-art deepfakes are genuinely hard to detect. Don't promise students that the tools you showed them will always work. Epistemic honesty: *"These tools help. They don't solve the problem."*
