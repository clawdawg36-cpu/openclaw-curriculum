# Lab: OpenClaw Bias Testing Workshop

**Module:** 6 — Make Your Agent Ethical
**Embedded In:** Lesson 6.2 — What's Fair, and Who Decides?
**Duration:** 1 class period (~45–50 minutes)
**Type:** Hands-on investigation / systematic testing workshop
**Grade Level:** 7–8

---

## Overview

Students work in small groups to systematically probe OpenClaw — a shared AI assistant — for evidence of bias across multiple dimensions: gender, race, geography, age, and mainstream vs. niche knowledge. They document their findings using a structured template, then pool results as a class to look for patterns.

The goal is not to "break" OpenClaw or to prove it's bad. The goal is to practice **systematic thinking about AI behavior** — forming hypotheses, designing tests, documenting findings, and reasoning about causes.

> **How This Differs from the KEY LAB**
>
> In the **Key Lab (Audit and Govern Your Agent)**, students audit *their own personal agent* — its specific knowledge, configuration, and outputs — and then write policies and guardrails for it.
>
> In **this workshop**, students test a *shared instance of OpenClaw* using a structured protocol. The focus is on developing the testing mindset and understanding where bias comes from in AI systems generally. Think of it as learning the tool before using it on your own project.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lab, students will be able to:

- Define "AI bias" and give examples of different types (representation, association, accuracy disparity)
- Apply a systematic testing protocol to probe an AI system for bias
- Document findings with enough detail that others can reproduce them
- Generate and evaluate hypotheses about why an AI system might exhibit bias
- Distinguish between bias that can be easily "fixed" and bias that's structural

## Quick Setup

1. Ensure all students have access to OpenClaw on their devices (or share a class login)
2. Print `documentation-template.md` — one per group (groups of 2–3 recommended)
3. Assign or let students choose bias axes (see `protocol.md`)
4. Run the workshop (~45 min)
5. Pool findings using `aggregation-sheet.md` (digital spreadsheet or on whiteboard)
6. Close with discussion using `analysis-guide.md`

See `workshop-guide.md` for complete facilitation notes.
