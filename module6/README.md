## Overview

Students take their nearly-complete agents through an ethics and responsibility audit. They analyze their agent's outputs for bias, write a formal usage policy, and implement guardrail logic in OpenClaw that enforces behavioral limits. This module reframes ethics not as a theoretical topic but as an engineering problem: how do you actually build the rules into the system?

This is where abstract AI ethics becomes concrete design work.

## Essential Questions

1. What does it mean for an AI system to be "fair"? Is fairness objective?
2. Who is responsible when an AI agent does something harmful — the builder, the platform, or the user?
3. How do you write a usage policy? What has to be in it?
4. What's the difference between a guardrail that prevents harm and one that just prevents liability?

## Student "I Can" Statements

By the end of this module, students will be able to say:

- **I can** define algorithmic bias and identify it in AI outputs.
- **I can** audit my own agent's outputs for potential bias across different user inputs.
- **I can** write a usage policy that clearly defines permitted and prohibited uses.
- **I can** implement at least one guardrail in OpenClaw that enforces a behavioral limit.
- **I can** explain the difference between rule-based guardrails and model-level alignment.
- **I can** evaluate an AI system using an ethics framework and defend my assessment.

## Topics

- Algorithmic bias: what it is, how it gets in, why it's hard to remove
- Bias auditing: how to systematically test an AI system for disparate outputs
- Usage policies: purpose, audience, structure, and real-world examples (platform TOS, AI ethics guidelines)
- Guardrail design: content filters, topic restrictions, refusal logic
- OpenClaw guardrail configuration
- AI alignment: what it means to train a model to "behave well" vs. adding rules on top
- Responsible disclosure: what to do when you find a problem in a system you didn't build
- The builder's responsibility: if you made it, what are your obligations?

## Key Lab: Audit and Govern Your Agent

Students will:
1. **Bias audit:** Run 10+ test prompts designed to probe for biased, unfair, or harmful outputs. Document findings.
2. **Usage policy:** Write a 1-page policy for their agent: who it's for, what it can do, what it refuses to do.
3. **Guardrail implementation:** Add at least one rule to their OpenClaw configuration that enforces a specific behavioral limit.
4. **Verification:** Test that the guardrail works as intended with adversarial inputs.

## Assessment

- Bias audit report: what you tested, what you found, what you'd change
- Usage policy document (graded with a provided rubric)
- Lab: Working guardrail demonstrated in class
- Reflection: What surprised you about your agent's outputs? Did anything make you uncomfortable?
