# Activity Guide: Interactive Stakeholder Mapping

**Module:** 6 — AI Ethics, Bias, and Accountability  
**Tool:** Stakeholder Mapping Tool (`tool/index.html`)  
**Used In:** Lesson 6.7 (Fairness Proposal activity), activity-stakeholder-mapping  
**Time:** 20–40 minutes depending on depth  
**Group Size:** Individual, pairs, or small groups (2–3)

---

## What You're Doing

You're going to place stakeholders — the people and organizations affected by an AI system — on a 2×2 grid. The grid has two axes:

- **Left → Right:** From harmed to benefited  
- **Bottom → Top:** From less power to more power

Where a stakeholder lands tells you something important: **who pays the price, and who has the power to change it.**

This isn't a quiz. There are no objectively correct placements. The goal is to *argue well* about where things go and *change your mind* when someone has a better reason.

---

## Getting Started (2 minutes)

1. **Open the tool:** `tool/index.html` (bookmark it, or get the link from your teacher)
2. **Pick a scenario** from the dropdown at the top. Your teacher may assign one, or you may explore on your own.
3. **Read the scenario description** in the notes panel below the grid.
4. **Find the card tray** — it's on the left (desktop) or at the bottom of the screen (tablet). Those are your stakeholders.

You're ready. Start dragging.

---

## Step-by-Step: Your First Map (Individual, ~10 minutes)

### Step 1 — Place without overthinking (3 min)

Drag **all 10 cards** onto the grid. Don't debate yourself — go with your gut. You can move them later.

**Quick gut-check questions for each card:**
- Does this group *benefit* or *get hurt* by the system? → Left or right
- Does this group have *power to change* the system, or are they stuck with it? → Up or down

### Step 2 — Read the scenario notes on each card (2 min)

Hover (or tap) each card to see the scenario-specific note about that stakeholder. Does it change where you placed them? Move any cards that feel wrong now.

### Step 3 — Look at the bottom-left quadrant (2 min)

The **bottom-left** is labeled "Affected + Harm" — the red zone. Look at who's there.

Ask yourself:
- Could any of these people appeal the system's decision?
- Who speaks for them? Is anyone in the top half of the grid looking out for them?
- If you were in this quadrant, what would you want?

### Step 4 — Save your map (30 seconds)

Click **[Save Map]** and label it "Before discussion." This is your baseline — you'll compare it later.

---

## Step-by-Step: Partner Discussion (~10 minutes)

Find a partner (or discuss in your group of 3). You'll each have your own map. Now compare.

### Step 5 — Compare placements

Look at your two maps. Find **one card where you placed it differently**.

Don't just say "I put them in top-right and you put them in bottom-left." Explain:
- *Why* did you place it there?
- What assumption are you making about that stakeholder's power or their experience of harm/benefit?

### Step 6 — The argument round

For each card where you disagree, take turns:
- Person A: "I placed [Stakeholder] here because..."
- Person B: "I placed them here because..."
- Together: "After hearing both reasons, we'd place them here... because..."

This isn't about winning. It's about sharpening the reasoning.

### Step 7 — Reveal the "Consider" prompts

In the scenario notes panel, click **[Show Discussion Prompts]**. Your teacher may have hidden these during Step 5 — that's intentional. Read them now.

Did they surface something you hadn't thought about? Move any cards based on new thinking.

### Step 8 — Save again

Click **[Save Map]** and label it "After discussion."

---

## Step-by-Step: Group Debrief (~5–10 minutes)

Your teacher will lead a class debrief. Be ready to:

- **Name one stakeholder** from the bottom-left quadrant in your scenario and explain what you learned about their position
- **Explain one card you moved** during the discussion — what changed your mind?
- **Answer the big question:** Who has the power to make this system more fair? What would that take?

---

## Connecting to the Fairness Proposal

If you're using this tool as part of Lesson 6.7, you'll take your stakeholder map into the **Fairness Proposal activity**.

After mapping:

1. Identify the **most vulnerable stakeholder** (bottom-left) in your scenario
2. Write one sentence: *"The system harms [stakeholder] by [specific harm]."*
3. Now think about your own OpenClaw agent. Who is the most vulnerable user or affected party? Where would they land on the grid?
4. Use that position to draft your first Fairness Proposal commitment: *"I will [do X] to ensure [stakeholder group] is not harmed by [specific failure mode]."*

## Things Worth Arguing About

These are placement decisions where smart people genuinely disagree. Worth at least 5 minutes of debate in any scenario:

**Government / Regulators**
- They have power — but do they benefit from the status quo, or are they under pressure to act?
- Does it matter if the government *contracted* the system vs. just failing to regulate it?

**Journalists / Advocates**
- They have some power (voice, platform) — but are they usually harmed or benefited by AI systems?
- Their *goal* is to protect the harmed group — does that mean they're also "harmed" by seeing harm?

**Parents / Guardians**
- Their children may be in the bottom-left. Does that put parents there too?
- Or do parents have *more* power than their kids — more ability to complain, opt out, or sue?

**Marginalized Groups**
- This card often lands in bottom-left. Is that always right?
- What if marginalized groups are *also* organizing to fight back? Does agency change their power position?

## Using Discussion Mode

Click **[Discussion Mode]** to hide the quadrant labels and colors. The grid is still there — you can still place cards. But you have to argue about placement using your own words instead of relying on the label names.

This is harder — and more honest. Try it after you've already done a round with labels visible.

Questions to use in Discussion Mode:
- *"Where does this person end up — in a good position or a bad one?"*
- *"Do they have control over what happens to them?"*
- *"Are they better off or worse off because of this system?"*

Then reveal the labels and see how your placements align with the axis definitions.

## Facilitation Prompts (for Student Discussion Leaders)

If your class uses a Socratic seminar or student-led discussion format, here are prompts to keep the conversation moving:

**To open:**
- *"Before we talk, everyone point to the quadrant where [Stakeholder] landed on your map."* (Visible disagreement is instant engagement.)

**To deepen:**
- *"You said they're harmed. How? Be specific — what decision does the system make about them?"*
- *"You said they have power. Can they actually change the outcome? What would that look like?"*

**To push back:**
- *"What would [Stakeholder] say about where you placed them? Would they agree?"*
- *"Could someone be in the top quadrant and still be in a bad position? How?"*

**To synthesize:**
- *"Looking at the bottom-left quadrant — what do all of these groups have in common?"*
- *"If you wanted to make this system fairer, which quadrant would you go to first to find allies? Why?"*

**To close:**
- *"What one thing would have to change — in the technology, the law, or the organization — to move one card from the bottom-left into a better position?"*

---

## Quick Tips

✅ **Place fast, argue slow** — Your first instinct is a starting point, not an answer  
✅ **Move cards during discussion** — That's the whole point  
✅ **Custom cards are allowed** — If your group identifies a stakeholder that isn't there, add it  
✅ **Saving is easy** — Don't lose your work; save before the debrief  
✅ **Export if you need it** — Use Export Text to paste into your Fairness Proposal doc  

❌ **Don't treat placement as permanent** — If you don't move at least one card, you're not really discussing  
❌ **Don't skip the bottom-left** — That quadrant is why this tool exists  
❌ **Don't default to "it depends"** — Pick a quadrant. Defend it. Then update if you learn something.
