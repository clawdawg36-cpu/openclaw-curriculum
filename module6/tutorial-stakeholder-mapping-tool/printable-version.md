# Printable Stakeholder Mapping Tool

**Low-tech fallback for no-device or low-connectivity scenarios**  
**Module:** 6 — AI Ethics, Bias, and Accountability  
**Format:** Print + sticky notes + markers  
**Time:** 20–40 minutes (same as digital version)

---

## What You Need

**For each group of 2–4 students:**
- 1× printed stakeholder grid (see below — print at full page, landscape preferred)
- 10× sticky notes (standard size; one color per group, or different colors per student)
- Markers for writing on sticky notes
- Printed stakeholder card reference sheet (see Stakeholder Reference section below)

**Teacher prep:**
- Print the grid pages double-sided or print separately
- Pre-write stakeholder names on sticky notes for classes with time constraints (saves 2 min per group)
- For the whole-class debrief, tape or project a large grid on the board (butcher paper works well)

## Scenario Cards (Read Aloud or Print)

Print one scenario card per group (or read aloud for full-class version).

---

### Scenario 1: Facial Recognition in Schools

> A school district has installed AI-powered facial recognition cameras at all entrances. The system checks students' faces against a database and flags anyone not on the approved list. It also tracks attendance automatically and alerts staff if a person with a prior disciplinary record enters the building.  
>  
> The system was purchased from a private tech company and contracted by the school board. Students and parents were not consulted.

**Scenario Notes for Each Stakeholder:**

- **Students:** Scanned every day. Can't opt out.  
- **Teachers:** Get automatic attendance data. Receive alerts.  
- **Admins:** School board contracted it; principals enforce it.  
- **Tech Companies:** Sold the system; may retain facial data.  
- **Advertisers:** Not directly involved.  
- **Government:** May have funded it; regulators may investigate.  
- **Parents:** Not consulted; may support (safety) or oppose (privacy).  
- **Marginalized Groups:** Higher error rates documented for students of color.  
- **Journalists / Advocates:** ACLU has challenged similar deployments.  
- **Investors:** Shareholders benefit from school security contracts.

**Discussion Starter:** *"Where does the school board go — powerful and benefiting, or powerful and exposed to backlash?"*

---

### Scenario 2: Hiring Algorithm

> A large company uses an AI system to screen job applications. The algorithm scores each candidate 0–100. Candidates below 60 are automatically rejected without human review. The system was trained on the company's past ten years of hires — a workforce that was historically 80% white and male.  
>  
> Hiring managers see only the score. They don't see the algorithm's reasoning.

**Scenario Notes for Each Stakeholder:**

- **Students:** Recent graduates applying for entry-level jobs; screened without appeal.  
- **Teachers:** Not directly involved.  
- **Admins:** HR teams trust the scores; less manual work.  
- **Tech Companies:** Built and licensed the algorithm.  
- **Advertisers:** Not directly involved.  
- **Government:** EEOC may investigate if discriminatory patterns emerge.  
- **Parents:** Indirectly affected through family employment outcomes.  
- **Marginalized Groups:** Screened out at higher rates if algorithm replicates historical bias.  
- **Journalists / Advocates:** Journalists and orgs like AJL audit these systems.  
- **Investors:** Efficient hiring saves costs; shareholders benefit.

**Discussion Starter:** *"Who had input into how the training data was chosen? Does anyone in the bottom-left quadrant have a way to appeal?"*

---

### Scenario 3: Social Media Recommendation Algorithm

> A popular social media platform uses an AI recommendation algorithm to decide what appears in each user's feed. The algorithm is designed to maximize engagement — time spent on the app. Internal research showed that emotionally intense, divisive, and alarming content drives higher engagement.  
>  
> The algorithm isn't disclosed to users. There is no opt-out. The platform earns money from advertisers who pay based on engagement.

**Scenario Notes for Each Stakeholder:**

- **Students:** Primary users; feed shaped invisibly; may see amplified anxiety or misinformation.  
- **Teachers:** No control; deal with the effects on students.  
- **Admins:** Platform leadership decides the algorithm's objective.  
- **Tech Companies:** Designed the system; profits from engagement → ad revenue.  
- **Advertisers:** Pay for engagement; the algorithm serves their interest.  
- **Government:** Some regulate; others want data access.  
- **Parents:** Limited ability to restrict teen usage.  
- **Marginalized Groups:** Amplification of hate content disproportionately targets minority users.  
- **Journalists / Advocates:** Whistleblowers (e.g., Frances Haugen) exposed internal research.  
- **Investors:** Engagement-maximization drives revenue; shareholders benefit.

**Discussion Starter:** *"The platform says this is a free service — a benefit for users. Where does that argument break down?"*

## Step-by-Step Instructions (Post on Board or Read Aloud)

### Step 1 — Prep your sticky notes (2 min)

Write one stakeholder name on each sticky note. You'll have 10 notes total.

Blank sticky = wild card. You can add a stakeholder that isn't on the list.

### Step 2 — Individual placement (5 min)

Work alone. Place all 10 sticky notes on the grid. Go with your gut — you'll move them later.

Ask for each note:
- Is this group **harmed** or **benefited** by the system? → Left or right
- Does this group have **power** to change it, or are they stuck? → Up or down

### Step 3 — Read the scenario notes (2 min)

Your teacher (or scenario card) has specific notes for each stakeholder in this scenario. Read them. Move any notes that feel wrong after reading.

### Step 4 — Partner discussion (8–10 min)

Find a partner. Compare your two grids.

Pick **one note where you placed it differently.** Argue about it:
- Person A: *"I put them here because..."*
- Person B: *"I put them here because..."*
- Together: *"After hearing both, we'd put them here... because..."*

Move notes when someone has a better reason. That's the whole point.

### Step 5 — Class debrief (teacher-led)

Share out:
- *Who landed in the bottom-left (⚠ Affected + Harm)?*
- *Did anyone move a note during discussion? What changed your mind?*
- *Who has the power to make this system more fair?*

## Large-Group Version (Whole Class, One Grid)

For a single large grid on butcher paper or a whiteboard:

**Setup:**
- Draw the 2×2 grid on butcher paper (~2×3 feet) or project the grid on a screen
- Give each student one sticky note and one stakeholder card (assign randomly or let them pick)
- Each student is the "advocate" for their stakeholder

**Process:**
1. Students come up one at a time and place their sticky note
2. Before placing: *"Tell us who your stakeholder is and where you're placing them. 10 seconds."*
3. After all 10 notes are placed, step back and look at the full picture
4. Open discussion: *"Does this look right? What would you move?"*
5. Students who want to challenge a placement raise their hand, explain, and physically move the note (or don't, if the argument doesn't hold up)

**Why this works:** The physical act of walking up and placing a note is more memorable than a digital drag. The "advocate" framing creates investment. Students who see their stakeholder moved to the bottom-left have a visceral reaction.

## Connecting to the Fairness Proposal (Lesson 6.7)

After the activity, students keep their completed grid visible during the Fairness Proposal activity.

Ask students to circle the one note in the bottom-left quadrant they feel most concerned about. That stakeholder is the **anchor** for their Fairness Proposal audit question:

> *"The system harms [circled stakeholder] by [specific harm]. My fairness commitment for my own agent is: [commitment]."*

Students who used the physical version can photograph their grid (phone camera) for reference during the Fairness Proposal or for portfolio documentation.

## Tips for Running the Physical Version

**Sticky note colors:**  
If multiple groups work at the same table, use different sticky note colors per group. When comparing, you can overlay grids and immediately see where the colors cluster vs. diverge.

**Pre-writing notes saves time:**  
If you write stakeholder names on stickies before class (one set per group), you save 3–4 minutes. Worth it for 50-minute periods.

**Photograph everything before takedown:**  
Have students photograph their completed grids. This gives them material for the Fairness Proposal and you something to reference in future discussions. A good stakeholder map also makes excellent classroom display material.

**Don't laminate the grid for discussion use:**  
Lamination prevents writing on the grid. If you want annotatable grids for advanced use, keep them on plain paper. Reserve laminated versions for reference-only display.

**The argument is the activity:**  
Physical placement produces great arguments because people feel more ownership over paper notes than pixels. Don't rush to "correct" placements. A wrong-headed placement that generates 3 minutes of disagreement is more valuable than a technically correct one placed in silence.

**For limited sticky notes:**  
Write stakeholder names directly on the grid with a dry-erase marker, or use torn paper slips with tape. The tool is the conversation, not the materials.

## Troubleshooting

**"My class doesn't have sticky notes."**  
Use index cards. Use torn paper. Use dry-erase on a laminated grid. The material doesn't matter — the spatial argument does.

**"Students are placing everything in the middle."**  
This is the physical equivalent of digital hedging. Forbid the center: *"You can't put a note on a line. Pick a quadrant."* The forced choice produces the argument.

**"Groups are just agreeing with each other."**  
Assign devil's advocate: *"Person B — your job is to argue that [stakeholder] should be moved one quadrant over. Go."* Structured disagreement breaks groupthink.

**"The debrief runs long."**  
Cut straight to the bottom-left. *"Everyone point to what's in your Affected + Harm quadrant. That's the only thing I care about right now."* Five-minute debrief version: name what's there, name who should fix it, connect to Fairness Proposal.

**"Students don't know what 'Marginalized Groups' means."**  
Make it concrete: *"In this scenario — who is most likely to be misidentified by the facial recognition system? Whose hiring application is most likely to get filtered out? That's your marginalized group in this context."* Students can rename the card to be more specific.
