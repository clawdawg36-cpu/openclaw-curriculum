# Lesson 6.4 — Privacy and Your Data

**Module:** 6 — AI Ethics, Bias, and Accountability  
**Duration:** 1 period (~50 minutes)  
**Grade Level:** 7–8 (adaptable for 9–12)  
**Prerequisites:** Lesson 6.3 (AI Surveillance Simulator)

---

## 🔗 Through-Line Connection

> *"In 6.3, you felt what it was like to be surveilled. In 6.4, we're going to understand exactly what makes surveillance possible: data. Your data. Not data that was stolen — data that you left."*

After the emotional intensity of the Surveillance Simulator, Lesson 6.4 shifts register: from emotional to analytical, from simulation to reality. Students investigate their own data trails — the information they generate just by living and moving and clicking — and discover how much can be inferred from what they share. The key insight is **inference**: it's not just what you share, but what can be concluded from what you share.

**Bridge to Lesson 6.5:** The data trail activity shows students how personal data becomes a profile. Lesson 6.5 extends this into misinformation: when AI-generated content is designed to exploit that profile, what does accurate information look like?

---

## 🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. List at least five types of data they generate passively (without intending to share it).
2. Explain the concept of inference — how non-sensitive data can reveal sensitive information.
3. Identify at least three ways their data is collected, used, and shared without explicit awareness.
4. Evaluate the real tradeoffs in common data-sharing scenarios (convenience vs. privacy).
5. Describe at least two concrete actions that could reduce their personal data exposure.

## ⏱️ Lesson Outline

### Opening: The Data Trail We Don't See (8 min)

**No devices needed for opening — just imagination:**

Ask students to close their eyes (if comfortable) and walk through a typical morning:
- Wake up → phone alarm → check notifications
- Get dressed → bathroom
- Breakfast → family conversation
- Walk/bus/car to school → arrive

Now ask: *"Without trying, without choosing to, what data did you generate in that sequence?"*

Take responses. Build a list on the board. Students often start with the obvious (phone notifications, location) and miss the less obvious:
- Time you woke up (inferred from phone unlock)
- What you searched while you were bored on the bus
- Whether you walked or took transit (location granularity)
- What music you listened to
- What weather you checked (inferred: you were planning to go outside)

**Ask:** *"Does any of this feel private? Does that matter if no one's looking at it?"*

---

### Segment 1: Inference — The Hidden Layer (12 min)

**Concept: The data isn't the problem. The inference is.**

Explain: The most sensitive information often isn't shared directly. It's *inferred* from patterns of non-sensitive data.

**Three inference examples to work through together:**

**Example 1: Location → Health**
- You check in to a clinic three Tuesdays in a row
- The app doesn't know why you're there
- But the pattern tells an insurer: this person visits a medical facility weekly
- Inferred: chronic health condition → insurance pricing impact

**Example 2: Purchase Patterns → Pregnancy**
- Famous case: Target's data team inferred pregnancy from buying patterns (unscented lotion, large bags of cotton balls, hand sanitizer)
- Sent coupons to a teenager before her parents knew she was pregnant
- No one shared that data. The algorithm concluded it.

**Example 3: App Usage → Political Affiliation**
- Which news apps you use
- What podcasts you listen to
- Which petition links you click
- Inferred: political leaning → targeted political advertising

**Discussion:** *"Which of these bothers you most? Why? What's the difference between 'knowing' and 'inferring'?"*

---

### Segment 2: My Data Day Activity (15 min)

**The worksheet scenario:**

Students receive "My Data Day" — a fictional character named Taylor's Tuesday. A timeline of Taylor's day with 15 numbered data events (app opens, location check-ins, purchases, messages, searches).

**Part A (5 min, individual):**
Students categorize each data event:
- 🟢 I'd be fine if this was shared
- 🟡 I might care, depending on who sees it
- 🔴 I don't want this shared

**Part B (5 min, pairs):**
Share and compare. Note: partners often categorize the same events differently.
- *"Why did you mark that green when I marked it red?"*
- *"What does that tell us about privacy — is it objective or subjective?"*

**Part C (5 min, whole class):**
Instructor reveals: all of Taylor's 🟢 events, combined together, can be used to infer Taylor's:
- Home address (location patterns)
- Health status (app usage + purchase patterns)
- Relationship status (messaging timing patterns)
- Financial stress (search history: "payday loan," "skip rent")

*"The green dots became the red dots. Not because someone was malicious — because of math."*

---

### Segment 3: Privacy Erosion — How It Happens (8 min)

**Three mechanisms of privacy erosion:**

1. **Aggregation** — individually innocuous data points combine into a sensitive profile
   - Example: Name + employer + neighborhood + daily schedule = stalking risk

2. **Repurposing** — data collected for one purpose is used for another
   - Example: Fitness tracker data used in insurance underwriting; period tracker data subpoenaed in abortion investigations

3. **Drift** — privacy settings change after you've already shared data
   - Example: A platform changes its terms of service; data you shared under old terms is now used differently

**Ask:** *"Which of these feels most unfair? Is there one you think is actually acceptable? Under what conditions?"*

---

### Closing: Agency and Action (7 min)

**Shift from analysis to practical agency:**

> *"You can't have zero data footprint in 2024. That's not a realistic goal. But you can make more informed choices."*

Three levels of action:
- **Minimal effort:** Review app permissions on one app you use often (location, contacts, microphone)
- **Medium effort:** Use a privacy-focused search engine (DuckDuckGo, Brave) for one week
- **High effort:** Read the privacy policy for one app you actually use — time it (average: 20 minutes to read fully)

**Exit ticket:** Students write one thing they'll actually do differently based on today's lesson.

> *"Not as homework. As a choice you're making for yourself."*

## 💬 Discussion Questions

**Warm-up level:**
- What's the difference between something being private and something being secret?
- If something is in "public" — posted publicly online — does that mean anyone can do anything with it? Should it?

**Core discussion:**
- Is privacy a right? A privilege? A setting? What's the difference?
- If you agreed to a company's Terms of Service, did you consent to what they do with your data? Is that real consent?
- Who should be responsible for protecting your privacy — you, the apps you use, or the government?

**Extension / advanced:**
- What is "differential privacy" and how does it allow data to be useful for research without exposing individuals?
- What is the GDPR (Europe) and CCPA (California) and what would it mean if those protections applied everywhere?
- Can you have privacy in a public space? What do legal scholars argue?

## 🎯 Differentiation

### Support Strategies

| Need | Strategy |
|------|----------|
| Students who feel overwhelmed | Normalize: "Knowing this doesn't mean you have to change everything. Knowledge is power even when you don't act on it immediately." |
| ELL students | Allow pair discussion in L1; provide simplified "My Data Day" with visual icons |
| Students without personal device access | Use Taylor's fictional profile only — no need for personal device audit |
| Students who've experienced stalking or identity theft | Opt-in approach to the data trail activity; frame it as educational analysis, not personal audit |

### Extension Strategies

| Need | Strategy |
|------|----------|
| Advanced learners | Research differential privacy — how Apple uses it in iOS analytics |
| Privacy-policy curious | Actually read one privacy policy (Instagram recommended) and summarize what surprised them |
| Policy-minded students | Research GDPR vs. CCPA — what rights do European vs. Californian users have that others don't? |
| Tech-curious students | Investigate browser fingerprinting — how websites identify you without cookies |

## ⚠️ Facilitation Notes

**Avoid inducing paranoia.** The goal is informed agency, not fear. Some students may react to this lesson with a desire to delete all their apps or stop using technology entirely. While that's a valid personal choice, it's not the lesson's goal.

Reframe: *"Privacy isn't about hiding. It's about having the ability to choose what you share, with whom, and when. Right now, that choice is often made for you without your awareness. This lesson is about reclaiming some of that awareness."*

**Be careful with the pregnancy/Target example.** This is a widely-used case study but it can feel abstract. If students connect it to reproductive health or abortion access — especially given recent legal changes in the US — hold that space. Don't deflect. *"Yes, that connection is real. Period tracker data has been subpoenaed in states where abortion is restricted. This isn't hypothetical."*
