## Slide 4 — What Is Inference?
**The hidden layer**

You didn't share that you're pregnant.
You shared that you bought:
- Unscented lotion
- Large bags of cotton balls
- Hand sanitizer
- Calcium supplements

*An algorithm concluded the rest.*

> **Inference: deriving sensitive information from non-sensitive data.**

---

## Slide 5 — Example 1: Location → Health
**You went to the clinic three Tuesdays in a row**

- You didn't share your diagnosis
- The pattern told an algorithm: regular medical appointments
- An insurer inferred: chronic condition
- Impact: insurance pricing

*The data wasn't the problem. The inference was.*

---

## Slide 6 — Example 2: Purchases → Pregnancy
**The Target case**

Target's data science team identified ~20 products that, in combination, indicated pregnancy.

Sent targeted coupons to a teenager.
Her parents found the mail before she told them.

*No one shared private health information. The algorithm concluded it from shopping patterns.*

---

## Slide 7 — Example 3: App Usage → Politics
**Your information diet is visible**

- Which news apps you use
- Which podcasts you listen to
- Which petition links you click
- What you search vs. what you share

Inferred: political leaning → targeted political advertising

*You didn't opt in to that profile. It was assembled.*

## Slide 10 — Three Mechanisms of Privacy Erosion

| Mechanism | What It Means |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Aggregation** | Safe pieces → dangerous profile |
| **Repurposing** | Collected for X, used for Y |
| **Drift** | Rules changed after you shared |

*Which feels most unfair? Is any of them acceptable?*

---

## Slide 11 — Privacy as a Spectrum
**Not binary. Not purely personal.**

- Privacy isn't "hide everything"
- Privacy is about **choosing** what to share, with whom, when
- Right now, that choice is often made for you

> *"You can't have zero footprint. But you can be more informed."*
