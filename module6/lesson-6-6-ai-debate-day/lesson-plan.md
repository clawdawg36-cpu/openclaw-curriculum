# Lesson 6.6 — AI Debate Day

**Module:** 6 — AI Ethics, Bias, and Accountability  
**Duration:** 2 periods (~100 minutes total)  
**Grade Level:** 7–8 (adaptable for 9–12)  
**Prerequisites:** Lessons 6.1–6.5

---

## 🔗 Through-Line Connection

> *"You've spent this module investigating AI bias, surveillance, privacy, and misinformation. Today you argue. About AI. With AI helping you. Yes — we know. That's the point."*

Lesson 6.6 is the intellectual capstone of the analytical arc of Module 6. Students take positions on contested AI ethics questions and defend them in structured debate — using their OpenClaw agents as research assistants. The embedded irony is pedagogically intentional: using an AI tool to prepare arguments about AI systems makes visible the layered complicity of AI in contemporary discourse. Students who notice this tension are thinking exactly right.

Structured debate is the right format here because it forces students to:
1. Encounter the strongest version of a position they disagree with
2. Defend a position with evidence, not just feeling
3. Distinguish between "I believe this" and "I can argue this"

The four debate topics were chosen to be genuinely contestable — no debate topic in Module 6 has a clear "correct" answer.

**Bridge to Lesson 6.7:** Debate Day surfaces the question "who is responsible?" repeatedly. Lesson 6.7 turns that question into a framework: the builder's responsibility triangle.

---

## 🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Articulate at least two evidence-supported arguments for a position on a contested AI ethics question.
2. Identify and respond to the strongest counterargument to their position.
3. Use an AI assistant (OpenClaw) to find, evaluate, and cite research — and critically evaluate what the AI produces.
4. Distinguish between empirical claims (what is) and normative claims (what should be) in an argument.
5. Change or refine a position based on argument and evidence.

**Topic assignment:** Assign topics and sides randomly (including side assignment). The goal is to practice arguing a position you may not hold — a core debate skill and a perspective-taking exercise.

## The Four Debate Topics

**Topic 1: Facial Recognition in Schools**
> *"School districts should be prohibited from using AI facial recognition systems on school property."*
- For: Student privacy rights, chilling effect on speech, disproportionate impact on students of color
- Against: Campus security, missing persons identification, parental consent frameworks

**Topic 2: Algorithmic Content Moderation**
> *"Social media platforms should use AI to automatically remove misinformation without human review."*
- For: Scale problem, human reviewers are too slow, clear harms from viral misinformation
- Against: Free speech concerns, who defines misinformation, over-removal risk, accountability

**Topic 3: AI in Criminal Justice**
> *"Courts should be prohibited from using AI risk scores in sentencing and bail decisions."*
- For: Documented racial disparities, due process, opacity of scoring
- Against: Human judges also have bias, consistency argument, risk management for public safety

**Topic 4: AI Creative Rights**
> *"AI-generated art and writing should be protected by copyright law."*
- For: Economic protection for AI developers and users, creative investment deserves protection
- Against: AI can't author (no personhood), trained on unconsented human work, chills human creativity

## ⏱️ Lesson Outline

### Period 1: Research, Prep, and Practice

#### Opening: Debate Format Briefing (8 min)

**Read and distribute the debate format reference card.**

**Debate format:**
1. **Opening statement** (2 min each side) — State your position and top 3 arguments
2. **Rebuttal** (1.5 min each side) — Respond to the other side's opening
3. **Cross-examination** (2 min) — Opposing side asks questions
4. **Closing statement** (1 min each side) — What should the audience take away?
5. **Audience Q&A** (2 min) — Class asks questions to either side

**Judging criteria:**
- Clarity of argument (do we understand your position?)
- Use of evidence (do you support claims with facts/examples/research?)
- Response to counterargument (do you engage the other side?)
- Persuasiveness (did you move anyone?)

**The OpenClaw Rule:**
> *"You may use your OpenClaw agent to find arguments and research. You must evaluate what it tells you before you use it. If you cite something your agent gave you and it turns out to be false, that hurts your score."*

*Build in this constraint explicitly. Students should ask their agents: "Is this claim well-sourced? What's the evidence?" and then verify independently.*

---

#### Research Prep Phase (30 min)

**Part A — Position and argument generation (10 min):**

Students receive their debate topic and side. They read the topic and answer on the research worksheet:
1. What is my position in one sentence?
2. What are the 3 strongest arguments for this position?
3. What are the 2 strongest arguments for the OTHER side?
4. How will I respond to those opposing arguments?

**Part B — OpenClaw research (15 min):**

Students query their OpenClaw agents. Suggested prompts:
- *"What are the strongest arguments for [position]?"*
- *"What evidence or case studies support [position]?"*
- *"What are the best counterarguments to [position]? How do proponents of [position] respond to them?"*
- *"Are there specific examples I can cite of [relevant case study]?"*

**OpenClaw Use Log:**
Students complete the Use Log on their worksheet:
| Query | What OpenClaw said | Did I verify? | Used in debate? |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| ... | ... | ✅ / ❌ | ✅ / ❌ |

**Part C — Practice round (5 min):**

Partners from the same side practice their opening statement. Keep time strictly — 2 minutes, hard stop.

---

### ⏸️ Period 1 Close (7 min — if split into 2 days)

Brief check-in:
- *"What did your OpenClaw agent give you that was useful?"*
- *"Did anything your agent say not check out when you looked into it?"*

Take 3–4 responses. This surfaces the lesson-within-the-lesson: AI assistants are useful but require critical evaluation.

---

### Period 2: The Debates

#### Opening: Set the Stage (5 min)

**Post the judging criteria visibly.** Remind the class:
- Judges are looking for argument quality, not personal conviction
- It's okay to argue a position you don't hold — that's a skill
- The point is to understand the strongest version of every position

**Schedule the debates:**

If time allows, run all 4 debates sequentially. Each debate takes ~12 minutes. Remaining class votes after each debate on who argued more effectively (not who they personally agree with).

If time is limited, run 2 debates in class and 2 as written submissions.

---

#### Debate Rounds (40 min, 4 × 10 min with transitions)

**Per debate:**

| Time | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| 0:00–2:00 | Team A: Opening statement |
| 2:00–4:00 | Team B: Opening statement |
| 4:00–5:30 | Team A: Rebuttal |
| 5:30–7:00 | Team B: Rebuttal |
| 7:00–9:00 | Cross-examination (any member can ask) |
| 9:00–10:00 | 30-sec closing each; audience Q&A |

**Teacher role during debates:**
- Keep time visibly (countdown timer on screen)
- Do not intervene in the argument — let it happen
- Note factual errors for debrief (don't correct in real time)
- Watch for the students who are changing their minds — highlight this in debrief

---

#### Debrief: What Did You Learn? (10 min)

**Debrief questions (whole class):**
1. *"Did arguing a position you didn't personally hold change your thinking? How?"*
2. *"What was the strongest argument you heard on any side? What made it strong?"*
3. *"Did your OpenClaw agent ever give you something you couldn't verify? What happened when you tried to use it?"*
4. *"The fact that you used AI to prepare arguments about AI — does that feel like a problem? Why or why not?"*

**The meta-lesson:**
> *"You just experienced something important: AI can help you construct an argument, but it can't decide what you believe or evaluate evidence for you. Those are human jobs. The better the AI assistant, the more important it is to maintain that distinction."*

---

#### Closing (5 min)

**Written reflection:**

Students write (journal or exit slip):
> *"After today's debates, where do I stand on [my debate topic]? Did I change my mind? What evidence would change my mind further?"*

---

## 💬 Discussion Questions

**Warm-up level:**
- What's the difference between "I think X is wrong" and "I can argue X is wrong"?
- Is it possible to argue a position you disagree with and do it well? What does that require?

**Core discussion:**
- Did using AI to help you prepare for a debate about AI feel ironic? Problematic? Fine? Why?
- What's the difference between citing evidence and citing your AI agent's summary of evidence?
- Which debate topic was hardest to argue? What made it hard?

**Extension / advanced:**
- The structure of formal debate requires treating contested questions as binary (pro/con). Is that a strength or a limitation when it comes to AI ethics?
- What's the difference between "AI should be regulated" and "AI should be regulated by [this specific mechanism]"? Why does the specificity matter?

## 🎯 Differentiation

### Support Strategies

| Need | Strategy |
|------|----------|
| Students anxious about public speaking | Allow written debate submission instead of live; or assign them to the questioning/judging role |
| ELL students | Allow notes to be read from; extra prep time; partner support during cross-examination |
| Students who struggle with research | Provide a curated article list for each topic so research phase isn't open-ended |
| Students who can't argue against their beliefs | Acknowledge this is hard: "Real lawyers argue positions they disagree with. It's a skill, not a compromise." |

### Extension Strategies

| Need | Strategy |
|------|----------|
| Advanced debaters | Assign devil's advocate role after the main debate — they must find the flaw in the winning argument |
| Policy-minded students | After the debate, write a one-page policy brief on the topic: what would you actually recommend? |
| Research-curious students | Investigate academic literature on their debate topic — what does empirical research say? |
| Fast finishers | Design a fifth debate topic for Module 6 — what important AI ethics question wasn't covered? |

## ⚠️ Facilitation Notes

**Random side assignment is important.** Students who only argue positions they already hold don't develop the perspective-taking and evidence skills that are the lesson's real goal. Hold firm on the random assignment. If a student genuinely cannot argue a position due to personal experience or safety concerns, offer a private check-in and provide an alternative role (observer, judge, questioner).

**The OpenClaw irony is a feature, not a bug.** If students push back on using AI to prepare for a debate about AI ("isn't that cheating/hypocritical?"), lean into it: *"That tension you're feeling is exactly the right question. What does it mean to use a tool when you're also evaluating that tool? Does using it make you complicit? Does critical evaluation make it okay? These are the questions builders have to ask."*

**Watch for AI-generated misinformation in debate.** Students may use their agents' claims without verifying them. If a factual error appears in a debate, note it but don't correct during the round — wait for debrief and use it as a teaching moment about AI verification.
