# Lab: Day of AI Surveillance Simulator

**Module:** 6 — Make Your Agent Ethical
**Embedded In:** Lesson 6.3 — Who Gets to Decide?
**Duration:** 2 class periods (approximately 90–100 minutes total)
**Type:** Role-based simulation / ethics workshop
**Grade Level:** 7–8

---

## Overview

Students inhabit different stakeholder roles in a realistic scenario: a school district is considering deploying an AI-powered surveillance system that monitors student behavior, social media, and physical movement on campus. Each student advocates for their role's interests, then the class debates, negotiates, and reflects together.

This is **not** about AI system-building. It's about experiencing how real decisions about AI deployment get made — and who gets a seat at the table.

> **Connection to KEY LAB:** In the Key Lab (Audit and Govern Your Agent), students write policies and guardrails for *their own* agent. This simulation shows why that matters — every AI system exists in a social context, with stakeholders who have competing interests. Policy-writing is real work, not a formality.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lab, students will be able to:

- Describe at least three different stakeholder perspectives on AI surveillance
- Identify who benefits and who is harmed by a specific AI deployment
- Articulate the rights at stake in AI surveillance scenarios
- Practice structured argumentation and perspective-taking
- Apply an ethics framework to a real-world AI decision

## Day-of-AI Connection

This lab is inspired by [Day of AI](https://dayofai.org), MIT's initiative to bring AI literacy to middle school classrooms. Before running this simulation, explore:

- **dayofai.org** — free lesson modules on AI and society
- [Day of AI: Fairness & Bias module](https://dayofai.org/curriculum/) — directly relevant background
- [Day of AI: Social Impact](https://dayofai.org/curriculum/) — introduces stakeholder analysis

Teachers are encouraged to show 1–2 short Day of AI videos as a warm-up before Period 1 of this lab.

## Quick Setup

1. Print role cards (one per student; assign roles before class if possible)
2. Arrange room in a fishbowl or small-group configuration (see `teacher-guide.md`)
3. Share or project `scenario.md` — students read individually first
4. Period 1: Role prep + small group discussion
5. Period 2: Full simulation + debrief + reflection

See `teacher-guide.md` for complete facilitation notes.
