# Debrief Guide: Day of AI Surveillance Simulator

**Time:** ~20 minutes (end of Period 2)
**Format:** Whole-class discussion, teacher-facilitated

---

## Purpose of the Debrief

The simulation creates the experience. The debrief creates the learning. Don't skip it, and don't rush through it. The questions below move from concrete → analytical → personal → forward-looking. Work through them in order when possible.

---

## Debrief Question Set

### Round 1: What Just Happened? (3–4 min)
*Concrete, descriptive — helps students process before analyzing.*

1. **How did that feel?** Which moment surprised you most?
2. **Did anyone change their position during the simulation?** What caused it?
3. **What argument was hardest to counter?** Why?

---

### Round 2: Who Benefits? Who Is Harmed? (5–6 min)
*Core ethics framing — directly tied to Module 6 content.*

4. **Who benefits from Project SAFE, and how?** (Push for specifics — not just "safety" but *how*, *when*, *for whom*)

5. **Who is harmed by Project SAFE, and how?** (Push for specifics — *which* students? *what kind* of harm? immediate vs. long-term?)

6. **Are the benefits and harms distributed equally?** Or do some people get most of the benefit while others take most of the risk?

   > 💡 *Teacher note: This is the equity question. Help students see that when harms and benefits are unevenly distributed along lines of race, class, or other identities, that's an equity problem — not just a technical glitch.*

7. **Who was NOT represented in the room?** (e.g., students with disabilities, students experiencing homelessness, students with immigration concerns, students who are LGBTQ+) What might they have said?

---

### Round 3: What Rights Are at Stake? (4–5 min)
*Connects to legal frameworks and the vocabulary in the scenario.*

8. **What rights came up in your discussions?** Make a list on the board as students name them.

   *Likely answers: privacy, free expression, freedom from unreasonable search, due process, right to know what data is collected about you*

9. **Are any of these rights absolute?** Or can they be limited — and if so, by whom, under what conditions?

10. **Who decides where the line is?** In the simulation, who had the most power to shape the outcome? Does that reflect how decisions like this get made in real life?

11. **What's a "chilling effect," and did you experience one in the simulation?** (Did playing a student role make anyone feel like they'd be more careful about what they said or did if Project SAFE were real?)

---

### Round 4: What Would You Change? (4–5 min)
*Design-thinking pivot — moves from critique to construction.*

12. **If you were the school board, how would you vote — and why?** (Quick show of hands, then ask 2–3 students to explain)

13. **Is there a version of this system you'd actually support?** What would have to change? (Push students beyond "none" — even skeptics should be able to articulate conditions)

14. **Who should be in the room when decisions like this are made?** In the simulation, all stakeholders were represented. Is that realistic? What happens when some voices aren't heard?

15. **What would a school that takes student privacy seriously actually look like?** What would it do differently?

---

### Round 5: Connect to Your Own Agent (2–3 min)
*Bridges to the KEY LAB — helps students see the personal relevance.*

16. **Your personal AI agent has access to your conversations, questions, and preferences. How is that different from Project SAFE — or is it?**

17. **When you write a usage policy for your own agent in the Key Lab, who are your stakeholders?** Who could be harmed by your agent? Who benefits?

18. **What's one thing you'd add to your agent's policy based on what you discussed today?**

---

## Key Concepts to Surface During Debrief

Write these on the board as they come up naturally:

| Concept | One-sentence definition |
|---------|------------------------|
| **Surveillance** | Systematic monitoring of people's behavior, activities, or information |
| **Algorithmic bias** | When an AI system produces systematically unfair results for certain groups |
| **Chilling effect** | When surveillance causes people to change behavior out of fear |
| **Disparate impact** | When a policy harms some groups more than others, even if not intentionally |
| **Informed consent** | Agreeing to something with full understanding of what you're agreeing to |
| **Data retention** | How long organizations keep data they've collected |
| **Accountability** | Clear responsibility for what a system does and who fixes it when it goes wrong |

---

## Closing the Debrief

End with one of these:

**Option A — Personal commitment:**
*"Before you write your exit ticket, finish this sentence in your head: 'After today, I think about AI systems differently because…'"*

**Option B — Class synthesis:**
*"If we had to write one sentence that captures what we agreed on today — even if we disagreed about everything else — what would it be?"*
(Take a few responses; look for common ground even across stakeholder lines)

**Option C — Question forward:**
*"What question is sitting with you that we didn't answer today? Write it down. It might be the best question we have."*

---

## After the Simulation: Grading and Assessment

This lab is typically ungraded or participation-graded. The primary assessment is the **exit ticket** and, optionally, the **reflection worksheet** from `student-reflection.md`.

If you want a more formal assessment, consider asking students to write a 1-page stakeholder statement *after* the simulation, from their original role — but now incorporating what they heard from other groups. This shows perspective-taking and argumentation.
