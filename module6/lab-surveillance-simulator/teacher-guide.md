# Teacher Facilitation Guide: Day of AI Surveillance Simulator

---

## At a Glance

| | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Total Time** | 2 class periods (~90–100 min) |
| **Room Setup** | Fishbowl or small groups of 5 |
| **Materials** | Printed role cards, scenario sheet, debrief worksheet |
| **Prep Time** | ~30 min (read scenario + role cards; arrange room) |
| **Sensitive Topics** | Race, privacy, authority, surveillance — see handling notes below |

---

## Preparation (Before Class)

### 1. Read Everything First
Read the full scenario, all five role cards, and the debrief guide before facilitating. You'll need to understand each stakeholder's interests, concerns, and negotiation positions to guide the simulation effectively.

### 2. Assign Roles in Advance (Recommended)
Assigning roles before class gives students time to read their cards and do light preparation. Alternatively, distribute at the start of Period 1 and give 15 minutes of individual reading time.

**Role assignment tips:**
- Consider which students will be most engaged/challenged by each role
- The Student role tends to be very natural for students — consider assigning students who are quieter or sometimes disengaged, as it may increase investment
- The Tech Vendor role is great for students who like to debate or defend positions they don't personally hold
- The Civil Liberties Advocate role works well for students who like research and evidence-based argument

### 3. Room Configuration

**Option A — Fishbowl (recommended for 25–30 students)**
- 5 inner chairs in a circle (one per stakeholder group, facing inward)
- Remaining students sit in an outer ring as "observers" who can later rotate in
- Fishbowl format: inner ring presents and debates; after 15 min, outer ring can "tap in" to replace a speaker

**Option B — Small groups (recommended for 30+ students)**
- Divide class into groups of 5, one student per role per group
- Each group runs the simulation simultaneously
- Debrief as a class afterward, pulling out themes across groups

**Option C — Full class (for classes <25)**
- All students participate simultaneously in their stakeholder role
- Groups of 2–4 represent each stakeholder collectively
- Teacher facilitates the whole-class discussion

### 4. Warm-Up Materials
Optional: Show a 5–8 minute Day of AI video before the simulation. Recommended:
- [Day of AI: Introduction](https://dayofai.org) — sets up AI decision-making context
- Search YouTube: "school surveillance AI" for recent news clips (preview first)

## Period 1: Setup and Role Preparation (~45 min)

### Opening (10 min)
1. Frame the lab: *"Today you're going to make a real decision that schools across the country are actually making right now."*
2. Distribute scenario sheets. Give students 5–7 minutes to read individually.
3. Brief whole-class check: *"What are your first reactions? What surprises you?"* (Don't go deep yet — just surface initial intuitions.)

### Role Card Distribution (5 min)
- Distribute role cards (pre-assigned or random)
- Students read their role card silently: 5 min

### Role Group Prep (20 min)
Students with the same role find each other and prepare together:
- What is your group's core position?
- What are your three strongest arguments?
- What questions will you ask other groups?
- What is your "red line" — what would you never accept?
- What might you be willing to negotiate?

**Circulate during this time.** Help groups who are stuck (see facilitation moves below). Avoid giving answers — push back with questions.

### Gallery Preview (10 min)
Optional: Each group gives a 60-second "preview" of their position to the class. This builds anticipation for the simulation and lets you check group understanding before the main event.

---

## Period 2: Simulation and Debrief (~50 min)

### Simulation Setup (5 min)
- Set up room in fishbowl or small-group format
- Remind students: *"Stay in your role during the simulation. Even if you personally disagree, argue for your stakeholder's interests. That's the point."*
- Establish ground rules: one speaker at a time, listen to understand not just to respond, no personal attacks (critique the position, not the person)

### Opening Statements (15 min)
Each stakeholder group gives a 2–3 minute opening statement. You can keep time with a visible timer.

**Suggested order:**
1. School Administrator
2. Tech Vendor
3. Parents
4. Students
5. Civil Liberties Advocates

### Open Discussion / Community Meeting (20 min)
Open the floor for cross-stakeholder debate. Your role is to:
- Manage turn-taking
- Ask follow-up questions that deepen the debate (see prompts below)
- Note key moments/arguments on the board
- Push groups that aren't engaging with each other
- Prevent one group from dominating

**Useful facilitation prompts during simulation:**
- *"[Stakeholder], how do you respond to what [Stakeholder] just said?"*
- *"Is there any version of this proposal you could support? What would have to change?"*
- *"[Students], I noticed the vendor said X. What's your reaction?"*
- *"What happens to the students who aren't in this room — who don't have advocates here?"*

### Closing Votes (5 min)
*"The school board now has to vote. Each group, give us your final position: approve, reject, or approve with conditions. 30 seconds each."*

Keep a visible tally. (The vote doesn't have to be realistic — it's a launch point for debrief.)

### Debrief (20 min)
Use `debrief-guide.md`. Don't skip this — it's where the learning crystallizes.

### Exit Ticket (5 min)
Distribute or project the exit ticket from `student-reflection.md`. Students complete individually before leaving.

## Facilitation Moves for Common Situations

### 🟡 Group Is Stuck / Has Nothing to Say

Try:
- *"What's the one thing your stakeholder is most afraid of in this scenario?"*
- *"Forget the policy for a second — what does your person actually *want* in their daily life?"*
- *"If this system goes wrong, who in your group gets hurt first?"*
- Give them a specific quote from the scenario to react to: *"DataGuard says this system has a 95% accuracy rate. What does your group think about that number?"*

### 🔴 Strong Emotional Reactions

Some students — particularly those who have experienced surveillance, policing, or discrimination — may react strongly to this scenario. This is appropriate. Do not shut it down.

**Do:**
- Acknowledge the emotion: *"This is real, and it matters that you're feeling that."*
- Connect it to the content: *"That feeling you're having? That's called the chilling effect. Let's put that on the board."*
- Invite the student to channel it into their role: *"That reaction is exactly what your role card is talking about. How do you want to say that in the meeting?"*

**Don't:**
- Tell students to "calm down" or "keep it academic"
- Dismiss personal experience as anecdote
- Move on quickly without acknowledgment

**If a student discloses personal experience with surveillance, policing, or discrimination:**
- Take it seriously
- If appropriate, step outside the simulation briefly: *"Let's step out of our roles for a second."*
- Follow your school's protocols for supporting students who disclose
- Circle back to the student after class

### 🔵 One Group Is Dominating

- Use the fishbowl to rotate speakers: *"Let's hear from a different stakeholder. [Group], you've been quiet — what's your response?"*
- Ask the dominant group to "step back": *"You've made your case clearly. I want to give other groups a chance. Hold that thought."*
- Use a talking stick or physical object to manage turn-taking

### 🟠 Students Aren't Staying in Role

This is fine early in the simulation — students often break role to express their own views. 

- Gently redirect: *"What would [your stakeholder] say about that?"*
- If a student is clearly more engaged out of role, you can lean into it: *"It sounds like you personally feel strongly about this. Let's hold that for debrief."*
- In debrief, explicitly ask: *"Did anyone find it hard to stay in their role? Why?"*

### 🟣 The Simulation Is Getting Heated / Unproductive

Signs: Students are talking over each other, personal comments, escalating frustration.

Steps:
1. Pause the simulation: *"Let's take 60 seconds. Everyone breathe."*
2. Restate ground rules: *"We're critiquing positions, not people. We're in roles."*
3. If necessary, de-role everyone: *"Step out of your characters for a moment. We're students in a classroom talking about a hard topic. What's happening right now?"*
4. Process the dynamics briefly, then either restart or move to debrief

Heated debate isn't a failure — if it's substantive and not personal, it means students are genuinely invested. Lean into it with facilitation rather than shutting it down.

## Extensions and Variations

**Shorter version (1 period):** Skip Period 1 role prep. Assign roles the day before and ask students to prepare homework. Run only the simulation and debrief.

**Deeper version:** Have students research real AI surveillance systems deployed in schools (there are many documented cases) and present findings before taking on roles.

**Cross-curricular:** Connect to social studies / civics (rights, governance, public meetings), ELA (persuasive writing — turn opening statements into a written argument).

**Guest speakers:** Invite a school counselor, a local civil liberties attorney, or a technology professional to observe the simulation and respond during debrief.
