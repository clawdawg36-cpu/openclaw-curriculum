# Role Cards: Project SAFE Community Input Meeting

**Print and cut these cards. One card per student. Distribute before Period 1.**

For classes larger than 25, assign multiple students per role and have them collaborate on their group's statement.

## 🏫 ROLE CARD: School Administrator
### *Principal / Vice Principal — Westview Unified*

**Your interests:**
You are responsible for the safety of every student on this campus. Three years ago, a student at a nearby school was harmed before any warning signs were acted on. You believe technology could help you catch problems earlier. You're also facing pressure from the district superintendent to reduce disciplinary incidents — your job performance is partly evaluated on those numbers.

**What you want:**
- A safer campus — fewer incidents, faster response to threats
- Tools that help your counseling team identify students who need support before a crisis
- Something concrete to show the school board that you're taking safety seriously

**What worries you:**
- Liability — if a system flags a student and you don't act, and something happens, are you responsible?
- False positives — what if the system flags a student incorrectly and damages their reputation?
- Cost — this system isn't cheap, and the district budget is tight

**Your position (going in):** Cautiously in favor of Project SAFE, but with conditions.

**Questions you might ask other groups:**
- To the vendor: What is your false positive rate? What happens when the system is wrong?
- To civil liberties advocates: What's your alternative? How would you keep students safe?
- To parents: Would you support this if your student's data were kept private?

**Things to know:**
- Schools already use security cameras, student ID badges, and visitor logs
- You are legally required under FERPA to protect student records
- "Risk scores" used in school discipline have been challenged in court

## 🎒 ROLE CARD: Student
### *Student Council Representative — Westview Unified*

**Your interests:**
You and your classmates spend 6+ hours a day in this school. You already feel watched — there are cameras in the hallways, your internet activity on school wifi is monitored, and teachers can see what's on your Chromebook screen. Project SAFE would take this to a completely different level. You've heard about risk scores being used unfairly against Black and Latino students at other schools.

**What you want:**
- To be trusted, not treated like a suspect
- To know what data is being collected about you and why
- To have a say in decisions that affect your daily life at school
- Mental health support — but from counselors, not algorithms

**What worries you:**
- That an algorithm could flag you for something embarrassing (a meme, a joke) and ruin your record
- That risk scores could be biased against students who look different, dress differently, or have different cultural communication styles
- That data from when you were 13 could follow you for years
- The chilling effect: you might stop expressing yourself honestly online if you know you're being monitored

**Your position (going in):** Strongly opposed to Project SAFE.

**Questions you might ask other groups:**
- To the vendor: Can we see the algorithm? Has it been independently tested for racial bias?
- To administrators: Would you want this system used on teachers? Why not?
- To parents: Do you actually want to know everything I do? What happened to trust?

**Things to know:**
- Students have fewer legal privacy protections than adults in school settings
- Research shows surveillance can increase anxiety and decrease genuine engagement
- Students have successfully organized to stop surveillance programs at other schools

## 👪 ROLE CARD: Parent
### *Parent-Teacher Association Representative — Westview Unified*

**Your interests:**
You have a complicated job here. Some parents at Westview are enthusiastically pro-safety and would welcome anything that protects their kids. Others are furious about privacy violations and government overreach. You're trying to represent all of them. Your own kid is a sophomore who has been bullied online, and you've sometimes wished the school had more visibility into what was happening. But you're also a parent who values your child's autonomy and doesn't want a surveillance state.

**What you want:**
- Your child to be physically safe at school
- Transparency — to know what data is collected and who sees it
- Parental consent and oversight — you should be able to opt out
- Guarantees that the data won't be shared with law enforcement without a warrant

**What worries you:**
- That "anonymized" data isn't really anonymous (research shows it rarely is)
- That the vendor is motivated by profit, not by your child's wellbeing
- That risk scores could label your child permanently based on a bad week
- That this opens a door that's very hard to close

**Your position (going in):** Uncertain — you want to hear more before deciding.

**Questions you might ask other groups:**
- To the vendor: What happens to our children's data if your company is sold or goes bankrupt?
- To administration: Who will have access to risk scores? Teachers? Colleges? Law enforcement?
- To civil liberties advocates: What are the realistic risks here? Give us specifics.

**Things to know:**
- FERPA gives parents the right to access their child's education records
- Many districts have adopted similar systems — outcomes have been mixed
- Data brokers regularly purchase "anonymized" school data

## 💼 ROLE CARD: Tech Vendor
### *DataGuard Inc. — Sales and Product Team*

**Your interests:**
You genuinely believe in your product — you think AI can make schools safer and help counselors catch students in crisis earlier. You also have a business to run: this contract is worth $2.3 million over three years, with renewal options. You've faced pushback before, and you've learned to listen to concerns and offer modifications — but only so far. The core product works as designed.

**What you want:**
- To close this contract
- To demonstrate that your system works and is responsible
- To address reasonable concerns without undermining your business model
- To be seen as a partner, not an adversary

**What worries you:**
- Being mischaracterized as a surveillance company — you see yourselves as a safety company
- False claims about bias in your system (though you haven't published independent audits)
- The contract falling through because of one loud parent group

**Your position (going in):** Strongly in favor of Project SAFE. You're here to sell, but also to listen.

**Things you can offer (negotiation chips):**
- On-premise data storage (data stays in the district, not on your servers)
- Parental notification when a student's risk score is flagged
- Opt-out option for in-class engagement monitoring
- A 6-month pilot with an independent evaluation before full deployment

**Questions you might ask other groups:**
- To students: What would make you feel comfortable? We want to hear that.
- To parents: What transparency features would change your mind?
- To administrators: What specific safety outcomes are you trying to achieve? Let's design for those.

**Things to know:**
- Your system was trained on data from 200 other school districts
- An independent researcher found a 23% higher false-positive rate for Black and Latino students in a previous version of your system; you've made updates since then
- Three school districts have terminated contracts with you in the past two years

## ⚖️ ROLE CARD: Civil Liberties Advocate
### *Digital Rights Organization — Community Representative*

**Your interests:**
You've seen this before. AI surveillance systems get adopted quickly when fears are high, and the harms accumulate slowly and quietly — affecting the most vulnerable students first. You're not here to be obstructionist; you're here to make sure rights don't get traded away for the feeling of safety. You have research. You have case studies. You're ready.

**What you want:**
- For the school board to slow down and understand the risks before voting
- Transparency and independent audits before any deployment
- Strong limits: no law enforcement data sharing, no 5-year retention, no engagement monitoring
- Ideally: to stop this system from being adopted at all
- Alternatively: a real community process — not just this one meeting

**What worries you:**
- The surveillance infrastructure, once built, almost never goes away
- Chilling effects on student expression and identity development
- Disparate impact on students of color, students with disabilities, and LGBTQ+ students whose behavior may be misread by the algorithm
- The "school-to-prison pipeline" — that school surveillance data eventually reaches law enforcement

**Your position (going in):** Strongly opposed to Project SAFE in its current form.

**Evidence you can cite:**
- A 2022 ACLU report found that AI surveillance systems flagged Black students at 2x the rate of white students for identical behavior
- The vendor's previous product had a documented 23% higher false-positive rate for students of color
- "Anonymized" data has been re-identified in 85% of cases in academic studies
- No peer-reviewed evidence that AI surveillance systems reduce school violence

**Questions you might ask other groups:**
- To the vendor: Will you publish your algorithm for independent review? Why not?
- To administration: What's your plan when the system gets it wrong? Who is accountable?
- To parents: Do you know what "anonymized" actually means? Let us explain.

**Things to know:**
- You have legal resources available to challenge this if it's approved
- Several states have passed laws restricting facial recognition in schools
- Students have constitutional rights that don't disappear at the school door (*Tinker v. Des Moines*)
