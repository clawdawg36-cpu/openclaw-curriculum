# Lesson 6.3 — AI Surveillance Simulator

**Module:** 6 — AI Ethics, Bias, and Accountability  
**Duration:** 2 periods (~100 minutes total)  
**Grade Level:** 7–8 (adaptable for 9–12)  
**Prerequisites:** Lessons 6.1–6.2

---

## ⚠️ Facilitation Priority Notice

**Lesson 6.3 is the emotional center of Module 6.** The AI Surveillance Simulator reliably generates strong reactions — including anger, discomfort, feelings of powerlessness, and occasionally distress. This is pedagogically valuable and should not be suppressed. However, it must be facilitated with care.

**Before teaching this lesson:**
- Read Section 9 of the Module 3 Teacher Guide in full
- Review the "Deescalation & Validation" section of this lesson plan
- Identify any students who may have heightened sensitivity to surveillance themes (undocumented students, students with incarcerated family members, students who've experienced discrimination by law enforcement)
- Consider alerting the school counselor that this activity may generate referrals
- Know your school's protocol for student disclosures

The strong emotional reactions this lesson generates are **not a problem to be managed** — they are **evidence of learning**. Students who feel the weight of surveillance are building the empathy required for responsible AI development. Your job is to hold that space safely, not flatten it.

---

## 🔗 Through-Line Connection

> *"You've seen bias in words and in hiring decisions and in who gets arrested. Now you're going to feel what it's like to be inside a system that watches everyone — and you're going to play every role in it."*

The simulation places students in three distinct positions: the surveilled citizen, the analyst interpreting data, and the city administrator making policy decisions. Each role sees different information. Each role has different incentives. By rotating through all three, students internalize the structural problem: surveillance systems produce harm not because the people running them are evil, but because each person in the system is doing their job.

This is the hardest idea in Module 6. The simulation is the vehicle for it.

**Bridge to Lesson 6.4:** The surveillance simulator ends with a feeling. Lesson 6.4 begins with data — specifically, the personal data trail that makes surveillance possible in the first place. Students carry their emotional response from 6.3 into the quieter, more analytical investigation of 6.4.

---

## 🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Describe how AI surveillance systems work from at least two different stakeholder perspectives.
2. Explain why surveillance harms can emerge from systems where no individual actor intended harm.
3. Analyze the tradeoffs between public safety and civil liberties in AI-assisted surveillance.
4. Articulate how algorithmic bias intersects with surveillance to concentrate harm on specific communities.
5. Identify at least one structural intervention (not just individual behavior change) that could reduce surveillance harm.

## 🤝 Community Agreements (Post Before Class)

Write these on the board or chart paper. Reference them at the start of the activity:

1. **What happens in the room stays in the room.** (Personal disclosures are not for gossip.)
2. **You can pass.** (No one is required to share a personal experience.)
3. **We listen before we respond.** (Especially when we disagree.)
4. **Anger is information.** (If you feel something strongly, that's data worth examining.)
5. **We're looking at systems, not people.** (Today is about structures, not villains.)

## ⏱️ Lesson Outline

### Period 1: Roles, Data, and the Simulation

#### Opening: Setting the Stage (8 min)

**Read aloud (slowly, don't rush):**

> *"In this activity, you're going to step into a city. It's not that different from where you live. AI cameras track movement through downtown. A predictive system flags 'persons of interest.' A dashboard lets analysts see patterns. The city uses this data to allocate resources — and sometimes, to intervene.*
> 
> *You'll each play a role. You'll see what your character sees. You'll make decisions your character has to make. And then we'll come back together and talk about what that felt like.*
> 
> *Some of what happens in this simulation might feel uncomfortably real. That's intentional. If at any point you need to step back, you can.*
> 
> *Let's begin."*

Post and read community agreements.

**Role assignment (3 min):**
Distribute role cards. Students silently read their role. They do not share with other groups yet.

#### The Three Roles

**Role A: The Surveilled Citizen**
- Character: "Alex" — 17 years old, lives in a neighborhood that's been "flagged" as high-risk by the city's AI system
- Alex has no criminal record. Alex takes the same route to work every day — a route that passes through two flagged zones.
- Alex's data: location history, purchase patterns, social media connections, device fingerprinting
- Alex's prompt: "You are Alex. You don't know you're being watched. You just know your life."

**Role B: The Analyst**
- Character: City security analyst reviewing the AI dashboard
- Has access to aggregated movement data, risk scores, and flagged individuals
- Is responsible for reviewing alerts and deciding whether to escalate to police
- Alex appears on the dashboard 3 times this week with a "risk score" of 73/100
- Analyst's prompt: "You have limited time and 47 other alerts today. What do you do with Alex?"

**Role C: The City Administrator**
- Character: Deputy director who approved the AI surveillance contract
- Believes the system reduces crime; has data showing 12% reduction in reported incidents in surveilled areas
- Is now fielding a complaint from a civil liberties organization about disparate surveillance of communities of color
- Administrator's prompt: "You signed the contract. You believe in the system. What do you say?"

---

#### Segment 1: In-Role Reading and Decision (15 min)

Each group reads their scenario packet. Packets contain:
- Character background (1 page)
- Simulated data relevant to their role (1 page)
- Decision prompt: What does your character do next?

Groups discuss within role: *"What would Alex do? What would the analyst do? What would the administrator do?"*

Each group writes their decision on a sticky note.

**Teacher: circulate, don't direct.** Listen for moments of discomfort. Note which groups are struggling — that's exactly right.

---

#### Segment 2: The Revelation (10 min)

**Bridge (say this):**
> *"Now I'm going to tell you what the simulation knows that the characters don't."*

Reveal:
1. Alex is flagged partly because of who Alex follows on social media — several friends who were arrested (but not convicted) show up as "connections to high-risk individuals"
2. The analyst's dashboard doesn't show *why* someone is flagged — only the score and frequency data
3. The administrator's 12% reduction in "reported incidents" includes incidents that residents stopped reporting because they distrusted the system

Let students react. Give them 60 seconds to sit with this before asking anything.

---

#### Segment 3: Cross-Role Discussion (12 min)

Rearrange into new groups: each new group has one Role A, one Role B, one Role C student.

Discussion prompts:
- *"What did your character know that other characters didn't?"*
- *"Could any one person in this system have stopped what happened to Alex? How?"*
- *"Is anyone in this simulation a villain? Why or why not?"*

**Teacher: listen for the key insight** — no individual actor needed to be malicious for the system to produce harm. Amplify this when you hear it.

---

### ⏸️ Period 1 Close (5 min)

**Anonymous reaction cards:**

Give each student an index card. They write (not put their name on):
- One word that describes how they feel right now
- One question they have that the simulation raised

Collect. Read a few aloud (not attributing to anyone). Honor what you hear.

> *"These reactions are real. They're pointing at something real. We'll keep going tomorrow."*

---

### Period 2: Analysis, Deescalation, and Structure

#### Opening: Coming Back In (5 min)

**Read back 3–5 anonymous reactions from Period 1** (your choice of which to highlight — include the angry ones, the sad ones, and the questioning ones).

> *"Yesterday you were inside the system. Today we're going to step back and look at the whole thing."*

---

#### Segment 4: What Just Happened? (15 min)

**Structured whole-class debrief** — use the whiteboard to build a collective map:

1. Draw three circles: Surveilled Person | Analyst | Administrator
2. In each circle: What did they know? What did they decide?
3. Between circles: What information was missing? What created the harm?

Guide students toward this framework:
- **Information asymmetry** — each actor has partial information
- **Accountability diffusion** — no single person is clearly responsible
- **Algorithmic opacity** — the score exists but its logic doesn't
- **Feedback loop** — flagged neighborhoods get more attention → more flags

Write these terms on the board. Students copy to journals.

---

#### Segment 5: The Real-World Anchors (10 min)

Connect the simulation to real systems:
- **Palantir and city surveillance contracts** — widely used by police departments across the US
- **China's Social Credit system** — population-scale scoring of citizen behavior
- **Chicago's Strategic Subject List** — predictive "heat list" of people likely to be involved in violence
- **Amazon Ring + police partnerships** — private surveillance infrastructure shared with law enforcement

Ask: *"Which of these feels most like the simulation? Which feels most different? Why?"*

This is not a rhetorical question — students may have more nuanced answers than you expect.

---

#### Segment 6: Structural Interventions (10 min)

**Shift from analysis to design:**

> *"We've been good at identifying what's broken. That's necessary — but it's not enough. What would you change? Not about Alex's behavior. Not about the analyst's attitude. About the structure."*

Introduce three levels of intervention:

| Level | Examples |
|-------|---------|
| **Technical** | Require explainability (show why the score, not just the score); bias audits before deployment |
| **Organizational** | Require human review before any action on AI flags; ban certain data types from scoring |
| **Legal/Policy** | Require city council approval for surveillance contracts; ban predictive policing outright |

Groups: Each group proposes ONE structural intervention. Defend it to the class.

*Listen for: Students often want to "fix the algorithm." Push them toward structural and policy interventions — the technical fix doesn't help if the system is structurally unjust.*

---

#### Closing: The Way Through (10 min)

**Full class, open floor:**

> *"Before we close, I want to name something: this lesson is hard. Surveillance, profiling, and the way AI can amplify existing inequalities — these are real harms. You've felt some of that today. That feeling is appropriate. It means you understand what's at stake.*
> 
> *But here's the other side: you're learning to build these systems. That's not a small thing. The people who build AI get to make choices about how it's designed, what safeguards exist, and who it's accountable to. You're going to be in that role — or watching people in that role — for the rest of your lives.*
> 
> *What do you do with this?"*

Open for 5 minutes of genuine student response. Don't rush to a neat conclusion. The ambiguity is the point.

**Exit ticket:**
Students write two sentences:
1. *"The structural intervention I'd most want to see in real surveillance systems is ___."*
2. *"What I carry out of this simulation is ___."*

---

## 💬 Discussion Questions

**Warm-up level:**
- If you knew a camera was tracking you right now, would you change your behavior? How?
- Is there a difference between "being watched for safety" and "being surveilled"? What is it?

**Core discussion:**
- In the simulation, did any character do something wrong? Or did the system produce harm without anyone being at fault?
- What's the difference between a system that watches "everyone equally" and a system that surveils equally?
- Who should be allowed to use data collected for one purpose (crime prevention) for a different purpose (immigration enforcement, labor organizing monitoring)?

**Extension / advanced:**
- What is the "chilling effect" and how does surveillance produce it even when no one acts on the data?
- What are the strongest arguments for AI-assisted surveillance in public spaces? Are there cases where you think it's justified?
- How would you design a transparency system that would allow citizens to audit surveillance systems being used in their city?

## 🚨 Deescalation & Validation Guide

*(Referenced from Module 3 Teacher Guide Section 9 — adapted for surveillance context)*

### If a Student Gets Angry at the Simulation

**Don't suppress it.** Anger at injustice is an appropriate response. Validate and redirect:
> *"That anger is telling you something important. What is it telling you?"*

If the anger is escalating toward shutting down discussion:
> *"I want to give everyone a chance to process this. Let's take 60 seconds of quiet — you can write, draw, or just breathe — and then we'll come back."*

### If a Student Shares a Personal Connection to Surveillance or Policing

- Thank them for sharing: *"I hear you. That takes courage."*
- Don't make them the lesson's representative: *"What [student] described is a real experience, and it connects directly to what we're studying."*
- After class: Check in privately. Does the counselor need to know? Does the student need support?
- If they seem distressed: *"Do you want to step out for a few minutes? I can have someone come with you."*

### If a Student Defends the Surveillance System

This is a legitimate position that deserves engagement, not dismissal:
> *"Let's take that seriously. What specific harm does surveillance prevent? And who bears the cost of that protection?"*

Use the Socratic moves from Module 3 Section 9:
- *"What would have to be true for this system to be fair?"*
- *"If you lived in Alex's neighborhood, would your assessment change? Why or why not?"*

### If Students Become Paralyzed ("Nothing Can Change")

This happens. Address it directly:
> *"The feeling that this is too big to change is actually a product of the system — systems that feel inevitable are harder to challenge. But these systems were designed by people, adopted by governments, and can be changed by organized human effort. Let me show you three examples where they were."*

Then briefly mention:
- San Francisco's ban on city use of facial recognition (2019)
- Detroit's ACLU challenge to facial recognition police use
- New York City's ban on predictive policing tools in schools

### Signs You Need to Pause the Simulation

- Multiple students visibly distressed (not just thoughtfully quiet)
- A student makes a disclosure that requires mandated reporting
- The room becomes too chaotic to facilitate safely

If you pause: *"We're going to take a break. This is hard material and we're all human. [Pause 5 minutes.] We'll come back when everyone is ready."*

## 🎯 Differentiation

### Support Strategies

| Need | Strategy |
|------|----------|
| Students who find role-play uncomfortable | Allow "journalist" role — observe and report instead of playing a character |
| ELL students | Provide simplified character cards in L1; allow pair discussion in L1 |
| Students with trauma history related to policing | Opt-in role assignment; always have the option to observe and reflect in writing |
| Students who struggle with abstract structural thinking | Use the "Who knew what?" chart as a concrete anchor for the discussion |

### Extension Strategies

| Need | Strategy |
|------|----------|
| Advanced learners | Research one real-world surveillance contract (Palantir, Axon, ShotSpotter) — what data does it collect and who has access? |
| Policy-minded students | Draft a model city ordinance regulating AI surveillance — what would it require? |
| Tech-minded students | Investigate what data inputs produce a "risk score" in predictive policing tools — what are the proxy variables? |
| Fast finishers | Write a letter from Alex to the city council explaining what the simulation felt like from the inside |
