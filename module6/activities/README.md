## 📋 Activity Summaries

### 1. Stakeholder Mapping (4 files)
Students analyze who wins and who loses from a given AI system. The universal template can be used with any AI system; three case study sheets provide structured scenarios with built-in context and reflection questions.

- **Case Study 1:** AI hiring algorithm (bias in automated resume screening)
- **Case Study 2:** Predictive policing AI (feedback loops, racial disparate impact)
- **Case Study 3:** Social media recommendation algorithm (engagement optimization, teen harm)

**Estimated time:** 30–45 minutes per case study

---

### 2. AI Debate (2 files)
Students prepare for and conduct structured class debates on contested AI ethics questions. The prep guide walks students through building arguments, anticipating counterarguments, and writing an opening statement. The format cards provide 4 debate topics and a complete moderated debate format.

**Debate Topics:**
1. AI should make final decisions in schools (grades, discipline, college recommendations)
2. Social media companies should be legally required to publish their recommendation algorithms
3. AI surveillance in schools (facial recognition, behavior monitoring) should be permitted
4. Developers should be personally and legally liable when their AI causes harm

**Estimated time:** 1–2 class periods (prep + debate)

---

### 3. Real or AI? (1 file)
Students evaluate 20 examples (10 text + 10 images) and decide whether each was created by a human or AI. Includes detection heuristics, a scoring section, and reflection questions about what the activity reveals about media literacy.

**Note:** Teacher must supply the 10 image examples (displayed via projector or printed handouts). The image section includes blank evaluation rows for each.

**Estimated time:** 30–40 minutes

---

### 4. Data Trail (1 file)
Students trace every data point they generate in a single school day — from morning routines through after-school activities. Includes structured tables for logging, a deeper analysis section, and reflection on privacy laws (FERPA, COPPA).

**Estimated time:** 45–60 minutes (can be assigned as homework + in-class discussion)

---

### 5. Redesign It (1 file)
Students select an AI system they believe is unfair, document the problem, define fairness principles, propose specific changes, and honestly assess trade-offs. Includes a before/after comparison table and a 3-sentence pitch.

**Estimated time:** 45–60 minutes (works well as in-class or homework)

---

### 6. Fairness Proposal (1 file)
Students write a formal 1-page proposal calling for change to a specific AI system or practice. The template provides a section-by-section structure with a built-in rubric (24 points) and a self-assessment checklist.

**Estimated time:** 60–90 minutes writing time + revision

## 🖨️ Print Notes

All files are designed to be both print-ready and usable digitally.

- **Stakeholder Mapping Template:** 2 pages recommended (template + questions)
- **Debate Format Cards:** Print and cut — each card fits on a half-page
- **Real or AI Sheet:** 4–5 pages; consider double-sided printing
- **Data Trail:** 4 pages; works well as a booklet
- **Redesign It:** 3–4 pages
- **Fairness Proposal:** 2 pages (template + rubric)
