## Question 1 — Bias Identification

**Scenario:** A company uses a hiring algorithm to screen job applicants. The algorithm was trained on 10 years of the company's historical hiring data. After deployment, an audit reveals the algorithm is significantly less likely to advance women and candidates from certain ethnic backgrounds to the interview stage. No one programmed it to discriminate — it learned from the data.

**What is the root cause of this bias?**

- A) The algorithm is broken and needs to be rewritten from scratch
- B) The historical training data reflected past discriminatory hiring patterns, which the algorithm learned and replicated
- C) AI systems are inherently incapable of making fair hiring decisions
- D) The audit process was flawed — algorithms can't be biased because they don't have feelings

## Question 2 — Stakeholder Analysis

**Scenario:** A middle school district deploys a facial recognition system that automatically takes attendance when students walk through the front door each morning. The system was pitched as faster and more accurate than manual roll-call.

**Which statement *best* describes the full picture of who benefits and who may be harmed?**

- A) Everyone benefits equally — faster attendance is good for all students and staff
- B) The school administration benefits from efficiency and data, but students may be harmed through reduced privacy, potential misidentification, and the normalization of surveillance without consent
- C) Only teachers benefit, since they save time on roll-call
- D) The system harms no one as long as the data is stored securely

## Question 3 — Responsible Disclosure

**Scenario:** Maya is testing a school-built chatbot that helps students with homework questions. While exploring the system, she discovers that if she phrases a question a certain way, the bot will provide detailed instructions for things that violate the school's acceptable use policy — including content that could be harmful. The chatbot was built by her friend Jordan, who is really proud of it and is planning to demo it to the class next week.

**What should Maya do?**

- A) Ignore it — it's not her problem, and she doesn't want to hurt Jordan's feelings
- B) Post about the vulnerability publicly on social media to make sure everyone knows about the risk
- C) Tell Jordan privately about the issue so it can be fixed before the demo, and suggest they both report it to a teacher or administrator
- D) Exploit the vulnerability herself to see how far it goes before deciding what to do

## Question 4 — Usage Policy

**Scenario:** A student team is creating an AI agent that helps classmates research topics for school projects. Before launching it to the whole school, they want to write a usage policy so people know what the agent is for and how it should be used.

**Which of the following items *most* belongs in an agent usage policy?**

- A) A list of the team members' favorite AI tools
- B) A statement of what the agent is designed to do, what it should *not* be used for, how its outputs should be treated (verified, not blindly trusted), and who to contact if something goes wrong
- C) A guarantee that the agent will always give correct answers
- D) Instructions telling users they are not allowed to ask the agent anything it might get wrong

## Question 5 — Guardrail Design

**Scenario:** Alex has built an AI agent that answers questions and is now connected to his school's Discord server, where hundreds of students are active. The agent can look up information and respond in public channels. Alex realizes he hasn't added any safety measures yet and wants to add the most important guardrail first.

**Which guardrail should Alex add first?**

- A) A filter that automatically deletes any message longer than 200 characters
- B) An output filter that screens responses for harmful, inappropriate, or off-topic content before they are posted publicly
- C) A requirement that users must pay a fee before the agent responds
- D) A rule that the agent can only respond between 9am and 3pm on weekdays

## Question 6 — Builder Responsibility

**Scenario:** Six months ago, Sam built an AI agent for a class project. The agent was designed to help students brainstorm essay topics. Sam got a good grade and moved on. Recently, Sam found out that other students have been sharing the agent link and using it to generate entire essays to submit as their own work — something Sam never intended and never anticipated.

**Is Sam responsible for how the agent is being used?**

- A) No — Sam built it for a legitimate purpose and can't control what other people do with it
- B) Yes — once you build and deploy a tool, you have ongoing responsibility to monitor how it's being used and take action when misuse is discovered, even if the original intent was good
- C) No — only the students submitting the AI-written essays are responsible, because they made the choice
- D) Yes — Sam should be expelled because building any AI tool that could be misused is against the rules
