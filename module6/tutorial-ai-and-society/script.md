# Script: AI and Society
**Module 6 — Online Tutorial**
**Target duration:** ~8 minutes (480 seconds)
**Format:** Narrated animation/screen-recorded presentation with on-screen graphics

---

## [00:00–00:45] INTRO — The Story of Jordan

*[Narrator speaks over an illustrated opening scene: a person at a desk, laptop open, applying for jobs]*

> "Meet Jordan. Jordan just graduated from college with a degree in marketing, a solid GPA, and two internships on their résumé. They spent three weeks carefully applying to 47 jobs online. For 31 of those jobs, they never heard back. Not a rejection email. Not a phone call. Just silence."

> "Jordan figured maybe they had the wrong experience, or maybe the job market was just tough. What Jordan didn't know — what Jordan *couldn't* know — was that many of those applications were reviewed not by a human, but by an algorithm. A piece of software that scanned the application, assigned it a score, and filtered it out before any human ever saw it."

> "This tutorial is about that algorithm — and what it represents. Because Jordan's story isn't unusual. It's happening to millions of people every day. And as AI systems become more powerful and more widespread, understanding how they work, who builds them, and who they affect isn't optional anymore. It's essential."

**[On-screen text]:** "AI and Society: Who builds it. Who it affects. What we can do."

---

## [00:45–01:45] WHO BUILDS AI?

*[Visual shifts to a stylized "behind the scenes" view: an AI company office, diverse-looking but skewed demographic chart, engineering team]*

> "Let's start with a basic question: who actually builds AI systems?"

> "The teams that design AI tools — especially commercial ones used in hiring, lending, healthcare, and criminal justice — are overwhelmingly concentrated in a small number of large tech companies. Most of those companies are headquartered in a handful of cities: San Francisco, Seattle, New York, London, Beijing."

> "And within those companies, the engineering and machine learning teams that build AI are not representative of the broader population. Surveys consistently show that AI teams skew heavily toward men, toward people with advanced degrees from elite universities, and toward people who are white or Asian. The communities most *affected* by high-stakes AI — working-class communities, communities of color, people with disabilities, people without college degrees — are drastically underrepresented among the people *building* it."

> "Why does that matter? Because who builds a system shapes what that system sees as normal, what it optimizes for, and what problems it even notices. It doesn't mean AI engineers are bad people. It means they have blind spots. And blind spots, at scale, cause real harm."

**[On-screen callout]:** "Homogeneous teams → homogeneous assumptions → biased systems"

---

## [01:45–02:45] WHO AI AFFECTS — PART 1: JORDAN'S APPLICATION

*[Visual returns to Jordan's story. Animated flowchart: résumé → scan → score → filtered]*

> "Let's go back to Jordan. When they submitted a job application, it entered an Applicant Tracking System — or ATS. These systems are used by over 98% of Fortune 500 companies and many smaller employers too."

> "The ATS does several things: it parses your résumé into structured data, it compares that data to the job description using keyword matching and sometimes a machine learning model, and it scores each applicant. The top scorers move forward. Everyone else is filtered out — often automatically, with no human review."

> "Here's the thing about Jordan: their résumé used a two-column layout because they thought it looked professional. Some ATS systems can't correctly parse two-column PDFs. The system may have seen a partially blank résumé — and scored it near zero."

> "Or maybe the job description asked for 'marketing automation experience' and Jordan wrote 'email campaign management' — same skill, different words. The algorithm didn't know they were equivalent."

> "Or maybe — and this is where it gets harder — the model was trained on résumés from employees who were historically hired at that company. And historically, that company hired mostly from certain schools, certain neighborhoods, certain backgrounds. Jordan didn't fit that pattern. Not because of their skills. Because of who they are and where they came from."

**[On-screen callout]:** "Same skill, different words — filtered out. Same qualifications, different background — scored lower."

---

## [02:45–03:45] HOW BIAS ENTERS SYSTEMS

*[Animated diagram: data pipeline showing bias entry points — historical data, proxy variables, feedback loops]*

> "How does bias end up in an AI system? It's usually not intentional. But it is predictable. There are three main entry points."

> "**First: historical data.** AI systems learn from data — and data reflects the world as it was, not as it should be. If a company historically hired mostly men for engineering roles, a model trained on that hiring history will learn that 'male' correlates with 'successful engineer.' It's not that the model is sexist; it's that the data was. The model faithfully learned the wrong lesson."

> "**Second: proxy variables.** Sometimes the algorithm doesn't use a sensitive attribute directly — like race or gender — but it uses something that *correlates* with it. Zip code can be a proxy for race. The name of a college can be a proxy for class. Even hobbies can carry demographic signals. Amazon famously had to scrap an AI hiring tool because it downgraded résumés that included the word 'women's' — as in 'women's chess club' or 'women's university.' It learned that pattern from historical hiring data. It wasn't told to penalize women. It figured that out on its own."

> "**Third: feedback loops.** When a biased model makes decisions, those decisions become the next round of training data. If the model keeps filtering out applicants from certain neighborhoods, those people never get hired, so the 'success data' never includes them, which reinforces the model's belief that they're poor candidates. The bias feeds itself."

**[On-screen callout]:** "Bias in data → Bias in model → Bias in outcomes → Back into data"

---

## [03:45–04:45] WHY AI DECISIONS ARE HARD TO CHALLENGE

*[Visual: Jordan trying to get answers. Phone call, email, dead ends. Then a "black box" graphic]*

> "Jordan wants to know why they weren't selected. They call the company's HR line. They're told: 'We receive a high volume of applications and aren't able to provide individual feedback.' They send an email. Same canned response."

> "Even if Jordan knew an algorithm was involved, challenging that decision is extremely difficult — for several reasons."

> "**Opacity.** Many AI systems used in hiring are proprietary. Companies are not legally required to disclose that they use AI screening, explain how the model works, or reveal the criteria it uses. Jordan can't appeal a score they don't know exists."

> "**Scale.** These systems make decisions about millions of people. No individual decision is reviewed unless someone flags it — and the systems are designed to minimize human review. The whole point is efficiency."

> "**Legality gaps.** In most U.S. jurisdictions, there is no clear legal right to be told you were screened by an AI, to know what criteria were used, or to challenge an automated decision. Europe is ahead of the U.S. here — the EU's GDPR includes a 'right to explanation' for automated decisions. But in many places, Jordan has no legal recourse."

> "**Plausible deniability.** If someone challenged a human hiring manager for discrimination, the manager's reasoning would be on record. An algorithm's decision is diffuse — the company can always say 'the system decided, not us.' Responsibility disappears into the model."

**[On-screen callout]:** "Black box + no disclosure + no appeal = no accountability"

---

## [04:45–05:45] WHAT ACCOUNTABILITY LOOKS LIKE

*[Visual: split screen — advocacy, regulation, auditing, transparency]*

> "The picture so far might feel pretty bleak. But accountability is possible — and it's being built, slowly, in several ways."

> "**Algorithmic audits.** Some companies now voluntarily submit their AI systems to third-party audits — independent evaluations that test whether a model produces discriminatory outcomes. New York City passed a law in 2023 requiring employers to audit AI hiring tools for bias and disclose results publicly. It's a start."

> "**Regulation.** The EU's AI Act, passed in 2024, classifies AI systems used in employment as 'high risk' — requiring documentation, transparency, and human oversight. Similar legislation is being debated in U.S. states and at the federal level."

> "**Litigation.** Class action lawsuits have been filed against companies whose AI systems have been shown to discriminate — in hiring, in facial recognition, in credit lending. Legal accountability is slow, but it exists."

> "**Community advocacy.** Organizations like the Algorithmic Justice League, the AI Now Institute, and local housing and labor advocates are documenting harms, pressuring companies, and educating affected communities. Sometimes that means helping someone like Jordan understand their rights."

> "**Design choices.** Individual engineers, product managers, and company leaders can choose to build more accountable systems — with bias testing baked in, with human-in-the-loop review for consequential decisions, with transparency about how models work. Accountability isn't just external pressure; it's also internal culture."

**[On-screen callout]:** "Audits · Regulation · Litigation · Advocacy · Design — accountability requires all of these."

---

## [05:45–06:45] THE BIGGER PICTURE — SYSTEMIC VS. INDIVIDUAL

*[Visual: zoom out from Jordan's laptop to a wide shot of a city, then a network of AI decision points]*

> "It's tempting to frame Jordan's story as bad luck, or as an edge case. But that framing misses something important."

> "Jordan's experience isn't a bug in the system. In many ways, it's the system working exactly as designed — optimizing for efficiency and pattern-matching, without any mechanism to check whether those patterns encode historical injustice."

> "The people most likely to be harmed by biased AI are the people who were already marginalized by the systems AI is learning from. Redlined neighborhoods. Underfunded schools. Industries that excluded women and minorities for generations. AI doesn't erase that history. It automates it."

> "And the scale is staggering. The same algorithm that filtered out Jordan is making similar decisions for thousands of people every day. One biased model, deployed across thousands of employers, can have a more profound effect on who gets opportunity than any individual hiring manager's prejudice ever could."

> "This is why AI fairness isn't just a technical problem — it's a social and political problem. The technical fix matters, but so does asking: who is this system designed to serve? Who has power over it? Who gets to challenge it?"

**[On-screen callout]:** "AI automates patterns — including unjust ones. Scale amplifies the harm."

---

## [06:45–07:45] WRAP-UP — WHAT YOU CAN DO

*[Visual returns to Jordan, this time looking up information, researching rights, connecting with others]*

> "So where does this leave us? And where does it leave Jordan?"

> "Jordan, in our story, eventually found a job — through a referral, from a friend, which bypassed the algorithm entirely. That's meaningful. But it also illustrates the problem: the people most likely to have the right referral are often the people who were already connected, already privileged. The system rewards what it always rewarded."

> "But here's what's also true: this is a moment of real change. More people are paying attention to algorithmic harm than ever before. Laws are being passed. Lawsuits are being won. Engineers are pushing back from inside companies. Communities are organizing."

> "**What can you do?**"

> "Learn the landscape — know that AI is making decisions about you, in hiring, in lending, in school discipline systems, in healthcare."

> "Ask questions — when you're affected by a decision, ask whether AI was involved and what your rights are."

> "Support accountability — advocate for transparency laws, support organizations doing this work, hold companies and institutions to higher standards."

> "Build differently — if you go into tech, build systems that are auditable, explainable, and designed with affected communities at the table."

> "And stay curious — because this field is moving fast, and informed citizens are its most important check."

**[On-screen text]:** "The algorithm doesn't have to be the last word."
