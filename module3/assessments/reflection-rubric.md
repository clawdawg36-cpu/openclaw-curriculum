# Reflection Rubric
## "One thing AI reasons well; one thing it doesn't — explain why"

**Module 3 — Written Reflection Assessment**

---

## The Prompt

> **Write a 1-page reflection** (approx. 250–350 words) responding to the following:
>
> *Based on your experience with OpenClaw this module — in the Logic Lab, the case studies, and building your Pet Advisor — identify ONE thing AI reasons well and ONE thing it doesn't. For each, explain WHY using what you learned about how AI actually works (features, patterns, decision trees, chain-of-thought, hallucination).*

**Format:** Typed or neatly handwritten. At least two paragraphs (one per claim). Include your name, date, and class period.

---

## Scoring Guide

| Score | What it means |
|:---:|---|
| **4 — Exceeds** | Insightful, specific, and grounded in evidence from class activities |
| **3 — Meets** | Clear, accurate, and complete; demonstrates understanding |
| **2 — Approaching** | Partially correct or vague; some understanding present |
| **1 — Beginning** | Claim is made but unsupported, or significantly inaccurate |

## Rubric Dimensions

### Dimension 1: Claim — AI Reasons Well

*Does the student make a clear, specific claim about something AI does well?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|:---:|---|
| **4** | Names a specific task or domain (e.g., "following multi-step arithmetic when prompted with chain-of-thought" or "routing questions to the right skill based on keywords"); the claim is precise enough that someone could test it. |
| **3** | Makes a clear claim (e.g., "AI is good at pattern recognition in language"); some specificity but could be more concrete. |
| **2** | Makes a vague claim ("AI is smart at math") without specifying what kind of task or under what conditions. |
| **1** | No clear claim; or claim is about AI in general ("AI can do a lot of things") with no connection to reasoning specifically. |

**Score:** _____ / 4

### Dimension 2: Claim — AI Doesn't Reason Well

*Does the student make a clear, specific claim about something AI struggles with?*

| Score | Descriptor |
|:---:|---|
| **4** | Names a specific failure mode (e.g., "AI hallucinates when asked about facts it wasn't trained on" or "it can't handle a scenario that falls outside its decision tree's branches"); ties to a real example from class or their own experience. |
| **3** | Makes a clear claim about an AI limitation (e.g., "AI struggles with common sense reasoning or novel situations"); reasonably specific. |
| **2** | Names a limitation but it's generic ("AI sometimes makes mistakes") or focused on something unrelated to reasoning (e.g., "it types slowly"). |
| **1** | Limitation stated but not meaningful (e.g., "AI can't feel emotions") or not connected to reasoning at all. |

## Exemplars

The following examples illustrate what each score level looks like in practice.

---

### Score 4 Exemplar (Exceeds)

> *"One thing AI reasons well is following multi-step logic when you tell it to think step by step. In the Logic Lab, I asked OpenClaw a word problem without any instructions and it got the wrong answer. Then I added 'think through this step by step' and it broke the problem into smaller parts and got it right. This happened because chain-of-thought prompting forces the model to use its pattern-matching abilities more carefully — instead of jumping to the most likely next word, it traces a path through intermediate steps, like following branches in a decision tree.*
>
> *One thing AI doesn't reason well is handling situations it was never trained on. When I built my Pet Advisor tree, I realized it couldn't recommend a pet for someone with severe allergies AND a small apartment AND a very busy schedule — that combination just wasn't in the paths I built. Real AI has the same problem: it hallucinates when users ask about facts outside its knowledge base, because it generates the most statistically likely answer rather than admitting it doesn't know. This is why hallucination is dangerous — the AI sounds confident even when it's wrong."*

**Why this is a 4:** Specific examples from class activities (Logic Lab, Pet Advisor); correct and contextual use of vocabulary (chain-of-thought, decision tree, knowledge base, hallucination, pattern-matching); clear causal explanation for both strength and weakness.

---

### Score 3 Exemplar (Meets)

> *"AI is really good at recognizing patterns in language. When we used chain-of-thought prompting in class, the AI was able to work through math problems pretty well as long as they followed a pattern it had seen before.*
>
> *But AI struggles with hallucination — making up facts that sound real. We talked about this in the case studies and it's a real problem because people trust AI and don't check. I think this happens because AI doesn't really understand things, it just predicts the next word."*

**Why this is a 3:** Both claims are present and reasonably specific; uses two vocabulary terms correctly (chain-of-thought, hallucination); references class activities; explanation is mostly correct but could go deeper on the "why."

---

### Score 2 Exemplar (Approaching)

> *"One thing AI is good at is math and logic. We saw it answer questions in class.*
>
> *One thing AI is bad at is when it lies. It can say wrong things very confidently. This is kind of a problem because people might believe it."*

**Why this is a 2:** Claims are present but vague ("math and logic" is too broad; "lies" isn't the right framing); no module vocabulary used; reference to class is too vague to confirm engagement; no explanation of WHY AI behaves this way.

---

### Score 1 Exemplar (Beginning)

> *"AI is good at a lot of things and bad at other things. I think it reasons well when you ask it questions. It doesn't reason well when it doesn't know the answer. I learned a lot in this module about AI."*

**Why this is a 1:** No specific claims; no vocabulary; no evidence; no explanation of reasoning mechanisms; could have been written without attending class.
