# 📋 Agent Design Journal — Teacher Guide

**Document:** `agent-design-journal.md`
**Curriculum:** OpenClaw AI Curriculum (7 Modules)
**Audience:** Teachers, Instructors, Workshop Facilitators

---

## Overview

The Agent Design Journal is the **primary portfolio artifact** for the OpenClaw AI Curriculum. Students maintain this document throughout all 7 modules, documenting the progressive development of their AI agent from a simple chatbot (Module 1) to a fully deployed, ethical AI system (Module 7).

The journal serves three purposes:
1. **Learning tool** — forces reflection and synthesis after each module
2. **Assessment artifact** — provides evidence of understanding for grading
3. **Showcase document** — shareable with parents, administrators, and at school events

---

## How to Distribute the Journal

### Digital Format (Recommended)
- Share `agent-design-journal.md` with students via Google Drive, GitHub, Notion, or your LMS
- Students keep their own copy and fill it in as they go
- Collect via submission link or have students share their copy with you

### Print Format
- The journal renders well when printed from any Markdown viewer (VS Code, Typora, GitHub, etc.)
- Recommend printing double-sided, booklet style (11×17 folded = A4 booklet)
- Students can paste screenshots and draw avatars by hand on printed copies
- Provide a 3-ring binder or folded booklet for physical storage

### Hybrid (Best of Both)
- Students keep digital copies for screenshots and code snippets
- Print the summary card and reflection pages for a physical display at AI Expo

## When to Check the Journal

| Checkpoint | When | What to Review |
|------------|------|----------------|
| **Module 1 Check-in** | End of M1 | Cover page complete, system prompt written, M1 reflection done |
| **Mid-course Check** | End of M3 | M1–M3 pages, capability tracker updated through M3 |
| **Ethics Review** | End of M6 | Usage policy, bias findings, guardrails — these matter! |
| **Capstone Review** | End of M7 | Full journal, "At a Glance" card complete, glossary has ≥10 terms |
| **Portfolio Submission** | Final week | Complete journal submitted as portfolio artifact |

**Tip:** Do a quick 5-minute journal check at the start of each module — it doubles as a warm-up review of what students built in the previous module.

## Grading Rubric

### Option A: Completion-Based (Recommended for Middle School)

Grade based on *completion and honest effort*, not correctness.

| Component | Points | Criteria |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Cover page | 5 | Name, agent name, avatar present |
| M1–M7 module pages | 5 pts each = 35 | Each section meaningfully filled in (not "idk") |
| Reflections (7 × 3 questions) | 21 | Each reflection answered in 1+ sentence |
| Capability tracker | 5 | Accurately checked, matches their agent |
| Glossary | 9 | At least 9 terms defined in student's own words |
| "At a Glance" card | 10 | Complete, thoughtful, filled in at Module 7 |
| Agent avatar / visual | 5 | Some visual representation present |
| **Total** | **90** | |

*Adjust weighting based on your class level and pacing.*

### Option B: Quality Rubric (Recommended for High School)

Use a 4-point scale per section:

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **4 — Exemplary** | Detailed, specific, shows deep understanding. Goes beyond the prompt. |
| **3 — Proficient** | Complete and accurate. Shows solid understanding of the concept. |
| **2 — Developing** | Attempted but vague, incomplete, or shows surface-level understanding. |
| **1 — Beginning** | Minimal effort. Filled in but does not demonstrate understanding. |
| **0 — Missing** | Section left blank or "N/A" without justification. |

**Sections to evaluate with quality rubric:**
- System prompt (M1) — does it actually shape agent behavior?
- Bias findings (M6) — did they look seriously, or just write "no bias found"?
- Expo notes (M7) — are they specific? Did they engage with audience questions?
- Reflections — are they genuine? Do they show growth?

### Grading the Ethics Section (M6) — Special Guidance

M6 deserves careful attention. Look for:
- **Honesty about bias** — students who say "I didn't find any bias" likely didn't look hard enough. Ask follow-up questions.
- **Specificity in guardrails** — "be nice" is not a guardrail. "Refuse requests to write insults about specific people" is.
- **Personal stance** — the final prompt ("I believe AI should be used responsibly because...") should feel personal, not copied.

## Using the Journal as a Portfolio Artifact

### For Parent-Teacher Conferences
The journal is an excellent conversation starter. Suggested talking points:
- *"Here's the AI agent your student built over the semester."*
- Show the capability tracker — it makes progress tangible.
- Read a reflection together — it shows metacognition and growth.
- The "At a Glance" card makes a great one-page leave-behind.

### For School Showcases / AI Expo
- Have students display their journal (printed or on screen) alongside their live demo
- The journal serves as "documentation" — it shows *process*, not just product
- Judges and visitors can flip through it while the student talks
- Consider awarding a "Best Journal" recognition alongside technical prizes

### For Administrators / Accreditation
The journal provides evidence of:
- Student learning progression over time (portfolio-based assessment)
- Integration of ethics and responsible AI into the curriculum
- Student voice and reflection (not just rote learning)
- Cross-curricular skills: writing, critical thinking, technical literacy

## Discussion Prompts by Module

Use these to spark class conversation when reviewing journals:

**M1 — Identity**
- "Who wants to share their agent's name and one personality trait?"
- "Did anyone write a system prompt that surprised them by how their agent behaved?"

**M2 — Vision**
- "What's the coolest thing your agent noticed in an image?"
- "Did anything go wrong when you connected the vision model? What did you learn?"

**M3 — Knowledge**
- "What does your agent know now that it didn't before?"
- "What's a question your agent still can't answer? Why?"

**M4 — Learning**
- "What was hardest about training — collecting data, preparing it, or evaluating results?"
- "Did your model learn something unexpected?"

**M5 — Voice**
- "What does it feel like to text your own AI agent?"
- "Did anyone get a response from their agent that made them laugh or confused them?"

**M6 — Ethics**
- "What bias surprised you most?"
- "Do you think your guardrails are enough? What would make them stronger?"

**M7 — Capstone**
- "What was the best question someone asked at the expo?"
- "What would you build next if you had six more months?"

## Differentiation Tips

### For Students Who Need More Support
- Pre-fill some fields with sentence starters (e.g., "My agent's name is ___ because ___")
- Allow voice recordings instead of written reflections
- Pair with a "journal buddy" who checks in each module
- Provide a simplified 1-page version of each module page

### For Advanced Students
- Require GitHub commits of their journal alongside code
- Ask them to include actual code snippets in the module pages
- Challenge them to write reflections as blog posts or video essays
- Have them create a "second agent" and compare journals

### For English Language Learners
- Allow reflections in native language with a brief English summary
- Provide the glossary terms pre-listed with simple example sentences
- Partner with a bilingual student for module page check-ins

## Common Issues & How to Handle Them

**"I don't know what to write for the reflection."**
> Ask: "What was one thing you typed into your terminal (or notebook) today that you didn't expect to work?" That usually unlocks something.

**Student fills in everything with one word or "it's cool."**
> Don't accept it. Ask them to read it back to you and explain. Then ask them to write what they just said. Verbal → written.

**Student skips the ethics section or writes "no bias found."**
> Prompt: "If your agent was trained mostly on data from one country or one type of person, whose experience might it not understand? Write about that."

**Student loses or forgets their journal.**
> This happens. Keep a backup copy in your LMS. If physical, scan completed pages periodically.

**Two students have nearly identical journals.**
> Check their agents — are they the same? If so, treat it like any co-created project (shared credit). If not, something's off. Have each student do a verbal walk-through of their M6 page.

## Printing & Formatting Notes

The journal is designed to be used as-is from the Markdown file. To print:

1. Open `agent-design-journal.md` in a Markdown viewer with good print support:
   - **VS Code** + Markdown PDF extension
   - **Typora** → File → Export → PDF
   - **GitHub** → view rendered → print from browser
   - **Pandoc** (command line): `pandoc agent-design-journal.md -o journal.pdf`

2. Recommended print settings:
   - Paper: Letter (8.5×11) or A4
   - Margins: 0.75 inch on all sides
   - Font: 11pt, readable
   - The ASCII box art renders correctly in monospace fonts

3. For a booklet format: print 2-up, double-sided, fold in half
